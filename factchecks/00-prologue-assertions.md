## Critical / Immediate Review

### CONTRADICTED #1 — "Twelve to fifteen percent of K-12 teachers use AI tools daily"

**Sentence (line 17):** *"On the other side of the desk, **only twelve to fifteen percent of K-12 teachers use AI tools daily**, and most of that use is concentrated in administrative tasks..."*

- **Verdict:** CONTRADICTED in framing — the 12–15% daily figure cannot be located in the cited EdWeek January 2026 article, and the closest comparable surveys point to different numbers.
- **Reason:** The EdWeek article cited ("More Teachers Are Using AI in Their Classrooms. Here's Why," 2026-01) reports that **61% of teachers used AI "in some capacity"** in 2025 (up from 34% in 2023 and 32% in 2024), but does not break out a daily-use figure of 12–15%. The Gallup/Walton "Teaching for Tomorrow" 2025 report — the dominant primary source on teacher-AI frequency — found 60% of K-12 teachers used AI during the 2024–25 year with **32% at least weekly**, but does not separate out daily users. There is no published primary survey I could surface giving the 12–15% daily figure as stated.
- **Recommendation:** Either soften to "fewer than a third of K-12 teachers use AI weekly" with a citation to the Gallup/Walton 2025 figures, or cite the specific table/page from EdWeek where 12–15% is reported (if it exists in the full PDF and not just the web summary). As written, the number reads precise but cannot be sourced.
- **Source attempted:** [EdWeek 2026-01](https://www.edweek.org/technology/more-teachers-are-using-ai-in-their-classrooms-heres-why/2026/01); [Gallup/Walton 2025](https://www.gallup.com/poll/691967/three-teachers-weekly-saving-six-weeks-year.aspx); [Walton "Teaching for Tomorrow" PDF](https://static.waltonfamilyfoundation.org/df/fb/eba12807470a9402d7433cc47dba/teaching-for-tomorrow-unlocking-six-weeks-a-year-with-ai-report.pdf)

### CONTRADICTED #2 — AACSB "7% daily, 18% weekly, 67% occasionally or rarely"

**Sentence (line 17):** *"In higher education, an AACSB survey of business-school faculty found only 7% report daily use and 18% weekly; 67% use AI occasionally or rarely."*

- **Verdict:** CONTRADICTED. The AACSB 2025 GenAI Adoption report does not publish the 7% / 18% / 67% breakdown as stated. The closest figures from the AACSB report are: 39% occasionally (a few times a month), 28% rarely (a few times a year), and 25% frequently or very frequently. Faculty have not been broken out by AACSB into 7% daily and 18% weekly publicly — those numbers may be inferred from the 25% "frequently/very frequently" total or may be from a different source entirely. The 67% sum is plausible if "occasionally" + "rarely" = 39% + 28% = 67%, which would make the chapter's "67%" defensible but the daily/weekly split misstated.
- **Recommendation:** Re-state as "Only 25% of business-school faculty use GenAI frequently or very frequently; 67% use it occasionally or rarely" and cite the AACSB report directly. The 7% / 18% daily/weekly figures need either a direct page-cite or removal.
- **Source:** [AACSB GenAI Adoption Report 2025](https://www.aacsb.edu/insights/reports/2025/genai-adoption-in-business-schools-deans-and-faculty-respond); [BestColleges summary](https://www.bestcolleges.com/news/business-school-ai-policies/)

## Single Sharpest Factual Problem

**The "twelve to fifteen percent of K-12 teachers use AI tools daily" figure on line 17.**

This is the prologue's load-bearing comparison number — it sits opposite the 92% student adoption figure and creates the structural "students-vs-teachers gap" that the entire book hinges on. If the gap is real, the book's argument lands. If the gap number is wrong, the framing of the book wobbles before chapter one begins.

The number is presented with the precision of a survey result ("twelve to fifteen percent") and is cited to EdWeek January 2026. But the EdWeek article cited does not publish a 12–15% daily-use figure. The dominant primary source on teacher-AI frequency (Gallup/Walton 2025) reports 60% any-use, 32% weekly — a substantially different picture, and one in which the student-teacher "gap" looks smaller than the prologue claims. Other surveys (Engageli aggregating multiple sources) report 53% of core-subject teachers using AI for instruction.

A reader who follows the EdWeek link and does not find 12–15% will lose trust in the whole chapter. A reviewer who pulls the Gallup data will notice that the most defensible "daily" figure is closer to 10–15% if extrapolated from the weekly figure, but that extrapolation is a guess, not a survey result.

**Fix:** Either (a) cite a specific survey that reports the 12–15% daily figure directly, with page or table cite, or (b) replace with "fewer than a third of K-12 teachers use AI weekly, and daily use among teachers is rare" citing the Gallup/Walton 2025 data, or (c) restate the gap in terms of *any* student use (62% middle/high) vs *weekly* teacher use (32% Gallup) — still a real gap, but one the data actually supports.

The 68% / chance-not-preparation claim on line 19 is also a candidate for sharpest-problem, but the author has self-flagged it inline. The 12–15% figure is unflagged and is doing structural work for the book's premise. That makes it the more dangerous error.
