### CRITICAL #1 — Demszky CS-course uptake gain misstated: chapter says 13%, paper reports 24%

**Sentence (line 73):** "In a randomized trial published in *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*, Demszky and colleagues gave treatment-condition instructors in Stanford's large free online CS course a weekly email containing personalized, automated feedback on their uptake score — generated by the NLP tool, delivered to an inbox, requiring no human coach, no released time, no additional training infrastructure. Treatment instructors who engaged with the emails increased their uptake of student contributions by approximately 13 percent relative to control. An email. Read it. Go back to teaching. Thirteen percent."

**Issue:** The paper — Demszky, Liu, Hill, Jurafsky, Piech (2024). "Can Automated Feedback Improve Teachers' Uptake of Student Ideas? Evidence From a Randomized Controlled Trial in a Large-Scale Online Course." *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis* — reports that the tool improves instructors' uptake of student contributions by **24%**, not 13%. This is in the Stanford-led RCT in Code in Place (online CS course, 1,136 instructors). The 13% figure is not found in the paper. **This is a factual error that materially understates the published finding.** Recommended fix: change "approximately 13 percent" to "approximately 24 percent" (or pin to the paper's specific quoted figure). The follow-up "An email. Read it. Go back to teaching. Thirteen percent." rhetorical kicker also needs the number updated.

**Suggested reference:** Demszky, D., Liu, J., Hill, H. C., Jurafsky, D., & Piech, C. (2024). "Can Automated Feedback Improve Teachers' Uptake of Student Ideas? Evidence From a Randomized Controlled Trial in a Large-Scale Online Course." *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*. https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/01623737231169270

Search results consistently confirm only the 20% headline increase, p<0.01.

**Suggested reference:** Name the Australian critic explicitly (likely candidates: Selwyn, N.; Williamson, B.; or Southgate, E.). For the M-Powering Teachers figure, verify: Demszky, D., et al. ### STAT — MINOR DISCREPANCY
**Assertion type:** BASIC
**Sentence (line 25):** "Translate 5.9 hours per week against a 36-week academic calendar and you get roughly six full school weeks per year."
**Claim checked:** 36-week academic calendar arithmetic.
**Finding:** Gallup itself uses 37.4 weeks ("over the course of a 37.4-week school year") to arrive at the "six weeks" headline. 5.9 × 36 = 212.4 hours ÷ 35 (a typical weekly load) ≈ 6.1 weeks; 5.9 × 37.4 ÷ 35 ≈ 6.3 weeks. Either denominator gets the chapter to "six full school weeks." The discrepancy is minor (36 vs 37.4) and the result is preserved. Recommend either tracking Gallup's 37.4 figure or noting that the chapter is using a slightly more conservative weekly count.
**Expert review needed:** Light — track Gallup's 37.4 or note the choice.
