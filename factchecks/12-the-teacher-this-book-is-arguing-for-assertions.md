## Critical / Immediate Review

### CRITICAL #1 — Opening vignette: composite disclosure arrives 16 lines after the vignette begins

**Sentences (lines 7–19, then line 23):** The vignette establishes a specific seventh-grade humanities teacher across four named weekdays with specific time stamps (7:42 AM, 8:10 AM, "fifteen minutes between her last morning class and her lunch supervision shift"), a named student ("Marcus"), named tools ("Diffit, MagicSchool, a direct Claude prompt"), a named protocol family ("Harkness-style guide"), and a specific student-outcome arc ("his older brother dropped out last spring"). The composite disclosure does not arrive until line 23: *"Let me be careful with what kind of claim the opening just made... This is not a vision of a classroom that does not yet exist. It is a description, assembled from documented practice, of what trained teachers who use AI purposefully are already doing in classrooms across the country."* And line 33: *"She is also not a composite who exists in abundant supply. She exists in fragments. A 2024 Edutopia profile here. A district fellows cohort there. A Khanmigo pilot in Puerto Rico."*

- **Verdict:** FLAGGED (disclosure gap).
- **Issue:** The opening reads as documented reportage for the entire vignette. A reader who does not continue to §"A description, not a vision" will leave with the impression that the chapter described a specific teacher's specific week. The Introduction (line 59) did disclose the AI+1 teacher as composite up front, which mitigates the issue at book level — but a reader who comes to Ch. 12 first (the book is explicitly designed to be readable in non-linear order — see Introduction line 88: *"each chapter is also written to stand on its own"*) will not have that priming. CLAUDE.md §6 (specificity), §7 hard rule 1 (no fabricated specifics), and the book's broader Montaigne-register commitment all push toward inline labeling.
- **Also at issue:** The §"A description" passage names fragments — "A 2024 Edutopia profile here. A district fellows cohort there. A Khanmigo pilot in Puerto Rico" — without specific citations. The Edutopia profile is not named, the district fellows cohort is not named, the Khanmigo Puerto Rico pilot is name-only (the pilot is real — see Ch. 8 fact-check, references — but the specific behaviors attributed to "the teacher" in the vignette are not pin-cited to any one of these fragments).
- **Action required:** Either (a) move the composite disclosure into the vignette opener ("In a classroom that does not yet exist anywhere intact but exists in fragments across the country, it is 7:42 on a Monday morning..."), or (b) replace the vignette with a single named teacher whose week is documented (the Edutopia profile, the Khanmigo pilot teacher, the Phone Fridays teacher from Ch. 9), or (c) add a one-line label at the top of the chapter ("*The following vignette is composite, assembled from documented practice across the chapters of this book.*"). Option (c) is the lightest fix.
- **Suggested reference:** Cross-reference the Introduction's composite disclosure (line 59) for the language to mirror.

**Sentences (chapter body):** The chapter does not reference Schoenfeld, the "army bus" problem, or *Mathematical Problem Solving* anywhere in the body text.

- **Verdict:** CONTRADICTED (orphaned citation).
- **Issue:** The back matter lists Schoenfeld 1985 as the first citation for Chapter 12, but the chapter body never invokes Schoenfeld, never uses the army bus problem (which is the Schoenfeld example from p. 28 of *Mathematical Problem Solving*: "An army bus holds 36 soldiers. The fact-check brief flagged this as a chapter element that should be verifiable; it is not present to verify. The brief's flag was based on prior outline material that did not make it into the draft.
- **Action required:** Either (a) remove the Schoenfeld 1985 citation from the back matter (if the army bus / mathematical-problem-solving move was cut from the draft), or (b) restore the Schoenfeld move to the chapter body (likely in §"For teachers" → "Treat AI output the way a serious clinician treats a lab result" where the "fluent practice / closed-book assessment / metacognitive miscalibration" theme would benefit from the army-bus illustration: students who answer correctly without checking whether the answer makes sense).
- **Cross-reference:** See 99-back-matter fact-check, EVIDENCE — CONFIRMED entry on Schoenfeld 1985 citation correctness. The citation is bibliographically right; it just doesn't have anything in the chapter to anchor.
- **Suggested reference:** Schoenfeld, A. H. (1985). *Mathematical Problem Solving*. Academic Press. ISBN 0-12-628870-4 — keep if restored to body, drop if not.
