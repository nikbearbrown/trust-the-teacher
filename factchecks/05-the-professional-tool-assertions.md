## Critical — Requires Immediate Expert Review

### CRITICAL #1 — Cook 2011 corpus counts overstated

**Sentence (line 41):** "In 2011, a team of researchers led by David Cook at the Mayo Clinic published a meta-analysis in *JAMA* that has become, in surgical education, something close to a canonical text. They screened 10,903 articles. They pooled 609 studies. They enrolled 35,226 trainees and compared the outcomes of simulation-based training — box trainers, virtual-reality surgical simulators, task trainers — to no intervention. The pooled effect on trainee skill outcomes was above $d = 1.0$. ... The pooled effect on *actual patient outcomes* — real patients, real operations, tracked afterward — was $d = 0.50$."

**Issue:** The Cook et al. 2011 JAMA paper ("Technology-Enhanced Simulation for Health Professions Education: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis," JAMA 306(9): 978-988) is the right paper. The "patient-related outcomes" pooled effect of d ≈ 0.50 (95% CI 0.34-0.66) from 32 studies is **correct**. However, the chapter's "609 studies, 35,226 trainees" appears to be a misreading. The Cook 2011 paper reports outcomes broken across categories: 118 studies for knowledge, 210 for time-skills, 426 for process-skills, 54 for product-skills, 20 for time-behaviors, 50 for other-behaviors, and 32 for patient effects — sums vary depending on how outcome categories are aggregated, and the total "609 studies / 35,226 trainees" figure could not be directly verified in this pass. The "10,903 articles screened" figure also could not be confirmed against the paper. The chapter should pin these numbers against the published Cook 2011 paper directly. The skill-outcome effect "above d = 1.0" needs the specific outcome category — Cook reported large effects for skills/knowledge categories but the precise d depends on which (skill-process vs skill-product vs time). The framing the chapter draws — "trainee skill outcomes above 1.0; patient outcomes ~0.50" — is qualitatively accurate but the precise n and study counts need verification against the paper text.

**Suggested reference:** Cook, D. A., Hatala, R., Brydges, R., Zendejas, B., Szostek, J. H., Wang, A. T., Erwin, P. J., & Hamstra, S. J. (2011). "Technology-Enhanced Simulation for Health Professions Education: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *JAMA* 306(9): 978-988. https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/1104300
