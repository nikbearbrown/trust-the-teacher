## Critical / Immediate Review

### CRITICAL #1 — Preface opener is a composite; in-line disclosure is missing

**Sentences (lines 35–37):** "A piece of software arrives in a school district. It costs $500,000. Nobody trains the teachers who are supposed to use it. Two years later, sixty-seven percent of the licenses are unused. The district buys new software."

- **Verdict:** FLAGGED (composite, disclosed only in back matter).
- **Issue:** Per the chapter's own back-matter Note 1 (line 45 of `99-back-matter.md`): *"The $500,000 unused-license scenario in the Preface is a composite drawn from multiple documented cases of district EdTech procurement failure, including the Evelyn Learning Solutions analysis of license-utilization data across U.S. districts and the LAUSD Yondr rollout subversion documented by EdSource and NBC News."* The composite labeling lives in an endnote. The reader experiencing the opening four sentences will read them as one concrete case — a documented $500K-per-district, two-year, 67%-unused pattern. CLAUDE.md §6 ("Specificity of reference: Name papers, people, products, institutions. Never 'some researchers'…") and §7 hard rule 1 (no fabricated specifics) both point toward inline labeling. The 67% unused-license figure is confirmed against Glimpse K12 (see Ch. 2 fact-check, CONFIRMED #2). The $500,000 dollar figure and the two-year timeline are the composite-specific details.
- **Action required:** Either (a) label the opener as composite in-line ("a pattern that has played out, with the same arc, in district after district..."), or (b) replace with a named documented case at the same level of specificity (Glimpse K12 audit pull-quote, LAUSD Yondr rollout figure, or the EdWeek Market Brief $1B figure), or (c) keep as written and accept that some readers will read the composite as reportage. Option (a) is the cleanest fit with the book's stated method.
- **Suggested reference:** Glimpse K12 / EdWeek Market Brief 2019: https://marketbrief.edweek.org/meeting-district-needs/k-12-districts-wasting-millions-by-not-using-purchased-software-new-analysis-finds/2019/05

### SPECIALIST — UNVERIFIED
**Assertion type:** BASIC
**Sentence (line 71):** "Cognitive offloading is real. Students using AI without scaffolding learn less than students using nothing. The fluency trap — the feeling of understanding that AI's smooth outputs produce — is one of the most documented failure modes in the recent literature."
**Claim checked:** "Fluency trap" as a documented term in the recent AI-and-learning literature.
**Finding:** Partially confirmed in substance. The mechanism — metacognitive miscalibration when interacting with smooth AI outputs — is documented in Bastani et al. (2025) (students predicted they had learned more than they had), in the Microsoft Lee et al. (2025) CHI paper on critical thinking, and in the broader judgment-of-learning (JOL) literature (Bjork & Bjork on desirable difficulties; Carpenter et al. on fluency illusions). The **specific term "fluency trap"** is the book's own coinage as far as search-result evidence reveals; it does not appear to be an established term in the cognitive-science literature in this form. The glossary in `99-back-matter.md` (line 317) defines it accurately and attributes the underlying mechanism to Carpenter et al. 2013 and Bjork & Bjork 1992, which is correct.
**Expert review needed:** Light — the framing "one of the most documented failure modes" is correct *for the mechanism*; the specific phrase "fluency trap" should be labeled as the book's own term rather than implied to be a literature standard. (The glossary handles this correctly.)
**Suggested reference:** Carpenter, S. K. et al. (2013) on judgments of learning; Bjork, R. A., & Bjork, E. L. (1992); Bastani et al. (2025) for the empirical demonstration.

**Expert review needed:** Light — strawman risk only.
