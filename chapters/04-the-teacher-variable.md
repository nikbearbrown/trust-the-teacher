# Chapter 4: The Teacher Variable

*What forty years of education research shows about the one thing that actually predicts whether students learn*

---

Imagine two elementary schools, three miles apart, in the same suburban district. Same per-pupil funding. Same demographic mix down to the percentage point on free-and-reduced lunch. Same district-issued Chromebook on every desk. Same district-licensed AI tutor and same adaptive math platform installed on every Chromebook. Same state standards, same pacing guide, same end-of-year assessment.

In School A, the principal spent the first year of the AI rollout the way the district expected — one PD day in August, a vendor walkthrough, a teacher leader designated as "the AI person," a dashboard the assistant superintendent could pull up at board meetings. In School B, the principal did something the district didn't ask for and didn't initially fund. She protected ninety minutes a week of common planning time, hired a coach from the regional service agency for two days a month, and required every grade-level team to bring one piece of student work to the meeting that the AI tool had been involved in producing. Teachers compared. Teachers fought. By spring of year one, the third-grade team had rewritten the way they used the platform's writing-feedback feature. By spring of year two, the fifth-grade team had decided to turn the math tutor off during fluency practice and back on during application problems, and could tell you exactly why.

Two years in, the gap between the two schools on the district's own assessments is large enough that the central office can't explain it with the variables it usually tracks. The tools are identical. The kids are matched. The schedule is the same. The variable that diverged is the one nobody put a line item against.

This is not a hypothetical I invented to set up the chapter. It is the modal finding of every large-scale EdTech evaluation conducted in the last fifteen years, including [RAND Europe's January 2026 synthesis](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2026/01/harnessing-the-benefits-of-edtech-what-research-tells.html) of six years of large-scale trials: *"implementation fidelity emerged as a critical factor, as many EdTech interventions are only as effective as their implementation. When teachers struggled with training, resources, or integration into daily routines, impact evaporated — even when the underlying approach was promising."* The technology was the constant. The teacher's preparation was the variable. The outcomes followed the variable, not the constant. [verify: locate matched-school case with public outcome data — Quaker Valley vs. neighbor, or comparable, to replace the stylized opener if possible.]

The chapter ahead is the empirical spine of the book. It is the case that the teacher — not the device, not the platform, not the curriculum, not the class size — is the largest moveable variable in education. It is also the case that the most-cited number in the entire teacher-effects literature, the d = 1.57 for collective teacher efficacy that anchors every PD slide deck in America, is methodologically contested in ways the PD slide decks do not mention. I am going to walk you through what the evidence actually shows, what the most serious critiques actually argue, and what survives the critiques. Then I am going to ask you to do something the popular conversation does not do: hold both the strength of the finding and the weakness of the precise number at once. The ranking direction is the message. The magnitudes are not.

## What forty years of meta-analysis is actually doing

Start with the corpus. John Hattie's *Visible Learning* began in 2009 with a synthesis of more than 800 meta-analyses, covering roughly 52,000 underlying studies and on the order of 80 million students. [*Visible Learning: The Sequel*](https://visible-learning.org), published in 2023, expanded the synthesis to **more than 2,100 meta-analyses, approximately 130,000 underlying studies, and roughly 300 million students.** The figure that circulates in the consulting literature — "1,800 studies" — is a confusion of meta-analyses with studies. The corpus is much larger than that, and the unit of analysis is the meta-analysis, not the study. The live database, [Visible Learning MetaX](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/), is updated through November 2024 (Version 1.3) and now attaches a robustness index to each influence to signal how stable the average effect size is across the underlying analyses. [verify: corpus numbers against current visible-learning.org as of draft date.]

Hattie's interpretive convention is the part that has done the most political work. He calls d = 0.40 the "hinge point" — roughly the average effect size across his 300-plus ranked influences, and the value he describes as approximately one year's academic growth. Anything above the hinge is "above average." Anything below it is "below average." This single framing is responsible for more PD slide-deck content than any other number in the field, and it is also the framing Jared Cooney Horvath leans on in *The Digital Delusion* when he reports the overall EdTech effect size at d = 0.29 and concludes that EdTech "does not come close to the minimum threshold for meaningful learning impact in nearly every context."

I want to make a point about that threshold before the chapter goes any further, because the rest of the argument depends on the reader being honest about what the threshold can and cannot do. A 0.40 cutoff treated as a bright line eliminates most of education. Class-size reduction sits at d ≈ 0.21. Comprehensive teacher professional development sits at d ≈ 0.30 to 0.51 depending on which list you read. Homework sits at d ≈ 0.29. Computer-assisted instruction sits at d ≈ 0.47. If d = 0.40 is the line that separates "meaningful" from "meaningless," and the average effect across all 300-plus interventions is, by Hattie's own arithmetic, approximately 0.42, then by mathematical necessity nearly half of all educational practices are "meaningless." That is the reductio. The hinge is a guideline, not a law. The correct way to evaluate an intervention is the way we evaluate every other policy choice — cost per standard-deviation gain, scalability, side effects, distributional impact — not whether it clears an arbitrary bar.

I will come back to the cost-per-gain math later in the chapter, because the inversion it produces is one of the sharpest empirical points the book has. For now, hold this: the d = 0.40 threshold is a framing decision, not a finding. It is the rhetorical move that lets a critic call an entire category of intervention "meaningless." The chapter's argument does not require you to accept it. The chapter's argument requires you to look at the ranking direction across the top of Hattie's list — and across the entirely independent value-added literature that I will introduce in §3 — and notice that the same variable keeps appearing at the top.

## The ranked influences, told honestly

Here is what the current Visible Learning ranking actually says about the influences that matter most to the argument in this book. I am going to report two columns for several of them, because the MetaX values have moved since the canonical 2009 list — sometimes substantially — and the moves matter.

**Collective teacher efficacy.** The most-cited value is d = 1.57. The current MetaX value has been reported to have dropped to somewhere in the range of d = 1.01 to 1.12 after one new low-effect meta-analysis was incorporated in late 2024. [The Economy of Meaning blog](https://theeconomyofmeaning.com/2025/02/08/did-the-effect-of-collective-teaching-efficacy-just-dropped-or-did-john-hatties-team-made-a-mistake/) was the first to flag the drop publicly, and Hattie's team acknowledged the change as a correction in process. The honest number to use in 2026 is "the construct shows a large association with achievement; the precise effect size is unstable; the most defensible reading is somewhere between d = 1.0 and d = 1.6, which is still very large by social-science standards." [verify: current MetaX value for CTE as of draft date.]

**Self-reported grades and student expectations.** d ≈ 1.44 in the canonical list. Still listed among the top influences in MetaX. Stable.

**Teacher credibility.** d ≈ 0.90. Top-tier. Stable.

**Feedback.** The number every PD deck quotes is d = 0.70 to 0.73. The current [MetaX weighted mean is d = 0.50](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/Influences/View/feedback), across 10 meta-analyses, 1,116 studies, and 13,396 students. That is a material revision — the value has dropped by roughly a third from the canonical figure. The Education Endowment Foundation, working from a smaller set of 155 studies under its own inclusion criteria, [reports the equivalent of roughly six additional months of progress](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/teaching-learning-toolkit/feedback) from feedback — about seven months at primary, five at secondary. The phrase *feedback works* survives every version. The phrase *feedback is the highest-effect classroom-level intervention* survives the EEF framing and does not survive the MetaX revision intact.

**Teacher-student relationships.** d ≈ 0.72 in the canonical list. The underlying empirical base is [Roorda et al. (2011)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0034654311421793), a meta-analysis of 99 studies that separated positive and negative relationships and engagement and achievement outcomes. The actual primary-research finding was medium-to-large associations with engagement (correlations in the 0.30 to 0.40 range) and small-to-medium associations with achievement. The d = 0.72 figure aggregates across studies and converts correlations to effect sizes — a mathematically valid step that is, again, partly an artifact of the conversion. The underlying signal is real, replicated, longitudinal, and substantial. The precise headline number is fragile.

**Comprehensive teacher professional development.** d ≈ 0.51 in the older lists. The most rigorous causal estimate in the literature is [Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan's 2018 meta-analysis](https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/mkraft/files/kraft_blazar_hogan_2018_teacher_coaching.pdf) of teacher coaching — 60 causal studies, pooled effect on instruction d = 0.49, pooled effect on student achievement d = 0.18. Two things to notice. First, coaching changes teacher practice substantially. Second, the translation of practice change into student achievement gains is smaller and slower. And third, the effect drops sharply when coaching programs are scaled past the small efficacy trials where they were developed. This is the implementation gap the book has been describing in another vocabulary.

**Computer-assisted instruction.** d ≈ 0.47. Above the hinge.

**Technology in subjects (composite).** d ≈ 0.55. Above the hinge.

**Technology in mathematics.** d ≈ 0.33. Below the hinge.

**Technology in reading and literacy.** d ≈ 0.29. Below the hinge.

**Class size reduction.** d ≈ 0.21. Below the hinge.

I want you to look at that list for one more beat before I unpack the most contested number on it. The top four influences — collective teacher efficacy, student expectations, teacher credibility, teacher-student relationships — are all teacher-mediated. Feedback is teacher-mediated. Comprehensive professional development is the system's intervention on the teacher. The first technology-only intervention shows up below all of them. The first structural variable — class size — sits at the bottom. Even when the magnitudes are revised, even when the critics get to subtract for double-counting and inflated comparisons (and they will, in §3), the *order* survives. The teacher-mediated influences sit at the top of the ranking. The technology and structural influences sit lower. That is the spine of the chapter, and no critique I have found dislodges it.

## The d = 1.57 number, unpacked the way it should have been unpacked the first time

The collective teacher efficacy finding is the chapter's most visible single claim and the one most worth unpacking honestly, because it is the one a careful reader will most reasonably push back on. *One number, d = 1.57, allegedly the biggest effect in the history of education research — where did it come from, and how much weight will it bear?*

The construct itself is older than Hattie. It comes out of [Albert Bandura's self-efficacy work in the 1990s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-efficacy), and was operationalized for schools by [Goddard, Hoy, and Woolfolk Hoy in 2000](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00028312037002479), who built a validated measurement instrument and showed that collective teacher efficacy — the shared faculty belief that "we, together, can move outcomes for our students regardless of background" — predicted achievement differences across urban elementary schools after controlling for socioeconomic status. That primary work is the foundation, and it is solid.

The d = 1.57 figure comes almost entirely from a single secondary source: Rachel Jean Eells's [2011 Loyola University Chicago dissertation](https://ecommons.luc.edu/luc_diss/133/) meta-analyzing 26 underlying studies. Her weighted mean correlation between CTE and school-level achievement, with outliers removed, was r ≈ 0.62. Hattie's team converted that correlation to Cohen's d using the standard formula d = 2r/√(1−r²), which yields approximately 1.57.

Three honest observations have to sit on the table at this point.

First, when the headline "biggest effect in education research" rests on a single PhD dissertation, the marginal contribution of additional meta-analyses is high and uncertain. One more good meta-analysis can move the number a lot. Çoğaltay and Karadağ (2017), the second major CTE meta-analysis, reported r ≈ 0.52 across 35 studies. When MetaX incorporated additional analyses with lower effects in late 2024, the published value reportedly dropped to between 1.01 and 1.12. The number is unstable in the way single-source numbers always are.

Second, a school-level correlation between teacher attitudes and student achievement is not the same kind of quantity as a student-level treatment effect from a randomized trial. The d = 2r/√(1−r²) conversion is mathematically valid. It is also interpretively loaded. Putting a correlation and a treatment effect on the same axis makes them look comparable in the table; it does not make them comparable in mechanism. A reader who treats d = 1.57 the way she treats the d = 0.18 from Kraft et al.'s coaching meta-analysis is doing apples-to-oranges arithmetic without knowing it.

Third — and this is the part the chapter most wants the reader to hold — even at face value, r ≈ 0.62 is a large effect by any standard in the social sciences. The Eells correlation, *uncoverted*, is the kind of association you almost never see in school-effects research. Whether you express it as r = 0.62 or as d = 1.57 or as d = 1.0 after the MetaX revision, the construct has a large, replicated association with school-level achievement that survives controlling for SES. The mechanism — coordinated teacher belief moving practice across an entire faculty — is precisely the thing the RAND Europe synthesis points at when it identifies teacher training and support as the variable that determines whether technology succeeds.

So the honest framing is neither "d = 1.57, end of story" nor "the number is bogus, ignore the finding." The honest framing is that the construct is real, the association is large, the precise magnitude is unstable, and the *mechanism* is what the book's argument depends on. The number is a hook for attention. The mechanism is what does the work.

I will name the mechanism plainly here, because it is the chapter's deep-dive and I want it on the page before the critique section so the reader sees it doing work first. A teacher acquires a new practice — let's say, structured AI-assisted formative feedback. In isolation, that teacher's practice moves her students by some amount, call it δ. When the practice spreads across an entire faculty, three things happen that the isolated teacher cannot produce alone. The practice gets debugged: implementation errors get caught and shared in the PLC. Student expectations rise across the school: a student arriving in next year's class already understands the routine. And teacher belief that the practice works compounds — Bandura's self-efficacy at the individual level becomes collective efficacy at the school level, and the faculty operates as a unit that expects to move outcomes. The aggregate effect on a student over years is larger than δ because the student encounters the practice from multiple teachers across multiple years. This is what "collective" does in collective teacher efficacy. It is the mechanism the RAND Europe synthesis is pointing at. It is the mechanism that distributing tablets, by itself, leaves entirely untouched.

A small analogy, because the chapter has earned one. A surgical outcome correlates more strongly with the *team* than with the lead surgeon. A hospital with one excellent surgeon and a poor anesthesiologist, weak nurses, and inconsistent pre-op protocols produces worse outcomes than a hospital with a competent surgeon embedded in a strong team. The variance in patient outcomes attributable to the team as a unit exceeds the variance attributable to the surgeon alone — this is well-documented in the [Michigan Surgical Quality Collaborative literature](https://www.msqc.org/) on hospital-level variation. [verify: cite Ghaferi, Birkmeyer, Dimick for surgical-team variance specifically.] The school is the team. The student is, over many years, the patient. Faculty-level coordinated belief and practice is the team-level variable that explains outcomes better than any individual. Where the analogy breaks is exactly where you would want it to break: a patient encounters a surgical team for four hours once; a student encounters a school for a thousand hours a year, for many years. The accumulation effect over time means the analogy *understates* rather than overstates the school-as-team finding. That is the kind of break worth naming explicitly, because letting the reader trip on the limit of the analogy is what makes the analogy stop teaching.

## Engaging the critics — seriously, by name

If the chapter stopped here, it would have done what most education-research popularizations do: cite Hattie, gesture at his vast corpus, report some effect sizes, and move on. That is not what this book does. The Hattie corpus has been the subject of the most serious quantitative critique any synthesis in the field has received. The critiques have names and citations. They need to be on the page.

**Robert Slavin** of Johns Hopkins, in [a widely-cited 2018 blog post](https://robertslavinsblog.wordpress.com/2018/06/21/john-hattie-is-wrong/) and in his decades of work building the Best Evidence Encyclopedia, made the input-quality argument. Hattie's meta-meta-analysis accepts the outputs of underlying meta-analyses without filtering for the design quality of the studies in them. Many of those underlying meta-analyses include studies that are small-sample, brief-duration, researcher-developed-measure, pre-post-without-control, or quasi-experimental. Each of those design features is known to inflate effect sizes. Averaging across meta-analyses that contain such studies inherits and compounds the inflation. Slavin's sharpest phrase, and worth quoting: Hattie is "merely shoveling meta-analyses containing massive bias into meta-meta-analyses that reflect the same biases." Slavin's counter-proposal — restrict to randomized trials of at least 12 weeks' duration with independent measures and adequate sample sizes — typically produces headline effect sizes in the range of d ≈ 0.10 to 0.20. Large, properly randomized classroom evaluations rarely produce effect sizes above d = 0.20.

That is the most serious empirical critique in the literature, and it has to be answered honestly. The answer is not "Slavin is wrong." The answer is: *the magnitudes are inflated by the input quality; the ranking direction is not.* If you apply Slavin's filter to every influence on Hattie's list and rescale, the teacher-mediated influences still appear above the technology-only influences. The numbers shrink. The order persists.

**Christof Wecker and colleagues** at Ludwig Maximilians University, in a 2017 analysis, showed that Hattie's meta-meta-analysis [double-counts and sometimes triple-counts the same underlying studies](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/feduc.2017.00067/full) across the meta-analyses it averages. Early meta-analytic practice excluded second-stage analyses with overlaps as small as 25 percent; Hattie's synthesis has overlaps that are sometimes much larger and that go unflagged. Wecker also challenged Hattie's "one year's growth equals d = 0.40" convention: an effect size of d = 0.30 produced over six weeks of intervention cannot plausibly equal less than a full year of normal schooling growth. The metrics are not commensurable. Wecker provided one worked example showing that using weighted averages corrected for overlap would move one of Hattie's top-26 influences to roughly 98th in the ranking. Hattie and Hamilton acknowledged overlapping samples as "a source of distortion" in their 2020 reply "Real Gold vs. Fool's Gold" — the closest concession to the Wecker critique that has appeared on the record. [verify full citation: Hattie & Hamilton 2020, "Real Gold vs. Fool's Gold".]

**Adrian Simpson** of Durham, across [a 2017 paper in *Journal of Education Policy*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02680939.2017.1280183) and follow-up work, made the most technically uncomfortable critique. An effect size, Simpson argued, is not a property of an intervention. It is a function of the intervention *and* the experimental design — specifically, the comparison group, the range of the participant sample, and the measurement design. Three studies of the same intervention with different controls, different sample compositions, or different outcome measures will produce different d's *without anything actually changing about what the intervention does.* The most important consequence is that averaging d's from studies with different design features compounds the design noise rather than canceling it out. Simpson's punch line: "while calculating an effect size may be simple enough for a first course in statistics, there are considerable subtleties in understanding it sufficiently well to ensure that the processes of combining effect sizes in meta-analyses allow valid conclusions to be drawn." His broader summary, the one that gets quoted: Hattie's synthesis is "like blending apples, oranges, and maybe a few wrenches." [verify: original source of the "apples, oranges, wrenches" line; possibly Simpson 2020 follow-up.]

**Pierre-Jérôme Bergeron and Lysiane Rivard**, writing as statisticians in the [*McGill Journal of Education* in 2017](https://mje.mcgill.ca/article/view/9475), identified the bluntest problem of all: *Visible Learning* contains mathematically impossible Common Language Effect probability values — reported, in the original text, as ranging from −49 percent to 219 percent. Probabilities cannot be negative. They cannot exceed 100 percent. The errors went undetected in the educational community for years until two statisticians outside the field surfaced them. Bergeron and Rivard's conclusion was unsparing: "John Hattie and his team have neither the knowledge nor the competencies required to conduct valid statistical analyses." They characterized the methodology as pseudoscience. Hattie has acknowledged the specific CLE calculation errors and corrected them in subsequent editions. He has not accepted the broader pseudoscience characterization.

Read carefully, these four critiques say four different things, and they say them about four different problems. Slavin: the input studies are too weak. Wecker: the same studies are being counted multiple times. Simpson: the d's are design-dependent and shouldn't be averaged across designs. Bergeron and Rivard: some of the specific arithmetic was wrong. Together they constitute a serious indictment of *Visible Learning* as a *precise quantitative instrument.* They do not, jointly or severally, constitute an indictment of the underlying ranking direction. The teacher-mediated influences sit at the top of the ranking even after the critics' corrections. The structural and technology-only influences sit lower. The order survives the critiques even as the magnitudes do not.

This is what the chapter must say plainly, and I will say it once more so it does not get lost. **The precise numbers are not the message. The ranking direction is the message. The most-criticized number in education research and the cleaner causal estimates from an entirely independent literature both point at the same variable.** That variable is the teacher.

The independent literature is the next move.

## The cleaner causal estimates — the value-added literature

The book's case does not depend on Hattie. There is a second body of evidence pointing at the same place, and it is methodologically much cleaner. It is the value-added literature, built largely by labor economists, using administrative panel data and quasi-experimental teacher transitions rather than meta-analytic averaging.

The foundational paper is [Rivkin, Hanushek, and Kain (2005)](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3598793) in *Econometrica*, working with Texas panel data. Their estimate of a semiparametric lower bound on the variance of teacher quality found that variations in teacher quality account for at least 7.5 percent of the total variation in student achievement — a lower bound that almost certainly understates the true effect. Their summary sentence is the one to hold onto: *the effect of a costly ten-student class-size reduction is smaller than the benefit of moving one standard deviation up the teacher-quality distribution.* The structural intervention loses to the within-classroom variable.

[Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff (2014)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.104.9.2633) in the *American Economic Review* used quasi-experimental teacher transitions and 2.5 million students linked to tax records to estimate the effect of teacher value-added on long-run outcomes. A one-standard-deviation improvement in a teacher's value-added raises end-of-grade scores by about 0.10 SD in English and 0.14 SD in math. A one-SD improvement in a teacher's VA in a single grade raises adult earnings by about one percent at age 28. Replacing a bottom-five-percent teacher with an average teacher raises the present value of students' lifetime income by approximately $250,000 per classroom. These are not Hattie numbers. They are administrative-data, tax-record-linked, peer-reviewed, replicated estimates from independent economists with no stake in the Visible Learning project. They point at the same place.

The Boyd-Loeb-Grossman-Staiger work in New York City, and subsequent value-added studies in multiple districts, have established what is now a stable empirical fact about within-school variation: the gap between top-quartile and bottom-quartile elementary math teachers in the same school is roughly 0.33 SD of student achievement per year. That is larger than the effect any platform intervention has produced in any randomized trial at scale.

I want you to hold the cost-effectiveness arithmetic next to these numbers, because it shows you what the comparison actually looks like when you take both costs and effects seriously rather than just asking whether something clears Hattie's hinge. Class-size reduction at roughly $3,000 per student per year, producing the STAR-Krueger d ≈ 0.21, costs approximately $14,286 per 0.10 SD of student gain. AI tutoring at roughly $50 per student per year, producing a cleaned meta-analytic d ≈ 0.17, costs approximately $294 per 0.10 SD of student gain. The AI tutor is **roughly 48 times more cost-effective per unit of student learning than the class-size reduction**, even though both fall below Hattie's 0.40 threshold and both would be coded as "meaningless" by Horvath's framing. This inversion is the heart of the argument. The bar is not whether the effect clears 0.40. The bar is what each marginal dollar buys you in student learning, and the second-best policy choice — funding teacher development in how to use the cheaper, more effective tool — is the policy choice neither camp is making. Class-size reduction with untrained teachers loses on cost-effectiveness. AI distribution without teacher training loses on absolute effect. Teacher training that converts the cheap tool into a real instrument is the trade neither budget line is funding.

The teacher-coaching meta-analysis from Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan sits in exactly the same place. Pooled effect on instruction d = 0.49; pooled effect on student achievement d = 0.18. Coaching reliably changes teacher practice. The translation to student achievement is real but smaller and slower. Effects shrink when programs scale past the conditions in which they were developed. The honest sentence: professional development reliably changes teaching; the achievement effect is real but smaller than the practice effect; the gap closes when PD is sustained, content-specific, coaching-anchored, and not scaled past where it works.

The What Works Clearinghouse synthesizes the tutoring literature the same way. Average tutoring effects of about 0.29 SD; effects of about 0.36 SD for elementary and middle students from low-income families; effects that shrink with scale — 0.44 SD for studies under 100 students, 0.30 SD for 100–399, 0.21 SD for 400–999, 0.16 SD for studies of 1,000 or more. The pattern is identical across the value-added literature, the coaching literature, the tutoring literature, and the RAND Europe synthesis. The teacher-mediated mechanisms produce real, replicated effects. The effects shrink when implementation quality is not maintained at scale. The variable that determines whether the effect shows up at scale is the same variable that determines whether the effect appears at all: the trained teacher exercising informed judgment.

## The class-size paradox — the move the chapter has to make

The class-size literature is the place where the chapter's argument becomes most concrete, because it is the literature that contains the cleanest natural experiment in modern American education policy.

Project STAR ran from 1985 to 1989 in Tennessee. Eleven thousand six hundred kindergarteners, and their teachers, were randomly assigned to small classes of about 15 students, regular classes of about 22, or regular classes with a teacher aide. Students stayed in their assigned condition for four years. It is the largest randomized class-size experiment in U.S. history, and the data have been worked over for thirty years.

[Alan Krueger's 1999 reanalysis](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2587015) in *Quarterly Journal of Economics* found effect sizes of d ≈ 0.20 in kindergarten, 0.28 in first grade, 0.22 in second grade, and 0.19 in third grade. The kindergarten effect was 64 percent as large as the white-black test score gap; the third-grade effect was 82 percent as large. Effects were larger for minority students and for students on free lunch. By the standards of large social-policy interventions, this is a real and durable effect.

[Eric Hanushek's 1999 reanalysis](https://users.ssc.wisc.edu/~gwallace/Papers/Hanushek%20(1999).pdf) in *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis* identified design and implementation issues — non-trivial attrition, teacher awareness of condition, contamination across treatment cells — that introduce real uncertainty about the magnitude of the treatment effects. Hanushek argued the commonly cited results were biased upward and noted that the broader, non-experimental class-size literature shows no consistent relationship between class size and achievement. The Hanushek-Krueger exchange is the closest thing education economics has to a defining controversy; both economists are still publishing on it. The honest middle is that STAR shows a real, positive, durable effect of class-size reduction in the early grades, the magnitude is contested in a range around d = 0.20, and the effects concentrate in the early grades and for disadvantaged subgroups.

Hattie's d = 0.21 for class size sits squarely inside the STAR-Krueger range. The number is not in dispute. *The interpretation* is what is in dispute, and the interpretation is the move the chapter wants.

Hattie's reading: if teachers do not change their methods when class sizes shrink, you would not expect smaller classes to produce large gains. The Tennessee STAR teachers, working in classes of 15, mostly taught the way they had taught in classes of 22. They did not redesign instruction to exploit the small-group affordances. The structural variable changed; the practice variable did not. Hattie's own sentence: *"If teachers were retrained to work with smaller class sizes then indeed many of these optimal strategies may take effect; but merely reducing the number of students in front of teachers appears to change little."* [verify exact quote location.]

This is the chapter's pivot. **Class size matters less than methods. Methods change through training.** Smaller classes without trained teachers produce d ≈ 0.20. Smaller classes with trained teachers, properly studied, would presumably produce more. The book does not need the second half of that sentence to be settled empirically — the literature has not run that experiment at scale. The book needs the first half to be visible: the gain from changing the *teacher's practice* is the gain that the structural change alone cannot produce.

This is also the AI+1 frame, and it is the frame the rest of the book turns on. AI in the classroom without a trained teacher is structurally identical to a class-size reduction without method change. The instrument shifts. The practice doesn't. The gain doesn't materialize. The technology is the smaller class. The teacher's training is the redesigned instruction. Buy one without the other and the second answer of 2025 — buy better software — fails for exactly the reason the structural answer fails. The variable that converts the structural change into student learning is the practice variable, and the practice variable only changes when teachers are trained to change it.

## What the SETDA report is telling us about who is paying for what

Title II, Part A of ESEA is the federal funding stream specifically designated for improving teacher quality. The [SETDA report from November 2025](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf) — *Improving Professional Learning Systems to Better Support Today's Educators* — found that in 2022-23, more than 60 percent of Title II-A funds were spent on professional development. Most of that PD took the form of short workshops rather than the sustained, coaching-anchored, content-specific work that the Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan meta-analysis identifies as actually moving teacher practice. Only nine states prioritized these funds for technology training. Fewer than 40 percent of local education agencies used them to advance technology-related professional learning at all.

The implication for this chapter is direct. The federal infrastructure designed to fund the exact intervention that the empirical literature identifies as highest-leverage — sustained, coaching-anchored teacher development — is being used to fund the lower-leverage version of itself. One-day workshops do not produce the practice change the literature requires. They produce the appearance of compliance. The investment that would actually shift the teacher-variable in the direction of the d = 1.0+ collective-efficacy mechanism is the investment districts are systematically not making, even when the dollars are sitting in the budget for them to make it.

The pouch line item and the workshop line item are the same kind of expenditure. They are spending designed to look like an intervention without producing the practice change that an intervention requires.

## The pattern the chapter has been building toward

I want to land the chapter's central frame here, because the rest of the book turns on it.

Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields against EdTech — and every empirical finding the buy-everything camp wields for it — is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The PISA correlations describe what happens when ambient screen exposure is the dominant variable in a student's day and no adult is exercising professional judgment about whether what is happening between her and the device counts as learning. The smartphone-ban gains describe what happens when the worst version of unmediated technology is removed from the room. The mode-effect findings — screens are worse than paper for expository reading, handwriting is better than typing for encoding — describe what happens when the choice between modalities is made by default rather than by a teacher who knows this student, this lesson, this moment. The implementation-fidelity finding in the RAND Europe synthesis names it explicitly: when teachers are trained and supported, technology improves outcomes; when they are not, impact evaporates.

These are not separate findings pointing in different directions. They are the same finding said in five different vocabularies. The finding is: *the technology variable runs through the teacher variable*. Take the teacher out of the loop and the technology becomes whatever its default deployment makes it — distracting, substituting, leveling down. Put a trained teacher into the loop and the same technology becomes an instrument that does what only she can decide it should do today, for this student, in this lesson. The data describing the first state of the world are abundant. The data describing the second state of the world are thin, because we have not yet built the cohort of trained teachers in sufficient numbers, with sufficient continuity, to study at scale what happens when they make the calls. That study has not been run because the country has not built the workforce that would let it be run.

This is what the chapter wants the reader to see: *Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The book is about who should be.*

The variable is the teacher. Every other variable — the device, the platform, the schedule, the class size, the curriculum, the assessment — runs through her. The structural changes do not work without the practice change, and the practice change does not happen without sustained, coaching-anchored, content-specific training. The PISA correlations and the mode-effect findings and the screen-inferiority findings are not the case against technology in schools. They are the case for the trained teacher who decides when the screen helps and when the paper helps and when the device goes in the drawer because today's lesson is encoding-heavy and the pen is the better tool. The argument is recursive: the same evidence that the ban-everything camp uses against EdTech is the evidence that argues for the variable they refuse to invest in, which is the teacher whose judgment determines whether the technology helps or hurts.

The next chapter — Chapter 5 — turns to what that variable becomes when she has the right instruments and the training to use them. The variable is the teacher. The instrument question is what she does with the variable once we have built her capacity to use it. That is where the book goes next.

## What the research leaves open

Several things this chapter has not settled, and which the evidence does not yet settle either. The current MetaX value for collective teacher efficacy is moving, and the chapter has reported the range rather than the single number for that reason; whether the d = 1.0 to 1.6 band tightens around a more specific point estimate as more meta-analyses come in is genuinely open. The correlation-to-d conversion at the heart of CTE, teacher-student relationships, and several other top-tier influences is mathematically valid and interpretively loaded, and there is no clean way to tell readers which interpretive weight to assign to a converted versus a treatment-effect d without a longer methodological excursion than this chapter has room for. The class-size literature remains divided in ways that matter: Krueger and Hanushek are both still publishing on STAR, the magnitude of the effect is in a contested range around d = 0.20, and the question of what the effect would look like if class-size reduction were paired with corresponding teacher training has not been studied at the scale the question deserves. The value-added literature is methodologically the cleanest part of the case, but it speaks to *variation in teacher quality given current preparation practices* — not to what the ceiling looks like if preparation practices were systematically improved. The whole literature, in some sense, has been measuring the floor.

## Still puzzling

The collective teacher efficacy mechanism is the variable that explains the most about which schools convert teacher practice into student learning at scale. We do not yet know whether what the CTE survey instrument is measuring is a single construct (faculty-level shared belief about joint capacity) or a bundle of correlated school-quality variables that the instrument happens to pick up — instructional leadership, hiring quality, scheduling structure, departmental coordination, principal stability. If it is the first, the intervention design is clear: build the faculty's coordinated belief and the practice will follow. If it is the second, the construct is a proxy for school-quality factors we should be addressing directly. The chapter cannot resolve this. The next decade of the literature should.

## What would change my mind

A large, well-powered, properly randomized trial showing technology-led instruction outperforming trained-teacher-led instruction at scale, on independent measures, with effects that survive replication and that hold across the implementation-fidelity range the RAND Europe synthesis identifies as the determining variable. To date, no such trial exists. If one is published that meets those criteria, the chapter's central claim — that the teacher is the largest moveable variable — would need to be substantially revised. The book would still have an argument; it would not be this argument.
