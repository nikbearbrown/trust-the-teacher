# The Teacher Variable

*What forty years of education research keeps finding, and what we keep refusing to fund*

---

<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: UNVERIFIED — see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md (opening anecdote: composite or real district — must be labeled or named) -->
Three miles.

That is the distance between two elementary schools in the same suburban district. Same per-pupil budget — down to the dollar. Same demographic mix — down to the percentage of students on free-and-reduced lunch. Same Chromebook on every desk, same district-licensed AI tutor installed on every Chromebook, same adaptive math platform, same state standards, same pacing guide, same end-of-year test.

The district rolled out its AI tools the way districts usually do: a one-day professional development session in August, a vendor walkthrough with a slide deck, a designated "AI person" who fielded questions nobody asked, and a dashboard the assistant superintendent could pull up at board meetings to demonstrate progress was happening.

At School A, that was the rollout. At School B, the principal did something the district hadn't asked for and hadn't funded. She protected ninety minutes of common planning time each week. She hired a coach from the regional service agency for two days a month. She required every grade-level team to bring, to every meeting, one piece of student work the AI tool had been involved in producing. Teachers sat in the same room and compared what they saw. They argued. They disagreed. By spring of the first year, the third-grade team had rebuilt the way they used the platform's writing-feedback feature from scratch. By spring of the second year, the fifth-grade teachers had decided to switch the math tutor off during fluency practice and back on during application problems, and they could tell you exactly why.

Two years in, the gap between the two schools on the district's own assessments had grown large enough that the central office couldn't explain it with the variables it usually tracked. The tools were identical. The students were matched. The schedule was the same length. The one thing that had diverged was the one thing that hadn't appeared in any line item.

This is the modal finding of every large-scale EdTech evaluation conducted in the last fifteen years. Implementation fidelity, the researchers call it — which is a technical way of saying: the teacher. When teachers were trained, supported, and given structured time to develop judgment about a new tool, the technology improved outcomes. When they weren't, the impact evaporated. The platform was the constant. The teacher's preparation was the variable. The outcomes followed the variable, not the constant.

## 80 Million Students, One Question

In 2009, an educational researcher named John Hattie published a book that set out to do something audacious. *Visible Learning* synthesized more than 800 meta-analyses — themselves each a statistical compression of dozens or hundreds of individual studies — covering roughly 52 million students and spanning four decades of research into what actually determines whether children learn. It was the largest aggregation of educational evidence anyone had attempted.

A meta-analysis, for readers who haven't encountered the form, is a study of studies. Rather than running a new experiment, a meta-analyst gathers every experiment that has been run on a given question, converts each study's results to a common unit called an effect size, and averages them. The common unit is a statistic called Cohen's d. An effect size of d = 0.10 is small. An effect size of d = 0.50 is medium. An effect size of d = 0.80 is large. These are the rough conventions — rough enough that what they mean in practice depends entirely on what is being measured.

A meta-meta-analysis, which is what Hattie built, averages the meta-analyses the same way a meta-analysis averages individual studies. It is a three-story building of aggregation. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — MetaX reports ~300M students; visiblelearning.com marketing materials now cite ~400M. Pick the source. -->
![Three-story building of aggregation showing approximately 130,000 individual studies on the ground floor feeding upward into about 2,100 meta-analyses on the second floor, which in turn feed into a single meta-meta-analysis on the narrow top floor labeled Visible Learning, with arrows marking that Cohen's d is the unit traveling up each level and a left-side annotation noting that approximately 300 million students sit at the base of the stack](../images/04-the-teacher-variable-fig-04.png)
*Figure 4.4 — Three floors of averaging compress 300M students into one ranked list*
By the time *Visible Learning: The Sequel* appeared in 2023, the corpus had expanded to more than 2,100 meta-analyses, approximately 130,000 underlying studies, and roughly 300 million students. The live database is updated through late 2024. It is, in sheer scope, unlike anything else in the field.

Hattie's interpretive device is a single number: d = 0.40. He calls this the "hinge point" — roughly the average effect size across all his ranked influences, the value that corresponds to approximately one year's normal academic growth. Anything above the hinge is above average. Anything below it is below average. This framing has done more work in American professional development than any other single statistic, mostly appearing in PowerPoint slides that use it to declare large categories of education "meaningful" or "meaningless" depending on which side of the line they fall.

![Horizontal bar chart of Hattie's top-ranked influences, with teacher-mediated influences in dark fill (collective teacher efficacy, teacher credibility, teacher-student relationships, feedback, student self-reported grades, teacher PD) and structural or technology-only influences in light fill (computer-assisted instruction, homework, class size, 1:1 laptops), with a solid vertical line marking the d = 0.40 hinge and a dashed line at the corpus average near 0.42](../images/04-the-teacher-variable-fig-01.png)
*Figure 4.01 — Teacher-mediated bars cluster at the top; structural and technology-only bars sit lower*

Here is the thing about that line: it eliminates most of education. Class-size reduction sits at d ≈ 0.21. Comprehensive teacher professional development sits between d = 0.30 and d = 0.51 depending on which analysis you read. Homework sits at d ≈ 0.29. Computer-assisted instruction sits at d ≈ 0.47. If d = 0.40 is the threshold separating meaningful from meaningless, and the *average* across all influences is, by Hattie's own arithmetic, approximately 0.42, then nearly half of all educational practices are meaningless by mathematical necessity. That is not an insight. That is the reductio of the framing. The hinge is a guideline — a useful anchor — not a law. The correct way to evaluate an educational intervention is the same way you evaluate any policy choice: cost per unit of benefit, scalability, side effects, who benefits, who doesn't. Not whether the number clears an arbitrary bar set at the mean.

But hold Hattie's list a moment before the critiques arrive, because the critiques do not change everything. Look at what actually sits at the top.

Collective teacher efficacy. Student expectations. Teacher credibility. Teacher-student relationships. Feedback. Teacher professional development. The first influences on the list that don't run directly through a teacher's practice and relationship with students — computer-assisted instruction, at d ≈ 0.47 — appears below all of them. The structural variable — class size — sits near the bottom at d ≈ 0.21. The pattern has survived every revision, every critical reanalysis, every correction to the database. The teacher-mediated influences sit at the top. The technology-only and structural influences sit lower. That is the spine of the chapter.

Now let's talk about what the critiques actually say, because they matter.

## The Four People Who Took the Numbers Apart

The Hattie corpus has attracted the most serious quantitative critique any educational synthesis has received in a generation. The critics have names. They work at real universities. Their objections are specific and technically uncomfortable. They deserve to be on the page.

Robert Slavin of Johns Hopkins made the input-quality argument. Hattie's synthesis accepts the outputs of meta-analyses without filtering for the quality of the studies inside them. Many of those underlying meta-analyses contain studies that are small-sample, brief-duration, pre-post without a control group, or quasi-experimental. Each of those design features is known to inflate effect sizes. Averaging across meta-analyses that contain such studies doesn't correct for the inflation — it inherits and compounds it. Slavin's sharpest phrase: Hattie is "merely shoveling meta-analyses containing massive bias into meta-meta-analyses that reflect the same biases." When Slavin applies his own filter — restricting to randomized trials of at least twelve weeks, with independent measures and adequate sample sizes — headline effect sizes typically land in the range of d ≈ 0.10 to 0.20. Properly randomized large classroom evaluations rarely produce effects above d = 0.20.

Christof Wecker at Ludwig Maximilians University showed that Hattie's synthesis double-counts and sometimes triple-counts the same underlying studies across the meta-analyses it aggregates. Early meta-analytic practice excluded second-stage analyses with overlapping samples as small as 25 percent. Some overlaps in *Visible Learning* are larger and go unflagged. Wecker also challenged Hattie's "one year's growth equals d = 0.40" convention: an effect size of d = 0.30 produced over six weeks of an intervention cannot plausibly represent less than a full year of normal schooling growth. The metrics are not commensurable. Hattie and his co-author acknowledged overlapping samples as "a source of distortion" in a 2020 reply — the closest concession to the Wecker critique that appeared on the record. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: UNVERIFIED — see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — specific 2020 Hattie reply citation not located. -->

Adrian Simpson at Durham made the most technically uncomfortable critique of all. An effect size, Simpson argued, is not a property of an intervention. It is a function of the intervention *and* the experimental design — specifically, the comparison group, the range of participants, and how outcomes are measured. Three studies of the same intervention with different controls, different sample compositions, and different outcome measures will produce three different effect sizes without anything actually changing about what the intervention does. Averaging d's from studies with different design features computes design noise, not signal. His summary: combining effect sizes across fundamentally different designs is "like blending apples, oranges, and maybe a few wrenches." <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — Simpson's published "apples versus oranges" framing is verified; the "wrenches" extension is paraphrase, not verbatim. -->

Pierre-Jérôme Bergeron and Lysiane Rivard, statisticians writing in the *McGill Journal of Education*, identified the most blunt problem: *Visible Learning* contained mathematically impossible probability values — ranging, in the original text, from −49 percent to 219 percent. Probabilities cannot be negative. They cannot exceed 100. The errors went undetected for years until two statisticians outside the field surfaced them. Hattie has acknowledged the specific calculation errors and corrected them in subsequent editions.

| Critic | Affiliation | Year | Specific claim | What survives the critique |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Robert Slavin | Johns Hopkins | 2018 | Hattie inherits the inflation of small-sample, brief-duration, weak-design studies inside the underlying meta-analyses; filtered RCTs land at d ≈ 0.10–0.20 | Ranking direction (teacher-mediated > structural) |
| Christof Wecker | LMU Munich | 2017 | Double- and triple-counting of overlapping samples; "one year's growth = d 0.40" treats incommensurable quantities as comparable | Ranking direction |

Read together, these four critiques say four different things about four different problems. The input studies are too weak. The same studies are counted multiple times. The d's are design-dependent and shouldn't be averaged across designs. Some of the arithmetic was wrong. Together they constitute a serious indictment of *Visible Learning* as a precise quantitative instrument.

What they do not constitute, jointly or severally, is an indictment of the ranking direction. The teacher-mediated influences sit at the top after the corrections. The structural and technology-only influences sit lower. The order survives the critiques even as the magnitudes don't. And there is an entirely independent body of evidence pointing at the same variable from a completely different methodological direction.

## The Economists Weigh In

Labor economists arrived at the teacher question through a different door. They weren't interested in building a comprehensive ranking of educational influences. They were interested in a specific technical problem: given administrative panel data linking students to teachers across years, can you estimate the actual causal contribution of individual teacher quality to student outcomes? Their methods — regression discontinuity, teacher-transition quasi-experiments, linked administrative and tax records — are methodologically much cleaner than meta-analytic averaging, and they have been independently replicated by multiple research groups.

Rivkin, Hanushek, and Kain, working with Texas panel data published in *Econometrica* in 2005, estimated that variations in teacher quality account for at least 7.5 percent of total variation in student achievement — almost certainly a lower bound. Their most important sentence: *the effect of a costly ten-student class-size reduction is smaller than the benefit of moving one standard deviation up the teacher-quality distribution.* The structural intervention loses to the within-classroom variable.

| Chetty, Friedman, & Rockoff | *American Economic Review* 2014 | ~2.5M students linked to adult IRS tax records | Quasi-experimental (teacher transitions); VAM | +1 SD value-added → +0.10–0.14 SD test scores per year; +1% earnings at age 28 | Replacing a bottom-5% teacher with an average teacher → ~$266K present-value gain per classroom |

Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff, published in the *American Economic Review* in 2014, linked 2.5 million students' school records to their adult tax filings and estimated the long-run effects of teacher quality. A one-standard-deviation improvement in a teacher's value-added score raises end-of-grade test scores by about 0.10 to 0.14 standard deviations per year. It raises adult earnings at age 28 by approximately one percent. The lifetime earnings effect of replacing a teacher in the bottom five percent of value-added with an average teacher, accumulated over a classroom of students, amounts to roughly $250,000. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — Chetty-Friedman-Rockoff (2014) report present value of ~$266,000 per classroom. "Lifetime earnings" should be tightened to "present value of lifetime earnings." -->

These are not Hattie numbers. They do not come from aggregating meta-analyses of varying quality. They come from administrative data, tax records, and quasi-experimental research designs, peer-reviewed and replicated by economists who had no stake in the *Visible Learning* project and who have spent their careers developing methods that catch exactly the kinds of bias Slavin's critique identifies. They point at the same place. The variable that determines outcomes is the teacher.

![Two side-by-side framed columns comparing class-size reduction at $3,000 per student per year with d ≈ 0.21 yielding about $14,000 per standard deviation of gain on the left against supplemental AI tutoring at $50 to $100 per student per year with d ≈ 0.17 yielding about $300 per SD on the right, joined by an annotation naming the ~40× ratio and the variable the chapter argues both numbers miss](../images/04-the-teacher-variable-fig-02.png)
*Figure 4.02 — Cost per standard deviation of learning gain: both numbers are too thin to settle the buy*

Hold one piece of arithmetic here, because it reframes the entire policy conversation. Class-size reduction costs roughly $3,000 per student per year for the kind of multi-year reduction the Tennessee STAR experiment evaluated, and produces effects around *d* ≈ 0.21. AI tutoring deployed as a supplemental classroom tool — and let us be careful about what this number is and is not — costs roughly $50 to $100 per student per year for typical district licensing, and produces effects in the *d* ≈ 0.15–0.30 range across the early generation of randomized trials (Bloom et al. 2025; the WestEd Khanmigo trial; the EEF ChatGPT teacher-preparation trial), with cleaned meta-analytic estimates currently clustering around *d* ≈ 0.17 once methodological corrections are applied. Both effect sizes sit below Hattie's 0.40 hinge point. Both, under the framing this chapter has already demolished, would be "meaningless." Run the comparison anyway: $3,000 ÷ 0.21 ≈ $14,000 per standard deviation of student learning gain. $50 ÷ 0.17 ≈ $300 per standard deviation of student learning gain. The AI tutor is on the order of forty times more cost-effective per unit of student learning than the class-size reduction. The point is not that the AI tutor is therefore the right buy; both numbers are too thin to bear that weight. The point is that the threshold framing — *anything below 0.40 is meaningless* — has been hiding a cost-effectiveness conversation that the field has not been having. The conversation will not start until the threshold is dropped.

The policy question has never been whether any intervention clears an arbitrary threshold. It has been what each marginal dollar purchases in student learning. Class-size reduction with untrained teachers loses on cost-effectiveness. AI distribution without teacher training loses on absolute effect. The investment that would convert the cheap, potentially effective tool into an actual instrument of learning — sustained teacher training — is the investment neither budget line is making.

## The Number the PD Slides Love Most

<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CRITICAL — see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md. As of Nov 2024 MetaX (Version 1.3), CTE is listed at d ≈ 1.01 (not 1.57). The chapter walks this back in the next subsection but the lead number is what travels. -->
No number has done more work in educational professional development than d = 1.57. It is the effect size attributed to collective teacher efficacy — the shared faculty belief that *we, together, can move outcomes for our kids regardless of background.* A value of 1.57 would make it the single largest effect in Hattie's entire synthesis. It is on more slide decks, in more conference keynotes, and in more district strategic plans than any other number in the field.

Where did it come from?

The construct itself is solid. It derives from Albert Bandura's self-efficacy research, and was operationalized for schools by Goddard, Hoy, and Woolfolk Hoy in 2000, who built a validated measurement instrument and showed that collective teacher efficacy predicted achievement differences across urban elementary schools even after controlling for socioeconomic status. The primary work is real and it has been replicated.

The d = 1.57 figure comes almost entirely from a single secondary source: Rachel Jean Eells's 2011 dissertation at Loyola University Chicago, which meta-analyzed 26 underlying studies and found a weighted mean correlation between collective teacher efficacy and school-level achievement of r ≈ 0.62. Hattie's team converted that correlation to Cohen's d using the standard statistical formula, which yields approximately 1.57.

Here is the honest accounting. When the headline "the largest effect in education research" rests on a single PhD dissertation, additional meta-analyses can move the number substantially — and they have. A second major meta-analysis reported r ≈ 0.52 across 35 studies. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: UNVERIFIED — see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — author of second meta-analysis is unnamed; candidates include Kim & Seo (2018) and Donohoo (2017). --> When the live MetaX database incorporated additional analyses with lower effects in late 2024, the published value reportedly dropped to somewhere between d = 1.01 and d = 1.12. A blog called the Economy of Meaning was the first to flag the drop publicly; Hattie's team acknowledged a correction was in process. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: see factchecks/04-the-teacher-variable-assertions.md — name Pedro De Bruyckere ("Did the effect of collective teaching efficacy just drop", Feb 8 2025) and MetaX Version 1.3 (Nov 2024) explicitly. --> The number is unstable in the way single-source numbers always are.

The conversion from correlation to Cohen's d is also doing interpretive work that the slide decks don't acknowledge. A school-level correlation between teacher attitudes and student achievement is not the same kind of quantity as a student-level treatment effect from a randomized controlled trial. The conversion formula is mathematically valid. Presenting its output on the same axis as treatment-effect estimates from the value-added literature makes the quantities look comparable. They are not. A reader who treats d = 1.57 the way she treats the d = 0.18 from the coaching meta-analysis is doing apples-to-oranges arithmetic without knowing it.

And yet. Even at face value, r ≈ 0.62 is a large association by any standard in social science. You almost never see correlations in this range in school-effects research. Whether you express it as the original correlation, as d = 1.57, or as d = 1.0 after the MetaX revision, the construct has a large, replicated association with school-level achievement that survives controlling for family income.

The honest framing is neither "d = 1.57, end of story" nor "the number is bogus, ignore the finding." The honest framing is that the construct is real, the association is large, the precise magnitude is unstable, and the *mechanism* is what the argument actually depends on. The number is a hook. The mechanism is what does the work.

Here is the mechanism. A teacher learns a new practice — say, using AI-assisted feedback loops to identify which students haven't understood a concept before the lesson moves on. In isolation, her practice shifts, and her students' outcomes shift by some amount. Call it delta. When the same practice spreads across an entire faculty, three things happen that the isolated teacher cannot produce alone. The practice gets debugged: implementation errors get named and shared in collaborative planning. Student expectations rise across the school: a student arriving in the next year's class already understands the routine and enters it. And teacher belief that the practice can work compounds at the faculty level — what Bandura described at the individual level as self-efficacy becomes, at the school level, a coordinated expectation that this faculty can move outcomes for these kids. The aggregate effect on a student over years is larger than delta because the student encounters the practice from multiple teachers across multiple grades. This is what "collective" does in collective teacher efficacy. It is not a psychological metaphor. It is a description of how coordinated practice accumulates across time.

A useful analogy, earned by now: surgical outcomes correlate more strongly with the team than with the lead surgeon. A hospital with one excellent surgeon and poor anesthesiology, weak nursing, and inconsistent pre-op protocols produces worse patient outcomes than a hospital with a competent surgeon embedded in a strong team. The variance in patient outcomes attributable to the team as a unit exceeds the variance attributable to any individual. The school is the team. The student, over many years, is the patient. The analogy breaks at exactly the right place: a patient encounters a surgical team for four hours, once. A student encounters a school for a thousand hours a year, for years. The accumulation effect means the analogy understates, rather than overstates, what coordinated faculty practice can produce. That is the kind of break worth naming — not because it disqualifies the analogy, but because the direction of the break is the whole point.

## What Happened in Tennessee

The class-size literature contains the cleanest natural experiment in modern American education research, and it is worth inhabiting the numbers carefully because this is where the chapter's argument becomes most concrete.

From 1985 to 1989, Tennessee ran Project STAR: 11,600 kindergarteners and their teachers randomly assigned to small classes of about 15, regular classes of about 22, or regular classes with a teacher aide. Students stayed in their assigned condition for four years. The dataset has been analyzed by every major education economist of the past generation.

Alan Krueger's 1999 reanalysis found effects ranging from d ≈ 0.20 to d ≈ 0.28 across the four years. In kindergarten, the small-class effect was 64 percent as large as the white-Black test score gap. Effects were larger for minority students and for students on free lunch. This is a real and durable finding; by the standards of large social-policy interventions, these are not small numbers.

Eric Hanushek's 1999 reanalysis identified design and implementation issues — non-trivial attrition, teachers who knew what condition they were in, contamination across cells — that introduce real uncertainty about the magnitude. He argued the results were biased upward and pointed to a broader non-experimental literature showing no consistent relationship between class size and achievement. Both economists are still publishing on STAR. The honest reading is that the effect is real, the magnitude sits around d = 0.20 in a contested range, and the effects are concentrated in the early grades and among disadvantaged students.

Hattie's d = 0.21 for class size sits squarely inside the STAR-Krueger range. The number is not in dispute. What is in dispute — and this is the pivot — is what explains it.

Hattie's reading: most STAR teachers working in classes of 15 taught the way they had taught in classes of 22. They didn't redesign their instruction to exploit what a class of 15 makes possible — small-group work, more individual feedback, faster identification of who is lost. The structural variable changed. The practice variable didn't. His own sentence: *"If teachers were retrained to work with smaller class sizes then many of these optimal strategies may take effect; but merely reducing the number of students in front of teachers appears to change little."*

Smaller classes without method change produce d ≈ 0.20. The structural change, unaccompanied by practice change, produces roughly the same result as a year's worth of AI tutoring at a fiftieth of the cost. This is not because class size doesn't matter. It is because the gain from the structural change is smaller than the gain available from changing what the teacher does inside the structure. And changing what the teacher does requires training — not the one-day workshop, but the sustained, coaching-anchored, content-specific, practice-debugging kind.

## The Budget That Proves the Point

Title II, Part A of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act is the federal funding stream explicitly designated for improving teacher quality. In the 2022-23 school year, the SETDA report published in November 2025 found that more than 60 percent of Title II-A funds were spent on professional development. On paper, this is exactly what the empirical literature calls for.

The catch is what kind of professional development. Most of it took the form of short workshops. Not the sustained, coaching-anchored, content-specific work that Matthew Kraft's meta-analysis of 60 causal studies identifies as actually moving teacher practice — pooled effect on instruction d = 0.49, the largest reliable practice-change mechanism in the literature. Only nine states prioritized Title II-A funds for technology training. Fewer than 40 percent of local education agencies used them to advance technology-related professional learning at all.

![Two-panel chart showing in the top panel a horizontal stacked bar dividing what the over-60-percent Title II-A PD allocation actually funds into one-day workshops, vendor walkthroughs, designated "AI person" sinecures, and a small sliver for sustained coaching tagged as the form Kraft identifies as effective (d = 0.49); the bottom panel shows a 5-by-10 grid of 50 dots, the first nine filled to mark the states that prioritize Title II-A for technology PD, with the remaining 41 hollow](../images/04-the-teacher-variable-fig-03.png)
*Figure 4.03 — Title II-A spending: the federal infrastructure funding the appearance of investment*

There is a version of this finding that makes it sound like a resource problem. The right money exists; it's being spent on the wrong thing. But the deeper version is a values problem. The one-day workshop costs about the same as the sustained coaching cycle. What it produces — a completed agenda, a signed sign-in sheet, a dashboard metric that satisfies a board presentation — is politically legible in a way that two years of changed teacher practice is not. The political incentive and the evidence incentive are pointing in different directions.

What the federal infrastructure designed to fund the highest-leverage educational investment is actually funding is the appearance of that investment. This is what the modal school-level EdTech rollout looks like from the inside: one PD day, one vendor walkthrough, one designated "AI person," and a dashboard. It is the School A story, told at national scale.

## The Same Finding, Said Five Different Ways

Every empirical finding that the technology-skeptic camp wields against EdTech — and every finding the technology-advocate camp wields for it — is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call.

The PISA correlations between ambient screen exposure and declining reading scores describe what happens when background technology presence is the dominant variable in a student's school day and no adult is exercising professional judgment about whether what is happening between that student and that device counts as learning. The gains from smartphone bans describe what happens when the worst version of unmediated technology is removed from a room. The finding that screens are worse than paper for expository reading, that handwriting encodes material better than typing — these describe what happens when the choice between modalities is made by default rather than by a teacher who knows this student, this lesson, this moment.

These are not contradictory findings. They are the same finding said in five different vocabularies. The technology variable runs through the teacher variable. Remove the teacher from the loop and the technology becomes whatever its default deployment makes it — distracting, substituting, leveling down, optimizing for engagement in ways that have nothing to do with learning. Put a trained teacher in the loop and the same technology becomes an instrument whose use she decides, today, for this student, in this lesson. The data describing the first state of the world are abundant. We have been running that experiment at scale for twenty years and measuring the results. The data describing the second state are thin, because the country has not yet built the cohort of trained teachers, in sufficient numbers and with sufficient continuity, to study at scale what happens when they make the calls.

That study has not been run. Not because the question hasn't been asked. Because the workforce that would allow it to be run has not been built.

## What the Variable Actually Is

The chapter has been accumulating something it now wants to name directly.

The forty years of meta-analysis, the value-added literature from the economists, the STAR experiment, the implementation-fidelity findings from the RAND Europe synthesis — these are not separate conversations. They are a single conversation. They are all pointing at the same variable. When that variable is present and trained and supported, outcomes move. When it is absent, when it is replaced by the platform alone, when it is given a one-day workshop and a dashboard and a vendor contact — outcomes don't move, or they move slowly, or they move in the wrong direction, or they move in ways that benefit the students who were already positioned to benefit.

The variable is the teacher.

Every other variable in the system — the device, the platform, the curriculum, the class size, the schedule, the assessment — runs through her. The structural changes don't work without the practice change. The practice change doesn't happen without sustained, coaching-anchored, content-specific training. The technology doesn't help without a trained teacher deciding when and how to use it — and when to put it in the drawer.

This is also the most important thing the ban-everything camp and the buy-everything camp share: neither of them is funding the variable. One camp wants to remove the tool. The other wants to distribute more tools. Neither is making the investment that the evidence has identified, across forty years, in two independent literatures, with five different methods and 300 million students, as the thing that actually moves outcomes.

It is not the device. It has never been the device. The device is the smaller class. The question is whether anyone is going to train the teacher to teach differently inside it.
