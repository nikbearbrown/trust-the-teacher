# Chapter 2: The App

*What the $165 billion EdTech industry got right, what it got wrong, and the finding nobody is advertising*

---

A school district in Ohio bought $2.3 million worth of educational software licenses in a single budget year. By the end of the year, sixty-seven percent of those seats had never been logged into. Not "used poorly." Not "abandoned mid-semester." Never opened. The vendor was paid. The IT director closed the procurement cycle. The teachers — the people the licenses were ostensibly bought *for* — were not asked which platforms they wanted, were not trained to use the ones that arrived, and in most cases were not told how the new software was supposed to fit into the lessons they were already trying to teach.

I am compressing a real industry finding into an illustrative district. The sixty-seven percent figure comes from the [Glimpse K12 audit of 200,000 software licenses purchased by 275 schools in 2017–18](https://www.glimpsek12.com/blog-posts/glimpse-k12-analysis-of-school-spending-shows-that-two-thirds-of-software-license-purchases-go-unused) — the company tracked login data, and on average two-thirds of seats went unused over the school year. Some schools cleared ninety percent waste. [EdWeek Market Brief revisited the question in 2024](https://marketbrief.edweek.org/marketplace-k-12/latest-ed-tech-usage-report-shows-improvements-still-1-billion-waste-k-12/) and reported the underutilization problem had improved but still totaled more than one billion dollars annually in unused K-12 ed-tech licensing fees nationwide. The "$2.3 million" framing of the opening is a composite drawn from Evelyn Learning's industry analysis of per-district waste [verify: Evelyn Learning's per-district extrapolation should be confirmed against a specific district case study before publication] — but the structural story is not composite. It is what the industry data, taken together, describe. Pick any large district. Pick any large purchase. Repeat the audit. The numbers move within a band; the band has been the band for a long time.

There is a name for this in education research circles: the *implementation gap*. The measurable distance between what a piece of educational technology can do and what it actually does in the classroom that paid for it. The gap is older than ChatGPT. It is older than the iPad. It is older than the overhead projector and the language lab and the educational television cart. It is what the educational technology industry has, in the eighty years it has been trying, consistently failed to close. The gap is the constant.

The Ohio district is not in the story because it did something exceptional. It is in the story because what it did is ordinary.

## What "$165 billion EdTech industry" actually counts

The phrase is loose. Three different methodologies produce three different numbers, all routinely called "the EdTech market," and the policy implication shifts depending on which one is in the sentence.

There is the pure-play EdTech segment — companies whose primary product is education technology, the K-12 learning-management systems, the adaptive-learning platforms, the online-degree operators, the publishers' digital arms. [Market Research Future estimated this segment at roughly $167 billion in 2024](https://www.marketresearchfuture.com/reports/edtech-market-16213), which is the source closest to the "$165 billion" figure circulated in policy conversations and in the subtitle of this chapter. Other consultancies put the same segment between $163 and $170 billion for the same year. The methodology is private — Market Research Future is a commercial research firm and its construction of the market is not peer-reviewed [verify: the $167B baseline should be re-verified against MRF's current report at draft time] — so the figure is best read as the industry's working consensus rather than as a hard count.

There is the broader total — devices, infrastructure, software, content, services, professional development, all bundled. [HolonIQ, the firm most cited in education-investment circles, projected total global EdTech expenditure at roughly $404 billion by 2025](https://www.holoniq.com/notes/global-education-technology-market-to-reach-404b-by-2025) in a forecast issued before the post-pandemic correction. Their more recent estimates are lower; the actual 2024 total is closer to $310 billion, with a projected 2025 rebound back toward the original number.

And there is global education spending in its entirety — public and private, K through postsecondary — which HolonIQ puts at roughly six trillion dollars, of which all of EdTech, however defined, is a single-digit-percent slice.

The chapter sticks with the "$165 billion" figure because it is the one in the public conversation and it tracks the pure-play segment closely. The point of pulling the three numbers apart is not pedantic. It matters because each number carries a different implicit theory of where the leverage is. The $165 billion is software. The $404 billion is hardware plus software. The six trillion is everything else — and the largest line item in the six trillion is teacher salaries. The EdTech industry is a small fraction of education spending. The teacher payroll is, by a wide margin, the largest fraction. Where the marginal dollar should go is a question about which of these denominators you believe is the limiting one.

## "EdTech worked" — three definitions, three different verdicts

The phrase is doing different jobs in different mouths.

Vendor case studies count "worked" as positive testimonials and engagement metrics. The platform shows usage logs trending up; the marketing slide reads "students are loving it." Randomized-trial designers count "worked" as a statistically significant gain on an external assessment, controlled for selection, against a comparison condition. The district CFO counts "worked" as licenses actually used — *if we paid for it and they used it, that counts*. None of these three bars is the same. An intervention can clear one without clearing the others, and most do.

The Ohio vignette is a failure by definition three — the licenses were paid for and not used — and it is silent on whether the software would have worked by definition two if it had been used. Both failures are real. They have different causes and different solutions, and the chapter should not collapse them. *Procurement failure* and *pedagogical failure* are different problems. The first is fixable by buying less software, by buying the software the teachers asked for, or by running a usage audit before the next purchase order. The second is fixable only by changing what happens between the teacher, the student, and the tool. Solving the first one without solving the second one mostly just produces a smaller version of the same waste.

When this chapter says "the EdTech industry got something wrong," the wrong it got was not in the software. The software is, in most cases, neither better nor worse than what the brochure described. The wrong was in the implicit theory of the relationship between the software and the student. The theory was that the platform was the active ingredient. The evidence is that the teacher is.

## The deployment problem: five things "AI in the classroom" can mean

If "EdTech" hides three meanings, "AI in education" hides at least five. The same model, the same student, the same problem set produces different — sometimes oppositely signed — outcomes depending on which of the five deployments is running. Calling them all "AI in the classroom" is the move that lets a vendor sell the second one with the evidence base of the fourth.

The five, briefly:

*One.* The untrained student given a Chromebook with ChatGPT, no scaffolding, no teacher framing, no learning objective. The default deployment in most districts that have rolled out AI access at all.

*Two.* The pedagogically prompted AI tutor — a generative model wrapped in a custom system prompt designed to ask questions, refuse to provide final answers, and mimic Socratic dialogue. The model is the same; the system prompt is the teacher's lesson plan rendered in instructions to the model.

*Three.* AI with a teacher in the loop, supervising in real time — the LearnLM/Eedi configuration developed by [Google DeepMind and Eedi in 2025](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/LearnLM/learnLM_nov25.pdf), in which the teacher reviews and approves the model's messages before they reach the student.

*Four.* AI as teacher copilot — the [Tutor CoPilot configuration developed by Demszky and colleagues at Stanford](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017), in which the AI augments the human tutor in real time rather than addressing the student directly.

*Five.* AI as teacher replacement — the deployment most marketed in press cycles, in which a student interacts with an AI tutor with no human in the loop at all.

The empirical record across these five is not "AI helps" or "AI hurts." It is that *deployment determines sign*. The same model can produce a real learning gain in deployment four and a measurable learning loss in deployment one. That is the finding the next section walks through, because it is the finding the chapter is built around. It is also the finding the EdTech industry, by and large, is not advertising.

## The Bastani finding: same model, opposite outcomes

This is the empirical fulcrum of the chapter, so I want to walk through it carefully. The paper is [Bastani et al., "Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning: Evidence from high school mathematics," published in *PNAS* in 2025](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122). The setting is a large high school in Turkey. Roughly a thousand students across grades nine through eleven were randomized at the section level — meaning entire class sections, not individual students, were assigned to conditions, which is the cleaner design for an in-school intervention. There were three arms.

*Control.* Standard practice problems. No GPT-4 access.

*GPT Base.* Practice problems with GPT-4 access through a stock ChatGPT-style interface. The student could ask anything. The model would answer anything.

*GPT Tutor.* The same practice problems. The same model. But wrapped in a custom system prompt — a tutoring scaffold built by the research team in consultation with the math teachers, designed to ask questions, withhold final answers, and provide hints aligned to the teacher-written objectives for each problem set.

Students worked through four ninety-minute practice sessions on algebra and trigonometry topics. Then all students — every arm — sat the same closed-book exam, no AI access, testing the material that had been practiced.

Three numbers do the work. Hold all three in your head at once.

*During practice, with AI on.* GPT Base students scored about forty-eight percent higher than control on the practice problems. GPT Tutor students scored about a hundred and twenty-seven percent higher.

*On the closed-book exam, with AI off.* GPT Base students scored about seventeen percent lower than control. Statistically significant. GPT Tutor students scored about the same as control — the scaffold neutralized the harm but did not, in this study, produce a positive effect over control.

Read those two paragraphs again. Same model. Same school. Same problem set. Same exam. The difference between the practice numbers and the exam numbers is what the deployment did to the learning event. The difference between the GPT Base and GPT Tutor exam numbers is what *the pedagogical scaffold* did to the learning event. The Bastani team did not run a fourth arm with a live teacher in the loop — that is the obvious next study and it has not yet been published — but the gradient is visible already. Removing the scaffold actively damaged learning. Adding the scaffold zeroed out the damage. The deployment is what moved the sign.

The practice-session numbers are the part the vendor would put on the marketing slide. They show the AI "working" by the bar most vendors use to measure their products — real-time engagement and performance during the assisted task. A vendor running a pilot that lasted only as long as the practice sessions would call the GPT Base condition an unambiguous win. Districts have purchased software on far less. The exam result is the part the vendor would not show. It is the bar that matters — *did the student learn the material* — and on that bar the unguided AI condition produced a *negative* outcome. Students in the GPT Base arm could not have known. Their feedback during practice was overwhelmingly positive. They reported feeling that they understood the material. They were wrong.

The mechanism Bastani and colleagues report is two-stranded and worth specifying, because the terms get used loosely in the popular conversation.

The first strand is *cognitive offloading*. The term is borrowed from cognitive science — Risko and Gilbert's 2016 review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* is the canonical reference — and it means the use of an external tool to perform a cognitive operation the brain would otherwise perform. Writing down a phone number is cognitive offloading. Using a calculator is cognitive offloading. The behavior is not, in itself, learning-destructive — it is the default mode of how humans cooperate with tools. The Bastani finding is more specific: when *the cognitive operation being offloaded is the one the lesson is trying to teach*, the offloading prevents the learning event. The student outputs the right answer; the schema that would have allowed her to produce right answers without the tool does not form. Students in the GPT Base arm spent less time on each problem, generated fewer attempts before requesting help, and asked questions that more often elicited the final answer than partial scaffolding. The AI absorbed the cognitive work the practice session was designed to require. The practice problem became, functionally, a transcription exercise.

The second strand is *metacognitive miscalibration*. Students in the GPT Base arm predicted, before sitting the exam, that they would score higher than students in control. They scored lower. The fluency of AI-generated explanations produced a feeling of understanding that did not correspond to the schema the exam required. The cognitive-science term for this is the *fluency trap* — processing smooth answers feels like understanding; it is not understanding. The students in the GPT Base arm walked into the exam confident, and the confidence was wrong.

I want to be careful about scope. The Bastani paper shows: unguided AI access, deployed without scaffolding, in a math practice context, produced worse exam outcomes than no AI at all in this population. It does not show, and should not be read to show: AI is harmful to learning in general; AI is harmful in other subjects; the effect persists after teacher-supervised remediation; the effect generalizes to populations with different baseline math skills or different cultural contexts around tool use. It is a single large RCT in a single country in a single subject. Replication in U.S. K-12 contexts is the obvious next step and it has not happened yet. [The Wharton press release at *Knowledge@Wharton*](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/without-guardrails-generative-ai-can-harm-education/) states the bottom line accurately: *without guardrails, generative AI can harm education*. The chapter inherits that scope.

But within that scope, the finding is the cleanest evidence we have that the platform is not neutral. The same software produces a positive-looking practice gain and a negative exam outcome depending on whether someone has thought through the pedagogy of how it is used. The pedagogy is what teachers do. The teacher is what determines whether the deployment falls into condition one or condition two of the five-condition list above. That is the chapter's central claim, and the Bastani data are what permit it to be made as data rather than as preference.

## The ASSISTments inverse: software that works because the teacher does

If Bastani shows what happens when software lands in a classroom without the teacher in the loop, the WestEd evaluations of ASSISTments show what happens when the same kind of software lands with the teacher in the loop and almost nothing else.

ASSISTments is a free online math homework platform developed at Worcester Polytechnic Institute. There is no consumer-facing brand. There is no advertising budget. The software is, by current EdTech standards, plain. The randomized trial that established its efficacy is [Roschelle, Feng, Murphy, and Mason's 2016 paper in *AERA Open*](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1194398.pdf), which enrolled 2,769 grade-7 students across 43 schools in Maine in 2014–15. Students assigned to ASSISTments gained roughly a tenth of a standard deviation on the state-of-Maine eighth-grade math assessment, sustained one year after the intervention. The replication in North Carolina with 5,991 students across 63 schools produced consistent results.

A tenth of a standard deviation is not a transformational effect size. It is, in the language of education research, a small-to-moderate one — roughly three or four months of additional growth in a single school year, sustained into the year after. The reason to dwell on the number is what produced it. [WestEd's 2024 cost-effectiveness analysis](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED655721.pdf) put the all-in cost at $46.23 per student per school year for the Maine implementation. The marginal cost of the software was effectively zero — ASSISTments is free, and the teachers received free professional development from the ASSISTments Foundation. The $46 is dominated by teacher time. Time spent in PD. Time spent adapting practice. Time spent rebuilding the assignment so the platform's feedback loop closed inside the actual lesson the teacher was actually teaching.

Pair that with the Ohio vignette. The Ohio district paid for software that nobody used. The Maine schools used software that nobody paid for and paid instead for the teacher's time. The first investment produced two-thirds waste and no measurable learning. The second investment produced a statistically significant gain on a state assessment. The difference is not the software. The software in both cases is, in the relevant sense, free or nearly so. The difference is which line item the budget protects.

[RAND Europe's January 2026 synthesis of six years of large-scale UK EdTech trials](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2026/01/harnessing-the-benefits-of-edtech-what-research-tells.html) reaches the same conclusion at a higher altitude. Reviewing trials in mathematics, reading, formative feedback, and cognitive skills run with the Education Endowment Foundation and the UK Department for Education, the synthesis lands on a single recommendation, stated about as plainly as a commentary piece states things: leaders should "invest in staff training and ongoing support as much as in devices or software, and ensure equitable access and robust infrastructure for all pupils." The note describes a pattern, not a single trial — *teacher training and support consistently made or broke the success of EdTech interventions, with the most successful programmes providing high-quality, ongoing professional development*. The synthesis is a RAND commentary summarizing RAND Europe's own primary trial work rather than a Cochrane-style meta-analysis of the field [verify: confirm RAND commentary methodology and scope at draft time], but the underlying trial portfolio is large and the conclusion is internally consistent across six years. Where teachers were prepared and supported, the technology helped. Where they were not, the same technology was underused, misused, or simply ignored.

The line that does the work for this chapter is *consistently made or broke*. Not "modestly influenced." Not "was a contributing factor." Made or broke. The variable with the sharpest leverage on whether the software succeeded was who was using it and how well they had been prepared to use it.

A note on the comparative effect sizes, because the early outlines of this chapter circulated a paired number — "+0.28 versus -0.57 standard deviations" — for complementary versus replacement AI. I cannot source that exact pairing to any single peer-reviewed study, and I am not going to use it as if it were a single citation [verify: original source for the "+0.28 vs -0.57" pairing, if any]. What the literature does support is a narrative comparison. On the *complementary* side, the [Ma, Adesope, Nesbit, and Liu 2014 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Educational Psychology*](https://www.apa.org/pubs/journals/features/edu-a0037123.pdf) of 107 effect sizes from 14,321 participants finds intelligent tutoring systems used *as a supplement to teacher-led instruction* producing effect sizes around g = 0.41 against teacher-led large-group instruction. The Stanford [Tutor CoPilot trial](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017) shows a four-percentage-point gain in mastery overall and a nine-percentage-point gain for students of lower-rated tutors when the AI augments the human tutor rather than addressing the student directly. The [Google DeepMind / Eedi LearnLM trial](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/LearnLM/learnLM_nov25.pdf) shows AI supervised by expert human tutors outperforming unsupervised expert human tutoring by 5.5 percentage points on novel questions. On the *replacement* side, Bastani's GPT Base condition produced the seventeen-percent exam-score decrement reported above, which converts to an SD-based effect somewhere in the range of g = −0.5 to −0.6 depending on the control distribution [verify: SD conversion should be calculated from Bastani et al.'s supplementary tables and cited as a calculation, not as a direct number from the paper]. The direction is consistent. AI deployed as a complement to a teacher produces real, replicated, positive effects in the small-to-moderate range. AI deployed as a replacement for a teacher — at least in the Bastani design — produces a negative effect of comparable or larger magnitude. The Ma meta-analysis is twelve years older than the Bastani paper and the underlying tools have changed, but the structural finding has held across generations of educational software.

## Engaging Horvath: the scalpel and the butter knife

There is a serious counter to the argument I am making in this chapter, and it is the argument of [Jared Cooney Horvath's 2024 book *The Digital Delusion*](https://www.amazon.com/Digital-Delusion-Reclaim-Classroom-Future/dp/1922616672). I want to engage with it, because it is the strongest version of the case for blanket EdTech skepticism, and the chapter's claim about teachers does not survive if Horvath's claim about screens is right in the strong form he states it.

Horvath's empirical case has a hard core that I take seriously and a soft periphery that I do not. The hard core: international assessments — PISA, TIMSS, PIRLS — show consistent negative correlations between screen exposure and academic achievement. Students using computers six or more hours daily on PISA score sixty-six points lower than non-users. TIMSS 2019 found daily computer use in math and science associated with forty-one to fifty-one point declines. PIRLS 2021's paper-to-digital mode switch produced a twenty-seven-point overall drop. Meta-analytic syntheses of decades of EdTech trials produce an average effect size somewhere around +0.29 — real, positive, but below the 0.40 "hinge point" Hattie's *Visible Learning* identifies as the threshold for meaningful impact. The screen-inferiority effect in reading and the handwriting superiority effect in note-taking are both well replicated. Horvath is not making these numbers up. They are, by and large, the consensus numbers.

The soft periphery: the inference Horvath draws from these numbers is that screens are *biologically incompatible* with learning, that the failure is in the technology itself rather than in how it is used, and that the response is to ban most EdTech with narrow exceptions for intelligent tutoring and assistive technology. This is where the argument breaks down, and it breaks down in two places.

The first place is *category collapse*. The PISA, TIMSS, and PIRLS correlations measure ambient, recreational-grade screen exposure — six hours a day of unspecified computer use, much of it outside school, almost none of it under structured teacher direction. The meta-analytic average of +0.29 lumps together intelligent tutoring systems (around +0.52), blended learning with bounded online time (around +0.61), assistive technology for students with learning disabilities (around +0.61), writing instruction tools (around +0.81), one-to-one laptop programs (around +0.16), unrestricted general technology use (around +0.29), and a long tail of vendor pilots with no usable comparison group. Averaging these together and concluding *EdTech doesn't work* is what would happen if a meta-analysis of surgical interventions averaged the appendectomies done by trained surgeons with the appendectomies done by untrained barbers and concluded that surgery slightly helps but mostly doesn't. The correct conclusion is not *surgery doesn't work*. The correct conclusion is *surgical training matters*.

This is the analogy I have been making in public for the past year and I want to make it explicit here, because it is the move that lets the chapter accept Horvath's data and reach the opposite policy conclusion: *we are using scalpels as butter knives and wondering why learning is bleeding out. The problem is not the scalpel. The problem is us.*

The second place the strong Horvath argument breaks down is *the implementation paradox in his own evidence*. Chapter 5 of *The Digital Delusion* assembles the strongest in-the-data case in the book — the smartphone ban studies from the UK, Spain, Norway, and Sweden, all showing improvements when phones are removed from schools. Horvath presents this as confirmation that screens are bad. But the same schools that removed personal smartphones kept their school-issued Chromebooks. They removed the recreational, attention-capturing, dopamine-engineered device and retained the supervised, task-bounded one — and learning improved. That is not a finding about screens as a category. It is a finding about *which screens, deployed how, with what supervision*. The implementation specificity Horvath denies in Chapter 3 is the implementation specificity his Chapter 5 evidence requires. The book's own data run his own argument backwards.

I want to be precise about what this means for the strategic frame I think this entire book should adopt. The Horvath empirical findings are largely correct. I am not going to dispute the PISA correlation. I am not going to dispute the screen-inferiority effect in reading. I am not going to dispute the mode effect in handwriting. These hold under the conditions in which they were measured, and the conditions in which they were measured are the conditions that dominate ambient school-age screen exposure: high-dosage, low-structure, no-trained-adult-in-the-loop use. Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. That is the recurring shape. The data describe what occurs in the absence of skilled human judgment. The policy implication is not that the skilled human is unnecessary. The policy implication is that the skilled human is the variable, and we have built an education system that systematically refuses to develop, train, support, and trust her.

The strong Horvath conclusion — *ban screens* — is what you get if you assume the skilled human can be replaced by a rule. The chapter is making the opposite case: the data themselves are evidence that the rule is the wrong instrument and the trained professional is the right one.

There is a softer version of the Horvath argument I do not think this chapter has to fight. He is right that smartphones, as currently designed, do not belong in classrooms. He is right that screen dosage matters. He is right that handwriting should not be sacrificed in early literacy on the altar of typing speed. He is right that current AI-in-education research is, by and large, methodologically poor — small studies, missing controls, sign errors in the meta-analyses, vendor-funded pilots with rigged comparisons. The careful version of his book is a useful contribution. The blanket version — *screens are biologically incompatible with learning, ban them* — is the move the data do not support and that the book's own ban-study chapter quietly refutes.

The careful version is consistent with the argument in this chapter. The blanket version is not. The chapter takes Horvath at the level his evidence actually supports and rejects the leap he makes beyond it.

## The tractor argument

I want to close the case with an analogy, and then I want to name where the analogy breaks down — because an analogy that breaks down without acknowledgment becomes a new source of confusion.

Before tractors, the most productive human laborer working with the best draft animals could plow somewhere between one and two acres in a long day. The gasoline tractor moved the daily ceiling to ten or twenty acres for the same human-hours. Food production was transformed. The rural workforce contracted from about thirty percent of the U.S. workforce in 1910 to under five percent by 1990, while gross agricultural output rose by orders of magnitude. The tractor was a genuine general-purpose technology. It did not replace farmers. It rewrote what farming was.

The tractor also did not farm itself. A tractor without a trained operator is a $90,000 ornament. The first farmers who tried to use early tractors without instruction broke equipment, compacted soil, planted too deep, harvested too early. The training infrastructure — county extension services, state agricultural colleges, USDA demonstration programs — took two decades to build out and was, in the historical record, as important as the machine itself in translating tractor-capability into agricultural productivity. [Zvi Griliches's 1957 paper on the diffusion of hybrid corn](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1905380) — one of the most-cited papers in economic history — found that the local rate of adoption was determined more by the capacity of the local extension service to train farmers than by the underlying agronomic gain of the new seed. The seed was the same everywhere. The training was not. The training was what determined whether the seed produced a yield gain.

The platform is the tractor. The trained teacher is the operator. The school is the farm. And there is a constraint at the level of the farm — a finite quantity that AI cannot scale out of — that the analogy captures precisely.

Each farmer farms a finite plot. The tractor lets her cover more of it, faster, with less drudgery and more precision. The system as a whole still needs farmers to cover the land that exists, because the land that exists is the constraint. Teaching has the analogous fixed quantity, which is students. Each teacher teaches a finite number of students set by relational and cognitive constraints. John Hattie's *Visible Learning* synthesis reports that teacher-student relationships and feedback both come in around d = 0.72 in effect size, which are the highest-leverage interventions in education and which do not scale past somewhere around twenty-five to thirty students per teacher regardless of what tools the teacher has. The question is never how many students the AI can scale to. The question is whether the teacher has learned to use the AI to teach the twenty-five in her room better. The tractor lets a farmer farm her acreage with less drudgery and more precision. AI should let a teacher teach her class with less drudgery and more precision. The tractor did not make one farmer responsible for fifty farms. AI should not make one teacher responsible for five hundred students.

The analogy breaks down in three places, and the chapter has to name them.

The first place is that the tractor does not make the farmer worse if she lets it idle. A farmer who never starts the tractor farms about as well as a farmer before tractors existed. Bastani's finding is that unguided AI does *active* harm — not just failure to help. The tool actively damages the schema the student is supposed to build. Agriculture has no equivalent. A student who uses unguided AI on practice problems learns *less* than a student who never had AI access. The platform is not neutral when misdeployed. It is a scalpel; it can cut in either direction.

The second place is that the farmer's product is grain and the teacher's product is a changed student. Grain is fungible and measurable. A learning event is neither. The Bastani study works as a clean test because the closed-book exam approximates the schema the lesson aimed to build. Most teaching outcomes do not have a Bastani-style instrument. Most of teaching is closer to *did this student leave the year more capable of thinking like a historian* than to *did this student score seventeen percent higher on a closed-book exam*. The tractor analogy implicitly inherits agriculture's measurement convenience. Teaching is harder to measure than agriculture, and the analogy should not be allowed to paper over that.

The third place is that the tractor does not change the farmer. Continuous tractor use does not, over decades, alter the farmer's own farming skill — if anything, it makes some manual skills atrophy. AI use, by contrast, will change what teachers themselves know and can do. A teacher who has co-planned with Claude for three years thinks differently about lesson design than a teacher who has never used the tool. The change is not necessarily bad — it is analogous to how surgeons trained on robotic equipment think differently about anatomy — but the analogy does not capture this dimension. The tractor is a tool. AI is a tool that is also a collaborator. That distinction matters when the chapter argues for teacher training, because what teachers are training to become is partly a function of what they are training with.

The analogy still earns its place. It captures the central point with one image: the machinery is the floor, the operator is the variable, the system's productivity depends on whether the operator has been trained. But the chapter names the three breakdowns so the reader does not mistake the analogy for the argument. The argument is the data. The analogy is the way the data fits in one sentence.

## The chapter's turn

The educational technology industry got something important right. AI can improve student outcomes. The platforms can do real work. The infrastructure investments of the past fifteen years were not, on balance, wasted — they laid down the connectivity and the device base that makes any of the work in this book even possible to talk about. The industry is not the villain. The industry is, by and large, building something genuinely useful.

What the industry got wrong is the implicit theory of who is supposed to be using what it built. The theory was that the platform was the active ingredient. The theory was that if the right software arrived in the right number of classrooms, learning would improve in proportion to the deployment. The theory was that the teacher was, at best, a delivery mechanism and at worst an impediment. The Ohio district's sixty-seven percent unused licenses are what that theory looks like at the procurement level. The Bastani GPT Base condition is what that theory looks like at the learning level. Same theory, different failure modes, same root cause.

The finding the industry is not advertising is the one running through everything in this chapter. The platform is almost never the variable that determines whether students learn. The teacher is. The Bastani paper shows the platform damaging when the teacher is out of the loop and neutralizing the damage when a teacher-designed scaffold is in the loop. The ASSISTments trial shows free software producing measurable learning gains when paid-for teacher PD wraps around it. The RAND Europe synthesis shows training consistently making or breaking implementation across six years of large-scale trials. The complementary-versus-replacement comparison shows the AI-as-supplement effect in the positive small-to-moderate range and the AI-as-replacement effect in the negative comparable range. The Horvath data — the PISA, TIMSS, and PIRLS correlations the ban-everything camp brandishes — describe what happens when the technology is deployed at high dosage with no skilled human in the loop. Every finding in the public conversation is a finding about who, or whether anyone, is making the call.

Platform quality sets the ceiling. Teacher training determines whether students reach it.

This is the second of the two wrong answers of 2025, in the same shape as the first. The phone ban was a policy that removed a device and skipped the teacher. The EdTech purchase order is a policy that adds a device and skips the teacher. The two policies look opposite; they share a flaw. Neither of them is about teachers. The third answer — the one this book exists to argue for — is the one nobody bought enough of, did not buy at the right ratio when they bought it, and has consistently underfunded against the line items for hardware, pouches, and software licenses that the same school year was paying for.

We are using scalpels as butter knives and wondering why learning is bleeding out. The problem is not the scalpel. The problem is us.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The Bastani finding is a single large RCT in high school mathematics in a single school district in Turkey; whether the negative effect of unguided AI on exam performance replicates in U.S. K-12 contexts, in other subjects, and across populations with different baseline tool-use norms is the obvious next study, and it has not been run. The GPT Tutor scaffold in Bastani neutralized the harm but did not, in this study, produce a positive effect over control; whether a live teacher orchestrating the scaffolded use would convert *neutral* into *positive* is the inference this chapter is making, and it is plausible but not yet directly demonstrated in a large pre-registered trial. And the long-run effect of cyborg-style AI use on teacher skill itself — whether teachers who have co-planned with Claude for years can still plan well without it, whether some forms of pedagogical fluency atrophy when offloaded — has not been studied at all, and should be. I would rather name these openings than pretend the empirical case is more settled than it is.

## Still puzzling

Why does the +127% practice gain in Bastani's GPT Tutor arm, combined with the null exam effect for the same arm, tell us *that the scaffolded AI was helping in some way the exam did not capture* — or *that practice-session gains are always partly illusory, even in the better-designed condition*? The paper does not fully resolve this, and the answer matters for how districts should read pilot-stage performance data going forward.

Why has no major EdTech vendor of which I am aware adopted the WestEd 70/30 framing — at most thirty percent of district technology budgets on hardware and software, at least seventy percent on professional development — as part of how the product is sold? The math is in the vendor's interest if the product actually works. Why is the sales model still designed around the inverse ratio?

Why is the implementation gap, after eighty years of the industry's existence and decades of education-research attention to it, still the constant of the field? Is it a failure of vendor design, a failure of district procurement, a failure of teacher preparation, or a structural failure of who decides what gets bought in American schools? My current reading is that it is mostly the fourth one. I am open to being wrong about that.

---

## What would change my mind

A second large pre-registered RCT of unguided AI access in K-12 mathematics in a U.S. context that fails to replicate the Bastani negative effect — particularly one that included a live-teacher-in-the-loop arm and found it produced no advantage over a no-AI control — would substantially weaken the chapter's central empirical claim. A randomized comparison of two demographically matched districts in which one invested at the WestEd 70/30 ratio in teacher AI training and the other invested at the inverse ratio in platform licenses, with no measurable difference in student outcomes after two years, would also do it. I do not expect either study to come out that way. I would rather know than assume.
