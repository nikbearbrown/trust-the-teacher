## The decade we ran the experiment without controls — and what it proved

The phone-ban debate that swept American state legislatures in 2024 and 2025 produced two answers to one question, and both answers were wrong in the same structural way. They both assumed the judgment call had to be made at the policy level, once, for all classrooms, by people who were not in the room.

The first answer was total prohibition: bell-to-bell bans, devices in pouches or lockers from first bell to last, administrative exceptions only for medical or IEP accommodation. It is the model Los Angeles and Florida and New York have moved toward, and it is the model that gets the fundamental problem right. Smartphones in classrooms degrade attention and learning — the evidence for this is consistent enough across enough studies that the chapter is not going to re-litigate it. The distraction is real. The harm is real. Total prohibition is a sensible response to a real problem.

It is also, in the implementation, a machine that distributes its costs unevenly. New York City ran a version of it from 2006 to 2015, and Mayor de Blasio rescinded it in March of that year. <!-- [verify 2026-05-16] De Blasio/Fariña announced the rescission on January 7, 2015, not March. Change to "January 2015." --> The reason the ban came undone was not that it was wrong about the harm. It was that enforcement fell hardest on low-income schools, which had metal detectors at the door and could actually enforce the rule, while wealthy schools quietly ignored it. The device-storage industry that grew up around the Manhattan ban — phone-storage shops outside school doors, charging families a dollar a day to hold what the policy had excluded — transferred a tax onto the families least able to pay it. Total prohibition, without infrastructure, gets unwound by its own enforcement asymmetries.

The opposite model — phones permitted, teachers manage it case by case in the moment — was what most U.S. schools ran between roughly 2010 and 2022. Teacher surveys from 2020 to 2022 showed this model collapsing in real time. Teachers reported spending twenty to thirty percent of class time on phone-related redirection. <!-- [verify 2026-05-16] Specific 20–30% range not pinned to a named survey. Name source or soften to documented surrounding stat (Pew 2024: 72% of HS teachers say cellphone distraction is major; IES: lenient-policy teachers 21pp more likely to lose >1hr/wk to phone management). --> Most reported no institutional support for their enforcement decisions — every call was theirs to make and theirs to defend when a student argued back or a parent called. Discretion without training is just inconsistency. The decision load falls on individual teachers without institutional backing, and the result is that norms erode unevenly across classrooms in the same building. Students learn quickly which teacher will enforce and which will not. The variance that the educational exemption is supposed to capture as professional judgment shows up instead as the demographic of luck — whose classroom you ended up in this period.

Hold both failures together, because the shared structure is what matters. The ban fails because the rule cannot adjust for the classroom and the learner in front of it; the enforcement cost lands on the children least equipped to absorb it. The no-rule fails because the call falls on a teacher who has not been prepared to make it well; the variance also lands on the children least equipped to absorb it. Both models are making the same mistake. They are trying to solve, at the policy level, a problem that lives at the level of the classroom, the lesson, and the specific student.

## Three things "discretion" means — and why only one of them works

Professional discretion is a phrase that sounds like one thing and means at least three.

The first kind is what the sociologist Michael Lipsky, writing in 1980 about police officers and social workers and teachers, called street-level bureaucrat discretion. <!-- [verify 2026-05-16] Lipsky is more commonly classified as a political scientist / public-administration scholar (MIT). "Sociologist" is loose shorthand; consider revising. --> His argument was that front-line public servants always have to exercise judgment, because the policy as written cannot anticipate every case they will meet. A rule comprehensive enough to eliminate the judgment call is a rule that has never met a classroom.

The second kind is the discretion that licensed professions confer on their members. A physician decides which treatment to prescribe inside a scope of practice defined by her training and her license. The discretion is real, but it is constrained — by what she has been trained to know, by the protocols of the institution she practices in, by the continuing competence she is required to demonstrate. The discretion is not arbitrary and it is not unsupported. It is *trained* discretion, *accountable* discretion, the kind of judgment that medicine extracts from its practitioners by paying for the preparation that makes the judgment trustworthy.

The third kind is what schools currently call discretion: the AUP that says "subject to teacher approval," the educational exemption written into the phone-ban law by a state legislature that handed the call back to the teacher without funding the training that would let two teachers in two different schools reach the same defensible answer for the same case. This is discretion in name only. It confers permission without preparation.

The phone-ban debate has been operating with the first kind, which it cannot escape, and the third kind, which it has badly designed, while treating the second kind as either unnecessary or already present. It is neither. The history teacher on Friday morning, reading the dashboard that shows which of her students is stuck on which paragraph — she is exercising the second kind of discretion. She has been trained to know what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what cognitive substitution looks like, what a lesson gains from devices and what it loses. Her call is not arbitrary. Her English colleague's call to run the Gatsby seminar without devices is not arbitrary either. Both calls are professional judgments made by teachers who have been prepared to make them. That preparation is the thing the policy debate keeps reaching for and failing to fund.

## Five components — and what's missing from the half-built versions

The design pattern this chapter is naming has five components. Together, they are what makes the history teacher's Friday lesson possible. Separately, none of them is sufficient.

The first is *default-off*. Devices are presumed absent. Use is the exception, not the assumption. This is what the prohibition statutes get right, and the third model does not abandon it.

The second is *scheduled structured-technology time* — a designated block when devices come out by design rather than by exception. The block exists to do two things the bell-to-bell ban cannot. It creates a space for supervised access to tools that low-income students otherwise encounter only at home, without adult guidance, in the fragmented hours after dinner. And it creates a context for digital literacy — you cannot teach students to use AI tools thoughtfully without a time and place in which to teach it. A school that bans devices for five days and then expects graduates who can navigate AI-assisted work is asking the next employer to do the teaching the school refused to do.

The third is *teacher discretion over the contextual decisions* inside the structured time. What is the device being used for in this lesson, with this group, for this assignment? Which students need the alternative track? When does the lesson stop using the device and return to the conversation in the room?

The fourth — and this is the component without which the third collapses back into total permissiveness on Fridays — is *trained discretion*. The teacher making the call has been prepared to make it. She has working knowledge of what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what cognitive offloading looks like. Without this component, the scheduled device block is just a scheduled distraction block with better branding.

The fifth is *institutional backup*. Administrative support for the teacher's enforcement and integration decisions. Acceptable-use policies written to define the educational exemption with enough specificity to be teachable, not just enough generality to be defensible. Principals who back the teacher's call when a parent phones to ask why her daughter's class had device time and her son's did not. Building-level coherence about what the structured time is for, developed through collaborative planning rather than left to each teacher to improvise alone.

Los Angeles Unified tried a different path and documented a different failure. The Yondr pouch is not a system. It is a structural intervention that bets the structure can replace judgment. When students find the structure can be defeated, what is revealed is that the judgment was never built underneath it.

Finland's 2025 legislation restricting student smartphone use built the discretion into the statute itself: students may use phones during lessons for learning purposes with permission from their teacher. The teacher has explicit statutory authority to allow educational use. Estonia has run BYOD as national policy since 2014, with digital literacy as a curriculum strand from grade one and an educational technologist in most schools; its 2025 AI Leap initiative placed AI tools in the hands of 20,000 high school students and 3,000 teachers, with training arriving alongside the tools. The principle Estonia demonstrates is the one this chapter keeps returning to: the tools and the training arrive together. Neither country maps cleanly onto American conditions. Both demonstrate that the design space this chapter is sketching has been entered at national scale, with measurable success. The pattern is not invented. It is unbuilt here.

The pattern repeats across the implementations the chapter has been tracing. Some get the first model right and stop. Some skip the trained-teacher layer entirely and call the structure the system. Two countries have actually built the second kind of discretion into the statute and the curriculum.

## What the trained teacher does that the algorithm cannot — five moves

The scheduled device block creates the context. The trained teacher creates the lesson. What the trained teacher actually does inside a room of thirty is what the policy debate has almost entirely failed to discuss.

Start with differentiation. Carol Ann Tomlinson spent three decades documenting how flexible grouping — groups that re-form every two or three weeks based on formative assessment, not fixed tracks — serves diverse learners better than static ability grouping. The framework has been known since the 1990s. It is also, in practice, extraordinarily time-consuming. To differentiate well, a teacher has to assess continuously, regroup frequently, and prepare different versions of materials for different configurations of her class. Most teachers, most of the time, cannot do this — not because they do not know how, but because the hours required do not exist.

What AI has changed is the time cost. A teacher who used to spend two hours preparing three reading-level versions of a primary source now spends twelve minutes. A teacher who used to look at exit-ticket responses overnight and regroup students for the next day can now look at student work in progress and regroup for the next ten minutes of the same lesson. The Tomlinson framework is the same; the friction that prevented it from being used consistently has been dramatically reduced.

But differentiation is a preparation move. The more important claim is about what happens in real time, during the lesson, when the teaching is actually occurring. For this, the most important study in the current literature is a 2024 experiment run at Stanford.

The Tutor CoPilot study gave human tutors an AI assistant during real one-on-one tutoring sessions and measured what happened. The headline finding was a four-percentage-point improvement in student mastery overall, and nine percentage points for students working with lower-rated tutors. The mechanism finding is the one that belongs in this chapter. Researchers analyzed more than 350,000 tutor-student message exchanges and found that tutors with the AI in their corner asked more probing questions and gave fewer generic responses. They reduced *good job, keep going* and increased *what would happen if we changed this variable?* The AI was not generating the questions and pushing them to the student. The AI was changing what the human tutor asked.

This is a precise description of what good teaching looks like, empirically induced by a tool that the tutor was using in the background. The student's experience was better human attention. The mechanism by which the better attention happened was AI augmenting the human's judgment in real time. The caveat is real: this is one-on-one chat-based tutoring, not classroom instruction, and the causal chain to a classroom of thirty has not been established in a randomized trial. But the M-Powering Teachers research from Demszky's lab provides a brick-and-mortar version of the same pattern — automated feedback on teacher questioning practice, in real K-12 classrooms, producing a 20 percent increase in focusing questions — and the direction is the same. When a trained human has AI in her corner, the human does more of what good teaching looks like, not less.

*Figure 9.02 — Tutor CoPilot: what an AI in the tutor's corner changes*

The cognitive division of labor that makes this work can be stated plainly. AI handles pattern recognition, recall, factual content delivery, routine first-pass feedback, summarization, translation, generating practice problems at a specified difficulty level. These are cognitively real tasks, but they are the kind of thinking machines do at scale, and having them done by machine is not a loss. What the teacher does that AI cannot is a different category: auditing whether the AI's output actually answers the question correctly in the context this specific student is working in; interpreting what a confused answer reveals about what the student is actually thinking; formulating which question to ask this student right now; and the hour-by-hour relational adjustment that comes from knowing this specific child across this specific year. These are the moves the history teacher is making when she walks the room with the dashboard. They are the moves the English teacher is making when she decides the Gatsby seminar does not need devices. They are not moves AI makes. They are the educational exemption. And the educational exemption only means anything when there is a trained teacher available to exercise it.

## The equity objection that must be taken seriously — and the answer it requires

Two objections to the third model are serious enough to deserve a full answer.

The first is the equity objection, and it has a legitimate history behind it. The documented pattern in American schools is that professional discretion often produces different educational experiences for similar students in the same building, and those differences tend to correlate with race and income in ways that are unjust. This is the reason the high-stakes-accountability movement of the 1990s and 2000s eroded teacher discretion in the first place — not out of contempt for teachers, but as a response to documented failures of unsupported discretion.

The answer is that the equity objection is an argument against *unsupported* discretion, which is also the chapter's argument. The variance the equity critique rightly fears is the variance produced when teachers exercise judgment without training, without collaborative norms, without institutional backing. Susan Moore Johnson's research across fourteen high-poverty Massachusetts schools <!-- [verify 2026-05-16] *Where Teachers Thrive* (Harvard Ed Press 2019) studies 14 high-poverty urban schools, with 6 explicitly in one Massachusetts city. Confirm whether all 14 are MA or only 6/14; consider softening to "fourteen high-poverty urban schools, including six in Massachusetts." --> found that teacher quality — and therefore the quality of teacher judgment — is a school-level variable as much as an individual one. A teacher in a well-organized school exercises better discretion than the same teacher in a poorly organized school. The third model is the affirmative case for discretion supported by training, by building-level coherence, and by administrative backing. The evidence against bad discretion is not evidence against the second kind of discretion. It is the argument for building the infrastructure that makes the second kind possible.

The second objection is simpler and also correct: students will try to abuse the educational exemption. This is true. It is not the problem the chapter is solving. Total prohibition makes the failure-to-abuse outcome certain and the failure-to-learn-with-the-tool outcome also certain. The third model accepts that a trained teacher will redirect some students who try to misuse the structured time, because she has been prepared to recognize the difference and has the institutional backing to enforce the redirect. The abuse rate under the third model is greater than zero. The not-learning rate under total prohibition is one hundred percent for the work the policy has prohibited. These are the trade-offs on offer. The third model accepts a small rate of in-the-moment abuse in exchange for a structured learning opportunity for the students who use the time as intended. That is the trade only a trained teacher can police. Which is, again, the point.

## What the principal deciding this week can actually do

Suppose you are the principal or the district leader reading this chapter, trying to figure out what Monday morning actually looks like.

Start with an audit. Pull whatever your current device policy says and mark it against the five components. Do you have a clear default-off rule? Do you have any scheduled structured-technology time when devices come out by design rather than by exception? Does your acceptable-use policy give teachers contextual authority to make calls inside the structured time, or does every decision route to your office? Have your teachers received training on what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what cognitive substitution looks like? Is the educational exemption defined specifically enough that two teachers in two different schools in your district would reach the same defensible answer for the same case? Most districts have the first component in some form. Most are missing components two, three, and four entirely. The audit takes half a day. The map it produces tells you where to spend the year.

Put the structured-technology time on the master schedule and protect it. The specific cadence matters less than the fact that it is scheduled, which means it cannot be quietly displaced by the next testing window or fire drill. The scheduled block is the surface where the educational exemption operates as a lesson rather than as an administrative favor. Without it, the exemption is back to being third-kind discretion: permission without preparation.

The teachers who are already the people other colleagues come to with AI questions exist in almost every building. Find them. Pay them to formalize that role. Cover one section of their teaching load with district funds and assign the time to peer coaching. One trained teacher-coach per school, running sustained coaching cycles, using Title II-A funds that are already in the budget and currently mostly paying for August vendor demonstrations that the research shows do not move practice.

Write the educational exemption's operational definition with your teachers, not for them. Convene a working group — the teacher-coaches, the subject-area leads, a special-education representative, and at least one teacher who is skeptical of AI in classrooms. Draft language specific enough that two teachers could reach the same defensible answer for the same case. Pilot it for a semester. Revise it. The norms developed inside a profession are the ones the profession will actually enforce.

And when the parent calls — when a parent calls to ask why her daughter's class had device time and her son's did not — give the honest answer and back the teacher who made the call. The honest answer is that the history teacher was running a differentiated primary-source lesson that used AI-generated reading-level versions, and the English teacher was running a Socratic seminar that does not. That answer requires a principal who has read the lesson plans, understands the difference, and will say out loud that both decisions were professional judgments made by teachers who were trained to make them. That is a different job than the one most AUPs currently assign to building administrators. It is the job the third model requires.

None of this needs federal action. None of it needs new state legislation. The cohort of buildings that has made this trade does not yet exist at scale. It will be built by district leaders making the trade, school by school, until the cohort exists.

---

The legislature wrote a rule that prohibits the device and an exception that requires the call. The rule handles the modal case — bell-to-bell absence, no personal smartphones, no ambient distraction. The exception handles everything the rule cannot. The exception cannot be made well by a teacher who has not been trained to make it.

The history teacher walking the room on Friday morning with her dashboard is not there because the law told her to be. She is there because the work she is doing — reading which students are stuck on which paragraph, pulling the right five students to the back table, deciding that this lesson earns the devices and that lesson does not — is work no statute and no algorithm can do. It is the educational exemption, in operation, in a classroom, on a Friday morning in Colorado.

The legislator at the desk wrote the rule. The algorithm in the cloud cannot make the call. The trained teacher in the room is the only one of the three who can. Everything the country has spent the last two years arguing about — the pouches and the bans and the exemptions and the distraction and the equity and the mental health — comes down to whether we are willing to fund the preparation that makes her call trustworthy.

We have not been. The argument of this book is that we should be. The room she runs is why.
