# Chapter 9: Phone Fridays and the Room the Teacher Runs

*What teacher discretion over technology actually looks like — and the infrastructure that makes it possible*

---

Picture a middle school somewhere in Colorado in the fall of 2026. The state's [House Bill 25-1135](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/hb25-1135) took effect on July 1, and every public school in the state — local board, district charter, institute charter — is now required by law to adopt, implement, and publish a policy on student communication-device use during the school day. The legislature did not specify what the policy must say. It specified that each school must have one and must make it public. The discretion was delegated downward.

The school I am asking you to picture chose this shape. Monday through Thursday, phones go into the locker at the first bell. Lunch, recess, between-period transitions — locker. No exceptions for "I want to check something." The default is off, and the default holds for four days a week. On Friday, the default changes. Devices are available during instructional time when the teacher in the room decides they should be. The history teacher uses the Friday hour for a primary-source analysis with AI assistance — three reading-level versions of a Reconstruction-era newspaper editorial, each student working through the version calibrated to her current reading level, the teacher walking the room with a tablet that surfaces which students are stuck on which paragraphs. The math teacher uses Friday for individual practice with immediate feedback — adaptive problem sets generated for each student's current weakness, the teacher pulling small groups to the back table for the conceptual moves the practice exposes. The English teacher does not use devices on Friday. She is running a Socratic seminar on *The Great Gatsby*, and the seminar requires uninterrupted attention to other humans in the room. Her students keep their devices in the locker the same as Monday through Thursday. She decides. Nobody overrules her.

The school I just described is a composite. I am not citing it. *No real school is currently running a branded program called "Phone Fridays."* The term is the one this chapter is using for a design pattern that exists in fragmentary practice — in scattered BYOD days, in 1:1 technology blocks, in the "Tech Tuesday" arrangements some elementary schools have run for years — but that the policy literature has not yet consolidated into a named third option. The chapter is doing prescriptive design work as much as descriptive case-study work. I would rather tell you that openly than borrow the name of a real program to mean something it does not mean. *[verify: as of press, no district has adopted the specific "Phone Fridays" branding the chapter uses. The design pattern this chapter is naming is real; the brand name is the author's.]*

This is the chapter that names a third governance model the phone-ban debate has refused to articulate clearly — and that argues for what it would actually take to make the third model work in a US public-school district.

## The two failed models, and what is missing from both

Education policy in 2025 produced two answers to the question of what to do about technology in classrooms. The first answer was *total prohibition*: bell-to-bell phone bans, with thin administrative exceptions for medical or IEP-related accommodation. The second answer was *total permissiveness*: phones permitted unless a particular teacher in a particular moment said otherwise, which was the de facto US default from roughly 2010 through 2022 and is still the default in many districts that have not yet passed a local restriction policy.

Both models have been tested at scale. Both have failed in characteristic ways.

The cleanest historical case study of total prohibition is New York City's 2006–2015 ban — bell-to-bell, district-wide, no educational exemption, enforced primarily through the metal detectors that were standard equipment in low-income high schools and rare in wealthy ones. Mayor de Blasio rescinded the ban in [March 2015](https://www.nyc.gov/office-of-the-mayor/news/013-15/mayor-de-blasio-chancellor-fari-a-lift-school-cell-phone-ban). The stated reasons — covered in detail in Chapter 1 — were that enforcement fell hardest on low-income schools, that the device-storage industry that grew up around the ban transferred a tax onto low-income families, and that parents wanted communication access with their children. The ban was not wrong about the harm. Every research review of the intervening decade found smartphones in classrooms degrade attention and learning. The ban was wrong about the implementation. *Prohibition without infrastructure gets unwound by its own enforcement asymmetries*, and the 2025 state-level statutes that are the descendants of NYC 2006 are partial corrections — most include educational exemptions, most leave storage to schools rather than to private lockers outside the door — but the central problem persists. A rule passed at the state level is being implemented in 13,000 districts, and the implementation falls hardest on the schools with the least administrative capacity to do it well.

The opposite model — phones permitted, teachers manage it case by case in the moment — was what most US schools ran between roughly 2010 and 2022. The evidence on this period forms the prosecution case in Chapter 1: declining attention spans, rising classroom disruption, displaced sleep and homework time, the downstream mental-health concerns the [Haidt-Twenge](https://jonathanhaidt.com/anxious-generation/) versus [Odgers-Orben-Przybylski](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-00902-2) debate continues to litigate. Teacher surveys from 2020 through 2022 showed the model collapsing — teachers reported devoting twenty to thirty percent of class time to phone-related redirection, and most reported that they had no institutional support for enforcement decisions. The American Academy of Pediatrics' [5 Cs framework](https://www.aap.org/en/patient-care/media-and-children/) — Child, Content, Calm, Crowding-out, Communication — was designed as a pediatrician-counseling-a-family framework. It is excellent for that purpose. Applied as a real-time classroom-management framework for thirty students by one teacher with no training in how to operationalize it, it is unworkable. The 5 Cs are not wrong. They are at the wrong abstraction layer.

What total permissiveness demonstrated is that *discretion without training is just inconsistency.* The decision load on the teacher is too high, the institutional backup is too thin, and the result over time is that classroom norms erode unevenly across teachers and classrooms in the same building. Students learn quickly which teacher will enforce and which won't. The variance the educational exemption is supposed to capture as professional judgment shows up instead as the demographic of luck — whose teacher you got this period.

Hold both failures in mind. The ban fails because the rule cannot adjust for the classroom and the learner in front of it; the implementation cost lands on the children with the least capacity to absorb it. The no-rule fails because the call falls on a teacher who has not been trained to make it well; the variance lands on the children with the least capacity to absorb that either. The shared structural fact is that the call is being made in both cases — *the question is by whom, with what preparation, and with what institutional backing.* The book has been making one version of this point chapter after chapter. This chapter is where it becomes a design.

Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The book is about who should be. The third model — the one the policy debate has not yet named clearly — is professional discretion within a trained framework. Devices presumptively absent. Designated structured-technology time when the teacher decides the device serves the lesson. Training that lets the teacher distinguish genuine AI-assisted learning from cognitive substitution. Institutional support — written AUPs specific enough to be teachable, principals who back the call, building-level coherence about what the structured time is for. This is not a new idea. It is the structure that exists in every other licensed profession. A physician practices inside a hospital's protocols *and* within her own clinical judgment. The protocol exists to handle the modal case; the judgment exists for the cases the protocol cannot anticipate. The phone-ban debate has tried to do everything with the protocol and nothing with the judgment, or everything with the judgment and nothing with the protocol. Neither half works alone.

## What discretion actually is — three different things wearing one name

I have been using the word *discretion* as though it pointed to one thing. It points to at least three, and the argument turns on which one we mean.

The first is what Michael Lipsky called [street-level bureaucrat discretion](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1662657/full) — the on-the-ground judgment a public-service worker has to exercise because the policy as written cannot anticipate every case she will meet. Police officers deciding whether to issue a citation. Social workers deciding what counts as an emergency. Teachers deciding which student needs the next minute of attention. Lipsky's 1980 argument, restated by the [2025 Frontiers in Education paper](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1662657/full) that extends the framework explicitly to faculty discretionary decisions about AI tools, is that this kind of discretion is *not* something the system grants. It is something the work structurally requires. A rule that strips it away does not eliminate the discretion. It just makes the discretion unsanctioned and inconsistent.

The second is professional discretion as it exists in licensed professions. The physician decides which treatment to prescribe inside a scope of practice defined by her training and her license; the discretion is constrained by what she has been trained to know and revisable through continuing competence she is required to demonstrate. This is the form of discretion this chapter is arguing for. It is *trained* discretion, *credentialed* discretion, *accountable* discretion. It is the kind of judgment medicine extracts from its practitioners by paying for the preparation that makes the judgment trustworthy. Chapter 8 spent its length on the infrastructure that would build this layer for teaching. Chapter 9 is what happens when the trained teacher uses the judgment in her room.

The third is administrative discretion as schools currently use it — the principal exempts a teacher's class from the rule, or the AUP says "subject to teacher approval." This is discretion in name only. It confers permission without the training that makes the permission meaningful. The educational-purposes exemption written into nearly every 2024–2025 state phone-ban law is this kind of discretion. The legislature handed the call back to the teacher and did not fund the training that would let two teachers in two different schools reach the same defensible answer for the same case. Chapter 3 took the exemption apart in this exact register. Chapter 9 is where the exemption is supposed to operate.

The current phone-ban debate is operating with the first sense (which it cannot escape) and the third sense (which it has badly designed) while pretending the second sense (the one that would work) is either unnecessary or already in place. It is neither. The chapter's affirmative case is that the second sense is what professional discretion in this domain *means*, and that the institutional conditions for it to operate well are exactly the ones Chapter 8 argued for and that almost no US district has built.

## What "Phone Fridays" actually looks like — the five components

The design pattern this chapter is naming has five components. Each is a thing a district can adopt. None is sufficient on its own.

The first is *default-off.* Devices are presumed absent — in lockers, pouches, drawers, or simply turned off in bags. Use is the exception, not the default. This is what the prohibition statutes get right. The attention-protection case for keeping personal smartphones out of the room is settled enough on the in-school evidence (Beland and Murphy; Figlio and Özek; the consistent pattern Chapter 1 walked through) that defaulting to off is the floor. The third model does not abandon the floor. It builds on it.

The second is *designated structured-technology time* — a scheduled block, whether a class period, a day a week, or a thematic unit, when devices are deliberately part of the lesson. The block exists to do two things the bell-to-bell ban cannot. It handles the equity problem — low-income students need supervised access to the tools the wealthy kids have at home, which Chapter 11 takes up directly. And it handles the training problem — *you cannot teach digital literacy without a context in which to teach it.* A school that bans devices for five days a week and then expects students to graduate digitally literate is asking the workplace to do the teaching the school refused to do.

The third is *teacher discretion over the contextual decisions* on the designated block. What is the device being used for in this period, with this group, for this assignment? Which students need the alternative track? When does the lesson stop using the device and go back to the conversation in the room? This is the discretion the educational exemption in every phone-ban law is reaching for and failing to support.

The fourth is *trained discretion.* The teacher exercising the call has been prepared to make it — has working knowledge of what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like, what cognitive offloading looks like, and what kinds of tasks AI handles well versus badly. The AI+1 training infrastructure Chapter 8 argued for is the precondition for this component. A school that has the first three components without the fourth is just running total permissiveness on Fridays, which is the model the 2010–2022 evidence already told us does not work.

The fifth is *institutional backup.* Administrative support for the teacher's enforcement and integration decisions. AUPs written to define the educational exemption with enough specificity to be teachable, not just enough generality to be defensible. Principals who back the teacher when a parent calls to ask why her daughter's class has more device use than her son's. Building-level coherence about what the Friday block is for, developed through collaborative planning rather than left to each teacher to improvise. This is the [Hargreaves "collective autonomy"](https://www.corwin.com/books/collaborative-professionalism-247835) move — teams of teachers in the same building making coherent decisions together, with norms developed inside the profession rather than imposed from outside.

Stillwater Area Public Schools in Minnesota is a useful contrast case. Stillwater's [Policy 524.5](https://www.stillwaterschools.org/our-district/school-board/district-policies/individual-policy/~board/school-board-policies/post/5245-policy-personal-electronic-communication-devices), effective for the 2025–26 school year in response to Minnesota's statewide statute, prohibits students preK–12 from using personal electronic communication devices on school premises from first bell to last, including instructional time, lunch, recess, and school-sponsored events. High school students hand devices to their last-period teacher; teachers maintain cellphone lockboxes. Exceptions are written for medical use, IEP-accommodation use, and "other exceptions at the discretion of the school principal." This is a well-implemented version of the total-prohibition model. The exception language is the place where the model concedes that judgment is unavoidable — but it routes the judgment to the *principal* rather than to the teacher, provides no training to make that judgment systematically, and offers no scheduled time when the educational exemption could be operationalized as a lesson rather than as a one-off favor. Stillwater has components one and five in a serviceable form. Components two, three, and four are absent. Add the designated structured time, the trained teacher discretion, and the AI+1 training infrastructure to Stillwater's foundation and the result is something like the third model. Stillwater is not the failure case. It is the half-built version that shows what needs to be added.

The Yondr-pouch deployments offer a different lesson. The Chatham County Schools pilot in North Carolina, [presented to the governor's advisory council in November 2025](https://www.ednc.org/11-20-2025-ahead-of-cellphone-restrictions-in-nc-schools-governors-advisory-council-hears-advice-from-two-districts/), reported teachers and administrators broadly supportive, parents mixed, students least supportive — only 77 of 649 student responses "strongly agreed" that the pouches had improved classroom engagement — and outcome variance across the six pilot schools that the district attributed to implementation quality rather than to student demographics. The pouch is not the system. The pouch creates the conditions under which a system might operate. Whether it does or not depends on the institutional capacity that follows. Los Angeles Unified spent approximately [$5.2 million on Yondr pouches](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/consumer/schools-spend-millions-yondr-pouches-store-cellphones-rcna230454) and discovered immediate workarounds — students used magnets, brought decoy phones, used the pouches as compliance theater while keeping devices in pockets. The LAUSD case is widely cited in the trade press, *[verify the dollar figure against LAUSD documentation before treating as load-bearing]*, and it is the cautionary case worth keeping. Technology by itself does not solve a behavioral problem. The pouch is the same shape of intervention as the ban — a structural removal that bets on the structure replacing the judgment. When students discover the structure can be defeated, the judgment has not been built underneath it.

The international comparisons help locate the design in the available space. Finland passed legislation in [2025 restricting student smartphone use during the school day](https://www.oph.fi/en/news/2024/finnish-national-agency-education-recommends-limiting-mobile-phone-use-schools-and), with the discretion *built into the statute itself*: students may use phones during lessons for learning purposes or for personal healthcare reasons with permission from their teacher or principal. The teacher has explicit statutory authority to allow educational use. This is closer to the third model than any US state law I have seen. The contextual caveat that has to come with it: Finland's teachers are uniformly master's-degree trained, the [Finnish DigiErko program](https://uefconnect.uef.fi/en/digierko-learning-and-teaching-in-digital-environments/) covered in Chapter 8 provided ongoing in-school coaching for technology integration through 2024, and the discretion Finnish law confers operates on top of an infrastructure US schools mostly lack. Estonia, in the inverse direction, has been running BYOD as national policy since 2014, with digital literacy as a curriculum strand from grade 1 and an [educational technologist in most schools](https://www.educationestonia.org/infrastructure/). The 2025 [AI Leap initiative](https://www.hm.ee/en/news/estonia-announces-groundbreaking-national-initiative-ai-leap-programme-bring-ai-tools-all) — 20,000 high school students and 3,000 teachers receiving AI tools with training on effective use — is the closest national analog to the AI+1 training the book is arguing for. The principle Estonia validates: *the tools and the training arrive together; the tools do not go in without the training.* Neither Finland nor Estonia maps cleanly onto US conditions. Both demonstrate that the design space the chapter is sketching has been entered at national scale by other countries, with measurable success. The pattern is not invented. It is unbuilt here.

## What the trained teacher does inside a room of thirty

The design pattern handles when the devices come out. The pedagogical claim of the book is about what the trained teacher does *during* the structured time that no untrained teacher and no algorithm could do. I want to be specific. Five moves a trained teacher with AI tools can make in a class of roughly thirty that change what is possible.

The first is *real-time peer grouping*. Carol Ann Tomlinson's [three-decade body of work on differentiated instruction](https://files.ascd.org/staticfiles/ascd/pdf/siteASCD/publications/books/differentiated-classroom2nd-sample-chapters.pdf) documents that flexible grouping — groups that re-form every two or three weeks based on formative data, not fixed tracks — is more effective than static grouping for diverse learners. The framework includes like-readiness groups, mixed-readiness groups, interest groups, learning-profile groups, with the teacher deciding which configuration the current lesson needs based on continuous assessment. What AI changes is the time cost of "continuous assessment." A teacher who used to look at exit-ticket responses overnight and regroup students for the next day can now look at student work in progress — AI parsing the pattern of errors across thirty submissions — and regroup the class for the next ten minutes of the same lesson. The granularity of differentiation moves from week-to-week to within-lesson. The Tomlinson literature is correlational and quasi-experimental; the AI-enabled real-time version has not yet been tested in a randomized trial. The chapter's claim is that *AI compresses the time cost of differentiation, which extends a framework whose effectiveness is already established* — not that we have a causally identified effect for the AI-enabled version specifically.

The second is *differentiated materials produced in minutes rather than hours.* The Education Endowment Foundation's [ChatGPT teacher lesson-preparation RCT](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/) — 259 teachers across 68 secondary schools, covered in detail in Chapter 6 — found a 31 percent reduction in lesson-preparation time with output quality maintained by blind review. The [Gallup-Walton Family Foundation survey](https://news.gallup.com/poll/691887/teachers-using-back-thousands-time.aspx) of 2,232 US public school teachers in June 2025 found weekly AI users reclaiming 5.9 hours per week, with 60 percent reporting more time for improving teaching, 60 percent reporting more time for feedback, and 57 percent reporting better insights about student learning data. The dividend is not abstract. The differentiation work Tomlinson's framework prescribes but most teachers cannot find time to do is suddenly tractable. The teacher who would have spent two hours producing three reading-level versions of Tuesday's primary source spends twelve minutes. She does it for every lesson rather than for the lessons she had the weekend to prepare. The honest limit, which Chapter 6 names: the time saved has to be reinvested in something. The Gallup self-report says teachers report reinvesting it in feedback and individualization. No direct causal study yet shows that the reinvested time produces measurably better student outcomes at scale. The component is the strongest empirical case the chapter can make for the *prep-time dividend*; the case for what the dividend buys downstream is plausible and not yet demonstrated.

The third is the load-bearing finding for this chapter, and I want to give it its own paragraph. The [Tutor CoPilot study (Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, Demszky 2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017) is the strongest direct evidence in the literature that AI-augmented teaching changes what humans do in the moment of teaching. Stanford researchers gave one-on-one tutors an AI assistant during real tutoring sessions. The headline result, covered in Chapter 5: a four-percentage-point mastery improvement overall, nine percentage points for students of lower-rated tutors. The mechanism finding the chapter needs to re-pull here is the one that does not fit on a press release. Analysis of more than 350,000 tutor-student message exchanges showed that Tutor CoPilot *increased the use of probing questions and reduced generic praise.* Tutors with the AI in their corner asked more cognitively demanding questions. They gave fewer direct answers. The AI was not generating the question and pushing it to the student. The AI was changing what the *human* asked. The student's experience was one of better human attention. The mechanism by which the better attention happened was AI-augmented pedagogical practice in the moment. This is the single empirical finding in the current literature that most directly supports the chapter's argument: when a trained human has AI in her corner, the human does more of what good teaching looks like, not less. The honest limit: this is one-on-one chat-based tutoring, not classroom teaching. The mechanism is plausible at the classroom scale and the studies are in progress. The chapter is making the analogical extension because the mechanism is the same. It is not yet making a causally identified claim about classrooms.

The fourth is *self-regulation scaffolding calibrated to individual student need.* The [M-Powering Teachers RCT (Demszky et al. 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0360131524001970), published in *Computers & Education*, is the closest brick-and-mortar evidence the literature has. 224 mathematics and science teachers in Utah K-12 classrooms; automated email feedback on teacher questioning patterns, particularly use of "focusing questions" — questions that press for student reasoning rather than recall. Teachers opened feedback emails 53 to 65 percent of the time. Result: 20 percent increase in use of focusing questions (p < 0.01), with treatment teachers by week five asking roughly 10 additional focusing questions per hour — a 59 percent increase over control. If AI feedback on the teacher's own questioning practice measurably moves that practice in a K-12 classroom, AI feedback on the student's self-regulation could plausibly move that as well. The chapter is making the analogical extension. The direct evidence for student-side self-regulation effects is the [PERTS Elevate program](https://www.perts.net/elevate) at Stanford, which during 2023–24 was implemented by 2,300 educators across 63,000 students in 271 middle and high schools across 94 districts. Reported outcomes: more than 75 percent of teachers improved classroom learning conditions across multiple cycles; students were 86 percent more likely to report higher sense of belonging when conditions improved; in a 2022 cohort, students were twice as likely to earn a B or better in math when they rated learning conditions most positively. Elevate is not causally identified at the same level as Tutor CoPilot, but it documents a large-scale implementation in which structured feedback loops measurably moved classroom conditions.

The fifth is the cognitive division of labor. *AI handles Tier 1 cognitive work; the teacher handles Tier 4 cognitive work.* This is the principle that needs the most careful specification because it is also the one that risks sounding condescending if specified badly. The taxonomy is the [Irreducibly Human framework](https://www.irreducibly.xyz/) — *my own contribution, building on the lineage of [Bloom's revised taxonomy](https://files.eric.ed.gov/), [Webb's Depth of Knowledge](https://oklahoma.gov/content/dam/ok/en/osde/documents/services/assessments/assessment-guidance/Unwrapping-DOK.pdf), and [Hess's Cognitive Rigor Matrix](https://www.karin-hess.com/cognitive-rigor-and-dok)*, *[verify the Tier 1/Tier 4 framing against the current irreducibly.xyz materials]*, and updated for the question of what generative AI does well versus what it cannot do. Tier 1 cognitive work is what AI handles well: pattern recognition, recall, factual content delivery, first-pass feedback on a routine problem set, summarization of a known source, translation, generating practice items at a specified difficulty. The work is structurally cognitive — it requires thinking — but it requires the kind of thinking machines do at scale. Tier 4 is the supervisory work AI does not do: plausibility auditing (does the AI's output actually answer the question correctly, in the context this student is working in?), interpretive judgment (what does this student's confused answer reveal about what she is actually thinking?), problem formulation (what is the right question to ask this student right now?), tool orchestration (which AI tool should be running for which task?), relational teaching (the hour-by-hour adjustment that comes from knowing this specific student). The Tier 4 capacities are the *educational exemption* every phone-ban law has been reaching for and has not been able to specify. They are the work the trained teacher does. They are not work AI does. A school that wants the educational exemption to mean something operational has to invest in the human capacity that can do Tier 4 work; otherwise the exemption is just permission to use AI for Tier 1 work, which is mostly what the bad implementations are already doing.

The within-30 frame is not arbitrary. It comes from the [Hattie effect-size literature](https://visible-learning.org/) that Chapter 4 walked through: the highest-d interventions are relational (teacher-student relationships d = 0.72, feedback d = 0.73, collective efficacy d = 1.57) and these cannot scale past roughly 25 to 30 students per teacher regardless of how good the AI tooling becomes. AI does not let one teacher serve five hundred. It lets one teacher do more for the thirty she already has. The within-30 frame is the chapter's protection against the EdTech industry's preferred reading of AI-in-education, which is that AI scales good teaching. The book's reading is that AI deepens good teaching within the optimal unit.

## The counter-arguments, taken seriously

Two objections to the third model are serious enough to engage on their own terms.

*Trained-teacher discretion just gives some kids better classrooms than others, correlated with which teacher they got — and the correlation tends to track student demographics.* This is the strongest critique of professional discretion in teaching, and it is well-documented. [Ian Taylor's 2007 review of teacher discretion in the post-NCLB era](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1741143207081063) argued that the high regulation and accountability infrastructure of the 1990s and 2000s eroded teacher discretion partly *as a response to* documented discretion failures — different educational experiences for similar students in the same building, often correlated with race and income in unjust ways. The chapter cannot pretend professional discretion has a clean record. The answer is the one [Susan Moore Johnson's *Where Teachers Thrive*](https://hep.gse.harvard.edu/9781682533581/where-teachers-thrive/) — 257 interviews across 14 high-poverty Massachusetts schools — established directly: *discretion quality is a school-level variable, not an individual-level variable.* A teacher in a well-organized school exercises better discretion than the same teacher in a poorly-organized school. The variance the equity objection rightly fears is the variance produced by *unsupported* discretion. The third model is the affirmative case for *supported* discretion — trained teachers, collaborative norms within buildings, principals backing the call, AUPs specific enough to anchor consistency across rooms. Chapter 11 picks up the equity thread directly. Chapter 9 has to make the structural argument: the alternative to bad discretion is not no discretion. The alternative to bad discretion is trained, supported, collectively accountable discretion. The 2007 evidence is evidence against the third sense of discretion (administrative permission without training). It is not evidence against the second.

*Kids will abuse the educational exemption.* They will try. This is also correct, and it is also not the problem the chapter is solving. Total prohibition makes the failure-to-abuse outcome certain and the failure-to-learn-with-the-tool outcome also certain. The third model accepts that some students will attempt to convert Friday device time into TikTok time and that the trained teacher will notice and redirect — because she has been trained to know what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what cognitive offloading looks like, and because she has the institutional backup to enforce the redirect. The abuse rate under the third model is greater than zero. The abuse rate under the second model (total permissiveness) is much greater than zero. The abuse rate under the first model (total prohibition) is zero by construction; the not-learning rate is one hundred percent for the work the policy disallows. Pick which failure mode you want to optimize against. The third model accepts a small rate of in-the-moment abuse in exchange for a structured learning opportunity for the thirty students who use the time as intended. The first model accepts zero abuse in exchange for zero structured learning. Both trade-offs are defensible. The chapter is arguing the third trade is the better one when the teacher in the room has been trained to make the calls the trade requires.

## What this means Monday morning

I want to address the rest of this section to the principal or district leader reader directly, the same way Chapter 8 did. The argument here is structural and national. The decisions that close the gap are local and specific. Five things you can do this week.

First, *audit your current policy against the five components.* Pull whatever your district's current phone or device policy is. Mark each component: do you have a clear default-off rule (component one)? Do you have any scheduled structured technology time when devices come out by design rather than by exception (component two)? Does the AUP language give teachers contextual authority to make the call inside the structured time, or does it route every decision to the principal (component three)? Have your teachers received any training on what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what cognitive substitution looks like (component four)? Does your AUP define the educational exemption with enough specificity that two teachers in two different schools would reach the same defensible answer for the same case (component five)? Most districts have component one in serviceable form and component three in the weak administrative-permission version. Components two, four, and five are mostly absent. The audit is a half-day exercise. The map it produces tells you where to spend the next year.

Second, *schedule the structured-technology time and protect it on the calendar.* Pick a frequency and a duration that fits your building — one period per week per class, one full day per cycle, one thematic unit per quarter — and put it on the master schedule. The point is not the specific cadence. The point is that it is *scheduled*, which means it cannot be quietly displaced by the next pep rally, fire drill, or testing window. The teachers in the building need to know the block is real so they can plan into it. The students need to know the block is real so they can stop angling for unscheduled exemptions. The structured time is the surface where the educational exemption operates as a lesson rather than as a favor. Without the schedule, the exemption is back to being administrative-permission discretion.

Third, *invest the AI dividend in component four, not in more devices.* The Gallup-Walton number is 5.9 hours per week reclaimed by teachers using AI weekly. If your teachers are not yet at that dividend, the limiting factor is almost always training and tool fluency, not access. Use Title II-A funds — the spending category Chapter 8 walked through — to send your teachers through sustained AI-pedagogy coaching cycles rather than through single-vendor August demos. The Kraft, Blazar, Hogan meta-analysis is the evidence base. The Finnish *tutoropettaja* model is the structural analog. Identify the two or three teachers in your building who are already the people other teachers come to with AI questions and pay them for the role. Cover one section of their teaching load with district funds. Assign the time to coaching colleagues. One tutor teacher per school. You will know within a semester whether the coaching cycles are moving practice. The infrastructure that makes the third model work is the infrastructure Chapter 8 argued for; the infrastructure Chapter 8 argued for is the infrastructure you can start building this fiscal year with money already in the budget.

Fourth, *write the educational-purposes definition with your teachers, not for them.* Convene a working group that includes your tutor teachers, your subject-area leads, a special-education representative, and at least one teacher who is skeptical of AI in classrooms. Draft a district-level operational definition of *educational purposes* specific enough that two teachers at different schools in your district could reach the same defensible answer for the same case. Pilot it for one semester. Revise it. The Hargreaves "collective autonomy" frame is the right one: the norms developed inside the profession, not imposed from outside, are the ones the building will actually enforce. The exemption written by lawyers in the state capital is not teachable. The exemption written by the teachers who will operate it is.

Fifth, *back the call when a parent calls.* The third model only works if the principal defends the teacher's contextual judgment when the parent on the phone wants to know why her daughter's class had device time and her son's did not. The honest answer — *because Ms. Velasquez's seventh-grade history lesson on Reconstruction primary sources used AI-differentiated reading levels and Mr. Liang's seventh-grade English class on the same day was running a Socratic seminar that doesn't use devices* — has to be one the principal will say out loud and the district will defend in writing. The Stillwater policy routes the discretion to the principal. The third model routes it to the teacher and asks the principal to back the teacher's call. The difference is whose professional judgment is treated as the load-bearing one. The third model treats the teacher's as the load-bearing one. The principal's job is to make the teacher's call defensible to the building, the parents, and the district. That is a different job than the one the current AUPs assign. It is the job the third model requires.

None of these requires federal action. None requires state legislation. None requires more money than you already have allocated under categories you are already spending it on. The trade — pouches for coaching, August demos for sustained coaching cycles, administrative-permission discretion for trained-teacher discretion — is a trade your fiscal year can make. The cohort of buildings that have done this trade does not yet exist at scale. It will be built by district leaders making the trade, school by school, until the cohort exists.

## The chapter's turn

The legislature wrote a rule that prohibits the device and an exception that requires the call. The call cannot be made well by a teacher who has not been trained to make it. The training infrastructure is what Chapter 8 was about. The room where the training pays off is what this chapter is about. The third model is the structural design that lets the training operate as a lesson rather than as an improvisation.

The English teacher running the Socratic seminar on Friday morning is not in the room because the law told her to be. She is in the room because the work she does there — the call about whether this lesson needs devices, the call about whether *that* student is ready for the AI-differentiated version of the reading, the call about which configuration of the class will move the math forward in the next ten minutes — is the work no statute and no algorithm can do. The room she runs is the unit the book has been arguing for since Chapter 1. The infrastructure that makes her room possible is the infrastructure the country has not yet built. The chapter is for the principal who is deciding this week whether to build it in one building.

The legislator at the desk wrote the rule. The algorithm in the cloud cannot make the call. The trained teacher in the room is the only one of the three who can.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. There is no direct randomized evaluation of the third model as an integrated design — default-off plus structured time plus trained teacher discretion plus institutional support — compared either to total prohibition or to total permissiveness. The components have evidence (coaching effects, Tutor CoPilot, Tomlinson, M-Powering, Elevate, the Finnish and Estonian national experiments at the principle level). The assembled design does not yet. The chapter is arguing for a structure that, as a structure, has not been put through the test it would need to be put through to settle the case. That is the right move when the policy debate is bad enough that the alternative needs naming. It is also the move that exposes the argument to being wrong about implementation details we cannot yet test.

The within-30 design principles have asymmetric evidence. Real-time peer grouping rests on the Tomlinson framework, which is correlational and quasi-experimental and which has not been tested in an AI-enabled version under controlled conditions. Differentiated materials in minutes rests on the EEF prep-time finding and on first-principles inference about what the saved time enables. Formative questions specific to student work rests on the Tutor CoPilot result, which is strong for one-on-one chat-based tutoring and an extension to classrooms. Self-regulation scaffolding rests on M-Powering's teacher-behavior result and the PERTS large-scale implementation evidence. The Tier 1/Tier 4 work allocation is my own framework, building on Bloom/Webb/Hess but not itself established curriculum scholarship. Different components have different evidentiary weights. The chapter has tried to mark them honestly. *[verify: full Irreducibly Human seven-tier mapping and the Tier 1/Tier 4 specifics with the current irreducibly.xyz materials before press.]*

And the named-programs question is the chapter's biggest honesty test. *No school is currently running a branded program called "Phone Fridays."* The case studies that exist (Stillwater, Chatham, San Mateo, LAUSD, Finland 2025, Estonia AI Leap) are mostly either total-prohibition models with Yondr pouches or national-level integration experiments without classroom-level design-pattern documentation. The chapter has used these cases as analogues rather than as proofs of the model it is naming. A school somewhere may already be running this design without calling it what the chapter calls it. The pattern this chapter has named is real in fragmentary practice. The named, branded, evaluated version of it is not yet documented in the literature this chapter could cite.

## Still puzzling

Why has no US district, in the 2024–2026 wave of phone-ban policy adoption, written a structured-technology-time block into its policy alongside the bell-to-bell prohibition? The components are not technically difficult. The schedule changes are within the existing master-schedule design space. The institutional capacity to enforce the structured time is the same capacity required to enforce the prohibition. The reason the third model has not been adopted is not a resource constraint or an evidentiary constraint. It is a political-economy constraint — districts have been moving toward the policy shape that is easiest to defend to the loudest constituents, and the easiest-to-defend shape is total prohibition. The third model requires a defense of teacher judgment, which is a defense the current public conversation has not been making well. Whether the trade is fixable depends on whether the constituencies that benefit from the third model — students, teachers, district leaders — can be assembled into a coalition the way the constituencies that built the 2025 ban wave were assembled. As of the date of this chapter, that coalition does not exist. Whether it can be built is the question the next two chapters take up.

## What would change my mind

A well-designed randomized trial showing that a school implementing the third model — default-off plus structured time plus trained teacher discretion plus institutional support — does *not* outperform a school implementing total prohibition on either student outcomes, teacher retention, or equity measures. Or evidence that trained-discretion implementations track teacher bias and produce worse equity outcomes than the rule-based prohibitions they replace. Either finding would substantially revise the chapter's argument. As of this writing, neither study exists in the literature. The recommendation rests on the component evidence and on the structural argument that the alternative to bad discretion is trained discretion, not no discretion.
