# Chapter 7: The Cognitive Offloading Problem

*What happens when students get the tool without the teacher — and why the results should alarm everyone building EdTech*

---

Students using unguided AI improved 48 percent during practice. Then scored 17 percent below students who had never used AI when tested without it. They thought they were learning more. They were learning less.

That is the finding from [Bastani et al., published in *PNAS* on 25 June 2025](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) — a pre-registered field experiment at a single large high school in Turkey with approximately 1,000 students across grades 9–11, randomized at the class-section level into three arms: a control group with no AI, a group given access to a stock GPT-4 chatbot during practice (call it GPT Base), and a group given access to GPT-4 with a custom system prompt designed to scaffold rather than answer (call it GPT Tutor). Each student went through four 90-minute sessions on algebra and trigonometry. Each session ran the same way: about thirty minutes of teacher review, about thirty minutes of assisted practice with the tool the treatment arm assigned, and about thirty minutes of unassisted closed-book exam on the material that had just been practiced. The closed-book exam was the same for every condition. Nobody had AI access during it.

The headline numbers Bastani and colleagues report come in pairs that need to be held together. During the assisted practice period, the GPT Base group scored about 48 percent higher than control on the practice problems. The GPT Tutor group scored about 127 percent higher. Both numbers are large. The vendor would print them on a slide. On the closed-book exam, with the AI gone, the GPT Base group scored about 17 percent *lower* than control — statistically significant, robust to specification. The GPT Tutor group scored statistically indistinguishably from control. The scaffolded prompt neutralized the harm. It did not, in this study, produce a positive learning effect over teacher-led practice without AI. It also did not damage learning the way the unscaffolded condition did.

The Wharton press summary characterized it carefully: ["without guardrails, generative AI can harm education"](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/without-guardrails-generative-ai-can-harm-education/). The paper's own subtitle is sharper: *"Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning."* In August 2025, *PNAS* published [a correction](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12403119/) to the paper. The correction is non-substantive — it fixes a single author affiliation. None of the numbers change. The +48 percent practice gain, the +127 percent practice gain with scaffolding, and the −17 percent exam decrement stand exactly as reported.

I want to pause here, because the design and the result are doing too much work for the chapter to move on without sitting with what they show. This is not a screen-time correlation. It is not a survey of student preferences. It is not an aggregate of confounded classroom evaluations. It is a randomized trial in which the same school in the same week, with the same teachers running the same lessons, produced a 48 percent *gain* during practice and a 17 percent *loss* on the test of what was practiced — for the same students. The students experienced both as success. Asked, before the exam, to predict how they would do, they predicted higher than control. Then they tested lower than control. The vendor sells the practice number. The classroom inherits the exam.

This chapter is about why. The mechanism is not mysterious. It is built into the architecture of how the brain converts experience into durable memory, and the architecture has been mapped in detail across four decades of cognitive science. Unguided AI bypasses the architecture. The student produces correct outputs without the underlying processes that would let her produce correct outputs when the tool is gone. She feels like she is learning. She is not learning. And the platform — by mechanism, not by accident — cannot tell the difference. Neither can she. Only the trained adult in the room can.

## The terms that have to come apart first

The chapter cannot make its argument until five words get pulled apart from each other, because EdTech marketing has spent the last three years treating them as if they were synonyms and the cognitive-science literature has spent the last forty years showing they are not.

**Cognitive offloading.** The phrase comes from [Risko and Gilbert's 2016 review in *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27542527/), and their definition is narrower than the everyday usage suggests: *the use of physical action to alter the information-processing requirements of a task so as to reduce cognitive demand.* Tilting your head to read a rotated document is cognitive offloading. Writing down a phone number rather than memorizing it is cognitive offloading. Using a calculator for long division is cognitive offloading. So is asking Claude to debug your code. The first thing to say plainly is that **cognitive offloading is not, in itself, learning-destructive.** It is the default mode of human–tool cooperation. The slide rule did not destroy the chemist; the Excel pivot table did not destroy the accountant. Offloading is what tools are for.

The Bastani finding is more specific. *When the cognitive operation being offloaded is the operation the lesson is trying to teach, the offloading prevents the learning event.* A student who offloads long division to a calculator on an assignment whose objective is "model an inventory problem" still learns to model an inventory problem. A student who offloads long division to a calculator on an assignment whose objective is "build division fluency" learns nothing the assignment was supposed to teach her. Same offloading. Different pedagogical position. Different learning outcome. "Cognitive offloading" is not a synonym for "using AI." It is a specific failure mode within a specific design — the one where the AI absorbs the cognition the curriculum was trying to install. Risko and Gilbert anticipated this directly: the propensity to offload is governed by metacognitive judgments about the difficulty of the task and the confidence in the tool, and those judgments, they noted, can be erroneous.

**Fluency.** Two meanings, used interchangeably in EdTech copy, distinguished sharply in the cognitive-science literature. *Processing fluency* is the subjective ease with which information is encountered — how smoothly a sentence reads, how immediately an answer comes to mind, how confidently an explanation feels right. AI output is overwhelmingly fluent in this sense. *Memory fluency* is the speed and reliability with which a piece of knowledge can be retrieved from long-term memory in the absence of the original stimulus. A student who can solve a chain-rule problem in 30 seconds without notes has high memory fluency for the chain rule. A student who cannot, doesn't.

The result that matters for this chapter is that **processing fluency systematically inflates judgments of memory fluency.** [Carpenter, Wilford, Kornell, and Mullaney (2013)](https://pure.royalholloway.ac.uk/ws/portalfiles/portal/25808257/Carpenter_et_al_JEP_A_Pure.pdf) showed that students predicted higher exam scores after watching a fluent lecturer than after watching the same content from a disfluent lecturer — and then performed identically on the actual exam. [Karpicke and Blunt (2011)](https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2011/2011_Karpicke_Blunt_Science.pdf), in *Science*, found that students rated elaborative concept-mapping as roughly equivalent to retrieval practice for their own learning, while retrieval practice actually outperformed concept-mapping by *d* = 1.50. The students' fluency-based judgment was almost perfectly inverse to their actual learning gain. Learners use fluency cues to estimate "have I learned this," and the fluency cues lie. ChatGPT delivers fluency at industrial scale. This is what the Bastani GPT Base students were experiencing.

**Schema.** A *schema* is an organized mental structure that lets a learner recognize a class of problems, predict what kind of operation will solve them, and execute the operation without reconstructing it from first principles each time. The trained mathematician's schema for "this is a related-rates problem" lets her see in five seconds what the novice will spend an hour discovering. Schemas are what working memory loads when a situation demands them; they are what gets built when learning is durable; they are what gets *not built* when offloading lets a student produce correct outputs without the underlying recognition. [Sweller's cognitive load theory (1988)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1207/s15516709cog1202_4) formalized the construction problem: working memory is severely capacity-limited, long-term memory is functionally unlimited, and learning is the migration of effortfully-handled patterns from working memory into long-term schemas that no longer load working memory at all. Sweller and Cooper's worked-example effect (1985) is the finding that students who study fully worked examples learn faster than students who attempt problem-solving from scratch — because the worked example reduces extraneous working-memory load and frees capacity for schema construction. The Bastani GPT Base condition *prevents* schema construction because the student is not studying a solution path at all; she is submitting prompts to a tool that returns finished outputs without the structural exposure that worked examples provide. A worked example is a teacher's product. An AI answer is a student's product produced by an AI. Same surface form. Opposite pedagogical position.

**Storage strength and retrieval strength.** This is Robert and Elizabeth Bjork's central distinction, laid out in their [1992 "New Theory of Disuse"](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/RBjork_EBjork_1992.pdf), and it is the conceptual instrument the chapter most needs. Every memory item carries two strengths, measurable independently. *Storage strength* is how thoroughly the item has been learned into long-term memory — a one-way ratchet that accumulates with use and never decreases. *Retrieval strength* is how accessible the item is to working memory at this moment — bidirectional, sensitive to recency and context. A song you learned at twelve and have not sung in thirty years has high storage and low retrieval; play the first three notes and the lyrics come back. A new password you set this morning and typed four times today has low storage and high retrieval; wait six months without typing it and it is gone.

The Bjorks' key claim is **the inverse coupling between current retrieval ease and the rate at which storage strength is being built.** When retrieval is easy — when the material is right in front of you, when the AI just told you, when you just re-read the page — the retrieval event does little to build storage. When retrieval is *effortful* and *successful*, it builds storage substantially. This is the principle that makes spaced practice, interleaving, and the testing effect work. It explains why re-reading feels productive and is not. It explains in one sentence why the GPT Base condition damages learning: **the AI maximizes retrieval ease, which minimizes the storage-strength gain per practice problem.** The Bjorks named the family of conditions that exploit this inverse coupling — spacing, interleaving, generation, retrieval — the *[desirable difficulties](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/04/EBjork_RBjork_2011.pdf)*. A difficulty the learner cannot overcome is not desirable; it is just frustration. The teacher's judgment is the variable that calibrates this. The AI by itself cannot.

**Learning.** The last specification, and the most important. What we colloquially call "learning" is at least four distinct phenomena, and AI affects each differently. *Performance* is producing a correct output right now with whatever tools are available; AI raises this dramatically. *Retention* is storage strength that persists across a delay; AI without scaffolding reduces it, per Bastani; AI with scaffolding leaves it roughly unchanged. *Transfer* is the ability to deploy a learned schema on a novel problem in a new context — the hardest of the four to measure, and the one Bastani's closed-book exam on practiced material does not test. Most vendor claims measure performance, implicitly claim the rest, and never test them. Bastani's contribution is to measure retention cleanly and to report that the deployment that boosts performance most is the deployment that damages retention most.

## What the Bastani study actually controls for, and what it doesn't

The chapter has to print the limits next to the headline numbers, because the paper is the chapter's most-cited source.

It shows: unguided generative-AI access, deployed without instructional scaffolding, during four 90-minute high-school math practice sessions in one Turkish school in 2023–24, with approximately 1,000 students randomized at the section level, produced a statistically significant negative effect on closed-book exam performance. It also shows that the same access with a system-prompt scaffold did not produce the negative effect. The pre-registered moderator analysis explicitly examined whether the harm differed across student subgroups by ability, resources, or effort — and the paper reports *"limited to no statistically significant support for heterogeneous treatment effects with either treatment, particularly with respect to unassisted exam performance."* The harm was distributed roughly across the sample. A subtler finding worth holding alongside this: both AI conditions produced their largest *practice-session* benefits for the weakest students, narrowing the in-class skill gap during AI-assisted practice. That narrowing did not persist on the exam. When AI was removed, the gap returned to roughly its baseline shape. The pedagogical reading is brutal: unguided AI offers the appearance of leveling the floor and the substance of removing the lesson — most visibly for the students whose progress is most often measured by what happens during the session.

It does not show that AI is harmful in general, in non-math subjects, or in age groups other than high school. It does not show that the harm persists after teacher-supervised remediation. It does not show that the GPT Tutor scaffold is sufficient in the absence of a live teacher — the Tutor was software deployed inside a class with a teacher running the session, so the comparison is "AI plus teacher with scaffolded prompt" versus "AI plus teacher without scaffolded prompt," not "AI tutor alone versus teacher alone." And it does not show that the design generalizes to settings where students self-select AI use.

A single large pre-registered RCT is the strongest empirical evidence the AI-in-education literature has on the unguided-access question. The chapter treats it as the strongest available finding, not the final word. It leans on the paper anyway for two reasons. First, the design controls for the confound that has plagued every prior "AI in classrooms" study: selection. Classes were randomized; baseline characteristics were balanced. The −17 percent can be attributed to the treatment, not to whichever students happened to opt into AI use. Second, the design separates the two things vendor marketing routinely conflates: performance with the tool, and learning that survives the tool's absence. Bastani reports that in the unscaffolded condition, *the larger the in-tool gain, the worse the without-tool outcome.* That dissociation is the chapter's argument in one experimental result.

## Why offloading damages learning: three biological events

Here is the deep-dive the chapter owes the reader, because the Bastani finding is not "AI is bad for learning" any more than the testing-effect literature is "studying is bad for learning." It is more specific than that. Learning, at the level of the brain, is a particular sequence of biological events, and unguided AI bypasses the sequence at three distinct points. I am going to walk through the three points and then show how the AI removes each one.

**Step one: prediction error.** The brain is, by the current consensus in computational neuroscience, a prediction machine. [Wolfram Schultz's foundational work on dopamine neurons in the primate midbrain](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9054347/) — the 1997 *Science* paper with Peter Dayan and P. Read Montague that formalized it — showed that these neurons fire not in response to reward per se but in response to *reward prediction error*: the gap between what the animal expected and what arrived. Get a reward you predicted with certainty, no firing. Get a reward you didn't expect, strong positive firing. Expect a reward that doesn't arrive, firing drops below baseline. Rescorla and Wagner had formalized the same logic behaviorally in 1972; Sutton and Barto formalized it computationally as temporal-difference learning, which is the algorithm underneath modern reinforcement learning; [Glimcher's 2011 review](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1014269108) consolidated the picture. The dopamine signal is the brain's *learning* signal. It tells the cortex and basal ganglia which synapses to strengthen based on how surprising the outcome was.

The pedagogical reading lands fast. A student who attempts a problem and gets it wrong generates a prediction error. The wrongness is the signal. The brain updates exactly because the prediction failed. A student who never makes a prediction in the first place — because the AI handed her the answer before she had a chance to commit to one — generates nothing. The dopamine system has nothing to fire on. The synapses that would have been strengthened are not strengthened. The student feels she understood. She did not learn. This is not a metaphor. It is the actual biological event the practice session was supposed to produce, and the AI made it not happen.

**Step two: effortful retrieval.** Granting that the brain learns from prediction error, the next question is what makes a memory *durable* once it has been encoded. The answer the cognitive-science literature has converged on across forty years is *successful effortful retrieval.* In the canonical [Roediger and Karpicke (2006)](https://learninglab.psych.purdue.edu/downloads/2006/2006_Roediger_Karpicke_PsychSci.pdf) *Psychological Science* paper, students studied prose passages, then either restudied them or took a free-recall test on them. Immediate measurement: restudy slightly ahead. Delayed measurement at one week: the testing condition substantially ahead. Students predicted restudy would win; retrieval did. The classroom replication came in 2011 — [Roediger, Agarwal, McDaniel, and McDermott](https://pdf.retrievalpractice.org/guide/Roediger_Agarwal_etal_2011_JEPA.pdf) brought the testing effect into sixth-grade social studies, where quizzed material was retained substantially better than non-quizzed material on the chapter exams and on the end-of-semester exam.

The biological mechanism is now well-characterized at the synaptic level. Successful retrieval reactivates the relevant circuit, drives calcium influx through NMDA receptors at the activated synapses, and pushes the protein-kinase-A / CREB cascade past the threshold into the structural-growth regime. A retrieval that requires effort and ends in success is functionally a training trial for the underlying synapses. A retrieval that is bypassed by reading the answer is functionally nothing. This is why re-reading feels productive and is not, why highlighting feels productive and is not, why watching a YouTube explanation feels productive and produces almost no measurable transfer to exam performance. None of these strategies engage the effortful-retrieval sequence that drives synaptic consolidation. They feel productive because they are *fluent.* They are not productive because they are not *effortful.* ChatGPT is the most fluent and least effortful retrieval substitute ever invented. That is not a value judgment. It is the design specification.

**Step three: schema construction.** Granting prediction error and effortful retrieval, the third event is schema construction — the migration of effortfully-handled patterns from working memory into long-term cortical representations that no longer load working memory. *Expertise is exactly the accumulation of schemas in long-term memory that let working memory handle complex situations by recognizing them as instances of known patterns.* The chess master is not faster than the novice; she is recognizing the board as an instance of a 50,000-position library her opponent has not built. The trained mathematician sees "related-rates problem" the way the chess master sees a Sicilian Defense.

The hippocampus-neocortex consolidation literature — McClelland, McNaughton, and O'Reilly's 1995 complementary-learning-systems model, refined by [Tse et al. in 2007](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41539-020-0064-y) — shows that schema construction happens through the slow integration of hippocampally-stored episodes into stable neocortical representations, and that *effortful* integrative engagement produces schema-ready representations where passive consumption does not. The Bastani GPT Base student is generating output — correct, fluent, satisfying — without engaging in the effortful integrative encoding schema construction requires. Her hippocampus indexes the episode of "I worked on a math problem today." Nothing migrates to cortex. The exam, two days later, finds nothing to retrieve.

The three events stack. Prediction error provides the learning signal. Effortful retrieval triggers the consolidation cascade. Schema construction is the durable end state. Unguided AI bypasses step one (no error, because the predicted answer matches the actual answer), bypasses step two (no retrieval, because the answer is supplied), and prevents step three (no schema construction, because there is no effortful encoding to consolidate). That is not "AI is bad for learning." That is *this specific deployment of AI bypasses the three biological events the lesson was designed to produce.* The teacher's job in the AI-augmented classroom is to redesign the lesson so the events still happen — withholding the AI for the retrieval phase, using the AI to generate a problem the student has to work, having the AI offer a wrong answer the student has to debug (prediction error reinstalled), or using the AI to handle parts where construction has already happened and the offload is genuinely helpful. That is the trained teacher's daily craft. The platform cannot do it alone. The student cannot do it alone.

## The fluency trap: why the student cannot tell

If unguided AI simply failed to improve learning, the chapter would be a "no harm done" story. The Bastani −17 percent says active harm. The mechanism that makes active harm possible is that *the student cannot tell.* This is what cognitive science calls the metacognitive illusion of competence. [Koriat and Bjork (2005)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15910125/), and Bjork, Dunlosky, and Kornell in their 2013 [*Annual Review of Psychology*](https://sanlab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/07/RBjork_Dunlosky_Kornell_2012.pdf) review, showed that learners systematically over-predict their own retention when learning has been fluent and under-predict it when learning has been effortful. The cues the metacognitive system uses to estimate "have I learned this" are largely fluency cues — precisely the cues that get inflated by AI-assisted practice and deflated by effortful retrieval.

A student whose metacognition is calibrated by fluency thinks she is learning when she is processing smoothly and thinks she is failing when she is struggling. *Effective learning conditions feel worse, in the moment, than ineffective ones.* This is the Bjorks' single most-quoted teaching point, and the most counterintuitive one for students who have spent their educational lives optimizing for the appearance of competence rather than its construction. Unguided AI maximizes the appearance and minimizes the construction. It is the metacognitive-illusion machine of the century.

The pattern extends past high school. [Lee and colleagues at Microsoft Research, in a 2025 CHI paper](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/the-impact-of-generative-ai-on-critical-thinking-self-reported-reductions-in-cognitive-effort-and-confidence-effects-from-a-survey-of-knowledge-workers/), surveyed 319 knowledge workers about their experience using generative AI and found that higher confidence in the AI was associated with less critical thinking; higher self-confidence in one's own abilities was associated with more critical thinking, at a perceived higher cognitive cost. The Microsoft study is self-report, not behavioral measurement, so its evidence weight is smaller than Bastani's, but the directional consistency is striking. [Kosmyna and colleagues at the MIT Media Lab](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08872) used EEG to measure neural activity in 54 participants writing essays brain-only, search-assisted, and LLM-assisted. LLM users showed reduced connectivity in EEG networks relative to brain-only writers and substantially worse recall of what they had just written when asked to rewrite their own essays without the tool. The authors framed the finding as "cognitive debt." [verify: preprint as of May 2026; EEG connectivity measures are notoriously sensitive to analysis-pipeline choices.] The chapter reports the MIT result because it is corroborative. It does not lean on it. The Bastani RCT does not need it. And survey research summarized by [the Hechinger Report](https://hechingerreport.org/proof-points-offload-critical-thinking-ai/) under the headline "University students offload critical thinking, other hard work to AI" found students self-reporting that they are using AI to "avoid the hard parts," and experiencing that bargain as a net win because the cognitive load went down and the outputs went up. The metacognitive literature predicts exactly this experience.

## Khanmigo, Kestin, and what the counter-evidence actually says

There is real counter-evidence to the chapter's central claim, and the chapter has not earned its closing move until it engages with the strongest of it. Three things to put on the table.

The first is **Khanmigo.** Khan Academy, in partnership with WestEd and other evaluators, has been running a sustained measurement program on Khanmigo, its AI tutor built on GPT. The most-cited result is a WestEd RCT across 47 schools reporting a statistically significant improvement in algebra-readiness scores after one semester of use — circulated in K–12 trade press as approximately 0.15 SD. [verify: the underlying WestEd report citation, sample composition, and statistical-significance threshold should be checked against the published evaluation before this figure is treated as load-bearing; Khan's own efficacy summary is [here](https://blog.khanacademy.org/khan-academy-efficacy-results-november-2024/).] On its face this is a positive AI-tutoring effect in a school context, in math, with a sample large enough to publish. It is the cleanest in-the-literature counter to the Bastani picture.

Khanmigo is also not the GPT Base condition. Khanmigo is a teacher-designed, curriculum-aligned, scaffolded tutoring system built by an education-first organization, deployed with teacher PD and curriculum integration wrapped around it. It is closer to Bastani's GPT Tutor than to GPT Base — and the GPT Tutor condition produced *zero* exam decrement, not a negative one. A modest positive effect from a heavily-scaffolded tutor inside a teacher-supported deployment is exactly what the chapter's framework predicts. Khanmigo is consistent with the argument, not a refutation of it. Khan Academy says so itself. The blog post summarizing the results notes, in language the chapter could not improve on, that the improvement in math is meaningful but modest and is not a replacement for skilled teaching. That sentence, from the vendor's own efficacy report, is the chapter's argument in seven words.

The second is **Kestin et al.** [Kestin, Miller, Klales, Milbourne, and Ponti (2025) in *Scientific Reports*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-97652-6) reported effect sizes of 0.73 to 1.3 SD for AI tutoring versus in-class active learning in a Harvard physics RCT — the most-cited "AI outperforms teachers" result in the 2025 literature. Combined with [Bloom's classic 1984 "two-sigma" finding](https://www.gwern.net/docs/psychology/writing/1984-bloom.pdf) that individual tutoring beats classroom instruction by roughly 2 SD, the case looks like "AI tutoring at scale approximates Bloom-tutor effects and beats the classroom." Chapter 2 took the design apart in detail. The shortest version: the Kestin AI tutor was *designed by the instructors* using the same active-learning principles they used in class. It is the instructors' pedagogy delivered one-to-one rather than one-to-many. The teacher's pedagogy is what made the tutor work. The tutor provided bandwidth. The teacher provided the design. "Teacher is the variable" survives Kestin intact.

The third is **the calculator objection.** "AI is just another tool. Teachers worried about calculators in the 1970s and we got over it. Students who learn with AI will be the engineers of 2040 and we should let them learn with the tools they will use." This is the most common objection in casual conversation, and it has enough surface plausibility to be worth meeting directly. The Bastani study answers it more cleanly than any argument from analogy. A calculator that returned the *answer to a word problem* — not the arithmetic, but the modeled answer to "if a train leaves Chicago at 60 mph and another leaves Denver at 80 mph" — would do to algebra exactly what GPT Base did to algebra in Turkey. The calculator did not in fact destroy math learning because the calculator was always offloading a sub-step (arithmetic) while leaving the schema-building step (problem modeling) intact. GPT Base offloaded the schema-building step. That is not the same kind of tool. The category of "tool" is too broad to do the analogical work the objection wants. The relevant question is always *which cognitive operation is being offloaded, and is it the one the lesson was trying to teach.* The calculator helps a chemistry student because the chemistry student is learning chemistry, not long division. ChatGPT damages a math student in a math lesson because the math student is learning math, and the math is what the chatbot just did.

There is one more challenge worth naming honestly, because it is the deepest one. [Neil Selwyn at Monash](https://research.monash.edu/en/publications/on-the-limits-of-artificial-intelligence-ai-in-education/) and a body of critical-edtech scholarship around him have argued that the AI-in-education conversation is already operating on a narrowed metric — that "learning" measured as scores on closed-book exams of practiced material is itself a technologized version of learning that may foreclose larger pedagogical questions. Selwyn would press the chapter on this: even Bastani accepts the metric as the measure. The chapter cannot dismiss the critique. The book's overall response — three measures, with professional judgment as the central variable — addresses much of it, but Selwyn's "recursive narrowing" warning belongs on the page. The teacher's judgment is the safeguard against the narrowing. That is the answer the chapter offers. It is not, by itself, an answer to the deeper version of the critique.

There is also a separate strand from Stanford's Center for Racial Justice and HAI suggesting that AI tutors may differ in the quality of feedback they give across student demographics — more detailed, development-focused feedback to students labeled "high-achieving" or "White," more spelling-and-grammar-focused feedback to students labeled "Hispanic" or "ELL," and a pattern of "feedback withholding bias" toward students labeled "low-achieving" or with learning disabilities. [verify: the underlying Stanford paper citation needs direct check; the press coverage I have seen is intermediary, including [an EdTech Innovation Hub summary](https://www.edtechinnovationhub.com/news/ai-tutor-bias-study-finds-unequal-feedback-for-students-across-race-gender-and-ability) and Stanford Law's analysis [here](https://law.stanford.edu/2024/06/29/how-will-ai-impact-racial-disparities-in-education/).] Even if the magnitudes need tightening, the directional finding is consistent: when AI is "working" by performance metrics, it may also be allocating instructional attention inequitably. This is a distinct layer of harm from Bastani's cognitive-offloading mechanism, and it lives in Chapter 11.

## The friction is the learning

There is a sentence that has been doing work in [Nik Bear Brown's *Computational Skepticism*](https://www.skepticism.ai/) writing and his Northeastern teaching materials, and the cognitive-science material in this chapter gives it the substrate to land hard. *AI fluency is not tool use. It is disciplined skepticism.* The artifact a student produces with AI is not evidence of learning by itself. What matters is process, friction, judgment, and transfer. Students still have to struggle, revise, defend, calibrate, and understand. The friction is the learning. Remove the friction and you have not made learning easier. You have made it not happen. [verify with Nik directly: the project notes reference a "GLP / Frictional" acronym for this framework; the canonical written statement of the framework — Substack URL, course material, or white paper — should be cited at the place the chapter most explicitly invokes the slogan.]

If the friction is the learning, then the policy question is not "should AI be in the classroom." It is *what is the right amount and kind of friction for this student on this concept today* — a question only the trained teacher can answer in real time. The trained teacher's daily craft is friction calibration. She knows when to make the math harder by withholding the calculator and when to make it easier by providing one. She knows when to make the writing harder by withholding the AI and when to make it easier by providing one. She knows because she has seen this student handle this kind of problem before. The AI has not.

The Bastani GPT Tutor scaffold is a first attempt to encode friction calibration into software. The system prompt that refuses to give the answer is a frozen, default-on, low-resolution version of what the trained teacher does at high resolution every day. *In a classroom with a teacher,* the scaffold neutralized harm. The chapter resists the temptation to over-claim what the scaffold *alone* could do. The paper does not separate "scaffolded AI without teacher" from "scaffolded AI with teacher running the session." That separation is the next study that needs to be run.

The reflexive caveat is worth stating: I am leaning hard on the Bjorks' framework — desirable difficulties, the storage-strength / retrieval-strength distinction, the metacognitive-illusion literature — and on Sweller's cognitive load theory, and on Schultz's prediction-error account of dopamine learning. Each of these is the strongest available instrument for the argument it does. None of them is a closed case. The Bjorks' inverse-coupling claim has been refined in detail since 1992; Sweller's theory has been challenged on the boundary between extraneous and germane load; Schultz's dopamine signal has been complicated by more recent work showing the system encodes more than scalar reward-prediction error. The point of citing these instruments is not that they are settled. The point is that *all three of them, in the form they are now most rigorously defended, predict the Bastani finding.* When three independent literatures converge on a prediction, and a pre-registered RCT produces exactly that prediction, the prediction is the right place to plant the argument's weight.

---

## What the trained teacher actually does that the platform cannot

The chapter's closing move is on the [Hattie effect-size literature](https://visible-learning.org/hattie-ranking-influences-effect-sizes-learning-achievement/), and specifically on the influence called *assessment-capable learners* — older label *self-reported grades* or *student expectations*. In Hattie's ranked-influence list, this construct consistently appears among the top three effects in the synthesis, *d* = 1.44. Chapter 4 took the methodological caveats around Hattie's effect-size database apart in detail. The same caveats apply to *d* = 1.44 — the magnitude is contested, the conversion from correlation to effect size is partly an artifact of the math, the meta-analytic input quality is uneven. The directional finding survives every critique: learners' ability to monitor their own learning is among the strongest predictors of achievement across the *Visible Learning* corpus.

The construct underneath the number is the one that matters. The assessment-capable learner can answer three questions at any moment: *Where am I now? Where am I going? What do I need to do to close the gap?* A student who can answer these questions has working metacognition. She can tell the difference between processing fluency (she just read the AI's answer and it sounded right) and memory fluency (she could solve the problem in 30 seconds with no tool). She can tell when she has actually learned the material and when she has only watched the material be solved. She is, in the strongest sense the cognitive-science literature can give the word, *the audience the AI cannot help by itself.*

She is also, in 2026, the rarest kind of student. Most students arrive at AI without a working metacognition. They have spent their educational lives optimizing for the appearance of competence rather than its construction. The AI rewards that optimization at a scale and a fluency no prior tool ever has. The Bastani −17 percent is what catches the students who have not been taught to monitor the difference. The chapter's closing claim is that the trained teacher's central craft, in the AI era, is producing the metacognition the student does not arrive with — building assessment-capable learners deliberately, as a primary outcome of the school day rather than an accidental byproduct of doing well. That craft can be named in five concrete moves, and naming them is the most useful thing the chapter can leave on the page.

She calibrates friction in real time. She knows that today, this student needs to do the algebra by hand. Tomorrow, with the schema built, she can offload it. She distinguishes fluency from understanding. She watches the student explain the problem aloud — a retrieval test that exposes fluency-without-storage instantly. The AI cannot run this test. She designs the prediction error. She gives the problem one notch above what the student expects to be able to do, so the productive struggle generates the dopamine signal that drives consolidation. The AI, optimizing for satisfaction, never produces this. She builds the metacognitive vocabulary. She teaches the student to recognize when she is processing fluently versus when she is actually retrieving — the Bjorks' single most important pedagogical contribution. The AI's interface actively obscures the distinction. She sees the wrong note. The student who produces correct answers but cannot explain why. The AI cannot hear this. It can only score the answer.

Every move on that list is teacher judgment of a kind the cognitive-science literature has identified as load-bearing for learning. Every move is one the trained teacher does and the untrained teacher does not, and one the platform does not do at all. The trained teacher is the only available instrument that can distinguish the student who is using Claude to think harder from the student who is using Claude to avoid thinking. The platform cannot make this call. The student herself, by mechanism, cannot make it either. The vendor's dashboard shows the +48 percent practice gain. The vendor's dashboard does not show the −17 percent exam decrement, because the exam was not part of the product. The teacher is the only person in the building who can see both.

The strategic frame this book has been holding throughout applies cleanly here. The strongest counter-evidence to the chapter's argument — the Bastani finding itself — is also, on closer reading, the strongest case for the argument. *Unguided AI access during practice damages closed-book exam performance by approximately 17 percent.* That is a sentence that sounds like a case against AI in classrooms. It is a sentence that turns into a case for the trained teacher the moment you read it carefully. The scaffolded condition did no harm. The trained-teacher-managed condition is the next study to run. The deployment that works is the deployment where someone in the room is making the call. The deployment that damages learning is the deployment where nobody is. The chapter's argument is not that AI should be removed. It is that the teacher is the variable that determines whether AI helps or hurts, and that we have systematically failed to invest in the only instrument that can make the difference visible.

The friction is the learning. Remove the friction and you have not made learning easier. You have made it not happen.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The Bastani GPT Tutor's neutral exam effect tells us that pedagogical scaffolding is sufficient to prevent damage. It does not tell us whether scaffolding is sufficient to produce learning beyond a teacher-led baseline. The chapter has resisted the temptation to claim that "AI plus trained teacher" demonstrably improves on "teacher alone" — the clean RCT for that question has not been run, and the honest reading is that scaffolded AI does no harm; trained-teacher-managed AI is the next question. The Bastani study is one school in Turkey in one academic year on one subject. Replication in US K–12, in non-mathematical subjects, in longer time windows, and with treatment-by-teacher-training interactions deliberately built in, is the obvious next step. The chapter has treated the finding as the strongest evidence available, not as the final word, and the next two years of the literature will determine how far it generalizes. And the calculator objection — that AI is "just another tool" and that students will adapt the same way every generation has adapted — is not a sentence that resolves into evidence. It is a wager about which cognitive operations the next generation needs and which the AI is now capable of offloading without cost. The wager is testable. We have not yet run the test on a generation.

## Still puzzling

The Bastani GPT Tutor produced zero exam decrement and zero exam gain. Why? Is the scaffold *sufficient* to prevent harm but *insufficient* to produce learning beyond teacher-led baseline — meaning the cognitive-offloading mechanism can be neutralized in software but the consolidation mechanism cannot be installed in software? Or is the scaffold absorbing some of the harm and adding some of the gain in roughly equal measure, with no net effect because the two are quietly canceling each other? The paper does not separate these. I find I cannot tell from the published data which one is happening, and the difference matters enormously for what we should build next.

---

## What would change my mind

A second large pre-registered RCT of unguided AI access in K–12 mathematics, in a US or non-Turkish context, with adequate sample and a closed-book exam measurement, that fails to replicate the Bastani negative effect — would substantially weaken the chapter's central empirical claim. A replication that *does* find the effect, and additionally finds that students with explicitly-trained metacognition are protected from it, would strengthen the chapter's closing argument considerably. The chapter is built to be falsifiable in both directions, and the next two years of the literature will tell.
