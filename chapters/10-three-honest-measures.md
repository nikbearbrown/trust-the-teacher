# Chapter 10: Three Honest Measures

*Why student satisfaction is not enough, what the research says about how to measure teacher effectiveness, and a framework that could actually work*

---

The instrument American higher education uses to decide who gets tenure, who gets contract renewal, and who gets dismissed correlates with how much students actually learn at roughly *r = 0.04*. Near zero. Slightly negative after you control for the grade the student expected. The single most consequential measurement decision the academy has institutionalized in the last half-century — *let the customers grade the kitchen* — is a measurement that does not, in any defensible sense, measure the thing it is being used to measure.

That number is from Uttl, White, and Gonzalez Morales's [2017 meta-analysis in *Studies in Educational Evaluation*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191491X16300323), the most methodologically careful synthesis of the student-evaluation-of-teaching literature ever published. They re-extracted effect sizes from the underlying multisection studies — the only design that can honestly answer the question — corrected for the small-sample artifacts and publication bias that had inflated every previous meta-analysis, and reported that once the corrections are applied, the correlation between student ratings and student learning is essentially zero. Their summary sentence, which I want on the page before any argument: *"Students do not learn more from professors with higher student evaluations of teaching."* [verify: the precise study counts and pooled correlation across analysis subsets in Uttl 2017 should be quoted from the methodology section of the paper directly; the "89 studies, 5.4M students" headline circulating in policy summaries should be cross-checked against the paper's own reporting before press.]

The follow-up paper from [Uttl and Cnudde in 2019](https://peerj.com/articles/7225/) is the one that should have ended the debate and did not. They examined whether the SET-learning correlation depended on who funded the underlying study. It did. Researchers with documented ties to the corporations that license SET instruments to universities reported large positive correlations. Researchers without identifiable conflicts of interest reported correlations of essentially zero. Studies published before 1981 — the era when SET vendors were establishing their market — reported correlations around *r = 0.31*. Studies published after 1981, as independent replication work began, reported correlations around *r = 0.06*. The historical evidence that SETs measure learning was, in substantial part, produced by the people selling SET instruments. This is the pharmaceutical funder effect, ported into educational measurement, never named as such by the field that adopted the instrument.

I want to make the structural observation before I make the empirical one, because the empirical case is going to follow from the structural confusion. The instrument simultaneously fails to measure learning, incentivizes grade inflation, and is systematically biased against the women and faculty of color most likely to push students through productive struggle. If you build the AI+1 teacher this book has been arguing for — the teacher who knows when to make the room harder, when to refuse the easier path, when to ask the question the student does not want to answer — and you evaluate her with the instrument the field currently uses, the instrument will punish her for being good at her job. That is not a marginal critique of measurement. That is the measurement system actively working against the educational practice the rest of the book is trying to build.

This chapter is about the measurement system the book needs to replace it with. Not because the current system is merely imperfect — every measurement system is imperfect — but because it is structurally wrong in the precise way that matters. It measures the wrong thing, with bias, against the wrong people, in a feedback loop that degrades both the signal and the practice it is supposed to assess. The chapter proposes three honest measures: student wellbeing, student learning gains, and teacher learning and improvement. The third one is the one nobody currently takes. Its absence is the reason the rest of this book's argument cannot, today, be operationalized.

## Four jobs wearing one phrase

"Teacher evaluation" is four different activities wearing one name, and almost everything that is broken about American teacher evaluation is the result of forcing one instrument to do all four jobs at once.

The first job is *formative feedback to the teacher.* Information she uses to improve. Low stakes. Confidential. The audience is the teacher and possibly a coach. The right instrument here is rich, narrative, specific, slightly embarrassing — the kind of feedback that costs nothing to act on and that you would never want in your personnel file.

The second job is *summative judgment about the teacher.* Information an administrator uses to decide pay, contract renewal, tenure, dismissal. High stakes. Personnel file. Audience: HR, eventually a lawyer if the dismissal is contested. The right instrument here is defensible, quantitative, comparable, legally bulletproof. It is the opposite of the right instrument for formative feedback.

The third job is *system-level measurement.* Information a state or country uses to decide how the system is doing. Audience: policy. The unit of analysis is the program, the district, the cohort. Individual teachers are not identified. The right instrument is sample-based, low-stakes, designed to detect drift over time without giving any single school or teacher an incentive to game it.

The fourth job is *accountability signaling.* Information posted publicly — RateMyProfessors, school report cards, league tables. Audience: parents, journalists, the next round of applicants. The right instrument is one that resists the gaming that the publication itself induces. There is no such instrument. This job, as currently performed, is mostly fiction with a metric attached.

These four jobs pull in different directions. The instrument that is best at the first is worst at the second. The instrument that works for the third would be useless for the fourth. The SET as universally administered in American higher education is being asked to do all four. It cannot do any of them well; it does the second badly enough to cause active harm; and the field has spent forty years pretending the confusion is a feature rather than the disease.

The specific instrument almost every American university uses is a five- or seven-point Likert questionnaire administered at the end of a course. It asks students to rate the instructor on prepared, knowledgeable, treated students with respect, was available, and — the item that does most of the personnel work — an *overall rating.* The well-engineered versions of this instrument (the [IDEA Student Ratings](https://courseevaluationsupport.campuslabs.com/hc/en-us/articles/360038347493-About-IDEA) developed at Kansas State, the ETS [SIR II](http://www.cetla.howard.edu/teaching_resources/Teaching_Evaluation/StudentEvaluations.html)) are psychometrically defensible at what they actually measure, which is *student satisfaction with the course at the moment the course ended.* The poorly-engineered versions are not even that. Neither version measures learning. The good instruments measure satisfaction reliably. The bad instruments measure satisfaction unreliably. Neither does the job the institution is using them for.

I want to specify the word "effectiveness" before going further, because the rest of the argument depends on the reader not letting it slip. *Effective at what?* Three candidates have been doing different jobs under the same word.

*Satisfaction.* Did the students enjoy the course and feel respected by the instructor? Real, measurable, and worth knowing.

*Learning gains.* Did the students know more, or do more, at the end than at the start, beyond what they would have learned with a different instructor? Different question. Different measurement.

*Downstream outcomes.* Do the students perform better in subsequent courses, graduate at higher rates, earn more later, behave differently as citizens? Different question again. Different measurement again.

A single instrument can do well on one and fail on the others. The SET literature is now closed on which: SETs measure satisfaction tolerably well and learning poorly. Conflating these is the move that lets the field cite SET results as if they were learning results. The conflation is not a mistake. It is the rhetorical move on which the whole edifice rests. Pull it apart and the edifice cannot defend itself.

## The multisection design, and what Uttl found

The gold-standard SET-and-learning design is the *multisection study,* and it is worth being specific about what it is, because almost nothing in the SET literature except the multisection studies can answer the question being asked.

The setup. One course — call it Calculus I. Many instructors teach many sections of it in the same semester. Every section uses the same syllabus, the same textbook, the same common final exam. Students are assigned to sections quasi-randomly through registration timing and scheduling constraints, so that the average ability of students in each section is roughly equivalent. At the end, you have for each section a mean SET rating (from end-of-course evaluations) and a mean exam score (ideally graded by someone other than the section's own instructor). You correlate the section's mean SET with the section's mean exam score. If high-SET sections also have higher exam means, then SET is partly tracking learning. If not, not.

This design is the only one that controls for the most pernicious SET confound: that students who like the instructor give better evaluations *and* may also study harder *and* may also have arrived in the section more motivated. The common syllabus, the common exam, and the quasi-random assignment pull these apart. The earlier meta-analyses — Cohen (1981) and Feldman (1989) — used this design and reported moderate positive correlations in the range *r = 0.30 to r = 0.47.* For four decades, those numbers were cited as the empirical foundation for using SETs to evaluate teaching.

Uttl, White, and Gonzalez Morales did three things in 2017 that prior meta-analyses had not done. Each is worth a sentence.

First, they weighted studies by the number of sections, not by the number of students or by some other criterion. The unit of analysis for a multisection correlation is the section. A study with five sections gives you a noisy correlation; a study with fifty gives a precise one. Prior meta-analyses had sometimes weighted by student count (which is dominated by big-class sections) or by no weighting at all. When you weight properly, the small-sample studies — which had the largest reported correlations — get appropriately down-weighted.

Second, they tested for publication bias and small-study effects using trim-and-fill and funnel-plot analyses. The pattern was textbook: tiny studies reported very high correlations, large studies reported near-zero correlations. This is the signature of publication bias plus the sampling noise that small studies amplify. The early literature was, in part, a noise artifact.

Third, they went back to the original studies and re-extracted effect sizes directly. Several of the correlations Cohen and Feldman had reported turned out to be transcription errors or non-standard effect-size choices. The cleaned numbers were smaller.

After proper weighting, bias correction, and re-extraction, the SET-learning correlation in the multisection literature is approximately zero. SETs explain at most one percent of the variance in student learning outcomes. After controlling for the well-documented effect of expected grade on SET, the correlation tips slightly negative. The defenders' rebuttals — that Uttl underweighted some rigorous small studies, that SETs measure inputs to learning rather than learning itself, that end-of-course measures are themselves noisy, that within-instructor designs show tighter correlations — have each been examined. None recovers a correlation large enough to justify the personnel decisions that have been made on SETs since the 1970s. [Hornstein's 2017 review in *Cogent Education*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2331186X.2017.1304016) reached the same conclusion through a different methodology: SETs do not measure teaching effectiveness; they measure student satisfaction, which is a related but distinct thing.

The Uttl result is the most robust finding the SET literature has. It also has a specific implication for the AI+1 frame this book has been building. A SET-driven personnel system rewards instructors whose students leave the course feeling good about it. An AI+1 instructor — one who knows how to push students through the productive-struggle phase of learning, who declines the easier path that AI offers when the easier path would substitute for the cognitive work, who refuses to do the offloaded thinking the student wants her to do — will, in many courses, leave students feeling worse about the course at the moment the course ends. Productive struggle does not feel good in the moment. Real learning often feels, at the end of a hard semester, like the instructor was harder than necessary. SETs penalize this. A system that uses SETs for tenure systematically promotes the instructors whose students were comfortable and dismisses the ones whose students were stretched. The cleaner this kind of selection works, the further the institution drifts from the practice the evidence says actually moves learning.

## The Carrell-West reversal

Then there is the experiment that should be required reading for every dean, and that is not.

[Carrell and West, in 2010, in the *Journal of Political Economy*](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/653808), exploited the cleanest natural experiment in the SET literature. The U.S. Air Force Academy randomly assigns cadets to sections of Calculus I, and every cadet then takes the entire sequence of follow-on courses — Calc II, Calc III, the engineering courses that depend on calculus — with common final exams not graded by the original instructor. Random assignment. Common downstream measurement. Thousands of students. This is what the Uttl multisection design aspires to.

The finding inverts forty years of conventional wisdom. Students taught Calc I by less-experienced, less-credentialed instructors got *higher grades in Calc I* — and *lower grades in Calc II and the courses that came after.* Students taught by experienced PhD-holding instructors got *lower grades in Calc I* — and *higher grades in everything subsequent.* The SET ratings tracked the Calc I grades, not the downstream performance. The instructors with worse SETs were producing the better long-run learning.

The mechanism is the one any honest teacher will recognize. Inexperienced instructors taught to the test of *this* course. They drilled the specific problem types that would appear on the Calc I final and produced students who could execute those types but lacked the foundational understanding that pays off when you have to apply calculus to a new problem in a new course. Experienced instructors taught for transfer. Their students struggled more in the moment, did better in the next moment, and rated their first-semester instructor lower. Both for the same reason. The discomfort of being asked to learn the harder version of the material *is* the discomfort that produces transfer. SETs cannot tell the difference between "this instructor was bad" and "this instructor was teaching me something difficult that I did not yet know I would need."

If the Air Force Academy had used Calc I SETs for tenure, it would have systematically promoted the instructors who hurt long-run learning and dismissed the ones who produced it. This is not a hypothetical. This is what the data say. SETs do not merely fail to predict learning. *In a non-trivial fraction of cases, SETs predict the opposite of learning.* The instrument is not noisy. It is, for the subset of teaching that produces transfer, anti-correlated.

I want to dwell on the AI+1 implication for one more beat, because it is the load-bearing connection between this chapter and the rest of the book. The Carrell-West reversal generalizes. Any instructor — Calc I or otherwise — whose teaching choice is *make the student do the cognitive work that produces transfer* will, on average, receive lower SET ratings than the colleague who lets the AI tutor produce a smooth, comfortable, on-rails learning experience that maximizes student satisfaction in the moment. The AI+1 teacher is the Carrell-West experienced PhD professor at scale. The non-AI-trained colleague who lets students offload the productive-struggle phase to ChatGPT is the inexperienced Calc I instructor at scale. SET-driven personnel systems will systematically select for the second and against the first. The book's entire argument about training the teacher to make the harder call collapses against a measurement system that punishes her for making it.

## What happens when you swap names

The SET-learning question is one thing. The SET-bias question is another, separable thing. Even if SETs measured learning — which they do not — they could still be systematically biased against particular instructors. They are, and the literature on this is no longer in dispute.

[MacNell, Driscoll, and Hunt's 2015 study in *Innovative Higher Education*](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10755-014-9313-4) is the experiment that should be cited every time anyone defends SETs on equity grounds. The design was clean. An online graduate course with two assistant instructors. Each instructor taught one section under a male name and one section under a female name. Students never saw the instructor; all interaction was text-based. The same person, the same words, the same feedback returned at the same time — but half the students thought the instructor was male and half thought the instructor was female.

The students rated the perceived-male identity significantly higher than the perceived-female identity, on nearly every dimension. The dimension that should have been impossible to bias was *promptness of feedback,* which was returned at literally identical times for both perceived identities. The perceived-male instructor was rated higher on promptness anyway. Students rated a man higher for the same act, performed by the same person, at the same moment in time. The sample is small — one course, forty-three students — which is why the replication question matters. The replication did the rest of the work.

[Boring, Ottoboni, and Stark in 2016](https://www.scienceopen.com/hosted-document?doi=10.14293%2FS2199-1006.1.SOR-EDU.AETBZC.v1), and Boring's individual follow-up in the [*Journal of Public Economics* in 2017](https://www.unlv.edu/sites/default/files/page_files/27/Engineering-GenderBiases-AnneBoring.pdf), did the population-scale version. Two datasets. The primary one was a five-year natural experiment at a French university — 23,001 SETs covering 379 instructors evaluated by 4,423 students across six mandatory first-year courses with random instructor assignment. The replication was the MacNell design at a U.S. university. The findings: gender bias against female instructors is large and statistically significant; the bias appears on items that should be objective (promptness of returning assignments, when the actual return times were identical); SET scores are more sensitive to student gender and grade expectations than to instructor teaching quality measured by subsequent learning; and the bias is large enough that *more effective female instructors get lower SETs than less effective male instructors.* That last finding is the one that should end the use of SETs for personnel decisions. The bias is not a small adjustment to the ranking. The bias is large enough to *invert* the ranking. Use SETs to compare a man and a woman with identical teaching, and you will systematically rank the woman lower. Use SETs to compare a better female teacher with a worse male teacher, and you will, often enough, rank the better teacher lower than the worse one.

The [American Statistical Association's 2019 statement on student evaluations of teaching](https://www.amstat.org/asa/files/pdfs/POL-ASAonStudentEvaluationsofTeaching.pdf) reflected this conclusion, drawing in part on Stark's broader advocacy. The ASA — not an advocacy group, the professional society of statisticians — wrote that SETs are not statistically valid measures of teaching effectiveness, that they should not be used as the primary basis for personnel decisions, and that their use in summative evaluation should be substantially constrained. When the field that invented effect sizes tells you your instrument is not measuring what you are using it to measure, the appropriate response is not to use it harder. [verify: ASA statement URL should be confirmed against the current ASA policy page before press.]

[Mitchell and Martin in 2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/ps-political-science-and-politics/article/gender-bias-in-student-evaluations/1224BE475C0AE75A2C2D8553210C4E27) added a content-analytic dimension. Examining the open-response sections of formal evaluations across identical courses, they found women were evaluated on personality and appearance more than men; women were more likely to be called "teacher" than "professor" — a status downgrade in the language students used to refer to their own instructors; and women received lower scores in identical online courses where the only thing students could see was the name. Different methodology. Same finding.

The racial-bias literature is structurally similar. [Bavishi, Madera, and Hebl in 2010](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2010-22434-001), in *Journal of Diversity in Higher Education,* showed students fictitious CVs of college professors with race and gender varied. Black professors were rated less competent than White and Asian counterparts; Black and Asian professors were rated lower in interpersonal skills than White professors. The ratings were generated *before any interaction with the professor.* The first day of class, in this measurement system, partially determines the SET. [Smith and Hawkins in 2011](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ942385), in *Journal of Negro Education*, found Black faculty mean scores were the lowest on both the multidimensional items and the global rating used in personnel decisions, across the institutional samples they examined. Multiple independent designs — lab, field, archival, content-analytic — converge on the same finding. SETs are systematically lower for women and for faculty of color in identical courses with identical teaching. The literature is closed.

I want to be specific about what this means for the rest of the book's argument. The teachers most likely to push students through the productive struggle that AI+1 pedagogy requires are not randomly distributed across demographic groups. The instructors with the strongest training in resisting cognitive offloading, in refusing to let the AI tool substitute for student thinking, in maintaining the slightly uncomfortable rigor that produces transfer — that population, in 2026, is disproportionately female and disproportionately includes faculty of color, partly because those are the demographic groups that have most invested in the pedagogy literature that this book is drawing on. A SET-driven evaluation system would not merely fail to identify those teachers. It would actively select against them, on top of the demographic bias that already lowers their scores. The bias and the practice penalty compound. This is not an incidental problem with measurement. It is the measurement system the book is implicitly arguing against, made explicit and quantitative.

## The grade-inflation feedback loop

There is one more empirical thread to put on the table before the constructive proposal, and it is the one that turns the SET system from a measurement failure into a corrosive equilibrium.

The mechanism. Departments tie contract renewal, salary, and tenure to minimum SET thresholds. Students rate easier courses and higher grades more favorably. Instructors learn this — there is no profession on earth that does not learn what its evaluators reward — and grade more leniently to protect their SETs. Grades inflate. The signal value of grades degrades. The signal value of SETs degrades, because SETs are now measuring lenient grading rather than teaching. The institution arrives at an equilibrium where neither grades nor SETs convey reliable information about either student learning or teacher quality. Both metrics are still administered. Both are still used for high-stakes decisions. Both are now substantially noise.

[Stroebe's 2020 analysis in *Educational Psychologist*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01973533.2020.1756817) — the canonical statement of this mechanism — laid out four propositions that the empirical literature now broadly supports. *Students reward good grades with positive SETs. Students reward easy courses with positive SETs. Students choose courses that promise good grades. Instructors want, and structurally need, good SETs.* These four together imply a stable bad equilibrium: instructors who want positive evaluations grade leniently and assign less work; students who want good grades shop for these instructors; the average SET drifts up; the average GPA drifts up; teaching quality, as measured by actual learning, drifts flat or down.

[Bachan in 2017, in *Studies in Higher Education*](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1144090), provided the macroscale empirical demonstration of the mechanism's effects. UK universities showed clear evidence of unexplained grade inflation from 2010–11 onward — the period coinciding with the major increase in English tuition fees, when universities began competing harder for fee-paying students. Scottish universities, which do not charge tuition fees to Scottish or EU students, showed the lowest grade inflation. The market mechanism that produces SET pressure (universities want satisfied paying customers) produces the inflation. Where the market mechanism is weaker, the inflation is weaker. This is consistent with Stroebe's propositions running in the real world, not just in the model.

I want to name a verification flag here because honesty requires it. A widely-circulated figure — "departments that tie contract renewal to minimum SET thresholds show approximately 0.27 GPA-point grade inflation relative to matched controls" — has been quoted in policy summaries of this literature, and the original source for that specific number traces to a 2025 [Preprints.org manuscript](https://www.preprints.org/manuscript/202509.1763) synthesizing the SET-bias-and-inflation literature. The preprint is not peer-reviewed. The mechanism the figure illustrates is well-established in the peer-reviewed literature; the precise 0.27 GPA-point magnitude rests on a non-peer-reviewed source and should be either replaced or labeled accordingly. The Stroebe analysis, the Bachan empirical work, and the broader Economics of Education Review literature on faculty contract uncertainty and grade inflation (see the [2021 paper on contract uncertainty and grading](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775721000455), which found that contract uncertainty inflates grades, particularly for female faculty) together establish the mechanism. The 0.27-point claim does not yet rest on equivalent ground. I have included the citation chain. The reader is asked to weight the mechanism strongly and the specific magnitude with appropriate skepticism. [verify: pursue a peer-reviewed magnitude estimate for SET-contract-linked grade inflation in time for press.]

The mechanism does not require the specific magnitude. It works at any level. Inflate grades to protect SETs, inflate SETs because students reward lenient grading, inflate the noise floor, lose the signal. The instrument was already not measuring learning. The feedback loop ensures that the instrument is no longer measuring satisfaction reliably either — what it now measures is the equilibrium between the instructor's incentive to inflate and the student's adjustment of expectations to the new normal. A measurement system that has eaten its own signal cannot be repaired by adjusting the instrument. It can only be replaced.

## What does work, partially

Before the constructive proposal, I owe the reader an honest accounting of the measurement designs that *have* survived methodological scrutiny — because the three-measure framework that follows draws from each of them, and pretending the field has no usable instruments would be both wrong and unkind to the people building them.

The strongest evidence that *some* measurement of teacher contribution to learning is possible at scale comes from the value-added literature. [Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff's 2014 paper in the *American Economic Review*](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.104.9.2633) — the second of two papers, the one that used IRS tax records linked to 2.5 million students over decades — found that students assigned to high-value-added teachers in primary school were more likely to attend college, earned more as adults, and were less likely to become teen parents. Replacing a bottom-five-percent teacher with an average teacher would raise the present value of a single classroom's lifetime earnings by approximately $250,000. This is not a SET-style finding. This is administrative-data, tax-record-linked, peer-reviewed evidence that teacher effects on learning are real, large, and traceable into adulthood.

What VAM measures is year-over-year change in students' standardized test scores attributable to having a specific teacher, after controlling for prior achievement and demographics. The honest reading is that VAM, properly estimated with large datasets and quasi-experimental identification, is a noisy but real signal about teacher contribution to learning gains, and the signal is large enough to matter at the policy level for long-run outcomes.

The honest reading is also that the Chetty et al. evidence does not license the use of VAM for individual personnel decisions, even though many state and district evaluation systems in the 2010s took it as such license. The estimates that work at the population level for policy decisions are too noisy for the individual personnel level. A specific teacher's VAM estimate has wide confidence intervals; a teacher labeled "bottom five percent" in one year can be average the next year, purely by chance. The Chetty work supports the *existence* of meaningful teacher effects on learning. It does not support pretending those effects are precisely measurable for any one teacher in any one year. This is a distinction that the policy world systematically failed to honor in the value-added era, with consequences — teacher dismissals based on statistical noise, careers ended by year-over-year drift — that have not been adequately reckoned with by the people who built those systems.

[The MET Project — Measures of Effective Teaching, a $45 million Gates Foundation study across seven school districts](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED540959.pdf) — is the closest the U.S. has come to building a multimeasure evaluation system on empirical grounds. Roughly 3,000 teachers volunteered. The project tested whether three measures of teaching, combined, could identify teachers whose students would learn more in a subsequent year. The three measures were value-added scores from state tests, classroom observations using validated protocols (Danielson's Framework for Teaching, CLASS, the Mathematical Quality of Instruction protocol), and student perception surveys (the Tripod survey).

Year one of the study was used to build a composite predictive model. Year two randomly reassigned students to teachers, and the composite was tested against subsequent learning. The composite did predict subsequent student learning under random assignment — the strongest evidence in the U.S. literature that some combination of measures can identify effective teaching prospectively. No single measure performed well alone. Student surveys alone were noisy. Observations alone were noisy. VAM alone was noisy. The optimal weight on test gains in the composite was lower than the weight policy advocates had assumed; observations and student surveys earned higher weights than the political conventional wisdom of the era had wanted them to. Even the composite had substantial measurement error — enough to differentiate top quartiles from bottom quartiles, *not* enough for the year-to-year individual personnel decisions districts attempted to make from it. The [National Education Policy Center's 2013 critique](https://nepc.colorado.edu/thinktank/review-MET-final-2013) argued that the MET effect sizes were overstated and that the composite was still too noisy for high-stakes use; the MET team responded with its own caveats. The honest middle is that MET demonstrated *better than SET-alone* measurement is possible, and that even the best composite is much noisier than its political proponents wished it were.

The Hattie meta-meta-analysis literature, with all the caveats of Chapter 4, establishes that the dimensions student-perception surveys *can* measure honestly — teacher-student relationships (canonical *d ≈ 0.72*), classroom climate, perceived rapport — are themselves substantial influences on learning. Hattie's headline numbers are contested in their precise magnitudes (the Slavin, Wecker, Simpson, and Bergeron-Rivard critiques apply here too), and the *d ≈ 0.44* sometimes attributed to "psychological safety" is not a labeled Hattie influence but is close to his hinge value and may correspond to classroom-cohesion or climate subcategories; the figure should be used with appropriate care. [verify: the specific Hattie subcategory underlying any "psychological safety = d ≈ 0.44" claim should be pinned down to a named MetaX influence before press.] The defensible claim from the Hattie literature is that the *relational and climate dimensions* of teaching matter for learning, that those dimensions are partially captured by well-designed student perception instruments, and that this is the case *for* keeping student feedback as one input — not the case for using it as the sole personnel input.

And there is the Finnish design — system-level measurement decoupled from individual personnel measurement — which Chapter 8 already described and which this chapter now needs. Finland's [FINEEC / Karvi system](https://www.karvi.fi/en/evaluations/pre-primary-primary-and-lower-secondary-education/learning-outcomes-evaluations) conducts national learning-outcome assessments on representative samples of schools and students, typically around ten percent of the relevant cohort. Results are used to evaluate the *education system,* not individual schools or teachers. No school-level rankings are published. No individual teacher evaluations are produced from these assessments. Teachers are evaluated locally through principal observation and professional dialogue. The high-stakes, individual-accountability layer is separated from the system-monitoring layer. The moment the assessment becomes high-stakes for individuals, individuals start gaming it; the moment it stops being high-stakes for individuals, the signal stays clean.

The Finnish *implementation* does not transplant directly into the U.S. — Finland's teacher corps is small, highly selected at entry, master's-trained, and embedded in strong professional cultural norms. Direct importation of the no-individual-stakes design into a context with much larger teacher quality variance and no master-trained floor would mean abandoning accountability without putting professional infrastructure in its place. But the *principle* — separate system-level diagnostic measurement from individual-personnel accountability measurement, and use different instruments for each — is portable. That principle is what the three-measure framework is built on.

## Measure One — Student Wellbeing

Keep the student-perception survey. Do not discard it.

The argument for keeping it is the Hattie argument plus the honest accounting of what well-designed instruments actually measure. The relational climate of a classroom — whether students feel safe, respected, fairly treated, intellectually challenged in a supportive rather than punitive register — is a real dimension of teaching, it matters for learning, and the best available measure of it is student perception. The IDEA instrument, the SIR II, the Tripod survey used in the MET project — these are well-engineered tools that measure what they measure with reasonable internal reliability. The problem has never been the instruments. The problem has been what the instruments were being used for.

The shift required is structural, not technical. Stop using student perception as the primary basis for personnel decisions. Use it for two narrower purposes. First, *formative feedback to the teacher* — report results to the instructor directly, in detail, with the open-ended student comments preserved, and let her decide what to do with the information. This is the use the literature actually supports. Second, *aggregate department-level monitoring* — pool the results above the level of the individual instructor to track whether the department as a whole is creating the relational climate students need to learn. This is the use the equity-bias literature can tolerate: gender and racial bias at the individual level partly washes out at the aggregate level, where the question is "is this department generally a place students feel respected" rather than "is this specific woman a worse teacher than this specific man."

The forbidden use is the one the system currently makes: using individual SET scores as the primary input to contract renewal, tenure, salary, or promotion decisions. The Boring-Stark evidence is sufficient to make this use legally indefensible, professionally indefensible, and ethically indefensible. The ASA statement says so explicitly. The institutions that continue this practice are doing so against the recommendation of the professional society of statisticians.

There is one specific operational principle worth naming. The student perception instrument should be administered for *the student's benefit and the teacher's,* not for the administrator's. That reframing — small in its rhetoric, large in its consequences — changes what the instrument is. Administered for the student's benefit, the survey becomes a check on whether students felt the course served them; this is honestly the question the instrument can answer. Administered for the teacher's benefit, the survey becomes formative information for practice change; this is the use Hattie's relationship-and-climate effect sizes support. Administered for the administrator's benefit — as legally defensible quantitative summative evidence for HR decisions — it becomes the instrument that fails to measure learning, biases against women and faculty of color, drives grade inflation, and corrupts the practice it is meant to assess. The same instrument, three different uses, three different defensibility profiles. The chapter's recommendation is to retain the first two uses and abandon the third.

## Measure Two — Student Learning Gains

Add a measure of what students actually learned in the course, designed under the constraints the Finnish principle implies.

The structural design. Pre-post assessment on common, externally-constructed items. Not graded by the section's own instructor. Administered under controlled conditions. Reported as *section-level gain* — not individual student score, not raw outcome score. A common pre-post controls for student incoming ability. External grading prevents grading-bias bleed-through (the instructor who wants to protect her gain score by grading the post-test leniently does not get to grade the post-test). Section-level reporting respects measurement error and prevents the false precision of individual scores from being weaponized into personnel decisions the underlying noise does not support.

The Finnish principle adapted. Use *sample-based assessment* for system-level monitoring — which programs are working, which schools need investment, where the curriculum is succeeding and where it is failing. Use *within-classroom pre-post* for formative feedback to teachers — did your students learn what you were trying to teach them, on the measures you and the department agreed would count. Use *neither* for direct personnel decisions about pay, promotion, or dismissal. The same evidence base that supports Chetty-style population-level claims about teacher effects on learning does not support individual-teacher-level claims with precision sufficient for personnel decisions. The honest use of the measurement is at the level the measurement can defensibly answer.

The honest limit. Pre-post measures only what is on the post-test. Teachers, being responsive to incentives, will teach to the post-test. This is acceptable if the post-test is well-constructed and measures things worth learning. It is corrosive if the post-test measures the wrong things — surface fluency rather than transfer, or the specific problem types that will appear on the exam rather than the deeper foundational understanding that pays off in the next course. The Carrell-West finding is the explicit warning: the instructors who taught to the Calc I test produced students who failed Calc II. The same dynamic applies to any pre-post measurement system if the post-test design is shallow. The countermeasure is to design post-tests with transfer in mind, to include items that the teacher cannot drill toward, and ideally to validate the post-test against subsequent course performance — to require, as Carrell-West required, that the assessment of this semester include some assessment of how this semester's students perform in the next semester. Most assessment systems are not built this way. They could be.

The relationship to the AI+1 frame. A learning-gains measure done well is the only summative measure that can recognize an AI+1 teacher's work. SETs cannot, because productive struggle feels bad in the moment. Observation protocols can partially — and the MET project showed they add real signal to a composite — but observation is expensive and the inter-rater reliability problems are large. Learning gains, with a well-designed post-test administered under controlled conditions and graded externally, measure the thing the AI+1 teacher's practice is producing: students who actually know more at the end of the course than they did at the start, beyond what they would have learned with a different instructor. This is the measurement the AI+1 frame requires the system to make. The system is not currently making it.

## Measure Three — Teacher Learning and Improvement

This is the measure nobody currently takes. Its absence is the reason this book's argument cannot, today, be operationalized.

The model is medical Maintenance of Certification, as Chapter 8 described it. Not "did the teacher attend the conference?" — which is the only question current state-licensure infrastructure asks — but "did the teacher's practice change as a result of what she learned, and what evidence does she have?" The American Board of Internal Medicine requires 100 MOC points every five years, including documentation of practice changes and outcomes review. The American Board of Family Medicine's five-year cycle requires sixty certification points (Self-Assessment plus Performance Improvement), two hundred CME credits, and twenty-five quarterly assessment questions or a one-day exam. The opposite, in every structural dimension, of what most American teachers experience as the audit of their professional learning.

The third measure for teaching would be, annually or biennially, a documented cycle: *identified growth area; professional development engaged with; evidence of practice change (observation, teacher artifacts, peer review, video of the changed practice in action); connection to student outcome data where the connection is defensible.* The cycle is the measurement. The unit is not the hour of seat time in a workshop. The unit is the demonstrated movement of practice in response to evidence.

The instrument adjacencies exist, even if the integrated system does not. [Danielson's Framework for Teaching](https://danielsongroup.org/the-framework-for-teaching/) defines what effective teaching looks like across four domains and twenty-two components; it has been validated as one of the observation protocols in MET; its primary limitation is that principals do not reliably discriminate among the twenty-two components, with ratings collapsing onto a general factor in practice. The [Marzano Focused Teacher Evaluation Model](https://marzanoevaluationcenter.com/evaluation/teacher-evaluation-model/), streamlined to twenty-three essential competencies, has similar adoption and similar use-case fit. Wieman and Gilbert's [Teaching Practices Inventory](https://www.lifescied.org/doi/full/10.1187/cbe.14-02-0023) — a fifty-plus-item self-report scoring STEM faculty on the use of evidence-based teaching practices — is not a measure of effectiveness but a measure of *practice,* designed for tracking change because each item is specific and replicable. The Classroom Observation Protocol for Undergraduate STEM (COPUS) is a behavioral count of what teachers and students actually do during class. None of these were designed to be the integrated growth-measurement system the chapter is calling for. They are the pieces. The integration has not been built.

The honest framing of what teaching currently requires versus what medicine currently requires is sharper than the bumper-sticker version. Doctors are required to demonstrate *change in practice tied to outcomes.* Teachers are required to demonstrate *time spent in professional development.* The difference is the difference between "did your patients get better as a result of what you learned at the conference" and "did you go to the conference." Teaching's compliance layer is closer to medicine's compliance layer than the bumper-sticker comparison suggests — Michigan, for example, requires 150 hours of education-related professional learning across a five-year renewal period, comparable to lower-end medical CME hour requirements. The structural difference is not in the hour count. It is in what the hours have to demonstrate. Medical CME documentation requires evidence of practice change and outcome review. Teacher PD documentation typically requires only attendance.

This is the gap the third measure would fill. And it is the only one of the three measures that creates the infrastructure for what the rest of this book has been proposing. SETs are backward-looking at a static state. Learning gains tell you what happened this semester. Only growth measurement tells you whether the teacher is on a trajectory of improvement. If you want to pay teachers to develop AI-using capability — to compensate the time costs of moving from the August-vendor-demo level of AI fluency to the genuinely AI+1 practice the book has been building toward — you need a measurement system that can recognize and reward the movement. Without growth measurement, the investment has no defensible basis. The state cannot pay teachers to improve at AI if it has no instrument capable of distinguishing the teacher who has improved from the teacher who has not. The instrument does not exist. Until it does, the AI dividend that this book has documented — the [Gallup-Walton Family Foundation survey](https://news.gallup.com/poll/691967/three-teachers-weekly-saving-six-weeks-year.aspx) finding that teachers using AI weekly save about six weeks per school year — will continue to flow to the teachers who already use AI well, while teachers in under-resourced districts who received no training save nothing. The dividend will compound inequities the system has no mechanism to address, because the system has no mechanism to recognize the capability the dividend is rewarding.

The third measure is the most important of the three because it is the precondition. The first two improve the existing measurement system. The third creates the *future* of the system — the infrastructure for paying teachers to develop, the basis for credentialing AI+1 practice, the audit trail that lets a state or a district know whether its training investment is producing the capability it was meant to produce. Without the third, this book's argument cannot be operationalized. It can be made; it cannot be built.

## What this means Monday morning

I want to be specific about what a district leader could do with this chapter on the Monday morning after she finishes reading it. Not in five years, not at the state level, not after the next legislative cycle. This Monday.

She can do five things, none of which require legislative action.

*First,* she can change the use of the SET data she already collects. The instrument does not have to be discarded. The *use* of it has to change. Move student perception data out of the personnel file. Send it directly to the teacher as formative feedback. Aggregate it at the department level for system monitoring. Communicate to the teaching staff, in writing, that individual SET scores will no longer be used as a primary basis for contract renewal, tenure, salary, or dismissal decisions. The ASA recommendation is sitting on the table. She does not need permission to follow it. The legal exposure of continuing to use a biased instrument that fails to measure what it is being used to measure is, by 2026, larger than the legal exposure of stopping. The political exposure is reversed only because nobody has yet stopped. The first district to stop will be cited by the second.

*Second,* she can pilot a learning-gains measure in one or two departments where the structural conditions support it — usually a department teaching common-curriculum courses across multiple sections with multiple instructors. Common pre-test, common post-test, external grading, section-level reporting. Run it as formative information for one academic year. Do not attach personnel consequences. Look at the results. See what the teachers do with them. The Finnish principle, scaled to one department. The hard part is the post-test design, and the post-test design problem is precisely what the department's content experts should be solving anyway; the assessment is also the curriculum, in the sense that it operationalizes what the department thinks students should know at the end of the course. The pilot also produces, as a side effect, a department-level conversation about what the department actually believes about its own subject. That conversation alone is worth the year.

*Third,* she can begin building the growth-measurement infrastructure with the budget she already has. The SETDA data from Chapter 8 are clear: most Title II-A money in most districts is being spent on professional development that the literature does not defend. Redirect a fraction — even ten percent — toward sustained, coaching-anchored cycles with documented practice-change evidence. Require participating teachers to document, in writing, the growth area they identified, the practice change they attempted, the evidence (observation, video, student work, peer review) of the change, and the connection to a student outcome where one exists. The documentation is not for the personnel file. It is for the teacher's professional portfolio and for the district's audit of whether the investment in PD is producing practice change. This is the third measure in seed form. It scales when the documentation infrastructure becomes routine — when the question "what did you change about your teaching this year and what evidence do you have?" becomes a normal year-end conversation rather than a confronting one. The cultural shift is most of the work. The documentation infrastructure is the smaller part. Start it.

*Fourth,* she can write the AI-specific growth area into the third measure from the beginning. Every teacher in the district, in 2026, is being asked to make calls about generative AI in her classroom. The third measure can recognize this. The professional-development cycle can include AI-specific growth — adoption of a documented practice, evidence of student-work change, peer review of the practice change, eventually connection to learning gains as the second-measure infrastructure comes online. This is how the AI dividend gets distributed equitably, rather than accruing only to the teachers who arrived already fluent. The district's growth-measurement instrument becomes the channel through which the training investment from Chapter 8 produces the practice change that Chapter 4 identified as the variable.

*Fifth,* she can communicate the change to the teaching staff, in writing, with the empirical evidence base attached. Teachers know the SET instrument is broken. They have known it longer than the literature has documented it. The single act of an administrator explicitly saying, on the record, *we have read the evidence, we are changing how we use this data, here is what we are replacing it with, here is what we are asking of you in return,* is itself a measurable change in the school culture. It is the kind of leadership move that, in the Kraft-coaching literature on what makes professional learning take hold, predicts whether the new instrument will be received as accountability or as opportunity. The framing decides.

What she cannot do this Monday: build the third measure at scale. That requires state and federal infrastructure that does not yet exist, the credentialing layer that the [Finnish DigiErko program](https://uefconnect.uef.fi/en/digierko-learning-and-teaching-in-digital-environments/) validated and that no U.S. equivalent has built, and the integration with state licensure that the medical specialty boards developed over fifty years and that teaching's analogous infrastructure has not begun. That is the policy work. Chapter 11 takes it up. The Monday morning version is the seed. The seed is enough to start.

## The chapter's turn

The SET correlation with learning is essentially zero in any design that does not have a stake in finding otherwise. The bias against women and faculty of color is large enough to invert rankings in identical courses. The feedback loop with grade inflation has corroded both signals. The instrument measures something — student satisfaction at the moment the course ended — but the thing it measures is not what the institution is using it to measure. The Carrell-West reversal makes the cost of the misuse concrete: a measurement system tied to SETs systematically promotes the instructors whose students were comfortable and dismisses the ones whose students were stretched. An AI+1 teacher who pushes through productive struggle, refuses cognitive offloading, and produces transfer rather than surface fluency will be punished by this system for the practice that produces the learning the system was supposed to be measuring.

The replacement is not one instrument. It is three: student wellbeing, student learning gains, and teacher learning and improvement, used for the purposes each can defensibly serve and not used for the purposes none can. The first improves an existing data source by changing its use rather than its content. The second builds a measurement design the literature has been describing for decades and the field has not built at scale. The third is the measure that does not currently exist anywhere in U.S. teaching at scale, that medicine has built across three layers over fifty years, that Finland validated through the DigiErko cohort and the tutor-teacher system, and that the rest of this book's argument depends on the country eventually building.

The three measures together are what the AI+1 frame needs to become operationalizable. Without them, the trained teacher whom this book has been arguing for can be produced — through the training infrastructure Chapter 8 described and the integration practices Chapter 9 named — but she cannot be *recognized,* and what cannot be recognized cannot be rewarded, and what cannot be rewarded does not scale beyond the individual teacher's own commitment. The measurement problem is the scaling problem. Solve the measurement problem and the scaling problem becomes tractable. Leave the measurement problem unsolved and every successful instance of AI+1 teaching is a singular event, dependent on the individual teacher's tolerance for being penalized by the instrument the system uses to evaluate her.

The book's central thesis — *the teacher is the variable* — does not survive a measurement system that punishes the teacher for being the variable. Replace the measurement system. Then the rest of the argument can be built. That is the chapter's claim. The next chapter takes up what the same measurement question looks like when the students at the bottom of the distribution are the ones the measurement is supposed to serve. That is the equity argument, and the measurement problem is not separate from it. The measurement problem *is* it.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The precise magnitude of SET-driven grade inflation tied to contract uncertainty is the cleanest example: the mechanism is established in the peer-reviewed literature, the Stroebe and Bachan work supports the existence and approximate shape of the effect, and the specific 0.27 GPA-point figure that has circulated in policy summaries rests on a 2025 non-peer-reviewed preprint that should be either confirmed against a peer-reviewed source or replaced before this chapter's argument relies on it. The interaction between SET bias and teaching style is not yet quantitatively resolved: we know SETs penalize women and faculty of color, and we know SETs penalize teachers who push productive struggle (Carrell-West), but the literature has not directly measured how much of the demographic SET gap is attributable to instructional rigor differences versus pure bias on otherwise identical practice. The integration of growth measurement with personnel decisions is a known unknown: medicine's three-layer system separates the high-stakes credentialing layer from the formative compliance layer in ways that work, but the empirical evidence on how to make analogous separation work in teaching, at U.S. scale, with adequate teacher buy-in, is thin. Building the third measure right is partly a policy design problem the evidence base cannot yet fully resolve.

## Still puzzling

Why has the American Statistical Association statement on student evaluations of teaching — published in 2019, signed by the professional society that invented the methods on which SET claims rest — not yet produced a measurable shift in how American universities use SET data for personnel decisions? The professional consensus exists. The legal exposure of continuing to use a biased instrument is non-trivial. The institutional inertia is enormous. What would it take? The honest answer is that I do not yet know, and that the absence of a clear answer is itself a measurement of how hard the cultural change is going to be. The instrument is not the thing keeping the instrument in place. Something else is. What that something is — administrator preference for legally defensible numbers, faculty resistance to non-quantitative alternatives, the sunk cost of decades of instrumentation, the simple difficulty of building a replacement — is the question the next decade of measurement reform has to answer if it is going to make progress.

## What would change my mind

A randomized trial in which one set of departments adopted the three-measure framework as described — student wellbeing for formative and aggregate use only, learning gains via common pre-post with external grading at the section level, growth measurement with documented practice-change evidence and credentialing tied to demonstrated AI+1 capability — and another set of matched departments continued with SET-driven personnel evaluation, and three years later there was no measurable difference in teacher practice change, student learning gains, or distribution of professional development effects across teacher demographics. If the framework produced no measurable improvement on what the framework was specifically designed to improve, the chapter's central claim — that the measurement problem is the scaling problem and that solving it is the precondition for the rest of this book's argument — would need to be substantially revised.
