## Acknowledgments

This book exists because of conversations with teachers, principals, district leaders, and education researchers who do not appear in the bibliography but whose patience with my questions over the past eighteen months made the argument possible. They asked me what the research actually showed and pushed me to be specific. They are the audience this book was written for, and they were also its first editors.

I am grateful to the Northeastern University students who took the AI Fluency certificate in its first three cohorts. They were the laboratory for many of the claims about what AI deployment with versus without scaffolding actually produces. They were also the early evidence that the next generation will absorb whatever cognitive habits we model. I tried to model better ones.

To the Humanitarians AI fellows whose work tested the book's arguments in adjacent domains — civic accountability, bioinformatics, music research, education technology — thank you for the constant pressure to make the framework more general and the examples more specific.

To Daniel Obregon at ISTE+ASCD, who saw the manuscript at its messiest and asked the right questions about whom the book was for.

To Nina Harris, who teaches branding at Northeastern with me and reminded me, at the right moment, that the right answer to "trust the teacher" is not a policy paper but a position you can defend in three sentences in front of a school board.

To the readers of early drafts of individual chapters — you know who you are, and you know which paragraphs are better because of you.

To my students at Northeastern, present and former, who hold me accountable in the only way that matters: by being the people who will inherit whatever we build.

Any remaining errors of fact, of interpretation, or of judgment are mine.

## A Note on the Absent Index

This book does not include a printed index.

This is not an oversight. *Trust the Teacher* is published as a Kindle-native e-book and integrated with the [Medhavy](https://www.medhavy.com/) intelligent textbook platform. A printed index — pages alphabetized by topic — is a navigational technology built for the constraints of bound paper. ## Glossary

**AI+1 teacher.** A teacher whose practice is augmented by generative AI tools used under sustained professional training. The "+1" denotes the complementarity: the teacher's pedagogy is the substrate; the AI extends what she can do within a normal teaching week, not what she can scale to outside of one. Contrasted with the "AI replaces teacher" deployment pattern that Bastani 2025 showed to be harmful.

**Cognitive offloading.** The transfer of a cognitive task from the human to an external tool (a calculator, a search engine, an AI assistant). Useful in many contexts; harmful in learning contexts where the cognitive task itself is what was supposed to be acquired. See Risko & Gilbert (2016) for the canonical definition; see Bastani et al. (2025) for the most consequential empirical demonstration in education.

**The educational exemption.** The four words ("except for educational purposes") appearing in essentially every U.S. state phone-ban statute, defining which uses of personal devices remain permitted. As of this book's writing, no state has provided a substantive definition of the term. The book argues that the absence of definition is not a drafting oversight but a structural concession: legislatures cannot specify what an educational use is from outside the classroom, so they have implicitly handed the definition to the teacher in the room without providing the training that would let her exercise it well.

**The fluency trap.** The psychological illusion that processing smooth, well-structured AI output is the same as understanding what the output says. Documented across studies on metacognition and judgments of learning (Carpenter et al. 2013, Bjork & Bjork 1992 on desirable difficulties); central to Bastani et al.'s explanation of why unguided AI users felt they had learned more even as they had learned less.

**Implementation gap.** The persistent and historically durable gap between the educational technology a district purchases and the use that technology gets in classrooms. Common manifestation: licenses purchased, licenses unused. Common cause: teachers untrained on the platform, undersupported in deploying it, evaluated on metrics unrelated to its use.

**Inverted-U dosage curve.** The empirical pattern (documented in PIRLS, PISA, and the meta-analytic literature on educational technology) in which low and moderate technology use is associated with learning gains while high-dosage use is associated with declines. Implication: the policy question is not whether to use technology but how much, when, and for what — exactly the judgment the trained teacher is supposed to exercise.

**Phone Fridays.** A design pattern, not a documented program, for partial-week structured technology time inside a phone-ban framework. Devices in lockers Monday through Thursday; available under teacher direction on Friday for purposes the teacher judges educational. The book uses the term to illustrate the broader principle that bans and uses can coexist when bounded by professional discretion.

**The strategic frame** (or the central move). The recurring rhetorical structure used throughout the book: every empirical finding that the ban-everything camp wields against educational technology is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call about how the technology is deployed. The findings are real; the blanket conclusions are wrong because they presume there is no skilled human in the loop to adjust deployment for the classroom and learner in front of her. The book is about who should be making the call.

**The teacher variable.** The argument, defended in Chapter 4 and built on Hattie's *Visible Learning* synthesis and the value-added literature (Chetty/Friedman/Rockoff, Rivkin/Hanushek/Kain), that teacher quality and the conditions under which the teacher operates account for substantially more of the variance in student outcomes than any single technology, curriculum, or structural reform. Implication: investments in teacher capacity dominate, on cost-effectiveness, investments in nearly anything else.

**The three honest measures.** The framework proposed in Chapter 10 for evaluating teachers: student wellbeing, student learning gains, and teacher learning and improvement. The third measure is the absent precondition for the book's argument — without measuring whether teachers are improving, the AI+1 teacher cannot be recognized, rewarded, or scaled.

**The within-thirty argument.** The claim, developed across Chapters 5, 6, and 9, that the AI dividend (the time AI saves a trained teacher in a normal teaching week) is best reinvested into the students already in front of her — the existing class of roughly twenty-five to thirty students — rather than used to scale her to additional students. The high-effect-size interventions in education are relational and do not scale past the optimal classroom size; AI does not change this and should not be deployed as if it did.
