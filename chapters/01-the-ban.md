# Chapter 1: The Ban

*Why removing the phone was right, what removing the phone alone cannot do, and who pays when we stop there*

---

In the first year of a Florida school district's bell-to-bell phone ban, suspensions for Black students rose by roughly sixteen percent. White and Hispanic students did not absorb a comparable spike. By year two, test scores rose for every group the researchers tracked. Unexcused absences fell. The aggregate verdict was clear: the policy was working.

That is the finding from David Figlio and Umut Özek's [National Bureau of Economic Research working paper 34388](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34388), the most rigorous causal analysis of a U.S. state phone ban yet published. The setting is one of the ten largest school districts in the country. The method is difference-in-differences with student-level administrative records. The headline that has traveled — *bans work* — is not wrong. The footnote that has not traveled is this: the year-one disciplinary cost was racially patterned, and it fell on the students who could least afford it. The [NBER digest summary](https://www.nber.org/digest/202512/school-cell-phone-bans-and-student-achievement) reports the sixteen percent short-term increase in suspensions for Black students. Secondary coverage in the [*Boston Globe*](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/10/23/nation/cellphone-use-in-schools-limited-without-academic-gains/) has characterized the disparity more sharply, with some references to subgroup effects nearer thirty percent for Black boys specifically. [verify: race × sex subgroup table needs direct check against Figlio & Özek WP 34388 — the digest cites Black students overall at sixteen percent; the boy-specific figure circulating in press summaries should be confirmed against the published heterogeneity tables before going to print.]

Either number tells the same story. A policy that delivers benefits over two years also delivers costs in the first one, and those costs are not distributed evenly. A student suspended out of a critical semester does not get those weeks back when the test-score gains arrive a year later. For her, *later* is not the same as *yes*.

Hold those two findings together, because they are the chapter in miniature. The aggregate gain is real. The distributional cost is also real. They sit in the same paper, on adjacent pages, and most of the public conversation about the ban has cited the first and skipped the second. The skipping is not accidental. The political coalition that built the 2025 ban wave needs the aggregate gain. It does not, politically, need the year-one suspension surge. So the digest gets cited, the press release writes itself, and the question of who absorbs the year-one cost gets handled — when it gets handled at all — as a regrettable footnote rather than as a design problem the policy could have been built to address. It could have been. It mostly was not.

This is not an argument against the ban. The ban is right. It is an argument about what bans, by themselves, cannot do — and about what we have systematically refused to do instead.

---

## Two answers, one missing variable

Education policy in 2025 produced two answers to the same question. The question was: technology is harming students. What do we do?

Answer one came from state legislatures. More than thirty states had enacted some version of a K-12 phone restriction by spring 2026 — Kansas became the [thirty-third state in March 2026](https://news.ballotpedia.org/2026/03/26/kansas-becomes-thirty-third-state-to-enact-a-k-12-cellphone-ban/), New Jersey and others were mid-process, and twenty-two of the bell-to-bell statutes were passed in 2025 alone, according to the [Civic IQ legislative tracker](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/10/26-states-now-require-school-cell-phone-bans-2026-k-12-policy-tracker-implementation-guide/). The count is moving fast. The shape of the policy is settled: remove the device, restore the classroom.

Answer two came from the educational technology industry. A $165 billion global market had a different prescription — don't ban technology, buy better technology. The right adaptive learning platform. The right AI tutor. The right dashboard. Trust the algorithm.

These two answers look like opposites. They are not. They share a flaw. Neither of them is about teachers.

A great teacher trained in how to use AI purposefully will outperform any expensive educational technology platform — and her presence determines whether a ban produces gains or absences, whether a tool teaches or substitutes, whether a hundred million dollars in device spending shows up in student learning or in a closet. That claim is the argument of the book this chapter opens. The claim of this chapter is narrower. It is about the first answer: the ban. The ban is the correct response to a documented harm. It is also a policy that lands on top of a system already failing the students it was most supposed to help. The chapter holds both.

## What the evidence on classroom phones actually supports

I want to draw a line through the middle of a fight that the public conversation has been having badly. There are two questions, and the second one is doing most of the political work, but it is the first one that justifies the ban.

The first question is whether personal smartphones disrupt classroom attention and degrade academic performance. The answer is yes, and the evidence is good. The [Beland and Murphy 2016 study](https://www.lpbeland.com/uploads/7/8/7/5/7875420/lpblabour_1-s2.0-s0927537116300136-main.pdf) of ninety-one English secondary schools, published in *Labour Economics*, found that test scores for sixteen-year-olds rose by about 6.4 percent of a standard deviation after phone bans were enacted. The bottom-quintile students gained 14.23 percent — more than twice the average effect. The Figlio and Özek paper extends the finding to the U.S. context with administrative-data rigor. Common Sense Media's 2023 [*Constant Companion* study](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/2023-cs-smartphone-research-report_final-for-web.pdf) tracked teen phone use directly and found the median teenager picking up the phone fifty-one times a day, with a median forty-three minutes of phone use during school hours and about twenty-three percent of all notifications arriving during class time. The high end of that distribution exceeded four hundred and ninety-eight pickups per day. [Pew Research's December 2024 *Teens, Social Media and Technology* report](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2024/12/12/teens-social-media-and-technology-2024/) finds teens averaging roughly five hours a day on smartphones overall, with about seventy minutes during the school day. These are not contested numbers. The phone is in the room, it is in the hand, and it is taking the attention the school is paying the teacher to capture.

Hold on the Beland and Murphy bottom-quintile result for a moment, because it does work the chapter needs. The students whose test scores rose most when phones were removed from English classrooms were the students who were doing worst before the removal. Twice the average effect at the bottom of the distribution. That is a finding consistent with the hypothesis that low-achieving students are disproportionately distracted by ambient phone use — they have less developed self-regulation, fewer parental scaffolds around technology, and they are harmed most when the device is in the room. The ban, on this evidence, is *progressive* at the level of in-classroom learning. This matters. It is one of the strongest empirical claims I can make in this chapter. And the chapter still ends up arguing that the same policy, written as it has been written and implemented as it has been implemented, is *regressive* at the level of the school day as a whole. Both can be true at once. The in-classroom gain accrues to the student whose home, after the bell, has broadband and a laptop and a parent who can explain why the homework matters. The in-classroom gain *also* accrues to the student whose home has none of those — but for that student, the same policy that delivered the in-classroom gain also took the only out-of-classroom internet connection she had. The first finding is real. So is the second. The policy that produced the first did nothing to address the second.

The second question is whether smartphones cause the post-2012 adolescent mental health crisis. This is genuinely contested, and the public conversation routinely confuses it with the first. Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024) argues yes — that phone-based childhood is *the* primary cause, with four foundational harms (social deprivation, sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, addiction). His book did much of the political work that built the 2024–2025 ban coalition. [Candice Odgers' 2024 review in *Nature*](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-00902-2) argued that Haidt's central claim is "not supported by science" — that the evidence is largely correlational, that hundreds of researchers (Odgers, [Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0956797619830329), and others) have looked for the large effects Haidt posits and produced a mix of "no, small, and mixed associations" rather than the consistent, large effects a strong causal story would require. Reverse causation is plausible. Effect sizes are small. The dispute is technical and ongoing.

I will not adjudicate that dispute here. I do not need to. The classroom-attention case alone is enough to justify the policy. A phone disrupting a math lesson is doing measurable damage to the lesson whether or not it is also doing damage to the student's long-run mental health. Conflating the two evidence bases — using the mental-health frame to carry a classroom-attention policy — is a move that makes the ban look more settled than it is, and it is a move this chapter declines to make. The ban is justified on the in-school evidence alone. That is the strongest ground to stand on. It is also enough.

## What "phone ban" actually means

The phrase "phone ban" is used in the press as if it names one policy. It names a family of policies that vary on at least four axes: which devices are covered, when the prohibition is in effect, where the device must be stored, and what counts as an exception. The differences matter, and the press coverage hides them.

There is the bell-to-bell prohibition with physical storage — personal smartphones, smartwatches, and earbuds locked in a Yondr pouch, a school-owned locker, or a centralized cart from first bell to dismissal. Florida's HB 1105 (2025) and [New York's Phone-Free Schools Law](https://www.governor.ny.gov/eliminating-distractions-new-york-schools/new-york-state-phone-free-schools-law) (2025) approximate this model. There is the instructional-time-only prohibition — phones banned during class but permitted at lunch and between periods. Florida's original [HB 379](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379) (2023), before its 2025 expansion, worked this way. There is state guidance that *encourages* district policy without mandating any specific shape — Connecticut, Maine, Minnesota, New Mexico, Washington, all classified as "guidance" states in the [Ballotpedia state-by-state tracker](https://ballotpedia.org/State_policies_on_cellphone_use_in_K-12_public_schools). And there is the state mandate that *requires* every district to have *some* policy, leaving the substance to school boards — New Jersey's 2025 legislation operates partly this way.

Most reporters and most parents use "phone ban" to mean "phones are not allowed at school." This is almost never what the law actually says. Almost every law contains a phrase like "except for educational purposes," "except when authorized by a teacher," or "except for documented medical or IEP-related need." The exemption is structural, not incidental. Chapter 3 takes the exemption apart in detail. For now, the point is smaller: a teacher in a "phone ban" state in 2026 is not enforcing a ban. She is administering a conditional prohibition with a teacher-discretion exemption written into the conditions. The legislators wrote *what* the rule is. They left *when it doesn't apply* to her.

## What the ban removes and from whom

The phone ban is facially neutral. Every student surrenders the device. The same lockable pouch closes on the wealthy kid and the kid on free lunch alike. There is something appealing about this — a policy that finally doesn't discriminate, that holds the line for everyone, that refuses to sort by who can afford what.

This is the feeling. Here is the fact: a phone ban that does not guarantee quality technology access during school hours is not neutral policy. It is a transfer of educational resources away from the students who have the least and toward the students who have the most. It is regressive in the precise economic sense of that word — structured so that the burden falls heaviest at the bottom.

The mechanism is the home internet connection. The [National Center for Education Statistics](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cch/home-internet-access), drawing on the American Community Survey, reports that roughly six percent of three- to eighteen-year-olds have no home internet of any kind. The proportion is larger if you ask the question more strictly — among adults in households earning under thirty thousand dollars a year, Pew has found that fifty-seven percent have home broadband and twenty-seven percent reach the internet only through a smartphone [verify Pew current-year mobile fact sheet]. For roughly a quarter of low-income students, the phone is not *a* device. It is *the* device. The 1:1 Chromebook that the school sent home with her does not connect to the internet without a connection — which the phone was providing.

For the student with home broadband, a laptop, and parents who work in technology, the phone is a convenience. It is one of several devices she carries. When it goes into the pouch at 7:45, she still has access at home to everything the phone provided — the research tools, the AI tutor she started using last spring, the messaging channels, the homework help. The ban removes a distraction from her school day. It touches almost nothing else.

For the student whose phone was the household's primary internet connection, the ban is a different instrument entirely. The pouch closes and the only quality technology access she will have for the next sixteen hours goes with it. She goes home to no broadband, no laptop that can reach the internet, no school-issued device that follows her past the parking lot in any useful way. What she has surrendered does not come back until morning.

Then the federal government took the substitute away.

In September 2025, on a 2–1 vote, the FCC adopted an [Order on Reconsideration](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/) removing off-premises Wi-Fi hotspots and school-bus Wi-Fi from E-Rate eligibility. E-Rate is the federal program that subsidizes internet connectivity for schools and libraries, funded through the Universal Service Fund and authorized under Section 254 of the Communications Act. The 2024 expansion under the previous FCC had added off-premises connectivity to the program's scope. The 2025 majority read the same statute as not authorizing that expansion and rolled it back. During fiscal year 2025, schools and districts had requested $42.6 million for hotspots and $15.3 million for school-bus Wi-Fi; 1,762 libraries had applied for hotspot funding. The FCC's order directed the Universal Service Administrative Company to deny the pending requests, including for the 2025–26 school year already underway. The American Library Association called it "[one more way to limit access to information](https://www.ala.org/news/2025/09/ala-disappointed-fcc-takebacks-lack-due-process-decision-end-library-hotspots-school)" and noted the lack of due process in the reversal. [K-12 Dive](https://www.k12dive.com/news/fcc-removes-school-bus-wi-fi-hotspots-from-e-rate/761481/) and [StateScoop](https://statescoop.com/fcc-e-rate-funding-for-wifi-hotspots-school-bus-connectivity/) covered the order. The legal question — whether Section 254 reaches off-premises support — is contested and beyond the scope of this chapter. The effect is not.

There is a third subtraction layered underneath the first two, and it is worth naming because the 1:1 device programs that many states have been quietly proud of for a decade are doing less of the equity work than the brochures imply. Many of the same states enacting phone bans have, over the past decade, mandated 1:1 device programs — one school-issued laptop or tablet for every student, a direct-to-classroom equity intervention. California. Indiana. Florida. The logic was: if every student has a device, the advantage of personal ownership disappears. Universal access closes the gap. The phone ban exposes the limit of this logic. School-issued devices are school-tethered devices. They work in school. They go home, technically, but they go home without the broadband connection that makes them useful — which is exactly what the E-Rate hotspot programs were trying to address, and exactly what the FCC just stopped funding. The 1:1 device sitting on a low-income student's kitchen table without a broadband connection is a piece of plastic. It is not infrastructure. The personal phone, used as a hotspot, was the connection. Take the phone, take the hotspot funding, and the 1:1 device program reveals itself as having been quietly dependent on the very household connectivity the policy regime just removed.

The state phone bans and the FCC rescission were not coordinated. Different actors. Different institutional logics. Different stated rationales. They were not designed as a combined policy. Their combined effect is a double subtraction of internet access from the same students. The state removes the personal device that was the household's primary internet access; the federal government removes the school-provided substitute that was designed to fill exactly that gap. Both decisions arrived in the same policy window — late 2024 through fall 2025. Both fell hardest on the same demographic. Nobody designed the combined outcome. Nobody is accountable for it. This is not a conspiracy. It is the ordinary failure mode of policies made by different agencies for different ostensible reasons, landing on the same students, in the same year, without anybody pausing to add them up.

Imagine a family on a tight food budget. The state passes a law removing sugary drinks from school lunches; obesity rates drop; the policy is right on the merits. The same week, the USDA cuts the SNAP benefit. The two policies were not coordinated. The combined effect on the family that was using SNAP to buy juice — because the school lunch had the only real meal the kid would eat — is that the family now has neither the school lunch sugar nor the SNAP juice money. Each policy is defensible in isolation. The combination is regressive. The food example is one click off the technology one, but the structure is the same: when two facially neutral subtractions land on the same household, the cumulative effect is not the sum of the individual ones. It is larger, and it is sharper at the bottom.

## The history nobody consulted

New York City has run this experiment before. The lesson did not travel.

Under Mayor Michael Bloomberg, beginning in 2006, NYC public schools prohibited personal cell phones on school grounds. Enforcement was conducted primarily through metal detectors, which were standard equipment in low-income high schools and rare in wealthy ones. Students at metal-detector schools either left their phones at home, paid bodegas a dollar a day to store them, or risked confiscation. Students at non-metal-detector schools — wealthier schools, by and large — carried their phones in their bags as before. The policy was facially neutral. Its application was sharply unequal.

In March 2015, Mayor Bill de Blasio rescinded the ban. The [stated rationale](https://www.nyc.gov/office-of-the-mayor/news/013-15/mayor-de-blasio-chancellor-fari-a-lift-school-cell-phone-ban) combined three threads. The first was the equity finding: the ban fell harder on low-income schools because the enforcement mechanisms were concentrated there. The second was parental communication — explicitly a post-Columbine, post-Sandy Hook concern that families wanted a working channel to their kids during the school day. The third was, in de Blasio's own framing, that the ban was "[out of touch with modern parenting](https://time.com/3657860/new-york-city-schools-cellphones/)."

In 2025, Governor Kathy Hochul signed New York's statewide Phone-Free Schools Law. New York City committed [$29 million to implementation](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2025/07/16/nyc-school-cell-phone-ban-funding-kathy-hochul-yondr/), much of it for Yondr-style pouches. The 2025 legislation does not address the structural inequity that brought down the 2006 ban. It does not require equity audits of enforcement. It does not mandate alternative parental communication channels. It does not require districts to provide supervised technology access to compensate for what the ban removes from low-income students' after-school hours. The decade-old reversal is a natural experiment the 2025 legislators chose not to consult. The historical record was available, well-documented, and on point. It was not the variable that determined what the new law said.

The pouch budgets are worth dwelling on for a moment, because they show what the policy is actually paying for. The Los Angeles Unified School District [contracted with Yondr for $5.2 million](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/consumer/schools-spend-millions-yondr-pouches-store-cellphones-rcna230454) in 2023–24 to roll out phone pouches across middle and high schools. Implementation problems emerged within months — students used magnets purchased on Amazon to defeat the pouches, [carried "burner" phones](https://edsource.org/2025/students-easily-subvert-lausd-phone-ban/730127) that went into the pouch while their real phone stayed in a pocket, or broke the pouch with sustained banging against a desk. Cincinnati Public Schools spent roughly half a million dollars on pouches for grades 7–12 and reported durability issues within the first year. Houston ISD allocated [$800,000 for phone storage solutions](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/education/2025/08/01/527760/houston-isd-announces-student-cell-phone-policy-to-comply-with-new-state-law/) in 2025. I do not lean on the pouch-defeat stories — students find workarounds is not a strong argument against the ban itself, and the workarounds will be patched, replaced, or simply lived with. The argument I do want to make is about what the pouch budget *displaces*. LAUSD's $5.2 million for pouches is approximately the cost of providing sustained AI-specific professional development for roughly two thousand teachers at the unit cost the [WestEd ASSISTments work](https://www.wested.org/) suggests is plausible [verify per-teacher PD figure against SETDA / WestEd at draft time]. The pouch line item is the teacher-training line item that was not spent. That is the trade, made silently, at the level of district procurement, with no district leader I am aware of having articulated it that way out loud. It is the trade the rest of this book is asking you to undo.

What changed between 2015 and 2025 is not the underlying equity dynamics — those have, if anything, worsened. What changed is the political coalition. The phone ban movement in 2025 is bipartisan, parent-driven, and backed by a body of international research that makes restriction easy to defend. This is enough to pass legislation. It is not, by itself, enough to make legislation just.

## What the ban also is: a statement about teachers

There is one more thing the ban is doing, and it is the move the rest of this book is about.

A rule written into statute is, among other things, a statement about who can be trusted to make a particular decision. When the legislature writes a rule that removes a class of decisions from the people closest to those decisions, the legislature is saying — explicitly or by implication — that those people cannot be trusted with the call.

This statement, applied to teachers and technology, has a defensible reading. Teacher technology decisions have been historically inconsistent and sometimes disastrous. The kindergarten teacher who lets students watch CoComelon during morning meeting and the AP chemistry teacher who treats Khan Academy as the curriculum are both "exercising teacher discretion" over technology, and the variance between them is enormous. Some students get scaffolded, intentional use. Some get cognitive babysitting. Some get a Chromebook running an unrestricted YouTube tab and a teacher grading papers in the back of the room. A statewide rule that removes the personal smartphone from the classroom is, among other things, a partial correction for this variance — a floor, beneath which no teacher can drop.

The defensible reading is correct as far as it goes. The trouble is that the rule does not eliminate the judgment call. It relocates it.

Every phone ban law in America contains an exemption for educational purposes. Florida's HB 379 prohibits use during instructional time "[unless directed to do so by a teacher for educational purposes](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379)." Indiana permits exceptions "for educational purposes or emergencies"; [pending Indiana SB 78](https://indianacapitalchronicle.com/2025/12/10/away-for-the-day-indiana-lawmakers-take-up-bill-expanding-cellphone-ban-to-entire-school-day/) would tighten the exception to school-issued devices only. New York authorizes use "by a teacher, principal, or the school district for a specific educational purpose." Arizona permits teachers to allow access to social media "to the extent necessary for educational purposes." Not one of the laws defines the term operationally. The teacher who could not be trusted to keep phones away from her students at 9:15 a.m. is now trusted, with no additional training, to decide what counts as "an educational purpose" justifying their reintroduction at 10:30 a.m. The statutory architecture removes professional judgment in the abstract and reinstates it in the particulars — without investing in the professional's capacity to exercise it well.

Try to apply your own district's exemption to three specific cases. A student is using Khan Academy on her phone during study hall to practice a math problem the textbook didn't have enough examples of. A student is using TikTok in study hall to watch a "5 tips for writing your college essay" video posted by a college admissions counselor with credentials. A student is using ChatGPT on her phone to summarize the chapter she didn't read. All three fall under "educational purposes" by some reading. None of them are obviously what the legislature meant. The teacher down the hall will produce three different answers from yours. So will the teacher next to her. The variance the statute was supposed to eliminate has been re-installed at the level of the exemption — without the training that would let two teachers reach the same defensible answer.

This is not a problem any rule can solve. The distinction between technology that extends a student's thinking and technology that substitutes for it is not in the device, the application, or even the assignment. It is in what the student's mind is doing while her hands are on the keyboard. No legislator can write a rule specific enough to distinguish these in real time, across every classroom, for every student, in every lesson. The only mechanism that can make the call is a trained professional in the room exercising professional judgment — one who has been specifically prepared to see what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like and what substitution looks like, and who can tell them apart at the speed a classroom moves.

The legislative move borrowed half of medicine's regulatory model. It took the rule and skipped the training. Doctors are also closest to clinical decisions, and they are also subject to standardized rules — but the rules are written *with* the profession, exceptions require trained judgment, and continuing education is mandatory. Fifty hours a year. License renewal tied to demonstrated learning. Specialization updates when practice changes significantly. Nobody argues that doctors should figure out new treatments on their own. The infrastructure for continuous professional development exists in medicine. It does not exist for teaching at any comparable scale. Chapter 8 develops what borrowing the *other* half of medicine's model would look like. Chapter 1 only needs to name the gap: the phone ban is, structurally, a statute that distrusts teachers in the abstract and depends on them in the particulars. That is a structurally unstable place to leave the policy.

## The pattern the rest of the book makes visible

There is a pattern in the empirical case for the phone ban that recurs across every piece of evidence in the EdTech debate, and I want to name it once here so you can see it for what it is when the later chapters return to it.

Every finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call.

Beland and Murphy's English study found classroom gains from banning phones. The implicit comparison is to classrooms where phones were not banned — meaning classrooms where no adult was deciding when the phone helped and when it hurt. Figlio and Özek's Florida study found the same. The PISA data Jared Cooney Horvath cites — students using computers six-plus hours daily score sixty-six points lower — describe what happens to learning when screen exposure is ambient and unmediated. The Common Sense Media notification data describe what happens when no one is filtering. The literature is consistent on this point, and the consistency has been read by the ban-everything camp as evidence that the technology itself is the problem. That is one reading. The other reading is that *nobody being in charge of the technology* is the problem, and that the trained professional whose job it is to be in charge of the classroom is exactly the missing variable. The data do not yet measure what happens when the call is made well, because we have not yet trained the people who would make it well. That study has not been run because we have not built the cohort that would let us run it.

So when this book argues, in the chapters that follow, that the teacher is the variable that determines whether technology helps or hurts, it is not arguing against the screen-time correlations. It is taking them seriously. The correlations describe a real failure mode. They describe the failure mode that arises when the device and the student are alone in a room with each other and no adult is exercising professional judgment about whether what is happening between them counts as learning. The ban is a structural correction for the worst version of this failure mode — the smartphone, optimized for distraction, weaponized for attention capture, monetized for habit formation. The ban is right because the call cannot be made *for* this device in any classroom by any teacher under any training regime. Removing it is the cleanest correction available.

But removing the worst device does not by itself produce the trained teacher who can make the call about the *next* device — the Chromebook, the AI tutor, the school-issued tablet, the writing-aid that might be cognitive scaffolding or might be cognitive substitution depending on how it's deployed. The ban subtracts. It does not build. And the FCC rescission did not build either. And the $29 million NYC spent on Yondr pouches did not build either. And the $5.2 million LAUSD spent on the same did not build either. The pouch budget is approximately the size of the teacher-training budget that was not spent. Trade one for the other and you get the policy this book argues for. Spend the first and not the second, and you get the policy we have.

## The chapter's turn

The phone ban is the correct response to a documented harm. The classroom-attention evidence supports it. The bipartisan coalition behind it is right that personal smartphones, designed as they are designed, do not belong in the room where the lesson is supposed to be happening. The first answer of 2025 — remove the device — is a defensible answer.

But the ban is not the answer to the underlying question. The underlying question is what should happen between students and technology during the school day, and the answer to that question requires professional judgment exercised in real time by an adult who has been trained to exercise it. The ban removes the worst case. It does not produce the judgment. And in the same policy window, while states were removing the device, the federal government was removing the substitute — pulling the after-school internet rung out from under the students who lost the most when the school-day device went into the pouch. Two facially neutral subtractions, landing on the same household, in the same year, with nobody adding them up.

The ban is not the problem. What we did instead of training the teacher is the problem. The first answer of 2025 — the device-ban answer — was a structural correction that left the teacher's capacity untouched. The second answer of 2025 — the buy-better-software answer the next chapter takes apart — left the teacher's capacity untouched too. Two answers, the same flaw. Neither of them is about teachers. The book that begins here is about the third answer, which has not yet been seriously tried at the scale the evidence demands. It begins by accepting what the ban gets right and naming what the ban, by itself, cannot do.

The ban took the backup. It did not fix the school. Fixing the school requires investing in the people who run it — not banning their tools and not replacing them with better ones. Training them. Trusting them. Giving them what medicine gives doctors: the expectation of continuous learning as a condition of professional practice. The educational exemption every phone ban writes into law is the legislature handing the question back to the teacher and saying *you decide*. Whether she can decide well depends entirely on whether anyone has helped her learn how. So far, almost no one has.

That is what the ban debate is not yet about. That is what it should be.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The Figlio and Özek paper's race × sex heterogeneity needs direct verification against the published tables; the "thirty percent for Black boys, near zero for white and Hispanic students" framing that has circulated in some press coverage is sharper than what the NBER digest reports, and the chapter's opening should not be allowed to overshoot the paper. The strong-effects causal claim about smartphones and the adolescent mental health crisis — the [Haidt/Twenge](https://jonathanhaidt.com/anxious-generation/) versus [Odgers/Orben/Przybylski](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-00902-2) dispute — is not resolved by the existing literature, and the policy case I have made here does not rest on resolving it. And the question of whether the long-run academic gains from bell-to-bell prohibition hold in districts with weaker enforcement is genuinely open; the Florida finding comes from a district that locked phones in backpacks all day, and the same finding may not replicate where the policy is looser. I would rather name these uncertainties than wave them away. The argument I am making does not need them resolved to land. It does require honesty about what we do not yet know.

## Still puzzling

Why does the political coalition that built the 2025 ban wave so reliably stop short of funding the supervised technology access the ban removes from low-income students? The pouch line item is easy to pass; the after-school-access line item is, repeatedly, not. What is the failure mode of the coalition, and is it fixable?

Why did the FCC rescission and the state phone bans land in the same policy window with no coordinating analysis between them? Is there an institutional mechanism that *could* have added the two policies up — a federal-state EdTech equity review, an OMB cross-cut, anything — and if so, why didn't it run?

Why has no state, in the 2024–2026 wave, written its phone ban *with* a defined "educational purposes" clause attached? The teacher discretion the exemption requires is a known unknown at the time the law is written. Why is no legislature treating it as a known unknown that the law should help solve?

What would a phone ban look like if it were written by teachers — not consulted, written?

## What would change my mind

A district that paired bell-to-bell phone prohibition with funded, supervised, teacher-led technology access during school hours for students without home broadband, *and* that showed two years later either (a) no equity dividend over a district that did the ban alone, or (b) no academic dividend over a district that did neither. Either would tell me the case for the third answer — the trained-teacher answer — is weaker than I think.
