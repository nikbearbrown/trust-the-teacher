# Chapter 11: The Equity Argument

*Why every problem in this book lands hardest on the students who can least afford it — and what that means for where the investment should go*

---

The students absorbing the disciplinary costs of the phone ban's first year are Black boys in high-suspension schools. The students losing their only after-school internet access are those in families without home broadband. The students whose schools made no AI training investment for their teachers are in the lowest-income districts. The students most likely to encounter generative AI without a trained adult in the room are the students in classrooms where no adult has been trained. None of these distributions is an accident. None of them is a coincidence. Every one of them is the predictable result of a facially neutral policy landing on an unequal field.

Read the four sentences again and notice what they share. They are not claims about technology. They are claims about distribution. The policy in each case — the bell-to-bell phone ban, the FCC E-Rate rescission, the unequal allocation of Title II-A dollars, the haphazard arrival of generative AI in classrooms without preparation — is the same policy for every student in the country. The harm is not. The harm is concentrated, with measurable severity, in the same demographic categories that already absorb most of American education's pre-existing inequities. The chapter ahead is about why this keeps happening, what the evidence actually shows about how AI tools behave under the conditions in which they would have to behave to make it stop happening, and what a district leader sitting in front of a budget on Monday morning can do about it.

I want to make the chapter's central claim before the chapter argues for it, because the claim is easily mistaken for two arguments I am not making.

The equity argument is not that AI is bad for low-income students. It is also not that AI is, by itself, the equalizer the vendor marketing material claims. The argument is narrower and harder. It is that every policy failure documented in the first ten chapters of this book — the unwritten exemption, the untrained teacher, the device without training, the dividend without distribution, the offloading without scaffold — lands disproportionately on the students who were already most vulnerable, and that the affirmative response that fits the evidence is *more* high-quality AI-supported teaching in low-income schools, not less, provided the teaching is what gets funded. This is the same shape as the central argument of the book — the trained teacher is the variable — applied to the population for whom the variable matters most.

The reason this is a hard argument to make is that the easy moves in both directions are available and wrong. The easy move on the left is to argue that AI is widening gaps and should therefore be deployed less in poor schools. The easy move on the right is to argue that AI is a free equalizer that will fix the gaps if we just let it. Both are forms of the same mistake: they treat AI as if it were the actor. The actor is the teacher. The chapter's job is to keep the focus there.

## The four harms, named together

There are four specific harm vectors documented across the earlier chapters of this book. Each is well-sourced on its own. The equity chapter does not add new evidence about any of them. It does something the rest of the book has been doing one chapter at a time and that no chapter has yet done all at once: it puts the four next to each other and names the population they overlap on.

**One: the year-one disciplinary surge.** [David Figlio and Umut Özek's NBER Working Paper 34388](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34388) reports a roughly sixteen percent short-term increase in suspensions for Black students in the first year of a Florida district's bell-to-bell phone ban, dissipating by year two as the test-score gains arrived. Secondary press coverage has characterized the disparity more sharply at the boy-specific level, with figures circulating closer to thirty percent for Black boys against near-zero for white and Hispanic students. [verify: race × sex subgroup breakdown against the published heterogeneity tables; the chapter falls back to "sixteen percent for Black students overall, concentrated in male students per the heterogeneity analysis" if the boy-specific number does not appear in the working paper.] Either number tells the same story. The benefits arrive evenly in year two. The costs arrive unevenly in year one. The pre-existing distribution of school disciplinary practice in the United States is already heavily racialized — Black students are suspended at roughly [three times the rate of white students](https://www2.ed.gov/about/offices/list/ocr/letters/colleague-201401-title-vi.html) for comparable infractions, per Department of Education Office for Civil Rights data, and the [GAO 2018 review](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-18-258) confirms the pattern at the national scale. A new enforcement category, applied through the same disciplinary apparatus, produces the same disparity at higher volume. The phone ban did not create this distribution. It interacts with it. Chapter 1 made the in-school case for the ban. The equity-chapter point is downstream of it: an at-risk twelfth-grader suspended out of a critical semester does not get those weeks back when the test-score gains arrive a year later. For her, *later* is not the same as *yes*.

**Two: the device that was infrastructure.** The [Pew Research Center's most recent measurement](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/01/08/internet-use-smartphone-ownership-digital-divides-in-u-s/) finds that about a third of adults in U.S. households earning under $30,000 a year are smartphone-dependent — they have a smartphone, no home broadband subscription. The comparable figure for households earning above $100,000 is four percent. Only fifty-seven percent of households below $30,000 subscribe to home broadband, versus ninety-five percent above $100,000. The [NCES *Condition of Education* indicator on Children's Internet Access at Home](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cch/home-internet-access), drawing on American Community Survey data, reports that about four percent of three- to eighteen-year-olds reach the internet only through a smartphone and another three percent have no home internet at all, with the smartphone-only and no-access shares concentrated in low-income households. [verify: pull the most recent ACS poverty-specific subset figures and replace the general percentages with the poverty-specific cuts before press.] [Common Sense Media's analyses](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/kids-action/publications/closing-the-k-12-digital-divide-in-the-age-of-distance-learning) put the K-12 homework gap at roughly fifteen to sixteen million students, with about four hundred thousand teachers themselves caught in it. For roughly one in four students from low-income households, the phone is not *a* device. It is *the* device. The school's 1:1 Chromebook that the district sent home does not connect to the internet without a connection, and the phone, used as a hotspot, was the connection. The ban removes a distraction from her school day; it also removes the household's only path to the internet at 3:00 p.m.

Then the federal government took the substitute away. The September 30, 2025 FCC [Order on Reconsideration](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/) removed off-premises Wi-Fi hotspots and school-bus Wi-Fi from E-Rate eligibility. FY2025 schools and districts had requested $42.6 million for hotspots and $15.3 million for bus Wi-Fi; 1,762 libraries had applied. The state legislatures and the FCC were not coordinating. Two facially neutral subtractions, landing on the same household, in the same year, with nobody adding them up. Chapter 1 named this *the double subtraction.* The equity-chapter version of it is shorter: the state took the device and the federal government took the substitute, and the students caught in the overlap are the students for whom the device was infrastructure rather than convenience.

**Three: training inequity on top of teacher-quality inequity.** The State Educational Technology Directors Association's [November 2025 report](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf) on Title II-A spending — based on research with twenty-four state education agencies and seventy-six local education agencies — found that over sixty percent of Title II-A funds were spent on professional development in 2022–23, but only thirty-nine percent of districts used any of those funds for technology-related PD, only five percent listed technology as one of their two largest PD expenditures, and only nine states formally prioritized Title II-A for educator training on integrating technology and AI. [EdWeek's coverage](https://www.edweek.org/technology/billions-of-federal-dollars-are-spent-on-teacher-training-less-than-half-goes-to-tech-pd/2025/11) of the same report puts the topline plainly: of the roughly $2.2 billion-per-year Title II-A stream, less than half of the technology-relevant spend is going to technology PD. SETDA's name for the gap is *the digital design divide* — the gap between districts that give teachers the time and the coaching infrastructure to design lessons using digital tools and the districts that buy the device and call the job done. The 2025 report is explicit that the digital design divide correlates with district resources. The high-resource districts can absorb the planning time, the substitute coverage, the consultant fees, the ongoing coaching cycles. The low-resource districts cannot.

This inequality does not arrive in a flat field. It arrives on top of an existing teacher-quality distribution that is already tilted against low-income students. [Goldhaber, Lavery, and Theobald (2015), in *Educational Researcher*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0013189X15592622), examined every measure of teacher quality they could pull from Washington State administrative data — experience, licensure exam scores, value-added — and found virtually every measure inequitably distributed against disadvantaged students. The [Clotfelter, Ladd, and Vigdor literature](https://scholarship.law.duke.edu/faculty_scholarship/1915/) on teacher sorting reaches the same conclusion at the national scale. The teachers most likely to receive AI training are working in the schools whose teachers were, on average, already the more credentialed, more experienced, longer-tenured, lower-turnover practitioners. The teachers least likely to receive AI training are working in the schools whose teachers were already the less credentialed, the less experienced, the higher-turnover, the more burned out. The new inequality compounds the old one.

**Four: cognitive offloading where the scaffolding is thinnest.** [Hamsa Bastani and colleagues' PNAS paper](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) on the high-school math RCT — about a thousand Turkish high school students, three arms — is the cleanest causal evidence we have on what happens when a generative-AI tool is deployed without scaffolding. GPT Base practice gains of forty-eight percent; GPT Tutor practice gains of one hundred twenty-seven percent; and, when AI access was removed for the exam, a seventeen percent decrement in the unguarded GPT Base arm relative to control. Bastani's own pre-registered moderator analysis on ability, resources, and effort found limited statistically significant heterogeneity in the unassisted exam-performance effect. The chapter must say this plainly: Bastani shows the *average* harm of unguarded access. He does not show, as a direct empirical finding, that the harm falls hardest on low-self-regulation students.

The inference that the harm falls hardest on the students least equipped to navigate it does not come from Bastani. It is a chain built from three independent literatures, and the chapter has to make the chain visible. [Evans and Schamberg (2009) in *PNAS*](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0811910106) documented that childhood poverty is inversely related to working memory in young adults, with the relationship mediated by elevated chronic stress — allostatic load — across the years of childhood exposure. [Clancy Blair and Cybele Raver's program of work](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1876285916000267) on poverty, stress, and brain development extends the finding: chronic exposure to poverty-related stressors alters the neurobiological systems supporting executive function, including the self-regulation systems that allow a learner to resist the in-tool shortcut and stay with the effortful work of schema construction. The third leg is the cognitive-science account of what the unguarded GPT environment is actually doing — providing a fluent, frictionless answer that produces the in-tool feeling of competence without the underlying schema, and asking the learner to choose between the effortful path and the shortcut at every problem. Bastani's average finding plus the SES-self-regulation literature plus the cognitive-science account of why fluent answers produce shortcut behavior gives you the conclusion the chapter wants: the unguarded version of this tool is most dangerous to the students for whom the cost of taking the shortcut compounds across the rest of the day, the rest of the year, and the rest of the developmental window. The chain is sound. It is a chain of inferences, not a single replicated finding. The chapter says so out loud so the reader can examine each link.

Four harms. Look at them next to each other. The disciplinary surge in year one. The home-internet subtraction. The unequal training pipeline on top of an unequal teacher distribution. The unguarded cognitive offloading falling where self-regulation has been most strained by chronic stress. They are not the same harm. They do not come from the same actor. They were not coordinated. They land on overlapping populations. They compound. *Compounding* is not metaphor; it is the empirical pattern of independent harms summing on a population whose capacity to absorb them is already below the mean. This is the structural shape of every harm vector in this book. Nobody designed the combined outcome. Nobody is accountable for it. The students absorb the sum.

## The affirmative case — what the evidence actually argues *for*

Here is where the chapter refuses the easy move. The easy move, having documented the compounding harm, is to argue that AI is widening gaps and therefore should be deployed less in low-income schools. The actual evidence does something different. It identifies a specific deployment pattern — trained teacher plus AI as instrument — and shows, across three independent professions and the most relevant in-domain study we have, that this deployment pattern produces its largest gains where the operator started furthest below the frontier.

Start with the in-domain proof. The Stanford team of [Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, and Demszky](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017) — *Tutor CoPilot*, the cluster RCT of nine hundred tutors and eighteen hundred students on the FEV Tutor platform — found a four-percentage-point improvement in overall mastery from real-time AI suggestions delivered to the tutor in the conversation, and a *nine-percentage-point* improvement for students paired with lower-rated tutors. The cost was about twenty dollars per tutor per year. Analysis of the 350,000-plus messages in the trial showed the tool increasing the use of probing questions and reducing the rate of generic praise. The student never saw the AI. The tutor remained the decision-making professional. The AI sat under her elbow in real time, suggesting moves she could accept, modify, or ignore. The tool augmented her practice; it did not replace her.

The equity reading is encoded in the heterogeneity result. Lower-rated tutors disproportionately serve under-resourced communities — they are the less-experienced, less-credentialed, lower-paid practitioners that under-funded tutoring programs can afford. The Tutor CoPilot finding is the cleanest available demonstration in K-12 academic context of what happens when the AI is paired with the practitioner at the lower end of the skill distribution. The tool levels the floor.

The same shape appears in [Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's *Generative AI at Work*](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161), the 5,200-agent customer-support study published as NBER WP 31161 and in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*: an average fourteen-percent productivity gain; about thirty-four percent for the bottom quartile of pre-AI performance; near-zero at the top. Same shape again in [Lan and Ban's 2025 meta-analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/17/17/8013) of precision-agriculture adoption — eighty-five empirical studies, 1,472 farm observations — where the gains in ROI, net profit, and nitrogen-use efficiency were concentrated in farms operating below the efficiency frontier, with already-efficient large-U.S.-farm gains near zero. Three independent literatures, three different professions, one consistent shape. Augmentation lifts the bottom.

These are professions, not classrooms. The generalization step is not automatic; the chapter's confidence in the analogy is bounded by it. But the convergence is strong enough to do the work it has to do. The pattern is not "AI is good for the strong." It is "AI is good for the trained, and especially for the trained who started furthest from the frontier."

And the affirmative case has a second front that I want to put on the page now, because it is the front the popular conversation most often misses. Even within the EdTech evidence base that Jared Cooney Horvath's *The Digital Delusion* assembles — the same evidence base whose averages he uses to argue for near-total elimination — the cases that survive his own meta-cleaning are the cases that matter most for the students this chapter is about. Intelligent tutoring systems sit at an effect size of about +0.52. Assistive technology for students with learning disabilities sits at about +0.61. Blended learning sits at about +0.61. Writing-instruction tools sit at about +0.81. [Pellegrini's 2025 meta-analysis](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/) on EdTech for disadvantaged students reports a pooled effect of about +0.20 — below the Hattie hinge that *The Digital Delusion* uses as a bright line, but positive, significant, and aggregated over millions of students. Some computer-assisted learning programs targeted at low-SES math have produced effects as large as +0.82 in particular implementations. [verify: pull the specific Pellegrini 2025 citation, the assistive-tech meta-analysis citation, and the CAL low-SES math figure to primary sources before press.] These numbers are not the message either. The message is what the numbers describe at the level of an actual student.

Read the effect-size numbers as effect-size numbers and you can dismiss them as mediocre by the bright-line standard. Read them at the level of a student who depends on the intervention, and the framing collapses. *For a student with dyslexia, text-to-speech software is not a 0.20-ES mediocre intervention. It is the difference between reading and not reading.* For a rural student with no AP courses in her school, an online-learning program is not a 0.29-ES weak intervention. It is the difference between college-ready and not. For a high school student in an under-resourced math classroom with a tutor she sees twice a week, AI-augmented coaching is not a 0.36-ES marginal intervention. It is the difference between the algebra credit she earns and the one she does not. The Hattie hinge applied to these populations does not classify their interventions as inadequate. It classifies the populations themselves as not worth counting. That is what an arbitrary threshold does to a class of intervention that benefits specific subgroups rather than averages: it makes the subgroups invisible. The corrected reading is straightforward. Effect-size thresholds were never designed to score subgroup-specific interventions whose value to a particular learner is the only metric that matters to her. The aggregate hides the population the chapter is most concerned with.

The investment that matters most in under-resourced schools, then, is not more hardware. The hardware-only ratio is what produced every harm vector in §1. The investment that matters is the training that turns the teacher in the under-resourced school into the operator the catch-up literature describes — the practitioner whose AI-augmented gains will be largest precisely because her starting baseline is, on average, where Goldhaber et al. find it to be. *More trained teachers in low-income schools, with the AI tools their work requires, in the deployment pattern the evidence supports.* This is what the affirmative case actually argues for. It is also exactly what the SETDA data say is currently not being funded.

The dividend point from Chapter 6 lands here too, and it lands the same way. The most-cited estimate of the teacher AI time-savings effect — about six hours a week of administrative and prep work reclaimed by teachers who use AI weekly — appears in the [Walton Family Foundation–Gallup educators survey](https://www.gallup.com/analytics/645445/walton-aieducation-report.aspx) and survives roughly across replications. The dividend exists where the training existed. In districts where no AI training was provided, the dividend is near zero. The same federal Title II-A dollar that produces a six-hour weekly dividend in a trained suburban district produces no dividend in an untrained urban one. The two districts are not playing the same game. The dividend itself is regressive when the training that produces it is unequally distributed.

## The strongest counter-arguments, engaged seriously

The chapter would be doing a disservice if it pretended the case against this argument were weak. It is not weak. There are two serious counter-positions in the current literature, and each has to be answered on its own terms.

The first is the *AI will widen gaps* literature. [Brookings has been the most consistent voice in this register](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/ai-and-the-next-digital-divide-in-education/) — Darrell West and colleagues, the Brookings Global Task Force on AI in Education, the synthesis pieces on the digital divide and on bias in school AI deployments. The [U.S. Commission on Civil Rights' November 2024 report](https://www.usccr.gov/files/2024-12/2024-ai-in-education.pdf) on AI in K-12 concludes that AI in schools "has the potential to reinforce patterns of discrimination and disparate impact faster, more efficiently, and in a way that is less transparent than previously seen." The [Stanford Center for Racial Justice's June 2024 analysis](https://law.stanford.edu/2024/06/29/how-will-ai-impact-racial-disparities-in-education/) identifies the specific predictive-analytics risk: models trained to minimize prediction errors overall may over-identify African American and Hispanic students as "at-risk" and route them to lower-expectation interventions. The longer scholarly tradition behind these reports — [Safiya Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression*](https://nyupress.org/9781479837243/algorithms-of-oppression/), [Ruha Benjamin's *Race After Technology*](https://politybooks.com/bookdetail/?isbn=9781509526406), [Cathy O'Neil's *Weapons of Math Destruction*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/241363/weapons-of-math-destruction-by-cathy-oneil/), the newer education-specific work on algorithmic bias in adaptive systems — names mechanisms the chapter has to take seriously: training data encoded with historical bias produces biased predictions; adaptive systems trained on disengagement signals can route low-engagement students to easier content rather than to better instruction; "personalization" at scale, deployed without pedagogical scrutiny, becomes another way of tracking students into lower expectations.

The chapter's response is not that this literature is wrong. It is that this literature identifies *exactly* the failure mode of AI-without-trained-teachers and is therefore the strongest available argument *for* the AI+1 deployment pattern this book defends. The bias story is what happens when the model is the decision-maker. The AI+1 model puts the trained teacher between the model and the student, *explicitly* to absorb the bias detection, the suitability judgment, and the moment-to-moment pedagogical calibration that the model cannot do. The critical literature is correctly diagnosing the harm of deployment-by-algorithm. It is not, on its own, an argument against deployment-by-trained-teacher. The chapter's contribution is to refuse the false dichotomy. The alternative to algorithmic harm is not no-AI; it is teacher-managed AI with the training that lets the teacher catch what the model gets wrong. The bias-in-AI literature should inform the training curriculum. It should not be used to refuse the deployment to the students who would gain the most from it well executed.

This is a substantive bet. Whether trained-teacher oversight can reliably catch the bias the model would otherwise enact is the chapter's deepest empirical wager. The evidence base for the wager is preliminary — the AI+1 model has been studied at scale only in Tutor CoPilot and a handful of analogues. The mechanism is identified. The replication is incomplete. I will name this in the chapter's *what the research leaves open* section, because pretending the wager is more settled than it is would itself be a betrayal of the equity argument.

The second serious counter is the *elite AI school* counter, and it is harder. Alpha School, the founding Texas private school built around the 2-Hour Learning AI platform, and the Unbound Academy charter expansion — Arizona, fall 2025, with rejections from Pennsylvania, Utah, Arkansas, North Carolina, and Texas — represent the loudest 2025 version of the AI-as-teacher-replacement model. The marketing claim is that students master core academics in two hours a day using AI tutoring and spend the rest of the day on passion projects with a reported 5:1 staff-to-student ratio and average performance "in the top 1-2 percent nationwide." [Hunt Institute](https://hunt-institute.org/resources/2025/06/ai-tutoring-alpha-school-personalized-learning-technology-k-12-education/) coverage gives the design summary. The claim, if it were true at face value and replicable, would be the strongest argument against the chapter — and it would arrive in a form that widens rather than narrows gaps, since the model is currently available at private-school tuition or in selectively-enrolled charter slots.

The serious critiques of Alpha matter, and the chapter takes them at face value. [Dan Meyer's reading](https://danmeyer.substack.com/p/the-truth-about-2-hour-learning-and) — Meyer, the former Desmos chief academic officer, one of the more thoughtful EdTech critics in the field — is that the marketing claim conflates the AI tool with the elite human infrastructure: the 5:1 guides, the motivated families, the $40,000+ private school tuition, the heavily selected student body. The replicable component of the bundle is the small one. The marketing claim is that the replicable component is the AI. [Scott Alexander's *Astral Codex Ten* review](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/your-review-alpha-school) of Alpha's published MAP score data is critical but not dismissive — the data supports some gain over baseline, but nothing like the marketing numbers, and the gains are confounded with selection effects severe enough that the underlying causal claim cannot be verified from what has been published. The [Chalkdust & Silicon critique](https://medium.com/chalkdust-silicon/innovation-without-critique-isnt-reform-it-s-marketing-c6f3bcf5062f) puts the equity point most directly: if the AI is not the engine, and the magic is in the motivation delivered by elite staff to selected students, then the model is not a scalable equity intervention. It is a curated experience.

The state-level charter rejections — Pennsylvania, Utah, Arkansas, North Carolina, Texas — cited, in most cases, the absence of measurable academic goals tied to state assessments and the lack of independent research on the instructional model. Arizona approved on a 4-3 vote with reduced enrollment caps. The pattern in the rejections is not anti-AI. It is the ordinary skepticism a serious charter authorizer brings to a model whose marketing outruns its evidence.

The equity reading of Alpha is therefore the inverse of the easy reading. Alpha is not evidence that the chapter's argument is wrong. Alpha is the cleanest available case study of the model the chapter argues *against*: AI presented as teacher-replacement rather than as teacher-instrument. The mistake the Alpha model makes is the inverse of the mistake the chapter is correcting in low-income public schools. Alpha claims that the AI is doing the work when in fact the trained guides plus the selected students plus the parental investment are doing it. Under-resourced public schools claim that the teacher will do the work without giving her the AI training the work now requires. Both errors misattribute where the lift comes from. In both cases, the answer is the same bundle — trained teacher with AI as instrument — and not either alone. Alpha is selling the first; the evidence supports the second; the public-school equity case the chapter is making is the version of the second one that scales.

I will note one further thing about the Alpha case. There is real signal in their results that personalization and pacing matter, even controlling for selection. The chapter does not need to over-attack Alpha to make its point. The model is interesting in the parts that are genuinely about pacing and individualization. The chapter's argument is narrower than a wholesale rejection: *the version of the AI-supported education model that scales to under-resourced public schools is not "AI tutor without teacher." It is "trained teacher with AI as instrument."* Alpha is the first; the evidence the chapter rests on supports the second; the policy implication is to invest in the second wherever the first is being sold as the answer to a problem the first is structurally incapable of solving.

## The central argument, restated

The chapter has been building toward a single sentence, and the sentence is best stated baldly so that it does not get lost.

*The equity argument is not that AI is bad for low-income students. It is that every policy failure documented in this book lands disproportionately on the students who were already most vulnerable — and that, where the policy succeeds, the population that gains the most is the same one. The variable is not the technology. The variable is whether the trained teacher is in the room.*

Walk back through the harms in §1 with this sentence in mind and the strategic frame becomes visible. The phone ban without conditions hurt low-income students because no one was making the call about the after-school internet they lost. The exemption without operational definition fails in under-resourced schools because no one trained the teacher to apply it consistently. The AI dividend without training equity accrues to the trained suburban teacher because no one funded the urban teacher's coaching cycles. The cognitive-offloading harm falls where it falls because no one scaffolded the unguarded tool. Each one is what happens when nobody is making the call. The pattern compounds at the bottom because the *nobody-is-making-the-call* problem is most acute in the schools where the call is hardest to make and where the support to make it has been most systematically withheld.

The corollary is that fixing the harm requires fixing the call. Putting trained teachers in the rooms where the call is hardest. Funding the training infrastructure that the *Train Like a Doctor* chapter argued for, with the explicit allocation logic that the schools whose teachers were least likely to have received AI training are the schools whose teachers should be funded first. This is what the catch-up evidence predicts will produce the largest gain. It is the policy that fits the data.

## What this means Monday morning

I want to address this section to a Title I principal or a low-income-district leader directly, because the gap between this chapter's argument and Monday morning is the gap where most equity arguments die. The argument is structural and national. The decisions that close the gap are local and specific. Five things you can do this week, in roughly the order they will take effect.

First, *run the catch-up math for your district.* Pull two numbers. Number one: your current per-teacher AI professional-development spend, calculated across all funding sources and divided by the number of certified instructional staff. Number two: the Title II-A allocation your district receives annually. The SETDA report gives you the national context — most districts are spending less than five percent of their Title II-A funds on technology PD; only nine states even prioritize the stream for it. Your district probably has more headroom in this allocation than your business office has told you. The audit is a one-week exercise. The conversation it enables — *we have the technical authority to redirect this stream toward sustained AI coaching for our highest-need schools, and we are currently not exercising it* — is the conversation that has to happen before anything else changes.

Second, *identify your three highest-need schools and saturate them first.* The instinct in equitable resource allocation is to spread thin. The catch-up evidence says the opposite: gains will be largest where the baseline is weakest, provided the dose is high enough to clear the Yoon threshold. Pick three Title I schools. Calculate the cost of placing a tutor teacher in each — one teacher per building, one section of load coverage paid by the district, the rest of her time spent coaching colleagues in AI-supported practice using the structure Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan documented. The cost is in the range of one teacher salary per building per year. Compare to your phone-pouch contract spend. Compare to your last hardware refresh. The trade is your fiscal-year decision, not Washington's.

Third, *audit the AI dividend distribution in your own buildings.* The six-hour-a-week time savings the Gallup data report is a real number. It exists where teachers have been trained well enough to use the tools fluently. In your district, ask: of the teachers reporting that they save time using AI, what is the demographic distribution of the schools where they teach? If the dividend is concentrated in the schools whose teachers already had the most preparation, what would it take to extend the dividend to the buildings where it is currently zero? This is not a research project. It is a survey question and an honest reading of the result. The dividend, where it exists, is not a free lunch — it is the return on a training investment that has been made unequally.

Fourth, *engage the home-broadband question directly, before the state phone ban deepens the inequity it has already created.* Pull the home-broadband data for your district from the most recent ACS five-year estimates, broken out by school attendance zone. Identify the families that lost connectivity in the FCC's E-Rate rescission. Decide whether your district will provide an alternative — supervised after-school technology access, district-funded hotspots through residual local funds, a partnership with the public library system, an evening-hours computer lab. If yes, name the cost out loud. If no, name the equity consequence out loud. The version of this decision that does the most damage is the one that gets made silently, by default, by no decision at all.

Fifth, *write the educational-purposes exemption for your own district, with your teachers in the room.* If you are operating under a state phone ban, the educational exemption that the legislature handed back to your teachers is the policy surface where their judgment will actually operate. Convene a working group — including the AI-fluent teachers you already have, who are doing this work uncompensated on the side of their actual jobs — and draft an operational definition specific enough that two teachers at different schools in your district would reach the same defensible answer on the same case. Pilot it for a semester. Revise it. The training is the precondition for the exemption working. The exemption is the surface where the training does its work. Your district can do this without waiting for the state to do it.

None of these requires federal action. None requires state legislation. None requires more money than your district has already allocated to professional development under categories you are already spending against. The decisions are at your level. The trade — pouches for coaching, hardware for training, ambient AI access for trained-teacher discretion, equity-as-aspiration for equity-as-fiscal-allocation — is your fiscal-year decision. The cohort of trained teachers the country needs will not be built by Washington. It will be built by district leaders making this trade, building by building, until the cohort exists. The schools at the bottom of the distribution are where the gains will be largest. The schools at the bottom of the distribution are where the urgency is greatest. The schools at the bottom of the distribution are where the trade has historically not been made. You can make it.

## The chapter's turn

The four harms in §1 are not arguments against AI in education. They are arguments against AI-without-the-trained-teacher in education. The three converging literatures in §3 plus the affirmative-case meta-analyses are not arguments that AI is the equalizer the marketing material promises. They are arguments that a specific deployment pattern — trained teacher plus AI as instrument, with the largest gains accruing to the practitioner who started furthest below the frontier — fits the evidence we have. The two are the same point told two ways. The harm is what happens without the trained teacher. The benefit is what happens with her. The variable is the same in both directions.

The students named in the opening of this chapter — the Black boys absorbing the year-one suspension surge, the smartphone-dependent students cut off from after-school connectivity, the students in the lowest-PD-investment districts, the students encountering generative AI without scaffolding — are the same students. The harms compound on overlapping populations because the underlying conditions are unequally distributed before any policy lands. The benefits, when they arrive, compound on the same populations for the same reason: the catch-up effect is a property of the operator skill distribution, and the operator skill distribution in this country is the teacher distribution. The teacher who would gain the most from sustained AI training is the teacher in the under-resourced school. The student who would gain the most from her trained AI-supported practice is the student in her classroom. The trade between the pouch and the coaching cycle, between the hardware refresh and the credentialed in-house specialist, between the August demo and the year-long tutor-teacher cycle, is the trade that converts the equity argument from a slogan into a budget line.

The next chapter is about what the trained teacher this book has been arguing for actually does in the room. The equity chapter is the moral case for putting her in the under-resourced classroom first, with the same support that the well-resourced district provides its own teachers — not as a token gesture, not as supplementary funding around the edges, but as the deployment pattern the evidence actually supports. The country has been investing in the device. The evidence says invest in the teacher. The equity argument says invest in *that* teacher first.

The harms compound at the bottom. So do the gains.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. Whether AI deployment is widening or narrowing K-12 achievement gaps overall is empirically open — the existing PISA evidence predates the post-2022 generative-AI era, [PISA 2025 will be the first round to include "Learning in the Digital World" as an innovative domain](https://www.oecd.org/en/about/programmes/pisa.html), and PISA 2029 will include "Media and Artificial Intelligence Literacy." The prediction this chapter makes — that AI plus training narrows gaps, that AI without training widens them — is a prediction the next two PISA rounds will test, not a settled finding. [verify: PISA 2025 release date once OECD's release calendar is updated.]

Whether the Tutor CoPilot replication holds at scale, in different platforms, with different student demographics, in classroom rather than tutoring settings, is an open question. The cluster RCT is rigorous within its frame. Replication outside that frame is the obvious next research priority and has not yet been published. The chapter's confidence in the affirmative case is conditional on the replication going through. If it does not, the strength of the in-domain pillar of the catch-up argument weakens and the case rests more heavily on the cross-profession evidence — which is strong but is, as I noted in §3, a generalization step rather than a direct demonstration.

And whether trained-teacher oversight can reliably catch the bias the model would otherwise enact — the chapter's deepest empirical wager, on which the answer to the critical literature in §4 turns — is the most important open question this chapter raises. The AI+1 model proposes the trained teacher as the bias-mitigation mechanism. The evidence that trained teachers can in fact serve as that mechanism, at the consistency the populations described in §1 deserve, is preliminary. A serious research program on teacher AI literacy as bias-detection literacy, with downstream measurement of which biases get caught and which slip through, has not been built. It should be. Until it is, the chapter is making the strongest case the existing evidence supports, while being honest that the evidence is still being assembled.

## Still puzzling

Why has no major foundation or federal funder, in the 2024-2026 window, commissioned a heterogeneity re-analysis of the Bastani RCT data with explicit SES proxies? The data exist. The chapter's central inferential chain — Bastani's average finding interacting with the SES-self-regulation literature to produce the equity reading — would be either confirmed or substantially complicated by a direct moderator analysis on the right covariates. The research is straightforward. The question is consequential. Nobody has funded it. Why not?

**What would change my mind:** A district that paired heavy Title II-A investment in AI-specific sustained coaching in its three lowest-income schools with a randomized comparison against a hardware-only allocation of the same dollar amount in three matched schools, and that showed two years later either (a) no learning dividend in the coached schools over the hardware-only schools, or (b) no equity dividend over a district that did neither. Either result would tell me the affirmative case the chapter is making is weaker than I think.
