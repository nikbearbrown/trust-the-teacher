# Chapter 12: The Teacher This Book Is Arguing For

*What the AI+1 classroom looks like, who is responsible for building it, and the work that remains*

---

She teaches twenty-eight seventh graders in a humanities classroom that has not been remodeled since 2003. On Monday at 7:42 a.m., she sits at her desk with a primary-source packet she pulled the night before — an excerpt from a Frederick Douglass speech on the economic structure of slavery. Her grade-level standards expect her to teach it three different ways: at grade level for most of the class, scaffolded for the four students still developing English-language proficiency, extended for the two students reading two grades above the rest. By 8:10 she has all three versions printed. She wrote a single prompt into Claude, pasted the source text in, told the model what reading level each version needed and what kinds of comprehension questions she wanted at the bottom of each one, edited the outputs against the standards rubric in her planning binder, and walked the stack to the copier. It took her eight minutes of model time and twelve minutes of her own judgment on top. Before AI, the same Monday task — done well — was an hour she did not always have.

On Tuesday she uses the hour she did not spend on Monday's differentiation to sit with Marcus. He has been producing correct answers all week on the Douglass questions. He can locate the tariff argument, identify the cotton economy, name the regional asymmetries. When she asks him *why* the tariff argument matters, his face changes. The words stop being his. He gives her a sentence that sounds like a search result. She has watched this pattern in him for three weeks. On Tuesday she has fifteen minutes between her last morning class and the lunch supervision shift, and she spends them at his desk, with a printout in front of him, asking the same question four different ways until something he actually thinks comes out of his mouth. The thing that comes out is not the right answer. It is an honest wrong one. She can teach from there.

On Wednesday she runs a structured peer discussion she has been wanting to design for two years and has never had the planning time to build. Fishbowl seating. A protocol she pulled from a Harkness-style guide she had open in another tab in the spring. Four students in the inner ring with the primary source; the rest in the outer ring tracking the moves of the discussion on a chart she taped to the wall. It takes the class about ten minutes to find the rhythm of it. By minute twenty, the class is doing the work she was trying to do alone three weeks ago. She is mostly silent. She is taking notes on which students said what, and on which students still have not said anything she can grade.

On Thursday afternoon she uses an AI-assisted formative tool — a quiz generator anchored to her unit standards, scored automatically against a rubric she set up at the start of the term — to look at where her class actually stands on the Douglass material before the unit test. The tool surfaces three patterns of confusion that she could have guessed and one she could not have. She rewrites the Friday lesson around those four patterns over coffee. On Friday morning she teaches the lesson she would have taught if she had three uninterrupted hours of analysis time, which she does not have, but which she now does not need.

That is one week. None of it is exotic. None of the tools she uses are special-procurement. The differentiation tool is one of half a dozen — [Diffit](https://www.studentcenteredworld.com/diffit/), MagicSchool, a direct Claude prompt — that take a primary source and return reading-level-adjusted versions in under a minute. The formative tool is in the same family as Khanmigo and MagicSchool's assessment generators and the [Eedi platform that ran the LearnLM RCT](https://www.eedi.com/). The scaffolded peer-discussion protocol is older than any of this — it is the [Hattie "classroom discussion" effect at *d* ≈ 0.82](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/) that has been sitting near the top of the meta-analytic rankings for a decade waiting for teachers who had the time to design it. The Tuesday move with Marcus is the oldest move in teaching. It is what *teaching* meant before the word had to compete with *content delivery*.

The teacher in this vignette is a composite. She is built from the named work of teachers in the [Edutopia 2024–25 differentiation coverage](https://www.edutopia.org/article/using-ai-tools-differentiated-instruction/), the [NEA "Teaching and Learning in the Age of AI" case studies](https://www.nea.org/resource-library/artificial-intelligence-education/iv-teaching-and-learning-age-artificial-intelligence), the [Edutopia tool demos of Diffit and adjacent platforms](https://www.edutopia.org/video/ai-tool-demo-differentiating-class-materials-with-diffit/), and the working pattern that several of those teachers describe in nearly the same words. I am labeling her as a composite because she is one. The honest version of this opening would name the teacher who actually teaches the Douglass unit in this configuration; I am still looking for that teacher and would rather flag the composite than invent her. [verify: name a real teacher from 2024–26 Edutopia/EdSurge/NEA coverage before press, with permission.]

## This is not a vision. It is a description.

I want to be careful with what kind of claim the opening just made, because the whole rest of the chapter — and the work-remaining sections that close the book — depend on the reader taking the vignette as an empirical claim rather than as an inspirational one.

This is not a vision of a classroom that does not yet exist. This is a description, assembled from documented practice, of what trained teachers who use AI purposefully are already doing in classrooms across the country. The Monday differentiation move is in the [Gallup-Walton 2025 survey of 2,232 nationally representative public school teachers](https://www.gallup.com/poll/691965/k-12-teachers-using-five-hours-week.aspx) under "modifying materials to meet student needs" (28% monthly use, growing). The Tuesday move with Marcus is what teachers who reclaim several hours a week from differentiation labor actually report doing with that time — the same Gallup survey finds that 60% of weekly AI users report more time for "individualized feedback and support." The Wednesday discussion is what 60% of weekly users report more time for "improving teaching and learning." The Thursday formative-data move is what 57% of weekly users report as "better insights about student learning data."

The pattern is not aspirational. It is what the AI dividend, when there is one, gets reinvested in *when there is a trained teacher to do the reinvesting.* That is the load-bearing qualifier. The Gallup data are the dividend; the reinvestment is what Chapter 6 spent a chapter showing requires a trained professional to make the call about. The reinvestment also requires the absence of the failure mode Chapter 7 spent a chapter taking seriously. Marcus is not a hypothetical. He is the student-level expression of the [Bastani PNAS 2025 finding](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) — the seventeen-percent closed-book performance drop that students in the GPT Base condition produced after fluent practice they predicted would *help* them. Marcus produces correct answers and cannot defend them because the practice he did under fluency conditions did not encode anything his teacher can build on. The teacher who sits with him on Tuesday is doing the diagnostic work the AI cannot do. The AI built the symptom. The teacher reads it. That is the asymmetry the whole book has been about.

The vignette is the AI+1 framework walking around a classroom for one week. The Monday move is AI doing the differentiation labor a human cannot do at scale and a human deciding what differentiation is needed. The Tuesday move is the human doing the diagnostic work that the AI cannot do at all. The Wednesday move is the human reinvesting the dividend the AI delivered, in the direction the meta-analytic literature has been pointing for fifteen years — structured discussion, student-to-student reasoning, the teacher as the conductor of a conversation she could not have designed if she had spent her preparation time copying and re-leveling worksheets by hand. The Thursday move is AI compressing a diagnostic loop the teacher then completes — formative data, surfaced fast enough to act on, used by a human who decides what reorganization the data warrants. Each move has the AI doing the labor the AI does well and the human doing the judgment the human is irreplaceable for. The framework Bear Brown's [Irreducibly Human](https://www.irreducibly.xyz/) project has been developing names two of the capacities this teacher is exercising: *Embodied Teaching* — presence, improvisation, emotional attunement, the embodied skills that make mentorship effective — and *Conducting AI* — plausibility auditing, problem formulation, tool orchestration, interpretive judgment, executive integration. The vignette is Embodied Teaching on Tuesday with Marcus and Conducting AI on Monday, Thursday, and most of the rest of the week. [verify: the underlying taxonomy is being developed at irreducibly.xyz; the public site currently presents five capacities; the seven-tier formulation referenced in early planning materials may be an upcoming revision. The chapter describes what is publicly available as of writing.]

She is "trained" in a specific sense. Not workshop-hour-trained. Not certificate-trained. Not vendor-demo-trained. Trained the way the [Finnish DigiErko program](https://uefconnect.uef.fi/en/digierko-learning-and-teaching-in-digital-environments/) trained the country's specialist tutor-teachers for seven cohorts — sixty European credits of post-experience university specialization, anchored in classroom research the teacher conducted in her own school, delivered through a consortium of universities together with a network of specialist peers. Trained the way the [Khanmigo Puerto Rico fellows year](https://www.khanmigo.ai/about) is training a first U.S. cohort. Trained in the same structural shape Chapter 8 spent a chapter arguing teaching needs — sustained, subject-specific, grade-level-specific, coaching-anchored, longitudinal — because that is the structural shape every meta-analysis of teacher PD that produced real effects shares.

She does not yet exist as the norm. She exists in fragments. A 2024 Edutopia profile here. A district fellows cohort there. A Khanmigo pilot in San Juan. A handful of grade-level teams whose principal protected ninety minutes a week of common planning time and put a coach in the building. The fragments are real. The infrastructure that would produce her at scale, in every district, in every grade, in every subject — that is the work that remains. The next four sections are what that work looks like, broken out by who is responsible for which piece of it.

## The work that remains

I am dividing the prescriptions by audience because the audiences operate at different scales and the moves they can make are different. None of these sections is exhaustive. Each is a short list of the highest-leverage moves the book's evidence base supports. The pattern across all four: every prescription is anchored to a chapter that did the empirical work and to a primary-source document the reader can take to a meeting on Monday.

### For policymakers — the people who write statute and federal funding

**Define the educational exemption before you enforce what it excludes.** Every state phone-ban law in America contains a phrase like *"except for educational purposes,"* *"unless directed by a teacher,"* or *"for legitimate instructional use."* Not one of the laws defines those terms operationally. Chapter 3 walked through the ten-state survey. The exemption is structural, not incidental — it is the place the legislature reinstates the professional judgment the statute removed in the abstract, without telling anyone what counts. The fix is not vaguer language. It is more specific language, written *with* teachers rather than at them, that distinguishes technology extending a student's thinking from technology substituting for it. Appendix D sketches model statutory language at the level of operational detail the existing statutes evade. The principle the language has to encode is the one Chapter 7 made concrete in the Bastani finding: the GPT Tutor scaffold produced gains; the GPT Base condition produced a seventeen-percent loss. The same word — *AI* — names both. A statute that does not distinguish them is a statute that authorizes whichever one the local procurement office bought.

**Fund teacher AI training through Title II-A at the 70/30 ratio.** Title II-A — Supporting Effective Instruction State Grants — is currently appropriated at [$2.19 billion for FY 2025](https://www.ed.gov/grants-and-programs/formula-grants/school-improvement-grants/supporting-effective-instruction-state-grants-title-ii-part). The November 2025 [SETDA national research finding](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf) is that fewer than 40% of local education agencies currently use Title II-A funds for technology-related professional learning. The reframe Chapter 8 made is that the limiting variable in EdTech outcomes is teacher capacity, not device count. The 70/30 ratio — at least 70% of any AI-related federal expenditure to sustained professional development, no more than 30% to hardware and licensing — is not current statutory or regulatory language. It is the book's recommendation for what statutory or regulatory guidance should look like. Hardware is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is the trained person who decides what the hardware is for. The funding should follow the bottleneck. The [2025 Title II-A hold and the 14-district lawsuit in U.S. District Court in Rhode Island](https://learningforward.org/2025/07/07/title-ii-a-funds-are-delayed-students-will-pay-the-price/) over the broader $6.2 billion freeze are a reminder that this funding stream is the one most likely to be cut, in any administration, when budgets contract. It is also the only one that funds the thing the evidence says actually moves outcomes. [verify FY26 Title II-A status before press.]

**Add teacher learning and improvement to every teacher evaluation framework.** This is Chapter 10 in one sentence. You cannot reward teachers for improving if you do not measure whether they are improving. The closest existing precedents are the [Tennessee Educator Acceleration Model](https://team-tn.org/teacher-evaluation-2-2/), which built a serious student-growth measure (35% of the rating, anchored in the TVAAS value-added system) into its statewide evaluation framework in 2011–12, and the [Massachusetts Educator Evaluation Framework](https://www.doe.mass.edu/edeval/), which has required every educator to develop at least one professional practice goal each year since 2011. Tennessee shows that the second of the three measures the book proposes — student learning gains — is operationally workable at state scale. Massachusetts shows that the third — teacher learning and improvement — is operationally workable at state scale. No state has yet integrated all three with comparable weight. The work that remains is integration, not invention. The pieces exist. They are in different states. Appendix B is the operational guide.

**Treat the screen-time evidence as a finding about who is in the room, not about whether screens belong in it.** The [PISA/TIMSS/PIRLS correlation](https://www.oecd.org/en/about/programmes/pisa.html) is real. Six-plus hours of daily computer use does correlate with sixty-six-point PISA score drops. The [handwriting/typing mode effect](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0956797614524581) is neurologically grounded. Screen reading of long informational text is worse than print. Each of these findings is exactly what Horvath's *Digital Delusion* and the broader ban-everything coalition wields. Each of these findings, read carefully, describes what happens when the technology is deployed without skilled human judgment in the loop — when nobody is deciding which lesson, which student, which moment calls for the screen and which does not. The findings are evidence *for* placing that call with a trained professional, not evidence for taking the call away from her by statute. The legislator who wants to act on the screen-time data should ask which infrastructure is missing such that the call is currently being made by no one — and should fund that infrastructure. That is a policymaker's move. Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The work that remains is to fund the person who should be making it.

### For district leaders — the people who set policy and evaluation across many schools

**Pair the phone ban with funded, supervised, quality technology access during school hours, scaled to replace what the ban removed from low-income students.** This is Chapter 1 in one sentence and Chapter 11 in another. The bell-to-bell phone ban without an access guarantee is the worst of both worlds — it takes the device that was the primary internet connection for roughly a quarter of low-income students, and it hands the substitute question back to building leaders who were never given the budget to answer it. The 2025 [FCC rescission of E-Rate eligibility for off-premises hotspots and school-bus Wi-Fi](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/) closed the federal substitute mechanism in the same policy window the state phone bans were closing the device side. The district that does not fund a building-level substitute is the district whose ban is regressive. The substitute is not exotic — supervised computer-lab access, library hours, after-school technology stations with quality broadband, school-issued devices that *connect* outside the building. Pouches are cheaper than infrastructure. That is why districts buy them. It is also why the equity-tilt the ban produces persists.

**Adopt the three-measure evaluation framework.** Keep the student-wellbeing measure most districts already have. Add a student-learning-gains measure of the kind Tennessee built. Add a teacher-learning-and-improvement measure of the kind Massachusetts built. None of the three on its own is sufficient. Together they create the structure within which the AI+1 teacher's work is visible to the evaluation system she is operating inside. The current system, in most districts, is the satisfaction survey plus a once-yearly walk-through. Neither sees the Tuesday move with Marcus. Neither sees the Wednesday discussion. Neither sees the Thursday reorganization. A framework that does not see the work cannot reward the work. A teacher who is not rewarded for the work eventually stops doing it.

**Stop buying licenses that go unused and start funding the coaching cycles that would make any license worth buying.** [Glimpse K-12 has documented that 67% of district EdTech licenses go unused](https://glimpseK12.com/) — over a billion dollars a year in licenses on shelves. The pattern across studies is monotonic: the school where the platform produces gains is the school that put a coach in the building and protected planning time for grade-level teams to make sense of the platform together. The platform is not the variable. The cycle is. A district that wants to spend AI dollars well should spend them on the cycle. Cut the unused-license budget in half; redirect the half to coaching. The math is not hard. The political work of doing it is.

**Build the analyst seat the AI dividend requires.** A district running AI-assisted formative-data tools at any meaningful scale is producing data the building principal cannot read alone and the teacher cannot read alone. A single district-level data analyst — someone whose job is to surface patterns across classrooms and feed them back to grade-level teams in a form the teams can act on — is an investment most districts have not yet made. The Thursday move in the vignette is the classroom-level version of work that does not scale without a district-level analog. The Tutor CoPilot study (Chapter 5) showed the gains from AI-augmented coaching were largest for the lowest-rated tutors — the floor moves more than the ceiling. The same pattern applies to the data layer. The district that builds the analyst seat is the district where the dividend reaches the teachers who would otherwise not know what the data is telling them.

### For school leaders — the people who run the building

**Phone Fridays. Lockers. Teacher discretion within a clear framework.** Chapter 9 walked through what the implementation actually looks like at building level — the principal who protects the policy floor, the teachers who exercise judgment within it, the explicit weekly cadence that lets students know what the rules are without leaving the rules to be re-negotiated every period. The shape of the policy is local and bounded. *Local* because the building, not the legislature, knows what the bell schedule and the classroom mix and the parent communication channels actually require. *Bounded* because the teacher discretion the exemption clause delegates needs a building-level framework she can apply consistently with the teacher next door. The framework is not vendor-supplied. It is the principal's job.

**Train your teachers in AI the way you would expect your staff to be trained in any other professional tool.** Chapter 5 was the cross-profession argument. Surgeons get laparoscopic training. Farmers get yield-monitor training. Customer-support agents get Copilot training. Nobody hands a trained professional a tool with a half-day demo and expects competence to follow. Teachers do not become competent with AI by attending the August walkthrough either. They become competent through the same structure that produces competence anywhere else: sustained practice on real problems, with feedback from someone further along, with the room to fail in front of someone who can debug the failure with them. The building leader can do almost all of this without state or federal authorization. Protect ninety minutes a week of grade-level common planning time. Bring in a coach for two days a month. Require every grade-level team to bring one piece of student work the AI tool was involved in producing, and have the team take it apart together. This is the [Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan 2018 meta-analysis](https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/mkraft/files/kraft_blazar_hogan_2018_teacher_coaching.pdf) at building scale. The headline finding is the one to keep: coaching produces *d* ≈ 0.49 on teacher practice. The book is asking you to fund the upstream variable. The building leader is the person who can fund it without anyone's permission.

**Build the collective version of what the trained teacher does.** [Collective teacher efficacy](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/influences/view/Collective_Teacher_Efficacy_(Trust_in_Action)) — the shared faculty belief that the joint practice can move outcomes for any student — is the highest-effect influence in the Hattie corpus, however contested the precise number is (somewhere between *d* = 1.0 and *d* = 1.6, depending on which meta-analysis is currently weighted in). The mechanism is faculty-level coordination of practice across years, debugged in PLCs, normed across grade-level teams. A single teacher who runs the vignette's week well moves her own classroom. A faculty that runs it well moves the school. The building leader's most underused tool is the protected planning time and the structured agenda that lets faculty practice spread, debug, and compound. That is collective efficacy. It is what the principal builds, by hand, over years, against every district pressure to schedule it away.

**Stop treating AI training as separate from subject-matter training.** The shape that worked in the [Khanmigo Puerto Rico fellows program](https://www.khanmigo.ai/about) and the shape that worked in DigiErko both shared one feature: AI competence was taught inside the subject and the grade level the teacher actually teaches. There is no generic AI professional development that produces a seventh-grade humanities teacher who runs the vignette's Thursday move. There is the seventh-grade humanities version, the third-grade reading version, the AP physics version, the K–2 phonics version, and they have to be built by people who teach those subjects at those grades and who use AI in them. The building leader who hires the wrong shape of consultant is buying the August workshop in a longer wrapper. The building leader who organizes the right shape — subject-specific, grade-specific, coaching-anchored — is doing the work that produces the teacher in the vignette.

### For teachers — the people in the room

**The AI dividend is real. Six weeks per year is real. The question is what you do with the time.** Chapter 6 walked through the [Gallup-Walton 2025 evidence](https://www.gallup.com/poll/691965/k-12-teachers-using-five-hours-week.aspx) — about 5.9 hours per week of self-reported saved time, concentrated in routine prep tasks, robust across the 2,232-teacher nationally representative sample. The self-report has limits. The pattern is real. Six weeks of reclaimed time per year is roughly what the math gives you if you do not lose any of it to scope creep, which most teachers will lose some of, which Chapter 6 was honest about. Even half the dividend is large. The question this chapter is asking you to hold is the question the dividend does not answer on its own: what do you spend it on?

**The highest-leverage thing you can spend it on is the time you spend in relationship with the specific students in front of you.** [Hattie's teacher-student-relationships finding sits at *d* ≈ 0.72](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/Influences/View/teacher-student_relationships). The [Roorda et al. 2011 meta-analysis](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0034654311421793) of ninety-nine studies separated engagement effects from achievement effects and found medium-to-large associations across both, longitudinally, controlling for student characteristics. Tuesday with Marcus is the chapter's case-level version of this finding. The fifteen minutes you spend hearing the wrong note in a student's reasoning is the work that Hattie's corpus is, at root, an extended argument for. The AI cannot do this work. It does not hear the change in Marcus's face when the question goes from *what* to *why.* It does not know that he has been compensating for three weeks. It does not know that his older brother dropped out last spring and that his attendance has been brittle since November. *You* know. The dividend is what gives you the time to act on what you know. Spend it there.

**Treat AI output the way a serious clinician treats a lab result — useful, suggestive, not authoritative.** The [Bastani PNAS finding](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) is the warning shot from Chapter 7. Students using unguided AI during practice produce fluent outputs in the moment, fail on closed-book assessment, and predict — before the assessment — that they will *outperform* control. The metacognitive miscalibration is the load-bearing failure mode. Your job, on the AI side of the room, is to design assignments where the fluency the AI provides is not the assessment. Closed-book moments. Defended explanations. Oral follow-ups. The Marcus diagnostic. The Wednesday discussion where the student has to produce reasoning in front of peers without the model in front of her. The AI is a fine first draft. It is a terrible last draft. The professional in the room is the one who knows which one she is looking at.

**Use the dividend to design the thing you have been wanting to design for two years.** Every teacher has a list of moves she has wanted to try. A protocol. A unit redesign. A formative routine. A discussion structure. A standards-rubric overhaul. The list is real, and almost nobody has ever had the planning time to work it down. The AI dividend is the move that lets you take one item off the list every quarter. Wednesday's structured peer discussion in the vignette is one item. The other forty are the ones you have, in your own head, that nobody is going to design for you. The dividend is what makes the design possible. The decision about which item to take next is yours to make. The book is not asking you to use AI more. It is asking you to use the time AI gives you on the thing that the meta-analytic literature has been pointing at and that you have always wanted to do and have never had time for.

## What the research leaves open

A few things this chapter has not settled, which the available evidence does not yet settle either.

Whether the AI+1 configuration scales nationally without producing the same equity tilt the rest of the book documents — wealthy districts implementing it well, under-resourced districts implementing it badly, the gap widening — is open. The pattern of every preceding wave of educational reform suggests the tilt will appear unless the funding mechanism is built deliberately against it. Chapter 11 made the affirmative case that the under-resourced district is where the gains from training are largest, citing the Tutor CoPilot catch-up effect and the Kansas farm-efficiency parallel. Whether the gains arrive faster than the equity-tilt failure mode does is empirical, and the experiment has not been run at the scale the question requires.

Whether the [Bastani finding replicates outside Turkey, high school, and mathematics](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) is open. It is one large randomized trial. The mechanism — fluency without encoding — is consistent with the broader cognitive-load literature and with the [Schoenfeld procedural-fluency-versus-conceptual-understanding tradition](https://faculty.tarleton.edu/brawner/wp-content/uploads/sites/11/2023/02/Schoenfeld_MathThinking.pdf) and with the [*How People Learn* expert-novice distinction](https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/9853/chapter/5), but the specific magnitude (-17% closed-book) is a single-RCT estimate. Three to five additional pre-registered replications in different subjects, ages, and country contexts would settle whether the finding is the warning the book has taken it to be or a Turkey-specific artifact. The book is making the larger bet that the mechanism replicates because the mechanism is consistent with twenty years of independent cognitive-science work. The bet could be wrong. If it is wrong in the direction that says unguided AI is not as damaging as Bastani found, the AI+1 case weakens at the cognitive-offloading edge — though not at the time-dividend or the teacher-mediated-influence edges, which sit on their own evidence bases.

Whether the three-measure evaluation framework can survive the political pressures that have crushed every previous attempt at evaluation reform is open. Tennessee TEAM has held for fifteen years and has been re-litigated repeatedly. Massachusetts has held for fifteen years with steady professional resistance to the goals layer. The integration both states have not yet done is the move the book is asking for, and the move is politically harder than either of the two precedents on its own. The book's argument is that without the third measure — teacher learning and improvement — the AI+1 configuration produces work that the evaluation system cannot see, which is a structurally unstable place to leave the work.

The honest position, on each of these, is the Montaigne position. The chapter has taken the evidence seriously enough to make a prescription. The prescription is the best reading of the evidence currently available. The evidence will continue to come in. The chapter expects to be revised, in print and in practice, as it does.

## Still puzzling

Two things I do not yet know the answer to, after eleven chapters and a capstone of trying.

Why the political coalition that built the 2025–26 phone-ban wave is so consistently willing to fund the pouches and so consistently not willing to fund the teacher training that would let the educational exemption mean something. The pouch line item passes. The Title II-A AI-training line item, repeatedly, does not. The structural reason is not technical — the dollars are comparable, the policy logic is cleaner for the training side. The failure mode is upstream of the evidence. I do not yet know whether it is fixable from inside the coalition or whether it requires a different coalition.

Whether the AI+1 teacher I have described in this chapter is, at scale, a configuration that survives twenty years of vendor incentive, district procurement, federal funding cycles, and the next three administrations. The configuration is fragile because it depends on infrastructure — coaching cycles, common planning time, evaluation that sees the work — that the political economy of American schooling has consistently failed to fund. The book is arguing that the configuration is worth the fragility. The honest open question is whether *worth the fragility* and *survives the fragility* are the same thing.

## What would change my mind

A multi-site, pre-registered randomized trial in U.S. K-12 contexts showing that unguided AI access — without trained teacher mediation, without coaching infrastructure, without the +1 in the AI+1 configuration — produces equal or better long-term learning outcomes than the AI+1 teacher this chapter describes. Such a study has not been done. Until it is, the evidence base the book has built across eleven chapters is the best available, and the prescription is the honest consequence of taking that evidence base seriously.

## The closing

In 2025, America wrote thirty-three state laws about phones. By spring 2026, the count was higher. We wrote almost no laws about teacher development. The [FCC, in the same policy window, removed E-Rate eligibility for the off-premises connectivity](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/) that was the substitute mechanism for the device the states were locking away. We spent [$5.2 million in Los Angeles](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/consumer/schools-spend-millions-yondr-pouches-store-cellphones-rcna230454), [$29 million in New York](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2025/07/16/nyc-school-cell-phone-ban-funding-kathy-hochul-yondr/), [$800,000 in Houston](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/education/2025/08/01/527760/houston-isd-announces-student-cell-phone-policy-to-comply-with-new-state-law/) on the pouch budget. We held the Title II-A funds that would have paid for the teacher training. We bought platforms whose [licenses sat 67% unused on the shelves](https://glimpseK12.com/) and called the spending an AI strategy.

The phone is in the pouch. The app is on the server. The teacher is in the room, making the call the legislature could not make, doing the work the algorithm cannot do, with the preparation we have consistently failed to provide.

That is the work that remains. It is not legislative work, though legislation is part of it. It is not technological work, though technology is part of it. It is the oldest work in education — recognizing that what happens between a skilled teacher and a student is the thing the building is for, that producing such a teacher requires sustained investment and trust and honest measurement, and that every shortcut we have taken around it for the last forty years has brought us back to the same place. We have not been short of theories of what would replace the teacher. We have been short of teachers prepared for the moment they were standing in.

Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The screen-time correlation. The mode effect. The cognitive-offloading drop. Each of them describes the room when the trained adult is absent or unprepared. Each of them, read honestly, is evidence for placing the call with the person whose job is to make it, and for finally building the infrastructure that would let her make it well. The book has been about who should be making the call. The work that remains is the work of preparing her to make it.

Singapore wrote three words on a wall in 2004 — [*Teach Less, Learn More*](https://www.nlb.gov.sg/main/article-detail?cmsuuid=bf38bb79-2b26-4068-828c-c89246c65c10) — and built a curriculum redesign around them. Finland wrote two — *phenomenon-based* — and built a national curriculum revision around them. The slogans worked because the structural commitments beneath them were real. A slogan that organizes infrastructure is a directive. A slogan that does not is wallpaper. The book's three sentences are a directive if the four sections above this one get built, and wallpaper if they do not. That choice is in front of every reader.

The teacher on Tuesday hears the wrong note in Marcus's reasoning. She sits down. She asks the question four different ways. She gets an honest wrong answer she can teach from. That is the moment the book has been arguing for. That is the moment the algorithm cannot produce, the legislature cannot mandate, and the platform cannot replace. It is also the moment that, eleven chapters in, we have failed to build the conditions to produce at scale. The conditions are not mysterious. They are funded coaching cycles, protected planning time, evaluation that sees the work, training that respects the subject and the grade and the room. None of them is new. All of them are absent at the scale the evidence demands. The work that remains is to build them.

Trust the teacher. Train the teacher. Measure what matters.
