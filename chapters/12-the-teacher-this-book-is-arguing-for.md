It is 7:42 on a Monday morning and she has twenty minutes before the bell.

She is a seventh-grade humanities teacher. Her classroom has not been remodeled since 2003. On her desk is a primary-source packet she assembled the night before — a Frederick Douglass speech on the economic architecture of slavery. Her grade-level standards require her to teach it three ways: at grade level for most of the class, scaffolded for the four students still developing English-language proficiency, extended for the two reading two grades above the rest. By 8:10 she has all three versions printed. She wrote a single prompt, pasted in the source text, specified what reading level each version needed and what kinds of comprehension questions she wanted at the bottom of each one, edited the outputs against the standards rubric in her planning binder, and walked the stack to the copier. Eight minutes of model time. Twelve minutes of her own judgment on top. Before AI, the same Monday task — done well — was an hour she did not always have.

On Tuesday she uses the hour she did not spend on Monday to sit with Marcus. He has been producing correct answers all week on the Douglass questions. He can locate the tariff argument, identify the cotton economy, name the regional asymmetries. When she asks him *why* the tariff argument matters, his face changes. The words stop being his. He gives her a sentence that sounds like a search result — fluent, organized, emptied of thought. She has watched this pattern in him for three weeks. On Tuesday she has fifteen minutes between her last morning class and her lunch supervision shift, and she spends them at his desk, with a printout between them, asking the same question four different ways until something he actually thinks comes out of his mouth. What comes out is not the right answer. It is an honest wrong one. She can teach from there.

On Wednesday she runs a structured peer discussion she has been wanting to design for two years and has never had the planning time to build. Fishbowl seating. A protocol she found in a Harkness-style guide she bookmarked in the spring. Four students in the inner ring with the primary source; the rest in the outer ring tracking the moves of the discussion on a chart taped to the wall. It takes the class ten minutes to find the rhythm. By minute twenty, the class is doing the work she was trying to do alone three weeks ago. She is mostly silent. She is taking notes on who said what, and on which students still have not said anything she can grade.

On Thursday afternoon she runs an AI-assisted formative check — a quiz generator anchored to her unit standards, scored against a rubric she set up at the start of the term. The tool surfaces three patterns of confusion she could have predicted and one she could not have. Over coffee, she rewrites the Friday lesson around those four patterns. On Friday morning she teaches the lesson she would have taught if she'd had three uninterrupted hours of diagnostic time, which she does not have, which she now does not need.

That is one week. None of it is exotic.

The differentiation tool is one of half a dozen platforms — Diffit, MagicSchool, a direct Claude prompt — that take a primary source and return reading-level-adjusted versions in under a minute. The formative tool is in the same family as Khanmigo and the Eedi platform. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: d ≈ 0.82 classroom-discussion figure is stable in Hattie editions but should be pinned to specific MetaX retrieval date or Visible Learning: The Sequel (2023). And the Tuesday move with Marcus is the oldest move in teaching. It is what *teaching* meant before the word had to compete with *content delivery.*

## A description, not a vision

Let me be careful with what kind of claim the opening just made, because everything that follows depends on the reader taking the vignette as an empirical claim rather than an inspirational one.

This is not a vision of a classroom that does not yet exist. It is a description, assembled from documented practice, of what trained teachers who use AI purposefully are already doing in classrooms across the country. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: 60% / 60% / 57% Gallup reinvestment figures may be transposed relative to the Gallup-Walton 2025 report (which reports 57% on grading/feedback and 61% on data insights per Ch. 6 fact-check). Pin to exact Gallup category labels. -->The Monday differentiation move is in the Gallup-Walton 2025 survey of 2,232 nationally representative public school teachers under "modifying materials to meet student needs." The Tuesday move with Marcus is what teachers who reclaim several hours a week from differentiation labor report doing with that time — sixty percent of weekly AI users in the same survey report more time for individualized feedback and support. The Wednesday discussion is what sixty percent report more time for in "improving teaching and learning." The Thursday formative-data move is what fifty-seven percent describe as "better insights about student learning data."

The pattern is not aspirational. It is what the AI dividend, when there is one, gets reinvested in when there is a trained teacher to do the reinvesting. That is the load-bearing qualifier. The dividend creates the possibility. The trained teacher is what converts the possibility into a Tuesday with Marcus.

Marcus himself is not hypothetical. He is the classroom-level expression of the Bastani finding — the seventeen-percent closed-book performance drop that students in the unguided AI condition produced after practice sessions they predicted would help them. Marcus produces correct answers he cannot defend because the fluent practice he did with the tool did not encode anything his teacher can build on. The AI built the symptom. The teacher reads it. That asymmetry is what the whole book has been about.

## For policymakers

**Define the educational exemption before you enforce what it excludes.**

Every state phone-ban law in America contains a phrase like *except for educational purposes* or *unless directed by a teacher* or *for legitimate instructional use.* Not one of the laws defines those terms operationally. The exemption is structural — it is the place the legislature reinstates the professional judgment the statute removed in the abstract, without telling anyone what counts. The fix is not vaguer language. It is more specific language, written with teachers rather than at them, that distinguishes technology extending a student's thinking from technology substituting for it.

The principle the language has to encode is the one the Bastani finding made concrete. The scaffolded GPT Tutor produced neutral outcomes. The unscaffolded GPT Base condition produced a seventeen-percent loss. The same word — *AI* — names both. A statute that does not distinguish them authorizes whichever one the local procurement office bought cheapest.

**Fund teacher AI training through Title II-A at a 70/30 ratio.**

<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: "Fewer than 40% of LEAs use Title II-A funds for technology-related PD" is plausible but should be pinned to SETDA Nov 2025 or USDE Title II-A summary. The 70/30 ratio itself is the chapter's authorial recommendation, not a ratio drawn from named policy literature — frame as recommendation. -->Title II-A — Supporting Effective Instruction State Grants — is currently appropriated at $2.19 billion annually. Fewer than forty percent of local education agencies use those funds for technology-related professional learning. The limiting variable in EdTech outcomes is teacher capacity, not device count. At least seventy percent of any AI-related federal expenditure should go to sustained professional development; no more than thirty percent to hardware and licensing. Hardware is not the bottleneck. The trained person who decides what the hardware is for — that is the bottleneck. The funding should follow it.

**Add teacher learning and improvement to every teacher evaluation framework.**

You cannot reward teachers for improving if you do not measure whether they are improving. Tennessee built a serious student-growth measure into its statewide evaluation framework in 2011 and it has held for fifteen years. Massachusetts has required every educator to develop a professional practice goal each year since the same period. Tennessee shows that a student-learning-gains measure is operationally workable at state scale. Massachusetts shows that a teacher-learning-and-improvement measure is operationally workable at state scale. No state has yet integrated both with comparable weight. The work that remains is integration, not invention. The pieces exist. They are in different states.

**Treat the screen-time evidence as a finding about who is in the room, not about whether screens belong in it.**

The PISA correlations are real. Six-plus hours of daily computer use does correlate with sixty-six-point score drops. Screen reading of long informational text is measurably worse than print. The handwriting encoding advantage is neurologically grounded. Each of these findings is exactly what the ban-everything coalition wields. Each of them, read carefully, describes what happens when technology is deployed without skilled human judgment in the loop — when nobody is deciding which lesson, which student, which moment calls for the screen and which does not. They are evidence for placing that call with a trained professional, not evidence for removing the call from her by statute. Every finding the ban-everything camp wields is a finding about what happens when nobody is making the call. The work that remains is to fund the person who should be making it.

## For district leaders

**Pair the phone ban with funded, supervised, quality technology access during school hours.**

The bell-to-bell phone ban without an access guarantee is the worst of both worlds. It removes the device that is the primary internet connection for roughly a quarter of low-income students and hands the substitute question back to building leaders who were never given the budget to answer it. The FCC, in the same policy window as the state phone bans, rescinded E-Rate eligibility for off-premises hotspots and school-bus Wi-Fi — closing the federal substitute mechanism in the same window the state bans were closing the device side. The district that does not fund a building-level substitute is the district whose ban is regressive. Pouches are cheaper than supervised computer labs. That is why districts buy them. It is also why the equity tilt the ban produces persists.

**Adopt the three-measure evaluation framework.**

Keep the student-wellbeing measure most districts already have. Add a student-learning-gains measure of the kind Tennessee built. Add a teacher-learning-and-improvement measure of the kind Massachusetts built. None of the three on its own is sufficient. Together they create the structure within which the teacher in the vignette's work is visible to the evaluation system she operates inside. The current system in most districts is the satisfaction survey plus a once-yearly walk-through. Neither sees the Tuesday move with Marcus. Neither sees the Wednesday discussion. Neither sees the Thursday reorganization. A framework that cannot see the work cannot reward the work. A teacher who is not rewarded for the work eventually stops doing it.

**Stop buying licenses that go unused. Start funding the coaching cycles that would make any license worth buying.**

Glimpse K-12 has documented that sixty-seven percent of district EdTech licenses go unused — over a billion dollars a year on shelves. The pattern across studies is monotonic: the school where a platform produces gains is the school that put a coach in the building and protected planning time for grade-level teams to make sense of the platform together. The platform is not the variable. The cycle is. Cut the unused-license budget in half. Redirect the half to coaching. The math is not hard. The political work is.

**Build the analyst seat the AI dividend requires.**

A district running AI-assisted formative tools at any meaningful scale is producing data the building principal cannot read alone and the classroom teacher cannot act on alone. A single district-level data analyst — someone whose job is to surface patterns across classrooms and feed them back to grade-level teams in a form the teams can act on — is an investment most districts have not made. The Thursday move in the vignette is the classroom-level version of work that does not scale without a district-level analog. The research on AI-assisted coaching shows that the gains are largest for the lowest-rated teachers — the floor moves more than the ceiling. The same pattern applies to the data layer. The district that builds the analyst seat is the district where the dividend reaches the teachers who would otherwise not know what the data is telling them.

## For school leaders

**Train your teachers in AI the way you would expect any professional to be trained in a new professional tool.**

Surgeons get laparoscopic training. Farmers get yield-monitor training. Customer-support agents get Copilot onboarding. Nobody hands a trained professional a new tool with a half-day demo and expects competence to follow. Teachers do not become competent with AI by attending the August walkthrough either. They become competent through the same structure that produces competence anywhere: sustained practice on real problems, with feedback from someone further along, with the room to fail in front of someone who can debug the failure. A building leader can do almost all of this without state or federal authorization. Protect ninety minutes a week of grade-level common planning time. Bring in a coach for two days a month. Require every grade-level team to bring one piece of student work the AI tool was involved in producing, and have the team take it apart together. The Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan 2018 meta-analysis found that coaching produces *d* ≈ 0.49 on teacher practice. The building leader is the person who can fund the upstream variable without anyone's permission.

**Build the collective version of what the trained teacher does.**

A single teacher who runs the vignette's week well moves her classroom. A faculty that runs it well moves the school. Collective teacher efficacy — the shared faculty belief that the joint practice can move outcomes for any student — is among the highest-effect influences in the meta-analytic literature. The mechanism is faculty-level coordination of practice across years, debugged in PLCs, normed across grade-level teams. The building leader's most underused tool is the protected planning time and the structured agenda that lets effective practice spread, get debugged, and compound. That is collective efficacy. It is what the principal builds, by hand, over years, against every district pressure to schedule it away.

**Stop treating AI training as separate from subject-matter training.**

There is no generic AI professional development that produces a seventh-grade humanities teacher who runs the Thursday move. There is the seventh-grade humanities version, the third-grade reading version, the AP physics version, the K-2 phonics version, and they have to be built by people who teach those subjects at those grades and who use AI in them. The building leader who hires a vendor to run a half-day workshop is buying the August walkthrough in a longer wrapper. The building leader who organizes subject-specific, grade-specific, coaching-anchored development is doing the work that produces the teacher in the vignette.

## For teachers

**The dividend is real. The question is what you do with it.**

The Gallup-Walton survey of 2,232 nationally representative public school teachers documented roughly 5.9 hours per week of saved time among weekly AI users — concentrated in routine preparation tasks, robust across the sample, corroborated by a randomized trial in sixty-eight English schools that measured a thirty-one percent reduction in lesson preparation time without detectable quality loss. Six weeks per year is the math if you don't lose any of it to scope creep, which most teachers will lose some of, which the book was honest about. Even half the dividend is substantial. The question this chapter is asking you to hold is the one the dividend does not answer on its own: what do you spend it on?

**Spend it on relationship.**

Teacher-student relationships sit at *d* ≈ 0.72 in the meta-analysis. Ninety-nine studies, separated by engagement effects and achievement effects, longitudinal, controlling for student characteristics. Tuesday with Marcus is the chapter's case-level version of this finding. The fifteen minutes you spend hearing the wrong note in a student's reasoning is the work that the meta-analytic literature is, at root, an extended argument for. The AI cannot do it. It does not hear the change in Marcus's face when the question moves from *what* to *why.* It does not know he has been compensating for three weeks. It does not know that his older brother dropped out last spring and that his attendance has been brittle since November. You know. The dividend is what gives you the time to act on what you know.

**Treat AI output the way a serious clinician treats a lab result.**

The Bastani finding is the warning. Students using unguided AI during practice produce fluent outputs in the moment, underperform on closed-book assessment, and predict — before the assessment — that they will outperform control. The metacognitive miscalibration is the load-bearing failure mode. Your job, on the AI side of the room, is to design assignments where the fluency the AI provides is not the assessment. Closed-book moments. Defended explanations. Oral follow-ups. The Wednesday discussion where the student has to produce reasoning in front of peers without the model in front of her. The AI is a fine first draft. It is a terrible last draft. You are the person who knows which one you are looking at.

**Use the dividend to design the thing you have been meaning to design for two years.**

Every teacher has a list. A protocol. A unit redesign. A formative routine. A discussion structure. The list is real, and almost nobody has ever had the planning time to work it down. The dividend is what lets you take one item off the list every quarter. Wednesday's structured peer discussion is one item. The other forty are in your head, and nobody else is going to design them. The book is not asking you to use AI more. It is asking you to use the time AI gives you on the thing the meta-analytic literature has been pointing at and that you have always wanted to do and have never had time for.

## What the research leaves open

Whether the AI+1 configuration scales nationally without reproducing the equity tilt the rest of the book documents — wealthy districts implementing it well, under-resourced districts implementing it badly — is open. The pattern of every preceding wave of educational reform suggests the tilt will appear unless the funding mechanism is built deliberately against it. Whether it arrives faster than the equity failure mode does is empirical, and the experiment has not been run at the scale the question requires.

Whether the Bastani finding replicates outside Turkey, outside high school, outside mathematics is genuinely open. It is one large randomized trial. <!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: "Twenty years of independent cognitive-science work" understates the timeline. Bjork & Bjork 1992 is 34 years old; Sweller 1988 is nearly 40; Schultz 1997 is nearly 30. Consider "three decades" or "thirty-plus years." -->The mechanism is consistent with twenty years of independent cognitive-science work — the Bjorks' storage-retrieval distinction, Sweller's cognitive load theory, Schultz's prediction-error account — and that consistency is why the book has taken it as load-bearing. But mechanism consistency and empirical replication are different things. Three to five additional pre-registered trials in different subjects, ages, and country contexts would settle whether the finding generalizes or is an artifact of the specific context. The book is making the larger bet. The bet could be wrong.

Whether the three-measure evaluation framework can survive the political pressures that have defeated every previous attempt at evaluation reform is open. Tennessee's framework has held for fifteen years and has been re-litigated repeatedly. Massachusetts's has held for fifteen years with steady professional resistance. The integration of both — neither state has managed it — is the harder political move. The book's argument is that without the third measure, teacher learning and improvement, the AI+1 configuration produces work the evaluation system cannot see. That is a structurally unstable place to leave the work.

## Two things still puzzling

Why the political coalition that drove the 2025-26 phone-ban wave is so consistently willing to fund the pouches and so consistently unwilling to fund the teacher training that would let the educational exemption mean something is a question I do not have a clean answer to. The pouch line item passes. The Title II-A training line item, repeatedly, does not. The structural reason is not technical — the dollars are comparable, and the policy logic is cleaner on the training side. The failure mode is upstream of the evidence. Whether it is fixable from inside the coalition or requires a different coalition, I cannot tell.

Whether the AI+1 teacher is, at scale, a configuration that survives twenty years of vendor incentive, district procurement cycles, and shifting federal priorities is the other one. The configuration is fragile because it depends on infrastructure — coaching cycles, common planning time, evaluation that sees the work — that the political economy of American schooling has consistently failed to fund at scale. The book is arguing that the configuration is worth the fragility. The honest open question is whether *worth the fragility* and *survives the fragility* are the same thing. Eleven chapters in, I am not sure they are. I am sure enough of the first to make the case for it. The second is what the next decade of the work will decide.

In 2025 and into 2026, America wrote thirty-three state laws about phones in schools. We wrote almost none about training the teachers those schools depend on. In the same policy window, the FCC removed the federal connectivity subsidy that was the substitute mechanism for the devices the states were locking away. Los Angeles spent $5.2 million on pouches. The phone is in the pouch. The app is on the server. The teacher is in the room, making the call the legislature could not make, doing the work the algorithm cannot do, without the preparation we have consistently failed to provide.

That is the state of things. It is also the sentence the book has been building toward for eleven chapters, because it names both the problem and the place the problem can be addressed. The call is being made — imperfectly, without sufficient training, inside an evaluation system that cannot see it — by the one variable the research keeps finding at the top of the list. What the book is asking is that we take that finding seriously enough to invest in it. Not as a vision. As a policy choice, made deliberately, at the scale the evidence demands.

Singapore put three words on a wall in 2004 — *Teach Less, Learn More* — and built a curriculum redesign beneath them. Finland wrote two — *phenomenon-based* — and rebuilt a national curriculum around them. Slogans work when the structural commitments beneath them are real. The book's three sentences — trust the teacher, train the teacher, measure what matters — are a directive if the four sections above this one get built, and wallpaper if they do not. That choice belongs to every person reading this, at whatever scale they operate.

On Tuesday morning, the teacher heard the wrong note in Marcus's reasoning. She sat down. She asked the question four different ways. She got an honest wrong answer she could teach from. That moment — the one the algorithm cannot produce, the legislature cannot mandate, and the platform cannot replace — is the moment the book has been arguing for. The conditions that make it possible at scale are not mysterious. They are funded coaching cycles, protected planning time, evaluation that sees the work, training that respects the subject and the grade and the room. None of these is new. All of them are absent at the scale the evidence demands.

The conditions that make the AI+1 classroom possible are not mysterious. They are also not present at scale.
