# Train Like a Doctor

*On what medicine spent fifty years building, and why we haven't built it for teachers*

---

The waiting room smells like industrial carpet and someone's lunch. A woman in her forties sits behind a desk, chart open, running fifteen minutes behind because the patient before this one brought in three family members who all had questions. She has a diploma on the wall from a state university, a board certification from her specialty, and a renewal notice in her inbox reminding her that she has until December to complete her continuing medical education hours for the year. One of the required tracks — opioid prescribing — was added in 2017, when the death statistics reached a point that licensing boards could no longer treat as a background condition. Another — suicide screening — was added in 2018. Both are mandatory. Not "recommended." Not "available if interested." Mandatory, tied to her license, verified by an independent accrediting body, updated whenever the field changes enough to require it.

She finishes the appointment. She closes the chart. She washes her hands.

Across the street, a woman in her forties sits behind a different desk, surrounded by twenty-seven seventh graders. She has a teaching credential from a state university, a license renewed every five years through a process her district mostly satisfies by scheduling one or two professional development days per year. In 2023, when her students started arriving with generative AI on their phones — using it for homework, for essays, for answers to questions she had not finished asking — her district responded with a two-and-a-half-hour vendor demo in August. The presenter walked her through three tools. By the following Monday, her students were using two the presenter had not mentioned and three the district had not licensed.

That was the preparation. The August demo. For what was about to happen in her room.

The pediatrician across the street could not lose her license for being two years behind on opioid prescribing guidelines. The teacher could not lose hers for being three model generations behind on generative AI. Both of them could lose students. Only one of them works inside a system that treats the second possibility as seriously as the first.

## The word the field is hiding behind

"Professional development" is one of those terms that has been doing too many jobs for too long. Every district uses it. Every state department of education uses it. Every federal funding stream uses it. Most teachers I know have a complicated relationship with it — somewhere between skepticism and exhausted resignation, with occasional genuine gratitude for the instance that actually helped.

The skepticism is earned. But before the argument can be made, the term needs to be pulled apart, because three very different interventions are all wearing the same name, and the research literature tells us very different things about each.

The first is *clock-hour compliance.* This is what state licensure mostly requires: accumulate some number of professional development hours over a renewal cycle, submit evidence, receive your renewed credential. The hours can be earned through almost anything labeled professional development. The August vendor demo counts. A summer institute counts. A conference session counts. The state's interest is in checking the box, not in what is in the box.

The research literature on this layer is blunt. A 2007 review by Yoon and colleagues, commissioned by the Regional Educational Laboratory Southwest, screened more than thirteen hundred candidate studies, found nine that met rigorous evidence standards, and reported that professional development programs averaging fewer than fourteen contact hours produced no detectable effect on student achievement. Most box-checking PD comes in well under fourteen hours. The compliance layer, taken on its own, is a layer that the research already told us does not move outcomes.

The second is *sustained coaching.* A 2018 meta-analysis by Matthew Kraft, David Blazar, and Dylan Hogan at Harvard — sixty causal studies of teacher coaching — produced pooled effects of $d = 0.49$ on instructional practice and $d = 0.18$ on student achievement. Those are real numbers. Coaching means a peer practitioner with additional training working alongside a teacher in her actual classroom, with her actual students, on a problem she is actually trying to solve — over months, through cycles of practice, observation, feedback, and revision. The unit is not the workshop hour. It is the cycle.

The third is *credentialed competence.* A teacher completes a graduate-level program in a specific area of practice — a reading specialist endorsement, a National Board certification — and earns a credential that signals demonstrated capacity and that updates as the field evolves. This layer barely exists in U.S. teaching at any meaningful scale. National Board certification reaches roughly four percent of the teaching workforce. There is no AI credential. There is no specialization track for any AI tool, any AI pedagogy, or any of the discipline-specific applications a serious teacher would want to know how to do. For the AI question, the credential layer is simply empty.

These are not three names for one thing. They are three different interventions with three different evidence bases. The compliance layer barely works. The coaching layer works. The credential layer would work if it existed. When a district announces it is "investing in AI professional development," the announcement does not specify which layer it means. The answer, almost universally, is the layer the literature has already told us does not produce sustained practice change.

Medicine has all three. Teaching has, at scale, only the one that does not work.

## What medicine actually built

I want to be specific about the structure of continuing medical education, because the analogy this chapter is making depends on what is actually inside it rather than a gesture toward it.

The first layer is *state CME for licensure.* Every U.S. state medical board sets a minimum number of continuing education hours a physician must accumulate to renew her license. Most states fall somewhere between twenty and fifty hours per year — the fifty-hour figure is the high end, not the average. This looks, on the surface, like what teaching has.

But medicine's compliance layer has done something teaching's has not. Over the last fifteen years, most state medical boards added *content-specific mandates* on top of the base hours. Opioid prescribing. Suicide assessment. Implicit bias. Pain management. Child abuse identification. Each addition is the regulatory infrastructure recognizing that the field has changed enough that licensure requirements should change with it. The mechanism exists to update what physicians must know as practice evolves.

Teaching's state-licensure systems almost never use this mechanism. Reading-science reforms transformed early literacy instruction over a decade without triggering mandatory reading-science hours for elementary teachers in most states. Generative AI has been in classrooms since 2023 without triggering mandatory AI hours in any state I am aware of. The compliance infrastructure exists in teaching. The content-update mechanism on top of it almost does not.

The second layer — and this is the layer that has no equivalent in teaching at all — is the *ACCME-accredited activity infrastructure.* The Accreditation Council for Continuing Medical Education, founded in its current form in 1981, accredits the providers who deliver educational activities and audits them for outcomes. Their 2023 Annual Data Report covers more than 247,000 educational activities in that calendar year, generating roughly 1.3 million AMA Category 1 Credits, with about 68 million learner interactions across the system. Total reported income across accredited providers reached $3.7 billion in 2024.

The numbers are not the point. The structure is. ACCME-accredited activities are required to assess competence at escalating levels: almost all assess whether the learner has acquired new knowledge; nearly half assess whether the learner's clinical performance changed afterward; and a meaningful fraction track actual patient health outcomes. These are independently audited. The system is not asking whether the doctor showed up. It is asking whether she learned, whether her practice changed, and whether her patients got better.

Teaching has no equivalent. The closest thing is the small handful of randomized trials of teacher coaching programs that produced Kraft's effect sizes — research artifacts, not infrastructure. There is no ongoing independent audit of whether the workshops the average American teacher attends produce learning, practice change, or student outcome improvement. The audit does not exist because the activity infrastructure that would be audited does not exist.

The third layer is *Maintenance of Certification* — the specialty-board layer that adds higher-stakes content requirements on top of everything else. The American Board of Family Medicine's current five-year cycle requires sixty certification points, two hundred continuing medical education credits, and twenty-five quarterly knowledge-assessment questions. Not a half-day exam every five years. Twenty-five questions per quarter, low-stakes per question, embedded in normal practice, written by content experts who update them as the field changes. The opposite, in every structural dimension, of what most American teachers experience as professional learning.

Medicine did not arrive at this three-layer system fully formed. It was built incrementally over fifty years, in response to specific failure modes — physicians whose knowledge had drifted, specialty areas that evolved faster than initial training could track, patient harm that was traceable to outdated practice. Teaching has the same failure modes, on a slower and harder-to-attribute curve. It has not built the infrastructure.

## Why coaching works when workshops don't

The Kraft finding is real. But the mechanism behind it matters as much as the number, because the mechanism is what makes the recommendation follow from the evidence rather than from the analogy.

Picture the teacher who just attended two hours of workshop on AI-assisted formative feedback. She has taken notes. She watched the presenter model the move on a contrived example. She knows, abstractly, what it looks like. She returns on Monday morning to twenty-seven seventh graders, three of whom are absent, two of whom are escalating an argument about a YouTube video, one of whom is in tears because his grandmother died on Saturday. She does not get to do the abstract version of the move. She has to do *the version of the move that survives the room she is actually standing in.* Almost nothing in the workshop prepared her for that translation.

The translation is where most workshop-based professional development evaporates.

Coaching closes the distance. The coach is in the room, or available on a same-day cycle. She watches the teacher try the move on real students. She sees what failed. She helps debug it in the next planning period, before the next attempt. The unit of learning is not the abstract move. It is the move-as-it-survives-this-room. The Kraft meta-analysis finds this moves instructional practice by nearly half a standard deviation. The student-achievement effect is smaller — $d = 0.18$ — because changing what a teacher does is upstream of changing what students learn, and the translation has its own losses. But $d = 0.18$ from a sustainable, professionally-led intervention is a real number. By comparison, the What Works Clearinghouse synthesis of high-impact tutoring produces effects in a similar range.

One more finding from the Kraft meta-analysis deserves its own sentence. *More coaching hours did not reliably produce larger effects.* Above a threshold of roughly ten contact hours, additional time did not predictably translate into additional gain. What mattered was the structure — the cycles, the content focus, the embeddedness in actual classroom practice, the focused-problem framing — rather than the raw hours. Add more time to a poorly structured intervention and you get more of something that is not working. Restructure the intervention and you get the gain.

The lesson for the AI moment is precise: the answer is not to make the August workshop a full day instead of half a day, or to require three vendor demos a year instead of one. The answer is to put a trained AI coach in the building for cycles of structured work with grade-level teams across the school year. The intervention has a shape. Adding more of the wrong shape does not produce the right shape.

Laura Desimone named the structural features in 2009: content focus grounded in subject-matter expertise, active learning where teachers do the work rather than watch it, coherence with the curriculum, sufficient duration spread over time, and collective participation by teachers from the same school or department. The Learning Policy Institute's 2017 review added two more: models of effective practice, and coaching with cycles of feedback and reflection. Seven features. Every one maps onto what medical CME does at its best. Almost none describes what the typical U.S. district one-shot AI workshop does.

The gap between what the literature says works and what the field actually funds is not a research gap. It is a procurement gap.

## Finland's experiment

The closest existing implementation of what this chapter is arguing for is Finland's *tutoropettaja* — tutor teacher — system. It is worth describing carefully, including its limits.

Beginning around 2017, the Finnish National Agency for Education funded a national network of tutor teachers with an initial allocation of 7.5 million euros, scaling to roughly 23 million euros across the government period. The target was one tutor teacher for every 220 students, across all 2,500 of Finland's basic schools. A tutor teacher is a peer practitioner — a working classroom teacher with additional training — whose role is to support colleagues in her own school as they adopt new pedagogy and digital practices. The training is ongoing and embedded in school life rather than delivered as detached workshops. This is the Kraft mechanism, scaled to a national workforce, funded by the state.

The higher-end specialization that fed expert tutor teachers into this system was *DigiErko* — formally, *Oppiminen ja opettaminen digitaalisissa ympäristöissä*, "Learning and Teaching in Digital Environments." The name comes from *erikoistumiskoulutus*, a distinct credential type in the Finnish higher-education system for post-master's professional specialization — the structural analog of what specialty-board certification is in medicine. DigiErko was a sixty-credit university program, roughly equivalent to a year of full-time graduate study delivered part-time to working teachers. It required a teaching qualification plus three years of classroom experience as prerequisites — deliberately post-experience, not initial preparation. It was delivered through a consortium of three universities and a network of specialist teachers. Participants conducted and applied education research in their own classrooms as part of the credential.

Stack the layers against medicine's and the parallels are precise. Per-school in-service time corresponds to state CME hours — the compliance layer that exists in both systems. The tutor-teacher network corresponds to the ACCME activity infrastructure — the ongoing, structured, content-evolving learning that does move practice, funded as a public good. DigiErko corresponds to Maintenance of Certification — the formal credentialing track for specialization, university-issued, with required evidence of competence. Finland built all three at one quarter the per-capita scale of a U.S. equivalent, in roughly a decade.

The honest counter is that Finland is small, demographically more uniform than the United States, and differently structured at every layer. The point of citing Finland is not that the United States should adopt the Finnish design wholesale. It is that Finland's experiment validates a set of design principles — peer-practitioner training, government-funded mandate, university-credentialed specialization, in-service embedding rather than detached workshop delivery — that are the same design principles the U.S. evidence base independently identifies as the ones that work. The country is small. The design principles are not. They scale by being adopted, not by being reinvented from scratch.

## The money is already in the budget

The standard objection to building this infrastructure is cost. It would be expensive. It would also be substantially cheaper than what the field is already spending on things the evidence does not defend.

<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG (#18): $2.19B / ~$2.2B for Title II-A FY24 is well-anchored — multiple secondary sources (EdTech Magazine, NEA, Department of Education budget docs) confirm. The [verify] flag can be removed; figure is solid. -->
The federal funding stream designated for improving teacher quality is Title II, Part A of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act, currently funded at approximately $2.2 billion per year. [*verify: confirm exact FY 2024 appropriation before press.*] The State Educational Technology Directors Association's November 2025 report — *Improving Professional Learning Systems to Better Support Today's Educators* — surveyed twenty-four state education agencies and seventy-six local education agencies on how this money is spent. The findings are bracing.

Over sixty percent of Title II-A funds in 2022–23 were spent on professional development. The money is being spent in the category. But only nine states prioritized Title II-A funds for technology training. Fewer than forty percent of local education agencies used Title II-A funds to advance technology-related professional learning at all. The dominant form of spending was short-term workshops centered on a specific tool — exactly the structure the Yoon and Kraft literatures identify as the form that does not produce sustained practice change.

The federal infrastructure exists. The funding stream exists. The technical authority to use it for sustained, coaching-anchored, AI-specific professional development exists. Almost no district is exercising that authority.

Here is a useful comparison. Los Angeles Unified School District spent $5.2 million on Yondr phone pouches. That is approximately the cost of sustained AI-specific professional development for thousands of teachers at the per-teacher unit cost the coaching literature implies. The pouch line item is the coaching line item that was not funded. Houston ISD's $800,000 for phone storage solutions is the same trade in miniature. The $29 million New York City spent implementing the state phone ban is the same trade scaled up.

Every one of those expenditures is defensible on its own terms. None is the highest-leverage use of those dollars relative to what the evidence says moves teacher practice. The procurement system is rewarding the line item that looks like an intervention without producing the practice change that an intervention requires.

## Puerto Rico, read honestly

The Estudia Khanmigo pilot in Puerto Rico is currently the most-cited U.S. case study of a generative AI tutor deployed in a public-school setting with serious research observation. What the case study actually shows is different from what its citations tend to imply.

Digital Promise, working with the University of Toronto, the University of Puerto Rico, the Puerto Rico Department of Education, and Universidad del Sagrado Corazón, released its Estudia Khanmigo report in September 2024. The pilot, funded under the Gates Foundation's Setting the Right Models initiative, deployed Khanmigo in two Puerto Rico schools with an embedded implementation team. Teachers received training on lesson planning, monitoring student progress, and reviewing assignments.

The findings the report itself emphasizes are not findings about Khanmigo's pedagogical effectiveness. They are findings about *what failed before pedagogy could be tested.* Infrastructure barriers were as significant as pedagogical ones. Classrooms lacked reliable internet, electrical power, and devices. The centralized IT department could not coordinate with individual schools quickly enough to resolve technical issues. The research team's strongest published conclusion was that successful AI deployment required engaging staff, investing in learning, and building cross-departmental bridges *before* rollout, not after.

The pilot is a case study not of best practice but of what happens when an AI tool lands in classrooms without the supporting infrastructure of teacher capability and institutional readiness. The Digital Promise team named this conclusion directly. It is the conclusion this chapter has been building toward. RAND Europe made the same finding across six years of large-scale European trials, in language worth repeating: *implementation fidelity emerged as a critical factor; when teachers struggled with training, resources, or integration, impact evaporated.* Puerto Rico is one more instance of the same pattern. The infrastructure is the variable. The tool is downstream of it.

## What three other countries are trying

Three countries other than Finland have been building the infrastructure this chapter is arguing for. None has built it completely. Each demonstrates a piece of what a full design would include.

Singapore is the closest analog to medicine's model on the pre-service side. The National Institute of Education's AI initiative embeds AI competencies across all teacher education programs, with a Certificate in Artificial Intelligence for Education launched in July 2025. The Ministry of Education runs ongoing training on each new tool the system deploys. The structural commitment is that AI fluency is a *condition* of being a teacher, not a workshop you sometimes attend. The training is built into the pipeline, not bolted on after the fact.

Estonia has been the most aggressive in speed of national rollout. The AI Leap initiative, launched September 1, 2025 by the Estonian Ministry of Education and Research, scoped twenty thousand students and three thousand teachers in its pilot phase, with partnerships announced with both OpenAI and Anthropic. The initiative builds on the legacy of earlier Estonian digital-literacy programs that trained roughly seven thousand teachers in informatics over their lifespans. Whether the AI Leap delivers what its design promises is an open empirical question. The design itself is what a small country with strong centralized planning capacity produces when it decides to take generative AI seriously at the national level.

Australia has the longest-running clock-hour mandate at scale. All Australian teachers must complete a hundred hours of professional learning per five-year registration cycle. The Australian Framework for Generative AI in Schools, approved by Education Ministers in October 2023, articulates principles and resources for teachers to use. The hours requirement is real and licensure-linked. The AI-specific overlay is voluntary. Australia has the compliance infrastructure; it has not yet added the content mandate on top of it, in the way state medical boards added opioid-prescribing hours on top of base CME.

The United States is the outlier in both directions. It has the largest education sector by an order of magnitude — roughly 3.5 million teachers, fifty million students. It has the least developed national infrastructure for keeping those teachers current. There is no national AI-in-education initiative. There is no federally funded teacher AI credential. There is no continuing-education infrastructure parallel to the ACCME for teachers in any subject, let alone AI specifically. The country that produced ChatGPT and Claude and the global edtech industry has the least developed teacher continuing-education infrastructure of any comparably resourced education system.

## What the international observation evidence actually shows

Before turning to the objections, a piece of international evidence that sharpens what the U.S. infrastructure is actually being asked to do.

The OECD's *Global Teaching InSights* study (2020) — a multi-country video observation of secondary mathematics teaching across eight participating systems — coded classroom practice along three domains: classroom management, social-emotional support, and instructional quality. The cross-country pattern was consistent. Teachers were strongest on classroom management (mean ratings in the 3.49–3.81 range on the study's four-point rubric), moderate on social-emotional support (2.62–3.26), and weakest on instructional quality (1.74–2.24). The weakest sub-domains within instruction were quality of subject matter and cognitive engagement — exactly the dimensions that determine whether a lesson produces durable learning rather than completion. The United States was not in the GTI sample. The cross-national finding is suggestive of where the marginal investment in teacher development should land. It is not on management.

The OECD's *Teacher Knowledge Survey* (2024–2026), the more recent companion study, did include the United States. The headline finding for the U.S. is striking in both directions. The U.S. has the world's highest share of teachers at the *advanced* level of general pedagogical knowledge — fifty-one percent at Level 3 of the survey's scoring. The U.S. also has an above-average share at the *foundational* level — twenty percent at Level 1. The interquartile range on teacher knowledge in the U.S. is fifty-seven points, the largest variance of any country in the comparison set. American teaching is not weak on average. American teaching is *spread* — more excellent teachers than anywhere else and more foundational-level teachers than most peers, in the same labor force, often in the same district.

This variance is the case for the three-layer infrastructure this chapter is recommending. Average-quality interventions miss both tails. The bottom quartile needs the coaching layer that the medical analog of CME provides — sustained, embedded, content-specific support that meets teachers where they are. The top quartile needs the credentialing layer that medicine's specialty boards provide — formal recognition and post-experience specialization that gives the best teachers a defensible path to deeper expertise in the subject they are teaching. A single uniform PD requirement, the form that most current state systems take, serves neither tail and is part of why the existing spend produces so little movement. The variance is the case.

## The objections, taken seriously

Four objections to this chapter's argument are serious enough to engage on their own terms.

*Teachers are already overworked.* This is correct, and it is the most important constraint on what comes next. The U.S. teacher workday is at saturation. Adding mandatory hours without subtracting other duties, paying for the time, or restructuring the workweek to absorb them is not an investment in teaching. It is an additional load on a labor force already losing the workers most willing to leave for better-compensated jobs. The medical analogy works on this front only when the corresponding labor-market commitments come with it. Physicians get paid CME time in most employed settings. Their employers absorb most of the cost. A serious teacher-development infrastructure requires corresponding investments in compensation, scheduling, and workload reduction. Finland funded its tutor-teacher system with real money. The lesson is not that we should require teachers to do more. The lesson is that we should fund teachers to do better.

The cumulative-expansion argument from the curriculum-reform literature sharpens this objection further. The Center for Curriculum Redesign and OECD's own analysis of teacher workload both document a sequence of expanding demands: core academic content first, then skills and character and meta-learning, then smartphone policy, now AI, and AR/VR arriving behind it. Each wave arrives with its own professional development requirement. The OECD's 2021 *Making the Most of Teachers' Time* report locates the saturation not in instructional work but in administrative and compliance load — the work that expands to fill every hour not otherwise claimed. Both findings are real. The cumulative expansion is real. The PD infrastructure required to absorb it does not currently exist at scale.

The AI+1 training this chapter proposes adds load upfront — the coaching cycles, the credentialing hours, the sustained subject-specific development are real costs on a saturated workday. The honest question is not whether the load is real but how quickly it is repaid, and whether the teachers who absorb it become measurably better at the work that matters. Not every new demand on teachers carries the same payback period. Character education added load and paid it back slowly, through engagement gains that compounded over years. Social-emotional learning followed a similar arc. Smartphone policy added enforcement load with no clear payback mechanism. The AI+1 investment is different in one specific way: the administrative load reduction that funds the training time begins accruing before the training is complete. The 5.9 hours per week documented by Gallup-Walton and the 31 percent reduction in lesson-preparation time documented by the EEF randomized trial, both established in Chapter 6, are on the non-teaching side of the ledger — available in the first semester of use, not at the end of a multi-year credential program. No previous instructional technology in the PD literature has produced administrative load reduction at that speed or at that scale. The case for AI-specific PD is not that it costs nothing. It is that the payback period is short enough, and the reclaimed time substantial enough, that the trade is worth making — provided districts protect the reclaimed time as capacity rather than capturing it as cost savings. Strip that protection away, and the argument breaks.

*Teachers already do professional development — it is just badly designed.* This is also correct, and consistent with what the SETDA data show. The money is being spent in the category. The structural problem is not the absence of PD spending but the form the spending takes. The fix is not "add more required hours." It is "change what counts as fulfilling the requirement." Hours of sustained coaching, embedded in school life, content-specific, cohort-based, with cycles of practice and feedback — these should count. Hours of vendor-led demo workshops in August should count differently. The recommendation is structural, not additive.

*The medical CME system has documented quality and capture problems.* This is the most serious objection. Pharmaceutical company funding accounted for roughly a quarter of ACCME-system revenue in 2023. Conflict-of-interest disclosure has been contested for decades. Many physicians describe their CME as performative compliance. A parallel teaching system built without awareness of these failure modes would reproduce them. The edtech vendor money that would flow toward a teacher continuing-education system would distort it in exactly the way pharmaceutical money distorted medical CME in the 1990s. A district that lets a single edtech vendor certify its teachers as AI-trained is reproducing the worst pathology of the medical system, not the best. The honest recommendation is to copy the structural commitments the medical system embodies — mandatory, ongoing, cohort-based, content-evolving, audited for outcomes — while building with awareness of the failure modes: university-issued credentials rather than vendor-issued, independent accreditation, transparent conflict-of-interest rules from day one. This is harder than copying the system. It is the only version of the project worth running.

*Generative AI might be a passing fad.* This is possible. But even granting it, the broader claim — that teachers need continuous, structured, mechanism-focused training on the tools their students are using — survives whatever the specific tool turns out to be. The infrastructure this chapter is arguing for is infrastructure for continuous adaptation, not infrastructure for any particular product. Build it for the tool we have now. It will outlast the tool we have now. The cost of building it is paid back across whatever comes next. The cost of not building it is paid by every cohort of students whose teachers face a new tool with the preparation of an August demo.

## What this means Monday morning

The argument above is structural and national. The decisions that close the gap are local and immediate. Five things you can do this week, in roughly the order they take effect.

*First, audit your Title II-A spend.* Pull the line items for the current and past two fiscal years. Sort them by type: workshops under fourteen contact hours, workshops over fourteen hours, sustained coaching programs, credential subsidies, anything else. The Yoon threshold is the rough cutoff for "evidence-supported." Most districts find, when they do this audit, that more than half their PD spend falls below that line. The SETDA numbers say only nine states even prioritize this stream for technology learning at all, which means most districts have not been asked to think about the allocation. The audit is a one-week exercise. The conversation it enables — *we are funding the version of PD the literature says does not work, and we have the authority to redirect* — is the conversation that has to happen before anything else changes.

*Second, identify your tutor teachers.* You almost certainly have two or three teachers in your district who have, on their own time, become the people other teachers come to with AI questions. They taught themselves Claude or ChatGPT or Khanmigo. They figured out how to use AI in their own classrooms in ways that produce better student work rather than worse. They are doing the tutor-teacher job already, unpaid, on the side of their actual jobs. Find them. Pay them for the role. Cover one section of their teaching load with district funds and assign that time to coaching colleagues in the same building. The Finnish target ratio was one tutor teacher per 220 students. Start with one per school. You will know within a semester whether the coaching cycles are moving practice. The Kraft meta-analysis says they will.

*Third, subsidize one credential per school per year.* Find a teacher who wants to deepen her AI fluency to the credential level. Pay her tuition for a graduate-level program — a master's, a graduate certificate, an endorsement — in instructional technology, learning sciences, or AI for education at an accredited university. The cost per teacher runs approximately $5,000 to $15,000 per year of program. The pouch budget from Chapter 1 will buy twenty to fifty of these. The teacher comes back as a credentialed in-house specialist. The credential lives with her if she leaves, which means the labor-market signal is honest. This is the credentialed-competence layer of medicine, in miniature, at the per-building scale. Twenty districts doing this for ten years builds the cohort the country does not yet have. One district doing it for one year is the door.

*Fourth, stop using single-vendor August demos as your PD.* The two-and-a-half-hour vendor walkthrough is not an investment in teacher capacity. It is a procurement event misclassified as a learning event. If you license a tool, the vendor provides an introduction — that is a sales appointment. The PD line item should fund what your tutor teachers and credentialed specialists deliver to grade-level teams in cycles across the school year: content-focused, active, coherent with curriculum, sustained, cohort-based. This costs roughly what the vendor demos cost. The effect on practice is not the same.

*Fifth, write the educational-purposes exemption.* If your district is operating under a state phone ban, the educational exemption that the legislature handed back to your teachers is the policy surface where their judgment will actually operate. Convene a working group — including the tutor teachers you just identified — and draft a district-level operational definition of "educational purposes" specific enough that two teachers at different schools would reach the same defensible answer for the same case. Pilot it for one semester. Revise it. This is the version of professional judgment that survives contact with the room. The training is the precondition. The exemption is the surface where the training does its work.

None of these requires federal action or state legislation. None requires more money than you already have allocated to professional development under categories you are already spending. The trade — pouches for coaching, August demos for sustained credentialed practice change — is a trade your budget can make this fiscal year. The cohort of trained teachers the country needs cannot be built by Washington. It will be built by district leaders making this trade, school by school, until the cohort exists.

## The chapter's turn

Medicine did not arrive at its three-layer system fully formed. It was built across fifty years in response to specific failure modes — physicians whose knowledge had drifted past safety, specialty areas that evolved faster than initial training could keep up with, patient harm that was traceable in retrospect to outdated practice.

The failure modes for teaching are slower to surface and harder to attribute. A student who learns less because her teacher has not been trained on the AI tool her students are using does not show up in a malpractice statistic. She shows up two years later as a slightly lower score on a state assessment, or four years later as a slightly lower college-going rate, or eight years later as a slightly lower wage. The mechanism is the same. The visibility is not.

The pediatrician across the street is forty-one. She has another two decades of practice in her. The tools she will use in 2035 do not yet exist. The tools she used in 2015 are mostly obsolete. She works inside a system that treats her continuing competence as a professional and public obligation — funded, mandated, audited, updated as the field changes. She did not build that system. It was built for her, over fifty years, by people who decided that what physicians know matters enough to be a public responsibility rather than a private hobby.

The teacher across the street is forty-one. She has another two decades of practice in her. Her students — twenty-seven of them this period, a hundred and forty across the day — are not hypothetical. They are sitting in front of her right now. What she knows about the tools they are using will shape what happens to those students in ways that no phone pouch and no August vendor demo will touch.

We can build the system for her too. The reason we have not is not that we cannot. The reason is that no constituency with budget authority has yet decided that we should — decided it seriously enough to redirect the money that is already in the budget, already designated for professional learning, already theoretically in service of the students she is teaching now.

The next chapter turns to what trained teachers do with the discretion the law has handed them in the form of the educational-purposes exemption. That chapter is about the room and the call. This chapter has been about the precondition. The call cannot be made well by a teacher who has not been trained to make it. The room is where the training pays off. The system is what makes the training possible. And the system, right now, is the August demo.

## What the research leaves open

Four things this chapter has not settled.

The structural ceiling on professional development is real and it limits how much the recommendations here can deliver if they are layered onto a teacher workday that does not first shrink. Kraft's coaching meta-analysis suggests diminishing marginal returns on PD contact time above roughly ten well-structured hours; TNTP's *Mirage* (2015) found no evidence that more PD consistently produces more teacher improvement under prevailing conditions. The cleanest recommendation this chapter can make — restructure PD around coaching and credentials rather than seat-time workshops — only survives if the AI dividend documented in Chapter 6 is genuinely captured as teacher capacity rather than as district cost savings. The political economy of that capture is not solved by this chapter. A district that responds to teachers saving six hours a week by cutting six teaching positions or by expanding class sizes by ten percent is making the recommendation in this chapter fail before it has been tried. The harder problem is institutional: how to build the funding and contracting structures that make capacity reinvestment the default rather than cost extraction. That problem is not solved here. It is, candidly, the next chapter someone needs to write.



The translation from teacher practice change to student outcomes — the gap between Kraft's $d = 0.49$ on instruction and $d = 0.18$ on achievement — is well-documented but not well-explained. We know practice change is upstream of student change and that the translation has losses; we do not know which losses are intrinsic to the mechanism and which are artifacts of how practice change has been measured. A serious longitudinal study of cohort-based AI-specific coaching, with downstream student-outcome tracking, has not been published. Until it is, the case for the coaching layer rests on the broader literature and on inference from analog domains. The case is strong enough to act on. It is not yet strong enough to predict the exact magnitude of the effect a fully built infrastructure would produce.

The current status of Finland's DigiErko program past December 2024 is genuinely open. If a successor program is funded with an explicit generative-AI specialization, the international case for the credentialed-competence layer strengthens. If the program lapses without replacement, the lesson is that even Finland — with the strongest design principles validated and the public-funding mechanism in place — found the political economy of sustaining specialized teacher credentialing harder than the design principles alone predicted. Either outcome matters for the recommendation this chapter makes. [*verify: status of DigiErko or its successor as of press date.*]

And the question of how to insulate a parallel teaching continuing-education system from edtech vendor capture — the failure mode that medical CME spent decades developing imperfect defenses against — is not solved. The chapter has named the risk and proposed structural commitments that would mitigate it. Whether those commitments would survive the first decade of a real funding stream is a question the existing literature cannot answer. The best available move is to name the risk in advance and build in the structural features — independent accreditation, university-issued credentials, transparent conflict-of-interest rules from day one — that the medical experience suggests are necessary. Whether they are sufficient is a question the next decade will answer.

## Still puzzling

Why has the medical CME system held up as well as it has under fifty years of sustained pharmaceutical-industry funding pressure? The system is not pristine — the capture is real, the disclosure rules are imperfect, the failure modes are visible. But the system has not collapsed into pure vendor certification. Something in its design — the independent accreditation council, the layered structure with state boards and specialty boards each holding different leverage, the registration-fee revenue base, the public-facing audit of patient-outcome assessment — has kept it broadly honest. If teaching is going to build a parallel system, we should know which features of medicine's version did the most to keep it from collapsing under commercial pressure. The post-mortem on medicine's design, written in a form that a teaching-system builder could lift from directly, has not been written. It needs to be.
