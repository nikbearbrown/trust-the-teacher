# Chapter 8: Train Like a Doctor

*What medicine built for continuous professional development — and how to build it for teaching*

---

Picture a pediatrician in suburban Ohio in the spring of 2019. She is forty-one. She finished residency in 2009. She has been a board-certified physician for a decade. Every year, she completes between twenty and fifty hours of accredited continuing medical education, depending on what her state and her specialty board require that cycle. She does not get to choose whether. She gets to choose the content — within tracks her specialty board updates as the field changes. In 2017, when the opioid crisis became a clinical emergency, her state added mandatory hours on safe prescribing. In 2018, when the suicide rate among adolescents jumped, her state added screening and assessment hours. In 2020, when COVID changed everything, the activity infrastructure that produces those hours pivoted inside a quarter. She did not have to figure out how to keep her practice current. The infrastructure did it for her — funded by registration fees, audited for outcomes by an independent accreditation council, enforced by her state medical board, layered on top by her specialty board's Maintenance of Certification.

Now picture the teacher across the street. She is forty-one. She finished her credential in 2009. She has been a licensed elementary teacher for a decade. Every five years she renews her license by accumulating a clock-hour total that varies by state and that her district mostly fulfills by serving her one or two professional development days a year, plus whatever she logs from a summer institute she sometimes attends because she wants to. In 2017, when reading-science reforms began transforming early literacy instruction in other states, no agency required her to learn the new methods. In 2018, when one in four of her students arrived in fall with documented anxiety on their intake forms, no specialty board added mandatory hours on classroom mental health. In 2020, COVID changed everything for her too. Nothing in her renewal requirements changed in response. In 2023, ChatGPT entered her classroom through her students' phones. Her district sent her to a two-and-a-half-hour vendor demo in August. The presenter walked her through three tools. By the time she got back to her classroom the following Monday, her seventh graders were already using two tools the presenter had not mentioned and three the district had not licensed. Her preparation for what was about to happen in her room was the August demo. That was the preparation.

The pediatrician across the street could not lose her license for being two years behind on opioid prescribing. The teacher could not lose her license for being three model generations behind on generative AI. Both could lose students. Only one of them works inside a system that takes the second possibility as seriously as the first.

This is the chapter that names what teaching has not built and that medicine has, and that argues for building it — with the failure modes of medicine's version named in advance.

---

## The vague word the field is hiding behind

"Professional development" is the term every district uses, every state department of education uses, every federal funding stream uses, and almost every teacher I know quietly distrusts. It is a phrase that has been doing several jobs at once for so long that it has stopped doing any of them well.

I want to specify before I argue, because the argument turns on what we mean. Three different things wear the name *professional development* in K-12 schools, and the empirical literature tells us very different things about each.

The first is *clock-hour compliance.* This is the state-licensure version. A teacher renews her credential every three to five years by submitting evidence that she has accumulated some number of professional development hours — typically in a range from about a hundred to a hundred and fifty hours per cycle. The hours can be earned through almost anything labeled professional development, including the August vendor demo. The state's interest is in checking the box, not in checking what is in the box. The Yoon, Duncan, Lee, Scarloss, and Shapley [2007 Regional Educational Laboratory Southwest review](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED498548.pdf) — still the most-cited threshold paper in the field — looked at more than thirteen hundred candidate manuscripts, found nine that met What Works Clearinghouse evidence standards, and reported that PD programs with fewer than fourteen contact hours produced no detectable effect on student achievement. Most box-checking PD comes in well under fourteen hours. The compliance layer, taken on its own, is a layer that the research literature has already told us does not move student outcomes.

The second is *sustained coaching.* This is the version the research literature defends. [Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan's 2018 meta-analysis](https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/mkraft/files/kraft_blazar_hogan_2018_teacher_coaching.pdf) of sixty causal studies of teacher coaching produced pooled effects of *d* = 0.49 on instruction and *d* = 0.18 on student achievement. Coaching means a peer practitioner with additional training who works alongside the teacher in her actual classroom, with her actual students, on a problem she is actually trying to solve — repeated over months, with cycles of practice, observation, feedback, and revision. The unit of the intervention is not the workshop hour. It is the cycle. The Kraft meta-analysis surfaces a finding that is easy to miss and important to keep: *more coaching hours did not reliably translate into larger effects.* What translated was the structure — content focus, embeddedness in classroom practice, sustained over time, anchored in cycles of feedback the teacher could act on tomorrow morning. This is the version of PD that works. It is also the version that costs more, takes longer, and resists being delivered through a slide deck.

The third is *credentialed competence.* This is the version that barely exists in U.S. teaching at any meaningful scale. A teacher takes a graduate-level program in a specific area of practice — a reading specialist endorsement, a National Board certification, a master's in special education — and earns a credential that signals demonstrated capacity in that area. The credential is issued by a university or an independent professional body, takes a year or more of part-time study, requires evidence of practice change, and updates as the field evolves. National Board certification is the closest thing American teaching has to this layer. It reaches roughly four percent of the K-12 teaching workforce in any given year. There is no AI+1 equivalent. There is no specialization track for any AI tool, any AI pedagogy, or any of the discipline-specific applications of AI that a serious teacher would want to know how to do. The credential layer, for the AI question, is empty.

These are not three names for one thing. They are three different interventions with three different evidence bases. The compliance layer barely works. The coaching layer works. The credential layer would work if it existed. The reason this matters is that when a district announces it is "investing in AI professional development for teachers," the announcement does not specify which layer it means — and the answer, almost universally, is *the layer the literature already told us does not work.* The compliance layer is what the announcement funds. The coaching layer is what would change practice. The credential layer is what would scale.

Medicine has all three. Teaching has, at scale, only the one that does not work.

## What medicine actually built, in three layers

I want to be specific about what continuing medical education looks like, because the analogy this chapter is making depends on what is actually in the analog rather than on the gestural version of it.

The first layer is *state continuing medical education for licensure.* This is the compliance layer, and it is the layer that looks most like what teaching has. Every U.S. state medical board sets a minimum number of CME hours a physician must accumulate to renew her license. The [Federation of State Medical Boards' continuing medical education table](https://www.fsmb.org/siteassets/advocacy/key-issues/continuing-medical-education-by-state.pdf), maintained by the body that represents every state medical board, documents the variation. Most states fall between twenty and fifty hours per year, expressed as annual hours or as a cycle total — California requires fifty hours every two years, Illinois requires a hundred and fifty over three, Arkansas and Louisiana require twenty per year. The typical state lands closer to twenty-five hours per year than to fifty; the fifty-hour figure is the high end, not the average, and I want to label it as such before it does any rhetorical work in this chapter. [verify: confirm exact state-by-state distribution against the live FSMB PDF before press.]

But the compliance layer in medicine has done something teaching's compliance layer has not done. Over the last fifteen years, most state medical boards have added *content-specific mandates* on top of the base hours — opioid prescribing, suicide assessment, implicit bias, pain management, child abuse identification. Each addition is the regulatory infrastructure recognizing that a field has changed and that licensure requirements should change with it. The mechanism is in place to update what physicians must learn as practice evolves. Teaching's state-licensure systems almost never use this mechanism. Reading-science reforms swept through the field over a decade without triggering state-mandated reading-science hours for elementary teachers in most states. Generative AI has been in classrooms since 2023 without triggering state-mandated AI hours in any state I am aware of. The compliance infrastructure exists in teaching; the content-update mechanism on top of it almost does not.

The second layer is *the ACCME-accredited activity infrastructure.* This is the layer that has no analog in teaching at all, and it is the layer that does most of the work. The Accreditation Council for Continuing Medical Education, founded in its current form in 1981, accredits the providers who deliver CME activities and audits them for outcomes. Their [2023 Annual Data Report](https://accme.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/2023-ACCME-Annual-Data-Report-1045_20240815.pdf) — the cleanest set of numbers I can cite — covers more than 247,000 educational activities in calendar year 2023, generating roughly 1.3 million AMA PRA Category 1 Credits, with about 68 million learner interactions across the system. Total reported income across accredited providers was about $3.3 billion in 2023; the [2024 release](https://accme.org/news/accme-releases-2024-data-report-evolving-impact-in-a-shifting-landscape/) reports a record $3.7 billion for 2024. Roughly half of provider income comes from registration fees, about a quarter from commercial support, the remainder from advertising and other sources. [verify: 2024 figures against the full PDF before press.]

The numbers are not the point. The structure is. ACCME-accredited activities are required to assess competence at one of three escalating levels: ninety-five percent of activities assess whether the learner has acquired the new knowledge, forty-six percent assess whether the learner's clinical performance changed afterward, and eighteen percent track patient health outcomes. These are independently audited. The system is not asking whether the doctor showed up. It is asking whether she learned, whether her practice changed, and whether her patients got better. Teaching has no equivalent. The closest thing is the small handful of randomized trials of teacher coaching programs that produced the Kraft et al. effect sizes — research artifacts, not infrastructure. There is no ongoing independent audit of whether the workshops the average American teacher attends produce learning, practice change, or student outcome improvement. The audit does not exist because the activity infrastructure that would be audited does not exist.

The third layer is *Maintenance of Certification.* This is the specialty-board layer, and it is the layer that adds higher-stakes content requirements on top of the activity infrastructure. The American Board of Internal Medicine's [MOC requirements](https://www.abim.org/maintenance-of-certification/moc-requirements/) call for one hundred MOC points every five years, plus a knowledge assessment on a longitudinal or one-day basis, plus ongoing professionalism compliance. The American Board of Family Medicine's [five-year cycle](https://www.theabfm.org/continue-certification/5-year-cycle/), effective January 2025, requires sixty certification points (Self-Assessment plus Performance Improvement), two hundred CME credits over the cycle, twenty-five quarterly exam questions or a one-day exam, and an annual fee. The longitudinal assessment is the mechanism worth dwelling on. Twenty-five questions per quarter, low-stakes per question, embedded in normal practice, written by content experts who update them as the field changes. The opposite of a half-day workshop. The opposite, in every structural dimension, of what most American teachers experience as their professional learning.

The honest reading is that medicine's three-layer system did not arrive fully formed. It was built incrementally over fifty years in response to specific failure modes — physicians whose knowledge had drifted, specialty areas that evolved faster than initial training could keep up with, patient harm that was traceable to outdated practice. Teaching has the same failure modes, on a slower and harder-to-attribute curve. It has not built the infrastructure.

## Why coaching works when workshops don't

I want to spend one section unpacking the mechanism behind the Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan finding, because the mechanism is what makes the chapter's recommendation follow from the evidence rather than from the analogy.

The Yoon 2007 review found that PD averaging about forty-nine hours of contact, spread over six to twelve months, produced an average effect on student achievement of about *d* = 0.54. PD lasting less than fourteen hours produced no detectable effect. The threshold finding is the one that gets cited. The duration finding — *spread over six to twelve months* — is the one that does the real work. Fourteen hours of contact in a single day is not the same intervention as fourteen hours spread over six months. The literature does not yet support the first as moving practice. It supports the second.

Why? The answer is the cognitive distance between learning something in a session and doing it in a classroom. A teacher who has just spent two hours in a workshop on AI-assisted formative feedback knows, abstractly, what the move looks like. She has watched the presenter model it on a contrived example. She has taken notes. She returns to her classroom on Monday morning and faces twenty-seven seventh graders, three of whom are absent, two of whom are escalating an argument about a YouTube video, one of whom is in tears because his grandmother died on Saturday, and the rest of whom are watching her to see whether today is going to be a real class or a holding pattern. She does not get to do the abstract version of the move. She has to do *the version of the move that survives the room she is actually standing in.* Almost nothing in the workshop prepared her for that translation. The translation is where most workshop-based PD evaporates.

Coaching closes the distance. The coach is in the room, or in the room next door, or available on a same-day cycle. She watches the teacher try the move on real students. She sees what went wrong. She helps the teacher debug it on the spot, or with the next class, or in the planning period before the next attempt. The unit of learning is not the abstract move. It is the move-as-it-survives-this-room. The Kraft meta-analysis finds that this kind of structured cycle moves teacher practice by about half a standard deviation. The student-achievement effect is smaller, about *d* = 0.18, because changing what a teacher does is upstream of changing what students learn and the translation has its own losses. But *d* = 0.18 from a sustainable, scalable, professionally-led intervention is a real number. By comparison, the [What Works Clearinghouse synthesis of high-impact tutoring](https://ies.ed.gov/use-work/resource-library/report/practice-guide/high-impact-tutoring) produces effects in a similar range. By comparison, the *d* = 0.10 to *d* = 0.20 range is what large randomized educational evaluations of any kind tend to produce at scale.

The Kraft meta-analysis surfaces one more finding worth holding. *More coaching hours did not reliably scale with larger effects.* Above a threshold of about ten contact hours, additional time did not predictably translate into additional gain. This is not a finding that less coaching is better. It is a finding that what matters is the structure — the cycles, the content focus, the embeddedness, the focused-problem framing — rather than the raw hours. Add hours to the same poorly structured intervention and you do not get a better intervention. Restructure the intervention and you get the gain. The lesson for the AI moment: the answer is not to make the August workshop a full day instead of half a day, or to require three workshops a year instead of one. The answer is to put a trained AI coach in the building for cycles of structured work with grade-level teams over the school year. The intervention has a shape. Adding more of the wrong shape does not get you to the right shape.

[Desimone (2009)](https://isidore.udayton.edu/access/content/group/48d85ee6-68d7-4a63-ac4e-db6c0e01d494/EDT650/readings/Desimone_Laura_M.pdf) named the structural features that the empirical literature identifies as making PD work: content focus (grounded in subject-matter expertise, not generic strategy), active learning (teachers doing the work, not watching it), coherence (aligned with curricula and school priorities), sufficient duration (sustained, not single sessions), and collective participation (teachers from the same school, grade, or department learning together). The [Learning Policy Institute's 2017 review](https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/sites/default/files/product-files/Effective_Teacher_Professional_Development_REPORT.pdf) added two more — models of effective practice and coaching/expert support with feedback and reflection. Seven features. Every one of them maps onto what medical CME does at its best. Almost none of them describe what the typical U.S. district one-shot workshop on AI does. The gap between what the literature says works and what the field actually funds is not a research gap. It is a procurement gap.

## Finland's tutor teachers, and what their experiment validated

Finland's version of the coaching layer is the closest existing implementation of what this chapter is arguing for. It is worth describing carefully, including its limits.

Beginning in approximately 2017, the Finnish National Agency for Education (Opetushallitus) funded a national *tutoropettaja* — tutor teacher — system. The [Finnish National Agency for Education's 2018 English-language summary](https://www.oph.fi/sites/default/files/documents/195451_oph_faktaa_express_3c_2018_englanti_sivut.pdf) documents the structure. An initial allocation of 7.5 million euros, scaling to roughly 23 million euros across the government period, supported a target of one tutor teacher for every 220 students across all 2,500 basic schools. The tutor teacher is a peer practitioner — a working classroom teacher with additional training — whose role is to support colleagues in their own school as they adopt new pedagogy and digital practices. The training is in-service, ongoing, and embedded in school life rather than delivered as detached workshops. It is the Kraft mechanism, scaled to a national workforce, funded by the state.

The higher-end specialization that fed expert tutor teachers into this system was *DigiErko* — formally, *Oppiminen ja opettaminen digitaalisissa ympäristöissä*, "Learning and Teaching in Digital Environments." DigiErko was a sixty-ECTS-credit university specialization program, roughly equivalent to a year of full-time graduate study, delivered part-time over a longer period to working teachers across all levels from early childhood through university. It required a teacher's pedagogical qualification plus three years of teaching experience as prerequisites — deliberately post-experience, not initial preparation. It was delivered through a consortium of three universities (Eastern Finland, Helsinki, and Turku) together with the [Innokas Network](https://www.innokas.fi/en/training/) of specialist teachers. It was research-based, in the European sense — participants conducted and applied education research in their own classrooms as part of the credential. *Erko* comes from *erikoistumiskoulutus*, a distinct credential type in the Finnish higher-education system for post-master's professional specialization. So DigiErko was, in the Finnish system, the structural analog of what specialty board certification is in medicine: a formal credentialing pathway, university-issued, sitting outside the basic licensure track.

I want to use past tense advisedly. According to the [University of Eastern Finland's English-language program page](https://uefconnect.uef.fi/en/digierko-learning-and-teaching-in-digital-environments/), the most recent DigiErko cohort completed in December 2024. Whether the program will be re-funded in some new form — perhaps with an explicit generative-AI specialization built in — is open as of the date of this chapter. [verify: current status of DigiErko before press; check whether a 2025 or 2026 cohort has been announced.] The point of citing it is not that Finland is currently running this thing. The point is that Finland *ran* it for about seven years, validated the design principles, and dispersed its graduates back into Finnish schools as tutor teachers — where the broader system continues to operate. The experiment ran long enough to demonstrate what it demonstrates. What comes next for Finland is open. What is settled about the design is not.

Stack the layers next to medicine's and the parallels are precise. Per-school in-service time corresponds to state CME hours — the compliance layer that exists in both systems and that mostly does not move outcomes on its own. The tutor teacher system corresponds to the ACCME activity infrastructure — the layer that produces the ongoing, structured, content-evolving learning that does move outcomes, funded as a public good rather than as a side commitment of individual teachers. DigiErko corresponds to specialty-board Maintenance of Certification — the formal credentialing track for specialization, university-issued, with required evidence of competence. Finland built all three, at one quarter the per-capita scale of a U.S. equivalent, in roughly a decade. The United States has only the first.

The honest counter is that Finland is small, demographically more uniform than the United States, and that its education system is differently structured at every layer. The point of citing Finland is not that the United States should literally adopt the Finnish design. It is that Finland's experiment validates a set of design principles — peer-practitioner training, government-funded mandate, university-credentialed specialization, in-service embedding rather than detached workshop delivery — that are the same design principles the U.S. evidence base independently identifies as the ones that work. The country is small. The design principles are not. They scale by being adopted, not by being reinvented from scratch for every school system.

## The money is already in the budget

I want to make the cost argument now, because the standard objection to building this infrastructure is that it would be expensive. It would be. It would also be substantially cheaper than what the field is already spending on things that the evidence does not defend.

The federal funding stream specifically designated for improving teacher quality is Title II, Part A of the Elementary and Secondary Education Act. It is currently funded at approximately $2.2 billion per year, according to the [Education Week coverage](https://www.edweek.org/technology/billions-of-federal-dollars-are-spent-on-teacher-training-less-than-half-goes-to-tech-pd/2025/11) of the most recent SETDA analysis. [verify: confirm the exact FY 2024 appropriation against the Ed.gov page before press.] The State Educational Technology Directors Association's [November 2025 report](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf) — *Improving Professional Learning Systems to Better Support Today's Educators* — surveyed twenty-four state education agencies and seventy-six local education agencies on how this money is spent. The findings are bracing.

Over sixty percent of Title II-A funds in 2022–23 were spent on professional development. The money is being spent in the category. But only *nine states* prioritized Title II-A funds for technology training. Fewer than forty percent of local education agencies used Title II-A funds to advance technology-related professional learning at all. The dominant form of spending was short-term workshops centered on a specific tool — exactly the structure that the Yoon and Kraft literatures identify as the form that does not produce sustained practice change. The federal infrastructure exists. The funding stream exists. The technical authority to use it for sustained, coaching-anchored, AI-specific PD exists. Almost no district is exercising that authority.

The framing in the broader edtech literature has a name that I want to introduce with appropriate hedging. There is a widely cited rule-of-thumb in K-12 technology budgeting that the visible hardware cost of an edtech program — the devices, the licenses, the installation — represents only about thirty percent of total lifecycle cost; the remaining seventy percent is the training, support, integration, refresh, and professional development that determine whether the hardware gets used. [verify: I cannot locate a specific WestEd publication that articulates this as a numbered rule; the formulation appears in industry IT-budgeting analyses and is consistent with WestEd's broader [insistence on true-cost accounting for edtech](https://www.wested.org/wested-bulletin/insights-impact/schools-and-districts-consider-true-cost-edtech/), but the attribution as a "WestEd 30/70 rule" should be either traced to a primary citation or reframed before press.] Most districts spend their edtech budgets the opposite way: most of the visible line item goes to the hardware, very little to the soft costs that determine whether the hardware produces learning. This is the trade that the SETDA data describes. The trade has been visible to the field for at least a decade. It is the trade the field keeps making anyway.

The pouch budget from Chapter 1 is one example of this trade. The Los Angeles Unified School District's $5.2 million for Yondr phone pouches is approximately the cost of providing sustained AI-specific professional development for thousands of teachers at the kind of per-teacher unit cost the coaching literature implies. The pouch line item is the coaching line item that was not funded. The Houston ISD $800,000 for phone storage solutions is the same trade in miniature. The NYC $29 million for implementation of the state phone ban is the same trade scaled up. Every one of these expenditures is defensible on its own merits. None of them is the highest-leverage use of those dollars relative to what the evidence says moves teacher practice. The procurement system is rewarding the line item that looks like an intervention without producing the practice change that an intervention requires.

## The Puerto Rico pilot, read honestly

I want to spend one section on the Estudia Khanmigo pilot in Puerto Rico, because it is currently the most-cited U.S. case study of a generative-AI tutor deployed in a public-school setting with serious research observation, and because what the case study actually shows is different from what its citations imply.

Digital Promise, working with the University of Toronto, the University of Puerto Rico, the Puerto Rico Department of Education, and Universidad del Sagrado Corazón, released its [Estudia Khanmigo report](https://digitalpromise.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/SRM-Gates-Khanmigo-Report-Final.pdf) in September 2024. The pilot, funded under the Gates Foundation's Setting the Right Models initiative, deployed Khanmigo in two Puerto Rico schools with an embedded implementation team. Teachers received regular online training covering lesson planning, monitoring student progress, reviewing assignments, and addressing common fears about AI. [verify: I have not been able to confirm the often-cited "2.5-hour training session" specification from the publicly accessible portions of the report; the figure may appear in the full PDF and should be checked against it before any specific number is used in print.]

The findings the report itself emphasizes are not findings about Khanmigo's pedagogical effectiveness. They are findings about *what failed before pedagogy could be tested.* Infrastructure barriers were as significant as pedagogical ones. Classrooms lacked reliable internet, electrical power, and devices. The centralized IT department could not coordinate with individual schools quickly enough to resolve technical issues. The research team's strongest published conclusion was that successful AI deployment required engaging staff, investing in learning, and building cross-departmental bridges *before* rollout, not after. The pilot is a case study not of best practice but of what happens when an AI tool lands in classrooms without the supporting infrastructure of teacher capability and institutional readiness. The Digital Promise team named this conclusion directly. It is the conclusion this chapter has been building toward.

The honest reading of Puerto Rico is the same as the honest reading of the SETDA data. The tool was deployed. The infrastructure to make the tool work was not built. The outcomes followed the infrastructure rather than the tool. The pattern is consistent enough across enough cases — the [RAND Europe synthesis](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2026/01/harnessing-the-benefits-of-edtech-what-research-tells.html) made the same finding across six years of large-scale European trials, in the language Chapter 4 cited: *implementation fidelity emerged as a critical factor; when teachers struggled with training, resources, or integration, impact evaporated.* Puerto Rico is one more instance. The infrastructure is the variable. The tool is downstream.

## What the international comparison actually shows

Three countries other than Finland have been visibly building the infrastructure this chapter is arguing for. None of the three has built it completely. Each demonstrates a piece of what a full design would include.

Singapore is the closest analog to the medicine model on the pre-service side. The [National Institute of Education's AI@NIE initiative](https://www.ntu.edu.sg/nie/aied) embeds AI competencies across all teacher education programs on a multi-year plan to 2026. Pre-service *and* in-service teachers receive training in foundational AI principles. NIE launched a Certificate in Artificial Intelligence for Education in July 2025. The Ministry of Education's EdTech division runs ongoing training on each new tool the system deploys. The structural commitment is that AI fluency is a *condition* of being a teacher, not a workshop you sometimes attend. The training is built into the pipeline, not bolted on after the fact.

Estonia has been the most aggressive in the speed of national rollout. The [AI Leap (TI Hüpe) Initiative](https://www.hm.ee/en/news/estonia-announces-groundbreaking-national-initiative-ai-leap-programme-bring-ai-tools-all), launched September 1, 2025 by the Estonian Ministry of Education and Research, scoped twenty thousand students and three thousand teachers in its pilot phase, with partnerships announced with OpenAI and Anthropic. The initiative is built on the legacy of the Tiger Leap and ProgeTiger programs of the 1990s and 2010s, which trained roughly seven thousand Estonian teachers in informatics and digital literacy over their lifespans. Whether the AI Leap delivers what its design promises is an open empirical question. The design itself is worth naming as the version of the question a small country with strong centralized planning capacity asks when it decides to take generative AI seriously at the national level.

Australia has the longest-running clock-hour mandate at scale. All Australian teachers must complete one hundred hours of professional learning per five-year registration cycle — about twenty hours per year on average. The [Australian Framework for Generative AI in Schools](https://www.aitsl.edu.au/tools-resources/resource/the-australian-framework-for-generative-artificial-intelligence-(ai)-in-schools), approved by Education Ministers in October 2023, articulates six principles and provides resources for teachers to use. The hours requirement is real and licensure-linked. The AI-specific overlay is voluntary. Australia has the compliance infrastructure; it has not yet added the content mandate on top, in the way state medical boards have added opioid-prescribing hours on top of base CME.

The United States is the outlier in both directions on this comparison. It has the largest education sector by an order of magnitude — roughly 3.5 million teachers, roughly 50 million students. It has the least national infrastructure for keeping its teachers current. State PD hour requirements vary widely and are typically compliance-oriented. Federal Title II-A is the only national stream for teacher development, and the SETDA data describe how little of it currently flows toward what the evidence supports. There is no national AI-in-education initiative. There is no federally funded teacher AI credential. There is no continuing-education infrastructure parallel to the ACCME for teachers in any subject, let alone for AI specifically. The country that produced ChatGPT, Claude, and the global edtech industry has the least developed teacher continuing-education infrastructure of any comparably resourced education system.

## The counter-arguments, taken seriously

Four objections to the argument I am making are serious enough to engage on their own terms.

*Teachers are already overworked; mandating more hours is a salary cut and a path to attrition.* This is correct, and it is the most important constraint on what comes next. The U.S. teacher workday is at saturation. Adding ten or twenty mandatory hours per year of "professional development" without subtracting other duties, paying for the time, or restructuring the workweek to absorb it is not an investment in teaching. It is an additional load on a labor force that is already losing the workers most willing to leave for better-compensated, less-saturated jobs. The medical analogy works on this front only when the corresponding labor-market commitments come with it. Physicians get paid CME time in most employed settings; their employers absorb most of the cost; the labor-market price of being a physician already includes the continuing-education load. A serious teacher-development infrastructure would require corresponding investments in compensation, in scheduling, and in workload reduction. Finland funded its tutoropettaja system with real money. The lesson is not that we should require teachers to do more. The lesson is that we should fund teachers to do better, and that the existing Title II-A stream is the place to start.

*Teachers already do continuous PD — it is just badly designed.* This is also correct, and it is consistent with what the SETDA data show. More than sixty percent of Title II-A funds were spent on professional development in 2022–23. The money is being spent in the category. The structural problem is not the absence of PD spending but the form the spending takes. The fix is not "add more required hours" but "change what counts as fulfilling the requirement." Hours of sustained coaching, embedded in school life, content-specific, cohort-based, with cycles of practice and feedback — these should count. Hours of vendor-led demo workshops in August should count less, or count differently. The recommendation this chapter is making is structural, not additive. Build the layer that works. Fund what the evidence already tells us moves practice. Stop funding the layer that already had a chance to prove it could move practice and could not.

*The medical CME system has documented quality and capture problems; replicating it might just create new compliance theater.* This is the most serious objection, and the one this chapter takes most seriously. Pharmaceutical company funding accounted for about a quarter of ACCME-system revenue in 2023. Conflict-of-interest disclosure has been the subject of decades of contentious negotiation between accreditors, providers, and regulators. Many physicians describe their CME as performative compliance. A parallel teaching system built without awareness of these failure modes would reproduce them. The edtech vendor money that would flow toward a parallel teaching CME system would distort it in exactly the way pharma money distorted medical CME in the 1990s. A district that lets a single edtech vendor — Khanmigo, MagicSchool, any of the others — *certify* its teachers as AI-trained is reproducing the worst pathology of the medical system, not the best. The honest recommendation is to copy the *structural commitments* the medical system embodies (mandatory, ongoing, cohort-based, content-evolving, audited for outcomes) while building it with awareness of the failure modes — university-issued credentials rather than vendor-issued, independent accreditation, transparent conflict-of-interest rules from day one. This is harder than just copying the system. It is also the only version of the project worth running.

*Generative AI might be a passing fad; building credentialing around it is premature.* This is possible. It is not what I think. But even granting it for the sake of argument, the broader claim — that teachers need continuous, structured, mechanism-focused training on the tools their students are using — survives whether the specific tool in 2030 is generative AI or its successor. The infrastructure this chapter is arguing for is *infrastructure for continuous adaptation,* not infrastructure for any specific tool. Build it for the tool we have now. It will outlast the tool we have now. The cost of building it is paid back across whatever tools come next. The cost of not building it is paid by every cohort of students whose teachers are facing a new tool with the preparation of an August demo.

## What this means Monday morning

I want to address the rest of this section to the principal or district-leader reader directly, because the gap between this chapter's argument and Monday morning is the gap where most book recommendations die. The argument here is structural and national. The decisions that close the gap are local and specific. Five things you can do this week, in roughly the order they will take effect.

First, *audit your Title II-A spend.* Pull the line items for the current and past two fiscal years. Sort them by type: workshops under fourteen contact hours, workshops over fourteen hours, sustained coaching programs, credential subsidies, anything else. The Yoon threshold is the rough cutoff for "evidence-supported." Most districts find, when they do this audit, that more than half their PD spend falls below the threshold. The SETDA numbers say only nine states even prioritize this stream for technology learning at all, which means most districts have not been asked to think about the allocation. You can think about the allocation. The audit is a one-week exercise for one staff member with access to the budget. The conversation it enables — *we are funding the version of PD the literature says does not work, and we have the technical authority to redirect* — is the conversation that has to happen before any of the rest of this changes.

Second, *identify your tutor teachers.* You almost certainly have two or three teachers in your district who have, on their own, become the people other teachers come to with AI questions. They taught themselves Claude or ChatGPT or Khanmigo or all three. They have figured out how to use AI in their own classroom in ways that produce better student work rather than worse. They are doing the tutor-teacher job already, unpaid, for free, on the side of their actual jobs. Find them. Pay them for the role. Cover one section of their teaching load with district funds and assign that time to coaching colleagues in the same building. The Finnish ratio target was one tutor teacher per 220 students. Start with one per school. You will know within a semester whether the coaching cycles are moving practice. The Kraft meta-analysis says they will. Your own teachers will tell you whether the math works in your building.

Third, *subsidize one credential per school per year.* Pick a teacher who wants to deepen her AI fluency to the credential level. Pay her tuition for a graduate-level program — a master's, a graduate certificate, an endorsement — in instructional technology, learning sciences, or AI for education at an accredited university. The cost per teacher is on the order of $5,000 to $15,000 per year of program. The pouch budget from Chapter 1 will buy you twenty to fifty of these. The teacher comes back as a credentialed in-house specialist; the credential lives with her if she leaves, which means the labor-market signal is honest. This is the credentialed-competence layer of medicine, in miniature, at the per-building scale. Twenty districts doing this for ten years builds the cohort the country does not yet have. One district doing it for one year is the door.

Fourth, *stop using single-vendor August demos as your PD.* The two-and-a-half-hour vendor walkthrough is not an investment in teacher capacity. It is a procurement event misclassified as a learning event. If you license a tool, the vendor should provide an introduction; that is a sales appointment, not professional development. The PD line item should fund what your tutor teachers and credentialed specialists deliver to grade-level teams in cycles over the school year, on tools your teachers are actually using with their actual students, using the structure Desimone and the Learning Policy Institute identified — content-focused, active, coherent with curriculum, sustained, cohort-based. The cost of this is roughly the cost of the vendor demos. The effect on practice is not.

Fifth, *write the educational-purposes exemption that Chapter 1 named.* If your district is operating under a state phone ban, the educational exemption that the legislature handed back to your teachers is the policy surface where their judgment will actually operate. Convene a working group of teachers, including the tutor teachers you just identified, and draft a district-level operational definition of "educational purposes" specific enough that two teachers at different schools in your district would reach the same defensible answer for the same case. Pilot it for one semester. Revise it. This is the version of professional judgment that survives contact with the room. The training is the precondition. The exemption is the surface where the training does its work.

None of these requires federal action. None requires state legislation. None requires more money than you already have allocated to professional development under categories you are already spending it on. The decisions are at your level. The trade — pouches for coaching, August demos for sustained credentialed practice change, ambient AI exposure for trained teacher discretion — is a trade your budget can make this fiscal year. The cohort of trained teachers the country needs cannot be built by Washington. It will be built by district leaders making this trade, school by school, until the cohort exists.

## The chapter's turn

Medicine did not arrive at its three-layer continuing-education system fully formed. It was built incrementally over fifty years in response to specific failure modes — physicians whose knowledge had drifted past safety, specialty areas that evolved faster than initial training could keep up with, patient harm that was traceable in retrospect to outdated practice. The failure modes for teaching are slower to surface and harder to attribute. A student who learns less because her teacher has not been trained on the AI tool that student is using does not show up in a malpractice statistic. She shows up two years later as a slightly lower score on a state assessment, or four years later as a slightly lower college-going rate, or eight years later as a slightly lower wage. The mechanism is the same. The visibility is not.

The argument of this chapter is that we should stop waiting for the failure modes to be visible enough to force the infrastructure to exist. We already have the evidence. The Kraft meta-analysis says coaching moves practice. The SETDA report says the money to fund it is sitting in the budget unspent. The Finnish experiment says the design principles scale. The Puerto Rico case says the cost of not building it is paid by the students in classrooms where the tools landed without the training. The medical analog says infrastructure of this shape, at this scale, is possible — and shows, by the parts of itself that have gone wrong, what to avoid in the version teaching builds.

The teacher across the street is forty-one. She has another two decades of practice in her. The tools she will use in 2035 do not yet exist. The tools she used in 2015 are mostly obsolete. The infrastructure that would let her grow with her field — that would treat her continuing competence as the country's responsibility rather than her hobby — is the infrastructure medicine built for the pediatrician across the street. We can build it for her too. The reason we have not is not that we cannot. The reason is that no constituency with budget authority has yet decided that we should. That constituency includes district leaders, school boards, state legislatures, and ultimately federal funders. Each of them can move at their own scale. None of them is currently moving as a body.

The next chapter — Chapter 9 — turns to what trained teachers do with the discretion the law has handed them in the form of the educational-purposes exemption. That chapter is about the room and the call. This chapter has been about the precondition. The call cannot be made well by a teacher who has not been trained to make it. The training infrastructure is what this chapter has been arguing for. The room is where the training will pay off. The book is about both, in that order.

## What the research leaves open

Three things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The translation from teacher practice change to student outcomes — the gap between Kraft's *d* = 0.49 on instruction and *d* = 0.18 on achievement — is well-documented but not well-explained. We know practice change is upstream of student change and that the translation has losses; we do not know which losses are intrinsic to the cascade and which are artifacts of how practice change has been measured. A serious longitudinal study of cohort-based AI-specific coaching, with downstream student-outcome tracking, has not been published. Until it is, the case for the coaching layer rests on the broader literature and on inference from analog domains. The case is strong enough to act on. It is not yet strong enough to predict the exact magnitude of the effect a fully built infrastructure would produce.

The current status of Finland's DigiErko program past December 2024 is genuinely open. If a successor program is funded with an explicit generative-AI specialization, the international case for the credentialed-competence layer strengthens. If the program is allowed to lapse without replacement, the lesson is that even Finland — with the strongest design principles validated and the public-funding mechanism in place — found the political economy of sustaining specialized teacher credentialing harder than the design principles alone predicted. Either outcome matters for the recommendation this chapter is making. [verify: status of DigiErko or its successor as of press date.]

And the question of how to insulate a parallel teaching CME system from edtech vendor capture — the failure mode that medical CME spent decades developing imperfect defenses against — is not solved. The chapter has named the failure mode and proposed structural commitments (university-issued credentials, independent accreditation, transparent conflict-of-interest rules from day one) that would mitigate it. Whether those commitments would survive the first decade of a real funding stream is a political-economy question the existing literature cannot answer. The best the chapter can do is name the risk in advance and propose the structural commitments that the medical experience suggests are necessary. Whether they are sufficient is a question the next decade will answer.

## Still puzzling

Why has the medical CME system held up as well as it has under fifty years of sustained pharmaceutical-industry funding pressure? The system is not pristine — the capture is real, the disclosure rules are imperfect, the failure modes are visible. But the system has not collapsed into pure vendor certification. Something in its design — the independent accreditation council, the layered structure with state boards and specialty boards each holding a different piece of the leverage, the registration-fee revenue base that does not depend on commercial support, the public-facing audit of patient-outcome assessment — has kept it broadly honest. If we are going to build a teaching equivalent, we should know what features of medicine's version did the most to keep it from collapsing under commercial pressure. The post-mortem on medicine's design has not been written in a form that a teaching-CME builder could lift from directly. It needs to be.

## What would change my mind

A serious longitudinal study showing that two-and-a-half-hour vendor-led workshops produce sustained changes in teacher AI practice and student outcomes at scale — currently the evidence runs the other way (Yoon, Kraft, Desimone, RAND Europe, SETDA). If such a study were published with the same evidentiary standards as the coaching meta-analyses, the case for the coaching layer would be weaker than I think and the procurement decisions districts are currently making would be more defensible than I have characterized them. Or: a published evaluation of a district-scale rollout of the Finnish tutor-teacher model in a U.S. setting, with two-year outcome data, showing no detectable practice change or student-outcome effect over a district that retained the workshop model. Either finding would substantially revise the argument. As of this writing, neither study exists. The recommendation rests on what is published.
