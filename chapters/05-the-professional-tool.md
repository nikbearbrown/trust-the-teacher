# Chapter 5: The Professional Tool

*What surgeons, farmers, scientists, and artists can teach us about what happens when trained professionals get the right instruments*

---

In the years after laparoscopic cholecystectomy — gallbladder removal through small incisions, guided by a camera — rolled out across American and European hospitals in the early 1990s, surgeons started cutting the bile duct. Not on purpose. The bile duct is a half-inch-wide tube draining the liver into the intestine, and it sits inches from the gallbladder in a region where a slip of an instrument is the difference between a routine same-day discharge and a patient on the transplant list. Under the old open procedure, with the abdomen opened and the surgeon's hand directly in the field, common bile duct injuries occurred in roughly two of every thousand operations. Under the new laparoscopic technique, in the first decade of its adoption, that rate rose to roughly five per thousand. [Archer, Brown, Smith, Branum, and Hunter's 2001 *Annals of Surgery* national survey](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1422078/) documents the spike. [The Southern Surgeons Club's earlier 1995 learning-curve paper](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7793496/) had already named the mechanism: ninety percent of the injuries occurred within a surgeon's first thirty laparoscopic cases. The first case carried a 1.7 percent chance of injury. By the fiftieth, the rate had fallen by an order of magnitude.

The instrument was unambiguously better. Smaller incisions, shorter recovery, less infection, fewer days in a hospital bed. Patients who had been told they would be out of work for six weeks were back at desks in ten days. The surgeons who had mastered it could do gallbladder operations that the previous generation would have considered miraculous. And the surgeons who had not yet mastered it were producing a measurable spike in catastrophic biliary injury that would take the profession a decade of explicit training reforms to bring back down. The tool did not change. The trained hand using it produced better outcomes than the open procedure had ever delivered. The untrained hand using it produced injuries the open procedure had been unlikely to produce. Same instrument. Two different objects, depending on who was holding it.

I want to start here because the rest of the chapter is about a pattern, and the surgical case is the cleanest place to see the pattern's shape. There is a particular shape of mistake the field is making about AI in classrooms, and the shape is the same shape the surgical literature spent a decade naming and correcting. Imagine, for a moment, a meta-analysis that pooled appendectomy outcomes across two groups: trained surgeons performing the operation in modern hospitals, and untrained village barbers performing it on kitchen tables. The trained-surgeon studies would show mortality reductions of ninety-plus percent. The barber studies would show mortality increases of thirty or forty percent. The pooled average would show that surgery "slightly helps but mostly doesn't." No one would believe such a meta-analysis. Anyone reading the methods section would notice that the variance the average is hiding *is the thing the data are actually about*. The average is the wrong question. The right question is what the trained operator does with the tool, and what the untrained operator does, and how large the gap is between them. The right answer is: enormous. Big enough that the average is a misleading number. Big enough that the variable the meta-analysis should be reporting on is the operator, not the operation.

Most of the empirical literature on AI in education is the appendectomy meta-analysis. It pools classrooms where teachers were trained to use the tool well with classrooms where the tool was dropped on the teacher in August with a vendor walkthrough and a dashboard. It averages the two. It produces a number that hides the variance the data are about. And it is then read, by people who should know better, as evidence that the tool itself is the question. The surgical case is the worked-out version of what the teaching case looks like if we get the question right. The tool is the laparoscope. The training is the variable. The trained professional with the tool outperforms both the untrained professional with the tool *and* the trained professional without it. That is the cross-profession pattern. It shows up in surgery, it shows up in farming, it shows up in customer support, and — as of late 2024 — it shows up in tutoring. The pattern is the chapter.

## What the surgical training literature actually proves

Hold on the laparoscopy case for a moment, because the literature on what fixed the early-1990s injury rate is unusually clean and tells the chapter most of what it needs.

[Cook, Hatala, Brydges, Zendejas, Szostek, Wang, Erwin, and Hamstra's 2011 *JAMA* meta-analysis](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/article-abstract/1104300) — *Technology-Enhanced Simulation for Health Professions Education* — is the synthesis the field built. They screened 10,903 articles and pooled 609 studies enrolling 35,226 trainees, comparing simulation-based training to no intervention across knowledge outcomes, time skills, process skills, product skills, and — crucially — direct effects on patients. The pooled effect on trainee skill outcomes was above d = 1.0, an order of magnitude larger than almost anything in the educational interventions literature. The pooled effect on actual patient outcomes was d = 0.50, which is moderate by social-science standards and very large by surgical-outcomes standards. Real patients were measurably better off when their surgeons had trained on simulators first. The number is approximately the same magnitude as the [MetaX weighted-mean for feedback in classroom teaching](https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/Influences/View/feedback) that Chapter 4 reported. A patient's chance of an uneventful recovery moves about as much when her surgeon has trained on a box-trainer as a student's achievement moves when her teacher gives her well-structured feedback. That is the size of the thing simulation training is doing for patients in the OR.

The cleanest single trial in the line is the [NOVICE Trial — Schäfer et al., *International Journal of Surgery*, April 2025](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39998559/) — a multicenter randomized study across eleven German surgical centers. Each first-year surgical resident, with no prior laparoscopic experience, performed a first laparoscopic cholecystectomy on a real patient. The procedure was videotaped and scored, blinded, on the Global Operative Assessment of Laparoscopic Skill rubric — a validated 25-point instrument. The residents were then randomized to one of two arms: six weeks of structured Lübeck Toolbox-Curriculum box-trainer simulation, or no additional training. They then performed a second cholecystectomy, again videotaped and scored blinded. The median improvement in the simulation group between the first and second operation was 8.5 GOALS points. The median improvement in the control group was 2 points. The instrument was constant. The procedure was constant. The surgeons were randomized. The difference between a 2-point and an 8.5-point improvement is six weeks of structured deliberate practice on a box trainer that costs less than a single hospital bed-day. The trial enrolled twenty-two residents with four dropouts. The precision is limited and the chapter should say so. The direction is not. The size is not.

The [Seymour et al. 2002 randomized trial](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12368669/) and [Grantcharov et al. 2004 trial in *British Journal of Surgery*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14760660/) had already established the transfer claim — virtual-reality-trained residents performed gallbladder dissection 29 percent faster than untrained controls, with non-trained residents five times more likely to injure tissue. By the time NOVICE replicated the finding in 2025, simulation training had been a credentialing requirement for laparoscopic privileges in major residencies for a decade. The instrument-without-training failure mode that produced the 1990s injury spike is gone. The instrument is the same. The injury rate is back to baseline. The variable that closed the gap was a six-week box-trainer curriculum that, before the field built it, did not exist. The instrument did not become safer. The profession built the training that turned the instrument into a capability.

Three things from this literature go straight into the chapter's argument and do not need to be qualified. First, a genuinely better tool can produce worse outcomes when its perceptual demands diverge from what the operator's training prepared her for. The fulcrum effect — instruments pivoting through a trocar invert the surgeon's hand motion and the felt force — is not a problem the open procedure had ever made the surgeon solve. Two-dimensional monitor view replaces stereoscopic vision. The haptic feedback of fingertips on tissue is replaced by the muted signal of a one-meter rigid lever. None of these are deficiencies of the laparoscope. They are properties of the laparoscope that the operator has not yet been trained to handle. Second, a structured deliberate-practice curriculum on a much cheaper proxy — a box trainer; a VR simulator — closes the gap in six weeks at trivial cost relative to the tool itself. The intervention is not exotic. It is what we know how to build for surgeons. We have not built the analogue for teachers. And third — this is the move that will recur three more times in the chapter — the gains from training are largest for the operators at the bottom of the skill distribution. The early-career surgeon improves enormously. The senior attending who has done four thousand cases improves modestly. The tool levels the floor. It does not flatten the ceiling.

## The agricultural case — yield monitors and the catch-up effect

The same shape shows up on a 4,000-acre Kansas wheat farm, in a literature that almost nobody writing about education reads, and that holds the chapter's argument up just as clearly as the surgical literature does.

The headline number is from [Lan and Ban's 2025 meta-analysis in *Sustainability*](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/17/24/11223), which integrated 85 empirical studies and 1,472 independent farm observations from across the global precision-agriculture literature. Their pooled finding: precision-agriculture technology adoption — yield monitors, grid soil sampling, GPS guidance, variable-rate fertilizer prescriptions — increased average return on investment by 22.3 percent and net profit by 18.5 percent. Nitrogen use efficiency rose 15.1 percent. Pesticide application fell 12.8 percent. Greenhouse gas emissions fell 9.4 percent. The aggregate number is real and substantial. It is also, as Lan and Ban themselves note, an average across enormous heterogeneity. The 22.3 percent pooled return masks weaker and less stable benefits for small-scale farms and developing-country contexts and concentrates the largest benefits among large-scale grain operations using variable-rate technology and auto-guidance. The paper concludes that technical literacy and adequate information access are crucial moderators of who actually captures the gain.

This is where it gets interesting, because the U.S.-only literature looks almost contradictory to the global headline. [Schimmelpfennig's 2016 USDA Economic Research Service report, *Farm Profits and Adoption of Precision Agriculture*](https://ers.usda.gov/sites/default/files/_laserfiche/publications/80326/ERR-217.pdf), worked from U.S. corn data for 2010–2012 and found that variable-rate technology raised profitability by about 1 percent and GPS maps by almost 3 percent. Those are not 22.3 percent numbers. They are an order of magnitude smaller. The two findings are not in conflict — return on investment is a different quantity from net profit on whole-farm revenue, and a 22 percent return on capital deployed on the tech can show up as a 1–3 percent effect on whole-farm profit. But the more important reconciliation, the one the chapter needs, is Schimmelpfennig's own framing: the U.S. sample was unusually well-managed. The corn farms in question were already near the efficiency frontier. Farms already at the frontier have less room to gain from any new tool. The 22.3 percent pooled global number absorbs the much larger gains available to farms farther from the frontier. The U.S. average is small because the U.S. average is being computed over a population that already had most of what the tool offered.

The cleanest demonstration of this is in [Fiechter, Schnitkey, and Langemeier's 2024 Purdue study](https://ag.purdue.edu/commercialag/home/resource/2026/03/extracting-value-from-precision-agriculture-technology-is-difficult/) using a 21-year panel of 570 farms from the Kansas Farm Management Association. They computed farm-by-farm efficiency relative to the best-performing farms in the same year using data envelopment analysis and merged the efficiency scores with KFMA's precision-ag adoption survey. The headline conclusion was deliberately understated: most of the seventeen technology combinations they studied were not associated with broad efficiency gains. Two combinations stood out — automated guidance, and the combination of yield monitors with grid soil sampling. But the sentence the chapter wants is one paragraph later: *less efficient farms gain the most from precision agriculture technology, with farms in the lower end of the efficiency distribution seeing meaningful gains from several technology combinations, while highly efficient farms saw little to none.* That is the catch-up effect, in the authors' own language, from a 570-farm 21-year panel. It is the strongest single piece of evidence for the trained-operator-with-tool-versus-untrained pattern outside of education itself.

There is a reason for it that I want to name plainly because it carries straight into the AI case. Schimmelpfennig's clean breakdown distinguishes two kinds of precision-ag technology. Auto-guidance is **embodied knowledge** — the tractor itself drives in a straight line, parallel to the previous pass, with sub-inch accuracy, whether or not the operator can. The tool internalizes the skill. The farmer gets the benefit without having to learn the skill the tool replaces. Yield monitors and grid soil sampling are **information-intensive** — the tool produces data. A yield monitor records the corn output per square meter across forty different conditions per acre over an entire harvest. Grid soil sampling produces a map of phosphorus, potassium, and pH levels across a forty-acre field. *Neither of these tools tells the farmer what to do.* The farmer's interpretive skill is what turns the map into a variable-rate prescription, the harvest data into a rotation decision, the soil profile into a fertilizer plan. The information-intensive technologies are the ones with the catch-up pattern. The farmer who knows what a yield map is telling her gets a 22 percent return. The farmer who looks at the same map and shrugs gets nothing. The tool does not change. The farmer's interpretive skill is the variable.

I will say the obvious sentence once and let it sit there: this is exactly the situation of a teacher with an AI tool. Some AI uses are embodied — auto-graded multiple-choice, lesson-plan templating, scheduling — and pay off without much teacher skill because the tool internalizes the work. Other AI uses are information-intensive — diagnosing where a student's misconception lives, drafting three differentiated explanations the teacher then chooses among, surfacing the wrong-note pattern in a class's exit tickets — and depend on the teacher's interpretive skill the way a yield map depends on the farmer's. The high-leverage uses of AI in teaching are not the embodied uses. They are the information-intensive ones. And those uses pay nothing to a teacher who has not been trained to interpret what the tool is putting in front of her. The chapter from Lan and Ban and Schimmelpfennig and Fiechter is the same chapter, told four years before AI arrived in classrooms, using yield monitors and a panel of Kansas wheat farms. Nobody writing about EdTech is reading it.

## The customer-support case — the cleanest single test

The third place where this pattern shows up is the one most directly comparable to AI in a classroom: AI on the desk of a working professional doing knowledge work, with productivity measured at the level of individual workers and rolled out in a way that makes the causal claim clean.

[Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's 2023 NBER working paper — and the 2025 *Quarterly Journal of Economics* publication — *Generative AI at Work*](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161) studied 5,179 customer support agents at a large software company over a year of staggered AI rollout. The intervention was a GPT-based real-time conversational assistant that monitors live customer chats and suggests responses to the agent. The identification strategy was clean: agents in the same job, same team, same shift, were randomly assigned to access the tool at different start dates. The outcome was the most concrete thing you could measure in customer support — issues resolved per hour. The headline finding: access to the tool increased productivity by 14 percent on average, including a 34 percent improvement for novice and low-skilled workers but with minimal impact on experienced and highly skilled workers.

Hold on the distribution of that effect for a moment, because it is the same distribution the chapter has now seen twice. The 14 percent average is what the press release reported. The 34 percent / minimal-impact split is what the data actually said. The bottom of the worker skill distribution gained enormously. The top of the distribution gained almost nothing. The mechanism, in Brynjolfsson et al.'s own words: *the AI model disseminates the best practices of more able workers and helps newer workers move down the experience curve.* The AI was not replacing the high-performer. The AI was teaching the new hire what the high-performer had spent five years learning to do intuitively. It is the surgical-simulation finding in a different vocabulary. It is the precision-ag catch-up effect on a help-desk floor. It is the same shape.

[Noy and Zhang's 2023 *Science* paper, *Experimental evidence on the productivity effects of generative artificial intelligence*](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adh2586), found the same shape one more time, in writing tasks. Four hundred and fifty-three college-educated professionals — marketers, grant writers, consultants, HR staff, data analysts — were randomized to ChatGPT access on incentivized occupation-specific writing tasks. Time taken decreased by 0.8 standard deviations. Output quality rose by 0.4 standard deviations. And — the part that matters here — inequality between workers decreased: ChatGPT compressed the productivity distribution by benefiting low-ability workers more. Same shape.

Three professions. Surgery, customer support, writing. Three different tools — box-trainer simulation, GPT-based chat assistant, ChatGPT — none of which had been deployed in the same setting before. Three independent literatures with no shared authors. Three different outcome measures, three different identification strategies, three different decades. All three show the same thing. The tool helps. The tool helps most where the practitioner started weakest. The tool produces near-zero gains at the top of the skill distribution. The variance among practitioners *gets compressed by the introduction of the tool*. This is the central empirical finding the chapter needs, and it is held up by triangulation from three independent literatures rather than by any single result. That is the strongest argumentative move the chapter has. I want it on the page.

## The tutoring case — the in-domain proof

Then, in October 2024, the same shape showed up in tutoring.

[Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, and Demszky's *Tutor CoPilot: A Human-AI Approach for Scaling Real-Time Expertise*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017) — the first randomized controlled trial of a human-AI system deployed in live tutoring — was a partnership between the Stanford SCALE Initiative, FEV Tutor (a chat-based virtual tutoring company), and a U.S. Southern school district serving Title I schools. Nine hundred tutors were in the broader sample. Seven hundred-plus tutors and a thousand-plus K–12 students were in the primary preregistered analysis. Period: March through May 2024. One-on-one chat-based math tutoring sessions. Tutor CoPilot — an open-source tool developed at Stanford — was embedded in the FEV Tutor platform. It monitored the live session and provided real-time pedagogical suggestions to the tutor: probing questions, scaffolds, hints, modeled on expert tutoring practice. The tutor remained responsible for what was actually said to the student. The AI was invisible to the student. Cost: approximately $20 per tutor per year.

The result: students working with tutors who had access to Tutor CoPilot were 4 percentage points more likely to master topics (p < 0.01). Effects were particularly strong for students paired with lower-rated tutors — a 9 percentage point gain. The 4 / 9 split is the same shape as the Brynjolfsson 14 / 34 split. The same shape as the Lan-Ban / Fiechter farm efficiency catch-up. The same shape as the surgical-simulation gain concentrated in residents early in the learning curve.

The mechanism analysis, from over 350,000 messages between tutors and students, is the single most important sentence in the chapter: *Tutor CoPilot promotes effective pedagogy, increasing the use of probing questions and reducing generic praise.* Read that one slowly. The tool is not generating answers for the student. The tool is changing what the *tutor* does. It pulls the tutor toward better pedagogical practice in real time. It nudges her toward Socratic probing — *what would happen if you tried it with a different value for x?* — and away from the cheap dopamine of *great job!* The student outcome improvement is a downstream consequence of upgrading the tutor's practice in the moment. The AI did not tutor the student. The AI made the tutor a marginally better tutor. The tutor tutored the student.

The chapter has to name the limits of the Tutor CoPilot study, because they are real. It is one subject — math. One platform — chat-based. One age band — K–12. One academic year. One AI model — a GPT-class model in 2024. Generalization to in-person tutoring, to writing or science, to longer follow-up windows, to different models, has not been studied. The effect size is modest — 4 percentage points on topic mastery is a real gain but it is a *marginal* gain on top of one-on-one human tutoring, which is itself, per [the Chapter 4 WWC synthesis](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/teaching-learning-toolkit/one-to-one-tuition), already the highest-effect intervention in the education literature. Average tutoring across the WWC-standards corpus produces roughly 0.29 SD in student achievement; tutoring for low-income elementary and middle students produces roughly 0.36 SD. The Tutor CoPilot gain *adds to* that baseline. It does not replace it. Comparing AI-augmented tutoring to classroom instruction would be a different study with a different and larger effect. The right way to read the 4 / 9 numbers is as the marginal gain on top of an already-strong intervention, and the right reading of the mechanism is that AI is lifting the *tutor's practice* in real time — exactly the mechanism the book is going to claim for AI in classrooms.

Tutor CoPilot is the in-domain proof of the cross-profession pattern. Same shape. Same distributional finding. Same mechanism — the tool dissemates the practices of the experts among the people doing the work, in real time. Same near-zero gain at the top of the practitioner skill distribution, where the tutor already does the probing-questions thing on her own. Same large gain at the bottom, where the tool's nudge is doing pedagogical work the tutor was not yet doing for herself. The pattern reaches across surgery and farming and customer support and writing and now tutoring. It will reach across teaching, when we get around to running the trial.

## The creative-arts case — the qualitative parallel

There is one more profession the chapter should mention, because it makes a different version of the point. The other four cases are about a tool that, untrained, produces *worse* outcomes than not using the tool. The creative-arts case is about a tool that, untrained, produces no output at all of the kind the tool was designed to produce.

[Adobe's published recommendation for the After Effects credential in its Adobe Certified Professional program](https://certifiedprofessional.adobe.com/after-effects) is that candidates have at least 150 hours of instruction and hands-on experience in the application prior to earning the certification. The credentialing infrastructure is run through Certiport, a Pearson VUE business; the exam costs $150 and is delivered both remotely and at testing centers globally. Industry training providers — Future Media Concepts, AGI Training, Noble Desktop — describe 80- to 160-hour curricula as the standard preparation pathway. None of this is peer-reviewed evidence. It is industry credentialing, and the chapter should call it that. But it is the most defensible quantitative anchor available for what professional-grade creative software requires of its operator before the tool starts producing the kind of output the tool was built for.

Open After Effects without training. The interface presents a timeline, a composition window, an effects panel, an expression editor, a render queue, and approximately three hundred keyboard shortcuts the documentation assumes you know. A trained motion-graphics designer who has spent her 150 hours can produce a thirty-second branding sequence with kinetic typography, parallax layering, and audio-synced cuts in an afternoon. An untrained user, given the same software and the same brief, cannot produce that sequence in a week, or a month. She is not producing it badly. She is not producing it at all. The output a trained operator delivers with this tool is not in the same category as the output an untrained operator delivers. The trained operator is producing motion graphics. The untrained operator is producing a thing she will quietly close and not show anyone.

This is the softest of the four professional cases — there is no randomized trial of After Effects training, and the chapter would be dishonest to pretend there is. What it is is a qualitative parallel: a powerful instrument whose payoff is not "more efficient" or even "slightly better." The payoff is the capacity to produce a class of work that the untrained user cannot produce at all. Adobe's 150-hour figure is the industry pricing the training gap. The chapter cites it for what it is. The pattern it parallels is the one the previous four cases establish empirically. The instrument is real. The training is what makes the instrument a capability.

## The "students aren't professionals" objection

I want to take the most serious objection to this chapter's argument head-on, because if I don't, the careful reader will, and she will be right to.

The objection is this: every case I have cited involves a *trained professional* using a tool in the course of professional work. A surgeon at year three of her residency, who has already passed the boards, who has been trained for a decade in surgical anatomy and asepsis and clinical judgment. A farmer with twenty years of experience, who already knows what nitrogen does to a corn plant and is using the yield monitor to refine decisions she was already making. A customer support agent who already understands the product and is using the AI to surface the right knowledge-base article in less time. A tutor who already has subject-matter expertise and is using the AI to refine her pedagogical moves in real time. None of these professionals is being asked to *learn* the underlying discipline from the tool. They are using the tool to do their professional work more capably than they could do it alone. *Students*, the objection runs, are not professionals. The student is using the AI to learn the underlying discipline for the first time. The cross-profession evidence does not transfer because the role is different. The teacher-with-AI is a professional with a tool. The student-with-AI is a novice trying to acquire the discipline. The two situations are not analogous, and the chapter's argument is built on the wrong analogy.

The objection is a real one, and the chapter has to take it seriously. Two responses.

The first response is that the cross-profession evidence is being mobilized for the *teacher* case, not the student case. The chapter's argument is not that a fourteen-year-old learning algebra is the same as a third-year resident learning laparoscopy. The argument is that *the adult professional in the room doing the pedagogy* is the same kind of operator. The surgeon is to the laparoscope as the teacher is to the AI tool. The student is the patient. The patient is not learning from the laparoscope; the patient is the person whose outcome depends on whether the trained operator uses the laparoscope well. Likewise, the student is not learning from ChatGPT in the way a third-year resident is learning from a box trainer; the student is the person whose learning outcome depends on whether the teacher in front of her uses the AI well. The Tutor CoPilot study is the place where this analogy becomes literal — the AI is suggesting moves to the tutor, who decides what to say to the student. The student does not interact with the AI directly. The student interacts with the tutor. The tool changes what the tutor does. The tutor changes what the student does. That is the pattern the rest of the book is about, and the objection — *the cross-profession evidence is about the practitioner, not the recipient* — concedes the chapter's actual claim. Yes. The cross-profession evidence is about the practitioner. The practitioner in the classroom is the teacher.

The second response is the harder one, and it requires the chapter to admit something. Students *do* use AI directly. A teacher with a class of thirty cannot supervise every interaction her students have with ChatGPT during a writing assignment. The student is, in those moments, the operator of the tool, and the analogy from the cross-profession literature is more strained. What the chapter wants to say honestly here is that the within-classroom evidence on student-directly-using-AI is still thin. The two best-known data points are from outside the K-12 context. [Anthropic's Education Report — *How University Students Use Claude*](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-education-report-how-university-students-use-claude) — surveyed how 18-to-22-year-old college students interact with Claude and found patterns of direct delegation that look like exactly the cognitive offloading the chapter on AI as a learning tool would have to worry about. The [Harvard physics study on AI as a learning aid](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-65459-6) found students learned more in less time when AI was scaffolded by the instructor than when they used AI freely. *Both of these studies point at the same place: when the trained professional structures the student's use of the tool, the tool helps the student learn. When the tool is dropped on the student without scaffolding, it substitutes for the cognitive work the student needed to do.* That is, again, the chapter's pattern. The teacher is the variable. The teacher's training is what determines whether the student's use of the AI is scaffolded or substituting. The chapter does not need to claim that the student is a trained professional. The chapter needs to claim that whether the student's interaction with the tool is productive depends on the trained professional in the room *designing the interaction*. The teacher is the laparoscope's surgeon. The classroom is the OR. The student is the patient. And nobody — not Khan Academy, not OpenAI, not the school district that bought 1,200 Chromebook licenses — is going to stand at the table in the teacher's place. [verify: confirm Harvard study citation Kestin et al. 2024 in *Scientific Reports*, and Anthropic Education Report direct-delegation framing, before press.]

## Applying the framework to teaching

Here is what the cross-profession evidence stacks up to, told as a table because that is the cleanest way to see the shape:

| Profession | The tool | What training does | Where the gains concentrate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Surgery | Laparoscope; 2D monitor; long-lever instruments through trocar | 6-week box-trainer simulation curriculum; NOVICE: GOALS +8.5 vs +2; Cook meta: patient outcomes d = 0.50 | Largest gains for residents early in the learning curve (Southern Surgeons Club: 90% of injuries in first 30 cases) |
| Farming | Yield monitor; grid soil sampling; variable-rate prescription | Operator interpretation of farm-level information output | Lan & Ban: 22.3% pooled ROI; Schimmelpfennig: 1–3% on already-efficient U.S. farms; Fiechter-Schnitkey-Langemeier (570 KFMA farms, 21 years): gains concentrated at the lower end of the efficiency distribution |
| Customer support | GPT-based real-time conversational assistant | Implicit — the AI surfaces high-performer practices in the moment | Brynjolfsson et al.: +14% average; +34% for novice/low-skill; ~0% for experienced |
| Writing tasks | ChatGPT | Implicit — the AI compresses the productivity distribution | Noy & Zhang: time −0.8 SD; quality +0.4 SD; inequality between workers decreased |
| Tutoring | Tutor CoPilot (real-time pedagogical suggestion to tutor) | Implicit — the AI promotes probing questions; reduces generic praise | Wang et al.: +4 pp average mastery; +9 pp for lower-rated tutors |

Four professions, plus a fifth in the same chart for completeness. Five different tools. The same shape every time. The tool helps. The tool helps most where the practitioner was weakest. The tool produces near-zero gains at the top of the practitioner skill distribution. The variance among practitioners gets compressed by the introduction of the tool. The mechanism, where the literature has named it explicitly, is that the tool disseminates the practices of the most able practitioners among the people doing the work — in real time, at scale, at trivial cost relative to the underlying training infrastructure.

Now place the teacher in this table.

The teacher with Claude is not a smaller version of an AI platform. She is a different instrument. The platform — the LLM, the vendor dashboard, the dispatching algorithm — handles what the platform handles: the lookup, the first draft, the templated worksheet, the auto-scored multiple-choice. *She* generates the specific question that unlocks this student's particular confusion about this concept on this day. She notices the wrong note — the student producing correct answers but unable to explain why. She groups the students who would benefit from working together today and separates the students whose pairing yesterday produced a fight rather than a conversation. She differentiates the same Civil War primary source at three reading levels in the time it used to take to write one. She catches the student whose home is rough this week and gives her the assignment as the version with the lower cognitive load, without making a thing of it, because the AI drafted both versions in the same prompt and the teacher decided which one this student should see. The tool handles what the tool handles. She handles what only she can handle. The classroom outcome depends on both moves working — and on her judgment about when each one applies.

This is not a metaphor stretched to make a point. The Tutor CoPilot mechanism is exactly this, told in a one-on-one chat context: the tutor decides what to say; the AI suggests pedagogical moves in real time; the *use of probing questions goes up*; *generic praise goes down*. The teacher-with-Claude operates the same way, more intermittently, across thirty students rather than one. The catch-up effect from Brynjolfsson and from Wang both predict the same distributional finding for teachers: the teacher who has been struggling — the second-year teacher whose classroom management isn't there yet, the science teacher whose feedback turnaround was three weeks because she taught five sections a day — is the teacher who gains the most. The teacher who was already excellent gains less because she was already doing most of what the tool can nudge her toward. The teaching profession has a long left tail, and the tool, used well, brings the left tail closer to the middle. The middle is where most students live. That is the shape of the gain.

The trained teacher with Claude is a different instrument from the untrained teacher with Claude, and a different instrument from the trained teacher without Claude. *Three* objects, not two. The structural conversation in education policy keeps treating it as if there are two: with-AI and without-AI. The data say there are three. The two AI conditions — trained and untrained — are as different from each other as either is from the no-tool baseline. The chapter the field has not yet written is the chapter that takes the trained-with-AI condition seriously enough to study it at scale. We have not built the workforce that would let that study be run. Building it is what the book is for.

## The chapter's turn

The laparoscope did not perform the surgery. The yield monitor did not plant the corn. The chat assistant did not resolve the customer support ticket. Tutor CoPilot did not tutor the student.

In every one of those cases, what happened was that a trained professional did the work with a better instrument in hand than the one she had before. The laparoscope did not make the surgeon. Six weeks of box-trainer simulation did. The yield monitor did not make the farmer. Twenty years of knowing what nitrogen does to corn, refined by a tool that surfaced patterns she could now act on, did. The GPT-based assistant did not make the customer-support agent good at her job. The combination of the agent's three years of product knowledge with the AI's surfacing of high-performer practices, in real time, made her measurably faster — and the gain was largest for the agent who had been there three months. Tutor CoPilot did not tutor the student. It pulled the tutor toward Socratic probing and away from generic praise. The tutor, doing slightly better pedagogy than she had been doing, tutored the student.

This is the pattern. Four professions deep, with the fifth — teaching — sitting in front of us waiting to be entered properly. The trained professional with the tool outperforms the untrained professional with the tool, and outperforms the trained professional without the tool. The biggest beneficiary in every case is the practitioner at the bottom of the skill distribution. The mechanism, where the literature has named it, is that the tool disseminates the practices of the most able among the people doing the work. This is the empirical foundation Chapter 6 will use to ask what trained teachers do with the time AI gives them back; the foundation Chapter 7 will use to expand the table above into a full cross-sector synthesis; the foundation Chapter 11 will use to name what it would take to build the workforce we have not yet built.

The point of this chapter is a different point. It is that the question *does AI help students learn* is the appendectomy meta-analysis. It is the wrong question. The right question is what the trained teacher does with the tool, what the untrained teacher does with the same tool, and how large the gap between them is. The right answer is: enormous. Big enough that the average is a misleading number. Big enough that the variable the meta-analysis should be reporting on is the operator, not the operation.

The laparoscope did not perform the surgery. The teacher does the teaching. The only question left is whether anyone has trained her to do it with the instrument we just handed her. So far, almost no one has.

That is the chapter the rest of the book is about.

## What the research leaves open

Several things this chapter has not settled, and which the evidence does not yet settle either. The Tutor CoPilot 4 pp / 9 pp finding is one math-focused study in one platform over one academic year with one AI model, and the durability of the effect, the generalization to other subjects and modalities, and the comparison with in-person tutoring are all genuinely open questions; the chapter has reported the result accurately and not stretched it. The agricultural literature reports the catch-up effect cleanly at the lower end of the farm-efficiency distribution, but the precise share of the 22.3 percent pooled ROI that is attributable to operator interpretive skill versus to scale, capital, and crop choice is not separated cleanly even in the Fiechter-Schnitkey-Langemeier panel. The Brynjolfsson and Noy-Zhang studies are clean on the productivity-compression finding but do not — and structurally cannot — settle whether the compression persists when the AI tool is taken away, or whether the low-skill worker has actually learned the high-performer's practice or is simply leaning on the tool that surfaces it. And the customer-support and writing-task contexts are knowledge work, not pedagogy; the assumption that the same compression pattern transfers cleanly to a teacher's interaction with thirty students across forty-five minutes is plausible from Tutor CoPilot but has not been demonstrated in a full classroom RCT. The book's argument does not require those open questions to be settled. The book's argument requires the cross-profession pattern to be visible, and it is.

## Still puzzling

The five literatures the chapter draws on — surgical simulation, precision agriculture, customer-support AI, writing-task AI, and AI-augmented tutoring — share authors with each other essentially never. The surgical literature is in *JAMA* and *Annals of Surgery* and the *British Journal of Surgery*. The agricultural literature is in *Sustainability* and the *Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics* and USDA-ERS reports. The labor-economics literature is in NBER and *QJE* and *Science*. The Tutor CoPilot paper is in the Stanford EdWorkingPaper series. None of these communities cites each other. The shape of the finding — the same shape, across all five — has not been named in the literature as a cross-domain pattern that anyone tracks. There is something puzzling and a little wonderful about a finding this consistent that nobody has organized into a name. I do not yet know whether the absence of an organizing name is a temporary lag of the literature, a deeper failure of disciplinary boundaries to see across themselves, or evidence that the shape is something I am picking up that, on closer examination, will dissolve into five different mechanisms that happen to produce similar-looking distributional plots. The chapter has been written as if the first is true. The second decade of the AI-at-work literature will tell us.

## What would change my mind

A well-powered, properly randomized trial of an AI classroom tool — at the scale of Brynjolfsson's 5,179 customer support agents, with implementation-fidelity controls of the kind the [RAND Europe 2026 synthesis](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2026/01/harnessing-the-benefits-of-edtech-what-research-tells.html) identifies as the determining variable — that showed the *opposite* distributional pattern: gains at the top of the teacher skill distribution and not at the bottom, or no distributional difference at all. That would tell me the cross-profession pattern does not transfer to teaching the way the chapter has assumed it does, and the implications for which teachers should get tool-training-priority would invert. To date no such trial exists. The Tutor CoPilot finding points in the opposite direction. If a careful study comes in showing otherwise, the chapter's argument about *who* benefits most from teacher AI training would need to be rewritten. The argument that *somebody* should be making the call — that the call cannot be made by the tool alone — would survive.
