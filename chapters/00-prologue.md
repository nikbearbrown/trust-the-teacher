# Prologue

*Why the book has to earn its title before the book begins.*

---

I have watched a teacher's face change in real time.

She was a middle-school science teacher, late forties, twenty years in the classroom, attending an evening AI workshop her district had finally scheduled. She came in skeptical. The first prompt I asked the model to handle was the kind of question her students typed at her every week — *explain the difference between mitosis and meiosis like I'm in seventh grade, and give me three analogies a kid would actually remember.* The model produced an answer in eleven seconds. It was better than the explanation I would have given. It was probably better than the explanation she would have given on a tired Friday afternoon. She read it once. She read it twice. She looked at me and said, *"How long has it been able to do this?"*

The honest answer was: about two years. The honest answer also was: she had no way to know. Her district's last technology professional-development day had been in March of the previous year. The session had covered Google Classroom, attendance digitization, and the difference between a Chromebook and an iPad. By the time she sat down in front of me, the version of GPT she had heard about in news clips eighteen months earlier had been superseded twice. The tool her students were using on their phones during her class — and they were using it, every day, in every class she taught — bore almost no resemblance to the tool she thought she understood.

This is the gap.

The data say it plainly. By late 2025, **somewhere between eighty-six and ninety-five percent of higher-education students were using generative AI regularly**, with UK Higher Education Policy Institute figures landing at 92% undergraduate utilization and 94% engagement with AI on assessed coursework ([HEPI / Higher Education Policy Institute, 2025–2026](https://innovationhub.howcollege.ac.uk/education/sector-research)). The Latin American higher-education figure is identical — 92%, with 67% reporting daily or weekly use ([Digital Education Council, 2025](https://www.digitaleducationcouncil.com/post/ai-adoption-is-nearly-universal-among-students-but-confidence-is-not)). At the K-12 level, US middle and high school student adoption sits at 62% ([RAND, 2025](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA4742-1.html)).

On the other side of the desk, **only twelve to fifteen percent of K-12 teachers use AI tools daily**, and most of that use is concentrated in administrative tasks — drafting emails, generating worksheet templates, writing newsletters ([EdWeek, *Teachers and AI*, 2026](https://www.edweek.org/technology/more-teachers-are-using-ai-in-their-classrooms-heres-why/2026/01)). In higher education, an AACSB survey of business-school faculty found only 7% report daily use and 18% weekly; 67% use AI occasionally or rarely ([AACSB, 2025](https://www.aacsb.edu/insights/reports/2025/genai-adoption-in-business-schools-deans-and-faculty-respond)).

And the teachers know it. **In a 2025 Our Hong Kong Foundation study of 1,200 principals, teachers, and students, educators rated their own AI competence at a mean of 5.0 on a 10-point scale. Their students rated themselves at 6.5** ([Our Hong Kong Foundation, 2025](https://www.thestandard.com.hk/news/article/321485/Students-outpace-teachers-in-AI-proficiency-Our-Hong-Kong-Foundation-survey)). Forty-nine percent of US K-12 teachers explicitly agree that their students are better prepared to use AI tools effectively than they are ([Youngstown State University Online, 2025](https://online.ysu.edu/degrees/education/msed/teacher-ai-usage-statistics/)). The data point Bear's outline calls for — 68% of students believing their teacher's AI competency depends on chance rather than preparation — `[verify: closest adjacent finding is the Hong Kong 5.0/6.5 split combined with the 49% teacher-side acknowledgment; the specific "chance not preparation" framing has not been traced to a single survey instrument in the literature available as of the writing of this prologue. Author to confirm or substitute.]`

Do not soften these numbers. They are what they are. The students have the tools. The teachers do not have the training. The gap between them is not opinion. It is measurement.

---

I want to be careful about the next sentence, because it is the sentence that earns this book the right to its title.

**This is not the teachers' fault.**

The pace of generative-AI capability release is approximately monthly. Significant model upgrades, new agentic features, new modes of interaction — they ship roughly every four to six weeks across the major providers. The cycle on which institutional professional development operates is annual at best, biennial at typical, and sometimes longer when budget years and contract cycles work against each other. By the time a curriculum committee approves a training module on a specific tool, the tool the module describes has been superseded twice. The training arrives obsolete.

This is not a moral failure. It is a structural mismatch. No individual teacher, however motivated, can fix it by trying harder. The system that was supposed to keep her current has been outrun by the technology it was supposed to keep her current on. When she walks into my workshop and asks how long the model has been able to do what it just did, the right answer is not *"you should have known."* The right answer is *"nobody set up the system that would have told you."*

The teachers themselves have been saying this for two years. The Education Week framing — directly from K-12 educators describing themselves, not from critics describing them — is *"We're at a disadvantage"* ([EdWeek, *What's Holding Educators Back*, 2026](https://www.edweek.org/technology/whats-holding-educators-back-from-adopting-ai/2026/02)). The empirical floor under that self-assessment: **forty-four percent of K-12 educators have received no AI professional development at all; twenty-nine percent have attended exactly one workshop; only eight percent have access to systematic, continuous training** ([same source](https://www.edweek.org/technology/whats-holding-educators-back-from-adopting-ai/2026/02)).

It also matters that the training, when it does arrive, is the wrong shape. A math teacher's AI gap is not the same as an English teacher's AI gap. Generic prompting workshops — the modal PD offering — do not close subject-specific deficits. The research on what does work points in one direction: subject-specific professional learning communities, where teachers practice the technology on the actual curriculum they teach, with colleagues who teach the same subject. A 2026 study of English Language Teaching instructors in such communities showed the pattern clearly — when AI training was integrated into a discipline-specific PLC, the technology stuck; when it was generic, it did not ([Cogent Education, 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/2331186X.2026.2626629)). This finding tracks the broader Pedagogical Content Knowledge literature that has been settled for forty years: domain-specific pedagogy outperforms generic pedagogy when content is held constant ([Shulman, 1986](https://www.aera.net/Publications/Educational-Researcher); [Hill, Rowan & Ball, *AERJ*, 2005](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00028312042002371)).

The teachers are not the problem. The infrastructure that was supposed to prepare them is the problem. And the infrastructure has been failing them for the entire window during which the technology became consequential.

---

Which brings me to the precondition.

I am about to spend the next twelve chapters of this book arguing that the central variable in whether educational technology works is teacher training, that the central variable in whether American education absorbs generative AI without harm is teacher training, that the policy decisions of the next decade should be organized around teacher training. The book's closing line is *Trust the teacher. Train the teacher. Measure what matters.* I want you to take that line seriously.

But you should not take it seriously unless you understand what it depends on.

*Trust the teacher* is only a defensible argument if the teacher has been given something worth trusting. The data in front of us — students at 92% adoption, teachers at 12% daily use, 44% of educators receiving zero AI professional development, 8% receiving any ongoing program — describe a workforce that has been set up to fail the trust the book is about to ask the system to extend. The argument cannot land on top of those numbers. The argument has to be read as a precondition: *trust the teacher, once we have built her the conditions under which trust is warranted.*

Every chapter that follows assumes a teacher who has been trained in the way medicine trains doctors — continuously, specifically, tied to demonstrated competence, evaluated on whether she is getting better at the work. Every chapter that follows is honest about how far most American schools are from being able to make that assumption today. The book is the case for closing that distance. It is not the case for pretending the distance does not exist.

I am writing this prologue first because the rest of the book reads differently after you have looked at the numbers in this one. Chapter 1 will argue that the smartphone ban, however justified the underlying concern, is the wrong instrument because it routes around the trained-professional judgment that has not been built. Chapter 2 will argue that the EdTech industry has been wrong about which variable predicts whether a platform produces learning. Chapter 4 will argue that forty years of education research has been pointing at the same answer — collective teacher efficacy, teacher-student relationships, feedback, comprehensive professional development — and the AI moment is the moment that answer becomes urgent. Chapter 8 will argue that medicine built the infrastructure for continuous specialty-specific professional development half a century ago and we have spent the intervening decades not building its analog for teaching.

The argument compounds. It only compounds if you are willing to start where the evidence actually is.

The teacher in my workshop, the middle-school science teacher who watched the model produce an explanation she could not have produced as quickly, did not need me to tell her the explanation was good. She needed me to tell her that not knowing the explanation existed was not a failure on her part. She needed an infrastructure that would have shown it to her two years earlier. She needed a system that treated keeping her current as seriously as the medical-CME system treats keeping her cardiologist current. None of that existed. None of it exists now.

The book is about how to build it.

The reason the title is *Trust the Teacher* and not *Train the Teacher* is that *train* is what the rest of the book is going to argue for, in detail, with evidence. The *trust* in the title is the political and institutional commitment the training has to earn. The order in the closing line is not accidental: *trust* comes first, because without it the training will not be funded. *Train* comes second, because the trust the title asks for is only defensible if the training has been done. *Measure what matters* comes third, because without it there is no way to know whether the training did what we needed it to do.

The data in this prologue is the evidence for why the trust has not yet been earned. The rest of the book is the case for the work that would earn it.

That is the argument. We can begin.
