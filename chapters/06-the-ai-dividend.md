# The Clock on the Wall

*Six weeks a year, and what a trained teacher does with the time*

---

It is 6:47 on a Tuesday morning in Arkansas and a secondary school teacher is already at her desk. This is not unusual. Teachers are almost always at their desks at 6:47 on Tuesday mornings, have been for decades, will be for decades more, regardless of what anyone in a state capitol decides about their profession. What is different this morning — what is quietly, unremarkably different — is what she is *not* doing.

She is not drafting the parent email that explains, for the fourth time this semester, why Marcus needs to come to school having read the chapter. She is not building the assessment rubric from scratch, the one she has built, in slightly different forms, approximately two hundred times in her career. She is not reformatting last year's worksheet into this year's format because the district changed its template again. These things are done. They were done in a fraction of the time they used to take, by a tool that does not teach but can draft, does not understand but can organize, does not know her students but can generate the scaffolding she will then reshape until it does.

She has, by her own accounting, about an hour and a half that she did not have last year.

She uses it to run intervention time for the students who are falling behind. Then enrichment time — actual enrichment, not free reading while she grades — for everyone else. Two slots. Neither existed a year ago. Neither was mandated by policy. Neither appeared in any vendor's pitch deck about the transformative potential of artificial intelligence in K-12 education.

They appeared because something else stopped consuming the hours that are now spent inside them.

---

This chapter is about a number, and about what the number can and cannot carry.

The number is 5.9. It is the mean number of hours per week that weekly AI-using teachers reported saving in a Walton Family Foundation–Gallup survey released in the summer of 2025, drawn from 2,232 U.S. public K-12 teachers across the RAND American Teacher Panel — the strongest available nationally representative sample of American educators. Gallup's researchers translated 5.9 hours per week against a 36-week academic calendar and concluded that it amounts to roughly six full school weeks per year. The headline writes itself, and has written itself, across roughly four hundred press summaries that have done exactly what press summaries do: taken the number and left the method behind.

The method is what makes the number defensible, and the method is what almost no one is discussing.

Here is what Gallup actually did. They asked weekly-AI-using teachers to estimate, task by task, across nine non-overlapping work categories, how many hours AI saves or adds in half-hour increments. The categories were designed so that the same hour cannot be counted twice — *preparing to teach* and *developing assessments* are different cells, and a savings in one cannot also appear as a savings in the other. Per-teacher totals were summed across all nine. Then — and this is the move that turns a number into a number worth using — outliers were trimmed at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles before the mean was taken. The teacher claiming to save eighty hours a week and the teacher claiming AI has cost her twenty: both are gone. What remains is the central ninety-five percent of the distribution, averaged, which is the standard procedure for exactly this kind of self-report data and exactly the move you want to see.

Call this the honest number, with the caveat that honesty here means *best available*, not *verified*.

Because the trimming does not fix everything. It does not correct for the general optimism that nudges people to report their best weeks rather than their worst. It does not establish causation — weekly AI users may simply be teachers with more organized workflows, and the AI may be fitting into a pre-existing efficiency rather than creating one. It does not address social desirability, which is the tendency of survey respondents to tell researchers what sounds good when the survey is literally about whether a technology helps. These are real problems with every self-report time study ever conducted, not special problems with this one. The chapter is not going to wave them away. But they are also, in combination, exactly what a randomized controlled trial is designed to address, and a randomized controlled trial is precisely what six hundred teachers in England were enrolled in while the Gallup survey was still in field.

---

The Education Endowment Foundation's *ChatGPT in Lesson Preparation* trial — a cluster-randomized controlled trial across sixty-eight secondary schools in England, published in December 2024 — is the strongest external corroboration the dividend story currently has. Two hundred and fifty-nine teachers. Thirty-four schools given ChatGPT access plus a structured implementation guide; thirty-four schools continuing without. The subject and year group: science, for students aged eleven to thirteen. Three outcomes measured: self-reported preparation time, blind-panel quality scoring across four dimensions, and teacher workload perceptions.

The lesson preparation finding is a 31 percent reduction. ChatGPT teachers spent 56.2 minutes per week on lesson and resource preparation. Control teachers spent 81.5 minutes. The difference — 25.3 minutes — is not enormous in absolute terms. But it is causally identified, not self-reported, and it survived randomization.

More important than the time finding is the quality finding, because the quality finding is where the most obvious objection comes to die. The objection is this: of course teachers produce worksheets faster when AI generates the worksheets. The worksheets are worse. A blind panel of five experienced science teachers scored thirty lesson resources — fifteen from the ChatGPT condition, fifteen from control — across clarity, engagement, age-appropriateness, and scientific accuracy. They found no statistically significant quality difference on any dimension. The time went down. The quality, as the panel could measure it, held.

To remain a serious objection after this, the critic has to mutate into a claim about quality dimensions the panel could not see, or about long-run effects the ten-week trial could not measure. Both are possible. Neither is certain. And the trial's most important implication for the book's argument is hiding in the design rather than the results: what was tested was not *ChatGPT* versus *nothing*. It was *ChatGPT plus structured guide* versus *nothing*. The implementation support was deliberate and explicit. The trained teacher, equipped with a framework for how to use the tool, is the unit that produced the result. Walk away from the guide, and the result is not guaranteed to follow.

This is not a weakness of the EEF design. It is its most honest and useful feature.

Now hold the two pieces of evidence together, because together is the only way they make sense. The Gallup figure is a self-report across nine task categories among American teachers who already use AI weekly. The EEF figure is a causally identified measurement of one task category among English secondary science teachers randomized into use. The numbers are not commensurable — 5.9 hours across nine cells versus 25.3 minutes in one — but their directions agree, their per-task magnitudes are in the same neighborhood, and the quality claim holds under conditions where quality was actually tested. The AI dividend is not a self-report artifact. It is a pattern robust enough to survive randomization, in at least one task and one context. How far it generalizes, and whether it persists, are genuinely open questions. What it is not is noise.

---

The question the Gallup data raises but cannot answer is what happens to the recovered time. Sixty percent of weekly-AI-using teachers say they have more time for improving teaching and learning. Sixty percent say they have more time for individualized feedback and support. Fifty-seven percent say they have better insights about student learning data. These are the numbers that justify the Arkansas teacher's two new slots, generalized across the sample.

But *say* is doing a lot of work. A teacher reporting that she now has more time for individualized feedback is not the same as a teacher who is actually providing it at a measurably different rate, in ways students can detect. The chain from *recovered time* to *better teaching* to *better learning* has three links, and the Gallup survey only reliably tests the first. For the second link — for the claim that recovered time can actually move a specific and measurable dimension of teaching quality — there is a different body of research entirely, and it comes from a Stanford lab that started by asking a question so simple it sounds obvious.

What if you could measure whether a teacher was actually listening to students?

---

Conversational uptake is the degree to which a teacher's next response builds on what a student just said. The teacher who hears *I think the answer might be related to what happened in the previous lesson* and responds *let's turn to page 47* has not taken the student's contribution up. She has moved past it. The teacher who responds *interesting — you're connecting this to something we saw before. What would that connection predict about the answer here?* has. Higher uptake at the classroom level is empirically associated with higher student achievement. This has been suspected for a long time. What was missing was a way to measure it that could scale.

In 2021, Dorottya Demszky and colleagues at Stanford published a paper in the proceedings of the Association for Computational Linguistics describing a computational method for measuring conversational uptake across full classroom transcripts. The method uses the statistical relationship between what a student says and what the teacher says next to generate a continuous score: how surprising, in the information-theoretic sense, is the teacher's response given the student's contribution? A teacher who tracks the student closely produces a response closely predicted by what came before. A teacher who ignores it produces a response that the model treats as essentially random with respect to the student's words. You can score thousands of classroom exchanges in minutes.

Once you can measure a thing, you can ask whether feedback about that thing changes it.

In a randomized trial published in *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*, Demszky and colleagues gave treatment-condition instructors in Stanford's large free online CS course a weekly email containing personalized, automated feedback on their uptake score — generated by the NLP tool, requiring no human coach, no released time, no platform beyond an inbox. Treatment instructors who engaged with the emails increased their uptake of student contributions by approximately 13 percent relative to control. The intervention is, by the standards of professional development research, almost nothing. An email. Read it. Go back to teaching. A 13 percent improvement.

In a follow-up published in *Computers & Education*, the same approach was applied to 224 mathematics and science teachers in real Utah K-12 classrooms, in collaboration with TeachFX, the classroom audio platform. This time the feedback targeted *focusing questions* — questions that require students to explain their reasoning rather than recall a fact. Teachers who opened the feedback emails showed a 20 percent increase in focusing questions relative to control, statistically significant at *p* < 0.01. By week five, treatment teachers were generating roughly ten additional focusing questions per hour — a 59 percent increase over the control group at that point in the study. The effect was dose-dependent: more recordings, more feedback, larger gains.

Here is what this means for the dividend. The Demszky research describes a mechanism that is cheap to run, that produces a measurable change in a specific high-quality teaching behavior, and that requires nothing more elaborate than a weekly email and a teacher willing to read it. If the AI dividend returns even two of the six hours per week to a trained teacher — let alone all six — that is more than enough slack to record lessons twice a week, read the feedback, and show up Thursday having thought about what the algorithm noticed. The instrument exists. The evidence that it works exists. The time to use it exists, for the teacher who has been prepared to collect the dividend.

The mechanism is not the proof. The Demszky work measures teacher behavior, not student achievement, and the chain from focusing questions to learning outcomes has not been closed in a single study that links all three. What the mechanism provides is something more precise than hope: a credible pathway from recovered time to a specific instructional improvement that the effect-size literature has reason to think matters. The pathway is open. Whether the profession will walk down it — at scale, durably, for the students who need it most — is the question that runs through every remaining chapter of this book.

---

There are four serious objections to the dividend story, and the chapter has to engage all of them, because the people making them are not wrong about the things they are right about.

The first comes from Leon Furze, an Australian AI-in-education researcher who has written some of the most careful criticism of the dividend framing. His argument, distilled: *time saved* is the metric tech companies prefer because it is the metric that makes their product look good. It is not the metric that predicts whether teachers stay in the profession. The workload literature is clear that burnout is driven not by total hours but by the texture of the hours — by autonomy, by respect, by realistic workload expectations, by the presence of mentoring structures. Removing an hour of worksheet generation does not touch any of these. Worse, Furze notes, the tasks AI is best at generating are exactly the compliance tasks that, when stripped away, leave the harder relational work unshielded. Easy work disappears; hard work does not become easier.

This is right. Every word of it is right. The chapter accepts it.

What it does not accept is the implied inference: that because the dividend does not solve burnout, it does not matter. The dividend is not a claim that AI fixes the structural conditions of teaching. It is a claim that AI creates slack, and that slack is the precondition for structural reforms to be physically possible to participate in. The teacher who is underwater on worksheet generation does not have the bandwidth to engage with the mentoring structure the system theoretically owes her. The structural reforms still have to be funded — the dividend does not pay for them. But the dividend is what makes them inhabitable when they arrive. That is the narrower claim. The chapter defends that one.

The second objection, made in parallel by Marc Watkins at the University of Mississippi and the *Chalkdust & Silicon* blog, targets the methodology. The core claim is based on self-estimation, not observed workflows or time audits. The report cannot provide evidence on instructional quality or student outcomes. The framing makes it easy for vendors and policymakers to move fast and cite the headline without reading the footnote. All true. The *Chalkdust & Silicon* author surfaces a finding that the celebratory coverage mostly omits: 52 percent of AI-using teachers taught themselves the tool. Only 31 percent received district-provided training. The dividend is being collected by autodidacts. The training infrastructure the book argues for does not yet exist in most places.

The third objection comes from the Center on Reinventing Public Education, which asked directly: if the recovered time does not demonstrably improve student outcomes, is the dividend just productivity rhetoric? The honest response to this is the Demszky mechanism — the pathway from saved time to measurably better teacher questioning. The honest response is also honest about its limits: the direct chain from saved time to student-outcome improvement has not been closed by any single study. That study is the one the field most needs and has not yet run.

The fourth objection is the one I take most seriously, and it comes from sociology rather than economics. Dan Lortie, in *Schoolteacher*, described in 1975 how teacher work is famously elastic: given more planning time, teachers tend to spend it on more elaborate planning rather than on rest or on relational work. This is Parkinson's Law with a pedagogical twist. The implication for the dividend is pointed: if teachers expand their lesson plans to consume the recovered six hours — adding more differentiation, more detailed rubrics, more elaborate parent communication — then the time is not recovered for high-effect-size relational practices. It is just rerouted.

The EEF trial is the cleanest evidence against this objection. Under randomization, with quality monitored by blind panel, ChatGPT teachers actually spent less time on lesson preparation. They did not expand their plans to fill the recovered minutes. But the EEF trial ran ten weeks. Lortie's effect operates over years. The honest sentence is this: *the short-window evidence is consistent with real time savings, but the long-run elasticity question is open and persistence cannot be assumed*. A multi-year time-audit study would settle it. No such study yet exists.

Together, the four objections set a real boundary. The dividend is documented in magnitude. The reinvestment story — that the recovered time flows into individualized feedback, intervention, and the relational practices with the highest effect sizes — is the part most worth defending and most vulnerable to overclaim. The Gallup data says it happens. The Demszky mechanism says it can happen in a specific and measurable way. Neither of them yet shows that the chain closes on student outcomes at scale. That proof is still open. The chapter's claim has to live inside that boundary. It does. The boundary is wider than nothing and narrower than the press headlines.

---

There is a use of the dividend the chapter wants to argue against, because it is the use the labor-market logic of the broader economy will push toward by default.

The default productivity story runs like this: AI gives teachers a 25 percent productivity gain on lesson preparation, therefore teachers can teach more classes, therefore one teacher can cover the work of 1.25 teachers, therefore the system needs fewer teachers, therefore class sizes can grow. This is the story every other industry's AI deployment is running. It is the story this book is arguing education should not run.

The reason is in the mechanism. The teaching practices with the highest effect sizes on student learning — feedback, teacher-student relationships, collective efficacy, responsive questioning — do not scale by adding students. They scale by adding depth per student. They are generated inside the relational space between this teacher and these students. The class-size literature gives a usable ceiling: the optimal class size for the highest-effect practices is roughly 25 to 30 students. Above that threshold, the relational mechanism degrades. Below it, you can run the mechanism. AI does not change that ceiling. AI does not let one teacher run 25-student feedback loops with 60 students in the room.

What AI does is let one teacher do more *inside the 30 she already has*.

Call this the within-30 argument. The dividend is reinvested in depth, not in scale. The Arkansas teacher's daily intervention slot and daily enrichment slot are within-30 reinvestments. They are not a way to serve more students — they are a way to serve the students she already has more fully. Sixty percent of weekly AI users reporting more time for individualized feedback is, if it is anything real, a within-30 reinvestment. The argument is not that AI allows a teacher to be replaced or multiplied. It is that AI allows a teacher to *teach more* inside the room she already occupies.

The standard productivity story says AI saves teachers time so they can teach more students. This chapter inverts it. AI saves teachers time so they can do more for the students they already have. Same kids. Same room. More of the work only she can do.

---

The 5.9-hour figure describes the 32 percent of teachers who use AI weekly. It does not describe the 40 percent who do not use AI at all.

Hold both populations in your head. Take a teacher who used AI weekly throughout the 2024–25 school year. By the Gallup data's most conservative reading, she generated something in the neighborhood of six full school weeks of recovered time. If she sustains that across a ten-year career — and the persistence question is genuinely open, but take the simplest version for now — that is sixty school weeks. Approximately one and a half years of additional relationship and intervention time, compared to a colleague three classrooms away who is working the same total hours, producing comparable outputs, and quietly falling behind by six weeks per year. The colleague is not lazy. She is not less talented. She is, for whatever reason — no district training, an early bad experience, a principal who told her not to bother — not using the instrument. The dividend is not being collected because the conditions to collect it are not in place.

Over a career, the gap is enormous. At any single point in time, it is invisible. Both teachers walk in at 7:15. Both walk out at 4:30. The difference is what happened inside the hours.

The same gap exists at the school level. A school in a district that invests in AI training collects the dividend. A school in a district that does not, does not. The students at the bottom of the equity distribution lose twice: first by attending schools less likely to fund AI training, and second by sitting in classrooms where the teacher has six fewer weeks per year to do the work that changes outcomes. A facially neutral arrangement — AI is available to every teacher — produces a sharply unequal result because the conditions for capturing the benefit are not equally distributed.

Every empirical finding the pro-AI productivity camp wields is, by symmetry, a finding about this gap. The 5.9 hours is not a finding about AI. It is a finding about what trained teachers do that untrained teachers do not. The variable that determines whether the dividend exists for a given teacher is whether that teacher has been prepared to collect it. The 52 percent of AI-using teachers who taught themselves are evidence that the dividend can be captured by individual effort. The fact that they had to teach themselves is evidence the system has not done its part of the work.

---

The AI dividend is the most plausible empirical finding the pro-teacher-AI argument currently has. It is real in magnitude, measured by the best available methods, corroborated by a randomized trial, and linked to a credible mechanism for pedagogical reinvestment. It is also, at its edges, underspecified in exactly the ways that matter most: it does not prove persistence, it does not measure student outcomes, and it does not address the structural drivers of teacher burnout.

The defensible claim is narrower than the headline. It is also, in what it implies, considerably larger.

The dividend creates slack. The slack creates the bandwidth in which the high-effect practices — feedback, intervention, responsive questioning, the daily enrichment slot that did not exist a year ago — become physically possible to run inside a normal teaching week. The slack does not guarantee they will be run. It guarantees only that the absence of time is no longer the reason they are not.

The trained teacher has the slack. The untrained teacher does not. The gap compounds silently, across days and semesters and careers, inside classrooms that look identical from the hallway.

The Arkansas teacher who told the Walton Foundation researchers what she had done with the time she got back gave, perhaps without knowing it, the most precise description available of what the dividend actually is. She did not say AI saved her time. She said AI saved her time, and here is what she built in the space where the time used to go. The productivity claim and the pedagogical claim, joined by a conjunction.

The productivity claim is what the vendors sell. The pedagogical claim is what justifies the first. Everything else in this book is about the distance between them, and what it takes to close it.
