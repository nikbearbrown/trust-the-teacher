# Chapter 6: The AI Dividend

*Six weeks per year, and what trained teachers do with the time*

---

An Arkansas secondary school teacher, asked in spring 2025 what she had done with the time AI had given back to her week, gave an answer that was not in any vendor brochure. *"Now I've been able to implement daily intervention time for at-risk students, as well as enrichment time every day for all students."* Two slots she did not have a year before. Two slots no policy mandate created. Two slots that appeared in her week because something else stopped consuming the hours that were now spent inside them. The quote surfaces in the [Walton Family Foundation's "AI Dividend" materials](https://www.waltonfamilyfoundation.org/the-ai-dividend-new-survey-shows-ai-is-helping-teachers-reclaim-valuable-time) accompanying the [Gallup–Walton 2025 teacher survey](https://news.gallup.com/poll/691967/three-teachers-weekly-saving-six-weeks-year.aspx), and again in Arkansas press coverage of the state-level results. [verify direct attribution of the quote to a named teacher and the original Walton microsite or PDF where it first appears.]

I want you to hold the structure of what she said, because the structure is the chapter. She did not say *AI saved me time*. She said *AI saved me time, and here is the slot of student-facing work that now exists where the saved time used to be*. The first half of the sentence is the productivity claim. The second half is the pedagogical claim. The productivity claim, by itself, is what tech companies sell. The second half is what justifies the first. A teacher who recovers six hours a week and uses them to leave the parking lot earlier — that is a quality-of-life win, and it matters, and the book will say so. A teacher who recovers six hours a week and uses them to add daily intervention time and daily enrichment time inside a classroom that did not have either — that is something else. That is the variable the book has been building toward. The dividend is not the hours. The dividend is what the hours are spent on.

This chapter is about the magnitude of the time recovered, the methodology by which the magnitude was measured, the corroborating randomized trial that gives the self-report its causal cover, and the gap between trained and untrained teachers that the dividend is silently widening. It is also about the four serious objections to the dividend story, each of which the chapter has to engage and none of which the chapter pretends away. The defensible claim at the end is narrower than the headline. It is also, I think, larger in what it implies.

---

## The six-week number, told the way it was actually constructed

The headline finding is one number, and almost every press summary of it elides the construction. The number is 5.9 hours per week, which Gallup translates into [roughly six full school weeks per year](https://news.gallup.com/poll/691967/three-teachers-weekly-saving-six-weeks-year.aspx) using a 36-week academic calendar and a 30-to-35-hour direct-work week. It comes from the Walton Family Foundation–Gallup study released in late June 2025, titled *Teaching for Tomorrow: Unlocking Six Weeks a Year With AI*. The sample is 2,232 U.S. public K-12 teachers; the field dates are March 18 through April 11, 2025; the sampling frame is the [RAND American Teacher Panel](https://www.rand.org/education-and-labor/survey-panels/aep/about.html), which is a probability-based, nationally representative standing panel of public school teachers. Weights calibrate the final responses to NCES national K-12 teacher demographics across school level, student poverty, student race and Hispanic ethnicity, school size, school urbanicity, teacher gender, teacher race and Hispanic ethnicity, and teacher years of experience. This is, as standing teacher panels go, the strongest American sampling frame available. It is what the [RAND program](https://www.rand.org/education-and-labor/projects/state-of-the-american-teacher-and-the-american-principal.html) describes as "the only three standing nationally representative samples of educators in the U.S." It is also, like every panel, imperfect — the overall empanelment rate is approximately 30 percent, which means roughly two of every three teachers RAND invites to join its panel decline, and the panel cannot fully correct for whatever those declines select on.

Hold that sampling frame in your head. It matters because what comes next is a number that has done more rhetorical work for the AI-in-education argument than any other number produced in the last three years, and the rhetorical work is doing the work in the press while the methodological footnote, which is what makes the rhetorical work defensible, is mostly missing.

The 5.9 hours is not a single-question estimate. It is constructed in four steps. Each weekly-AI-using teacher in the survey was asked, task by task across nine non-overlapping task categories, to estimate in half-hour increments how many hours AI saves or adds for them on that task each week. The nine categories were designed so that the same hour cannot be counted twice — *preparing to teach* and *developing assessments* are different cells, and a teacher saving an hour on one is not also reporting a saved hour on the other for the same work. Per-teacher totals are summed across the nine categories. Then — and this is the step that matters most — outliers are trimmed at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles before the mean is taken. The headline 5.9 hours is the trimmed weekly mean across the weekly-AI-using respondents.

The trimming is what makes the number defensible against the most obvious objection. A self-report time survey will always have a few respondents who claim heroic, implausible savings — *AI saves me 80 hours a week* — and a few who claim implausible costs. Trim the top 2.5 percent and the bottom 2.5 percent and the implausible extremes drop out. What remains is the central 95 percent of the distribution, averaged. This is a standard winsorizing move in self-report time research, and it is exactly the move you want the researchers to have made. The number that survives the filter is the number to discuss.

What the trimming does not fix is everything else. It does not correct for general optimism bias in self-report. It does not verify that the saved time exists outside the respondent's perception of it. It does not establish causation — weekly AI users may differ from monthly or non-users in ways that affect baseline workload independent of AI. It does not address social desirability — a teacher in a survey about whether AI helps may feel more comfortable saying it does. These are real limits and the chapter is not going to wave them away. They are also, in combination, what the EEF randomized controlled trial — the next section — is designed to address.

Before the chapter gets there, two pieces of the Gallup data deserve to sit on the table directly. The adoption denominators first. Sixty percent of U.S. public school teachers used at least one AI tool for work tasks during the 2024-25 school year. Thirty-two percent reported using AI at least weekly; twenty-eight percent monthly or less; and forty percent did not use AI for work at all. The 5.9-hour figure describes the thirty-two percent who use AI at least weekly. It does not describe the forty percent who do not use it. The chapter will come back to that asymmetry — to the gap it is opening between the two populations — at the end. For now, hold the structure: the dividend is a finding about teachers who use the instrument, not a finding about teachers in general.

The reinvestment percentages second. Among weekly AI-using teachers, sixty percent report having more time for *improving teaching and learning*. Sixty percent report more time for *individualized feedback and support* to students. Fifty-seven percent report *better insights about student learning data*. These three figures are the dividend's pedagogical claim — the second half of the Arkansas teacher's sentence, generalized across the sample. They are also another layer of self-report on top of the first. A teacher saying *I now have more time for individualized feedback* is not the same as a teacher *delivering more individualized feedback that students can detect in their work*. The chapter will return to this gap as well.

The honest summary of what the Gallup data can support, on its own, looks like this. Weekly AI-using teachers in a nationally representative sample self-report roughly six hours per week of time savings, with outliers trimmed, on a curated and non-overlapping list of nine task categories. A clear majority of those teachers — around sixty percent each — say they reinvest at least some of the recovered time in higher-quality instructional work and individualized feedback. The pattern is broadly consistent across task categories: between sixty and eighty-four percent of weekly users report some time savings on whichever task they use AI for. Very few — seven percent or fewer — report that AI makes any given task take *more* time.

What the data cannot support, on its own, is the stronger claim. It cannot show that six hours of real, audited time were saved. It cannot show that the saved time produced six hours of additional instruction-improving work. It cannot establish causation between AI adoption and time savings, because the comparison is cross-sectional between weekly users and others. And it cannot generalize to the forty percent of teachers who are not using AI. The same teachers who report saving time may be selecting in *because* AI fits their specific workflow; the non-users may be filtering out for reasons that would also block the time savings if they were forced to adopt.

That is the boundary the chapter has to work inside. The Gallup study is the strongest American teacher self-report on AI workload effects we have. The boundary on what self-report can prove is also real. The randomized British trial that landed six months earlier is the strongest external evidence we have that the central pattern is not a self-report artifact.

## The EEF trial — the strongest external corroboration the field has

The [Education Endowment Foundation's *ChatGPT in Lesson Preparation* trial](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/projects-and-evaluation/projects/choices-in-edtech-using-generative-ai-chatgpt-for-ks3-science-lesson-preparation-2024-teacher-choices-trial), published in December 2024, is the chapter's most important non-self-report evidence and the cleanest randomized test of the time-savings claim in the AI-in-education literature. It is a cluster-randomized controlled trial run in real schools, with school-level allocation and blind-panel quality assessment. The design is exactly what the Gallup self-report lacks.

Two hundred and fifty-nine teachers across sixty-eight secondary schools in England. One hundred and twenty-nine teachers in thirty-four schools were given ChatGPT access plus a structured implementation guide developed by Bain & Company's Social Impact practice with input from schools and education charities. One hundred and thirty teachers in thirty-four schools continued without ChatGPT or the guide. The subject and grade were Year 7 and Year 8 science — roughly U.S. grades 6 and 7. Three outcomes were measured: self-reported minutes per week on lesson and resource preparation; lesson-resource quality, scored by a blind panel of five science teachers across four dimensions (clarity, engagement, age and ability appropriateness, scientific accuracy); and teacher-reported workload perceptions and well-being. The trial ran during 2024 and reported in December of that year. [verify whether the randomization was at school level (cluster) or teacher level — the published materials describe a cluster design with school-level allocation; confirm against the full evaluation report.]

The headline findings published in [the EEF news release](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/news/teachers-using-chatgpt-alongside-a-guide-to-support-them-to-use-it-effectively-can-cut-lesson-planning-time-by-over-30-per-cent) and the [Schools Week coverage](https://schoolsweek.co.uk/using-chatgpt-can-cut-lesson-planning-by-31-eef-study/) are three. First: ChatGPT teachers spent 56.2 minutes per week on lesson and resource preparation. Control teachers spent 81.5 minutes. The difference is 25.3 minutes per week, a 31 percent reduction, statistically significant. Second: the blind panel found no statistically significant difference in lesson-resource quality between the two groups across any of the four scoring dimensions. The time savings did not come at the cost of detectable quality loss in the work the panel could see. Third: teachers in the ChatGPT condition reported lower lesson-preparation workload, with no detectable effect on broader well-being measures within the trial window.

The Gallup self-report and the EEF randomized result are not the same number. They cannot be directly compared. Gallup measures self-reported time savings summed across nine task categories among teachers who use AI weekly; EEF measures observed-by-survey time spent on one task category — lesson preparation — for teachers randomized into ChatGPT use for ten weeks. The Gallup figure is roughly 5.9 hours per week across nine cells. The EEF figure is 25.3 minutes per week in one cell. The two are not commensurable. What they do is sit on a consistent direction with magnitudes in the same neighborhood: 25 minutes saved on one task scales plausibly into several hours when the same kind of saving is repeated across nine of them. The EEF result is what gives the Gallup self-report causal cover for at least the lesson-prep portion of its claim.

The quality finding is the more important defense. The most plausible objection to any productivity gain in teaching work is the obvious one: *the teacher is producing the same worksheets faster because the worksheets are now worse*. Inside the EEF trial window, with blind panel scoring across four quality dimensions, that objection does not survive. Time goes down. Detected quality holds. The objection has to mutate — into a claim about quality dimensions the panel could not detect, or into a claim about longer-term elasticity the trial could not measure — to remain an objection at all.

There are real limits to the EEF result the chapter has to name. Year 7 and Year 8 science is one corner of the curriculum. Generalization to English literature, history, early literacy, or art is not established by this trial. The blind panel scored thirty lesson resources — fifteen ChatGPT, fifteen non-ChatGPT — which is a sample of output and not a measurement of student learning outcomes. The EEF report itself describes the quality finding as "promising" and cautions that the result "should be treated with caution." The implementation support was structured: teachers received a guide. What the trial tested is not *ChatGPT vs. control*; it is *ChatGPT plus structured guide vs. control*. For the book's argument this is a feature — *trained* AI use is the variable that produced the result — but it is a limit on any naive "give every teacher ChatGPT and walk away" reading of the finding. And the trial ran ten weeks. The persistence question — whether the time savings survive when teachers, freed up, expand the elaborateness of their lesson plans to consume the recovered time — cannot be answered inside that window. A second EEF trial, the [Aila/Oak National Academy follow-up with 450 KS2 primary teachers across 86 schools](https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/projects-and-evaluation/projects/aila-teacher-choices-trial), is in field and scheduled to report in autumn 2026. That trial tests a more pedagogy-aware AI assistant rather than raw ChatGPT, and pushes the evidence into primary school for the first time.

Hold the two pieces of evidence together. The Gallup figure is a self-report; the EEF figure is causally identified. The Gallup figure spans nine task categories; the EEF figure spans one. The Gallup figure comes from American teachers across all grades; the EEF figure comes from English secondary science teachers. The directions agree. The magnitudes are in the same neighborhood per task. The quality result, where it has been tested under randomization, holds. The honest summary is that the AI dividend is not a self-report artifact. The 5.9-hour figure is plausibly somewhere between an honest mean of teachers' best estimates and an optimistic upper-bound on what a future time-audit study would actually measure. *Somewhere* is not *exactly*. The chapter is not going to pretend it knows.

## The mechanism — what reinvestment can actually look like

The chapter has now established that the time exists, in some defensible magnitude, and that the quality of what is produced inside the time does not collapse when the time gets compressed. What it has not established is what the reinvestment of the recovered time can actually become — and this is the conceptual hinge the rest of the chapter turns on. The Gallup study reports that around sixty percent of weekly AI-using teachers say they reinvest the recovered time in individualized feedback and improving teaching and learning. *Say* is doing the work. We need an existence proof: a study that shows a measurable change in some specific teaching behavior, traceable to a low-touch intervention of the kind the AI dividend could plausibly fund. The Stanford [M-Powering Teachers research](https://edunlp.stanford.edu/projects/m-powering-teachers) from Dorottya Demszky's lab is the cleanest existence proof we have.

Start with the construct, because the construct is what makes the rest of the work possible. In a 2021 ACL paper, Demszky, Liu, Mancenido, Cohen, Hill, Jurafsky, and Hashimoto [operationalized something the education literature had long discussed but never been able to compute](https://aclanthology.org/2021.acl-long.130/): *conversational uptake*. Uptake is the degree to which a teacher's next utterance builds on what a student just said. A teacher who responds *good job, now turn to page 47* is not taking up the student's contribution; she is moving past it. A teacher who responds *so you're saying X depends on Y — what would happen if X were negative?* is. Higher uptake at the classroom level is empirically associated with higher student achievement. The Demszky paper measured it using pointwise Jensen-Shannon Divergence estimated through next-utterance classification — which, translated out of the NLP vocabulary, is a way of asking *given what the student just said, how surprising is the teacher's response*. A teacher who genuinely picks up the student's contribution produces a response that is closely tied to it; a teacher who ignores it produces a response that is, in the statistical sense, indifferent to what came before.

This matters because once you can measure uptake you can ask whether feedback can move it.

In a randomized trial published in 2024 in *[Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/01623737231169270)*, Demszky and colleagues did exactly that. The setting was Code in Place, Stanford's large free online introductory CS course. Approximately 1,136 instructors. Treatment-condition instructors received weekly emails containing personalized, automated feedback on their uptake of student ideas during 1:1 sessions, generated by the M-Powering Teachers NLP tool. Control instructors received no such emails. The treatment is, by intervention standards, almost nothing. No coach. No release time. No platform requirement beyond reading the email and going back to class. Treatment instructors who engaged with the feedback increased uptake of student contributions by approximately 13 percent. [verify the 13 percent vs. "roughly 10 percent" framing — the paper reports several specifications; the headline specification in the published EEPA version should be cited and the variation noted if relevant.]

Then they did it in K-12 classrooms. In a [follow-up trial published in *Computers & Education* in 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0360131524001970), 224 mathematics and science teachers in Utah K-12 classrooms received automated email feedback on their questioning patterns through a collaboration with TeachFX, the classroom audio-recording platform. The feedback targeted teachers' use of *focusing questions* — questions that press students for reasoning rather than recall. Treatment teachers opened the feedback emails 53 to 65 percent of the time. Treatment teachers showed a 20 percent increase in use of focusing questions, statistically significant at p < 0.01, relative to control. The effect was dose-dependent: teachers who recorded their lessons more consistently received more feedback and showed larger effects. By week five, treatment teachers were producing roughly ten additional focusing questions per hour — a 59 percent increase over the control group at that timepoint.

This is the existence proof. A low-touch, automated, weekly email about the linguistic quality of a teacher's classroom interactions measurably moved that quality. It did not require a coach. It did not require release time. It did not require platform adoption beyond TeachFX recording. It required the teacher to read the email Monday morning and to keep recording lessons. If the AI dividend gives a teacher back two hours a week — let alone six — that is enough slack to record her lessons twice a week, read the feedback, and produce a 20 percent improvement in the linguistic quality of her classroom questioning. The chapter is not arguing she will. The chapter is arguing the mechanism exists.

Be careful what the Demszky work establishes and what it does not. It does not measure student achievement. The brick-and-mortar trial measured teacher behavior — questioning patterns — and not student learning outcomes downstream. The Code in Place trial measured uptake, not student course outcomes in CS. The feedback targeted one specific discourse practice; whether broader pedagogical feedback would scale similarly is open. And TeachFX is a real platform with real costs. The trials do not establish that automated feedback is cost-competitive with the human coaching that the [Kraft, Blazar, and Hogan meta-analysis from Chapter 4](https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/mkraft/files/kraft_blazar_hogan_2018_teacher_coaching.pdf) identifies as the gold-standard intervention for teacher practice change (d = 0.49 on instruction, d = 0.18 on student achievement). The Demszky work is not a substitute for that literature. It is a demonstration that a much cheaper instrument can move a teacher-behavior variable that the achievement literature has reason to think matters.

The chapter's spine, said plainly: *time savings without quality reinvestment is a productivity story; quality reinvestment without time savings is an aspiration; the two together — a documented six-hour-per-week recovery on one side, and a documented mechanism for converting recovered time into measurably better teacher questioning on the other — is the AI dividend the book is arguing for*. That linkage is the part the field is still building toward, and the part the chapter wants the reader to be able to see.

## The serious critique — four ways the dividend story could be wrong

The chapter is committed, per the book's standing rule, to engaging the strongest counter-evidence before resolving its own claim. The Walton-Gallup headline number has drawn three distinct serious critiques in 2025, plus a fourth that runs through the broader teacher workload literature. None of them refute the dividend. All four of them sharpen what the dividend can be defended as.

**Leon Furze**, an Australian AI-in-education researcher who has written some of the most-read critical pieces in the field, published *["Time Saved" is the Wrong Measurement for Teacher Workload and AI](https://leonfurze.com/2025/06/30/time-saved-is-the-wrong-measurement-for-teacher-workload-and-ai/)* in June 2025 and a [follow-up in July](https://leonfurze.com/2025/07/07/beyond-time-saving-how-genai-might-actually-help-teacher-workload/). His argument, distilled: the *time saved* framing is the metric tech companies prefer because it is the metric that makes their product look good. It is not the metric that predicts teacher retention or burnout. The teacher workload literature is clear that what drives burnout is not total hours but *realistic workloads, adequate materials, mentoring, autonomy, and respect*. Saving an hour on a worksheet does not address any of these. Worse, Furze argues, the tasks AI is best at — generating worksheets, drafting parent emails — are exactly the compliance-administrative tasks that, when stripped away, leave the harder relational work behind. The hard work does not get easier because the easy work is gone. Therefore, presenting *AI saves teachers time* as the headline risks crowding out the structural reforms — planning time protected by policy, mentorship infrastructure, decision authority over classroom technology — that actually retain teachers.

Furze is right about the structural reforms. He is right that *time saved* is not *burnout solved*. The chapter has to accept both. What he is not right about is the implication. The dividend is not a claim that AI fixes burnout. It is a claim that AI creates slack — and that slack is the precondition for the structural reforms to have any chance of working. A teacher who is drowning in worksheet generation does not have the bandwidth to participate in the mentorship structure the system theoretically owes her. Six hours a week is the bandwidth. The structural reforms still have to be funded; the dividend does not pay for them. But the dividend is what makes them physically possible to participate in. That is the narrower claim. The chapter defends that one.

**Marc Watkins** at the University of Mississippi and the [*Chalkdust & Silicon*](https://medium.com/chalkdust-silicon/the-walton-gallup-ai-report-deserves-context-not-just-headlines-56164a7303a3) blog independently published more measured critiques making parallel points. The report's core claim is based on self-estimation, not observed workflows or time audits. True. The report does not — and cannot — provide evidence on instructional quality, student outcomes, or equity. True. The framing makes it "easy for vendors and policymakers to weaponize" as justification for rapid AI rollouts. Also true, and worth taking seriously in a chapter that does not want to be that weapon. Reported relationships are not necessarily causal: weekly AI users may differ from non-users in ways that affect baseline workload independent of AI use. True. The *Chalkdust & Silicon* author surfaces a finding the celebratory coverage glosses: 52 percent of teachers using AI taught themselves; only 31 percent received district-provided training. That is the chapter's hook into [Chapter 8](#) — the dividend is being collected by autodidacts, not by trained professionals. The structural training infrastructure the book argues for is still missing.

**The Center on Reinventing Public Education** published the most pointed version of the question, asking in summer 2025 *[The "AI Dividend" — Does AI Create More Time for Teachers?](https://crpe.substack.com/p/the-ai-dividenddoes-ai-create-more)* Their central observation: the survey does not measure whether the recovered time produces measurably better outcomes for students, and absent that link, the dividend is just productivity rhetoric. The response the chapter has to that critique is the Demszky M-Powering Teachers literature — the mechanism that closes the gap between teacher-behavior change and at least some intermediate outcomes plausibly connected to student learning. The response is honest only to the extent it acknowledges that the *direct* chain — saved time → reinvested time → measurable student-outcome improvement — has not yet been established by any single study. That study is the one the field most needs and has not yet run.

The fourth objection is the one I take most seriously, and it comes from outside the AI literature entirely. Dan Lortie's *Schoolteacher* (1975) and the broader teacher workload literature describe how teacher work is famously elastic. When teachers are given more planning time, the empirical pattern is that they tend to spend it on more elaborate planning rather than on rest or on relational work. This is a version of Parkinson's law specific to teaching. The implication for the dividend is sharp: if teachers expand their lesson plans to consume the recovered six hours — adding more differentiation, more detailed assessments, more elaborate parent communication — the time is not recovered for the high-effect-size relational practices the chapter wants to claim. It is just rerouted to different administrative work, possibly less burdensome but not pedagogically transformed.

The EEF trial is the cleanest evidence we have *against* this objection. In a randomized condition, teachers actually did spend less total time on lesson preparation — 56.2 minutes instead of 81.5 — and the quality of the resources produced did not detectably collapse. The teachers did not expand their plans to fill the time. But the EEF trial ran ten weeks. The Lortie effect operates over years. The honest sentence is *the short-window evidence is consistent with real time savings, but the long-run elasticity question is open and the persistence cannot be assumed*. Whether a teacher in year three of AI use still has the six hours, or whether her planning has expanded to absorb most of them while her relational work has not grown, is something only a multi-year time-audit study can settle. No such study yet exists.

Read together, the four critiques set a real boundary on what the dividend claim can carry. *Yes*: weekly AI-using teachers self-report saving roughly six hours per week, in a nationally representative survey with reasonable methodology, corroborated by a UK RCT showing a 31 percent reduction in lesson-prep time with quality maintained. *Yes*: most of those teachers say they reinvest at least some of the recovered time in higher-quality work. *Yes*: the Demszky research provides a credible mechanism for converting that reinvested time into measurably better teacher questioning. *Not yet*: direct evidence that the reinvested time produces measurably better student outcomes at scale. *Not addressed*: whether AI time savings persist beyond short trial windows, or whether they alleviate teacher burnout — Furze's structural-burnout objection is well-supported, and AI time savings on individual tasks probably do not, by themselves, solve the structural problem.

The chapter's claim has to live inside that boundary. It does. The boundary is wider than nothing and narrower than the press headlines. The dividend is the most plausible empirical finding the pro-teacher-AI argument currently has. It is not a guarantee. It is a window — six hours per week of slack the trained teacher has to do work the algorithm cannot do — and the question is what we build around that window.

## The within-30 argument — why the dividend has to be reinvested in depth, not scale

There is a use of the dividend the chapter wants to argue against, because it is the use the labor-market logic of the broader economy will push toward by default, and because it is the use that would erode the very practices the dividend is supposed to enable.

The default productivity story goes like this. AI gives teachers a 25 percent productivity gain on lesson prep. Therefore teachers can teach more classes. Therefore one teacher can cover the work of 1.25 teachers. Therefore the system needs fewer teachers. Therefore class sizes can grow. Therefore the AI dividend is a labor-substitution dividend, and the savings accrue to the budget, not to the student.

This is the story every other industry's AI deployment is running, more or less in these terms. It is the story the chapter is arguing teaching should not run.

The reason is in [Chapter 4](#). The teacher-mediated effect sizes — collective teacher efficacy, student expectations, teacher credibility, teacher-student relationships, feedback — live in the dyad and the small group. They do not scale by adding students. They scale by adding depth per student. Hattie's effect-size literature, the Chetty-Friedman-Rockoff value-added literature, and the Kraft-Blazar-Hogan coaching literature all describe the same surface: practices whose effect is generated inside the relational space between this teacher and these students. The class-size literature — the [Krueger STAR reanalysis](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2587015), the Hanushek dispute, the [Boyd-Loeb-Grossman-Staiger NYC work](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.104.9.2633) — gives a usable ceiling: the optimal class size for the highest-effect-size practices is roughly 25 to 30 students. Above that, the relational mechanism degrades. Below it, you can run the mechanism. AI does not change that ceiling. AI does not let one teacher run twenty-five-student feedback loops with sixty students in the room. It lets one teacher do more inside the twenty-five-student room she already has.

This is what the chapter is calling the *within-30 argument*, after the class-size ceiling. The dividend is reinvested in depth, not in scale. The Arkansas teacher's daily intervention slot and daily enrichment slot are within-30 reinvestments. They are not a way to teach more students; they are a way to do more for the students she already has. Sixty percent of weekly AI users reporting more time for individualized feedback is, if it is anything, a within-30 reinvestment. Fifty-seven percent reporting better insights about student learning data is, if it is anything, a within-30 reinvestment. The dividend lands inside the existing classroom, where the high-effect practices are, or it stops mattering for student outcomes.

The crosswalk to Hattie's ranking is direct enough that I want to put it on the page once. *If* the saved time is reinvested in feedback (d ≈ 0.50 to 0.73 in the canonical ranking, lower in the revised MetaX values but still above the hinge), in individualized teacher-student relationships (d ≈ 0.72), in coordinated faculty practice that builds collective efficacy (the contested but large CTE construct), and in the responsive questioning that the Demszky M-Powering Teachers work shows is movable through low-touch feedback — *then* the dividend cashes into the effect sizes that move student outcomes. The achievement-outcome literature gives us the *then*. The Walton survey gives us the *if* with reasonable evidence. The Demszky work gives us a mechanism for converting *if* into *then* on at least one specific practice. Closing the chain — building the workforce that actually does the reinvestment, at scale, durably — is the work the AI+1 training infrastructure of [Chapter 8](#) is supposed to do.

The chapter's distinctive rhetorical move sits here. The standard productivity story says *AI saves teachers time so they can teach more students*. The chapter inverts it. **AI saves teachers time so they can do more for the students they already have.** Same kids. Same room. More of the work only she can do.

The closing argument of the chapter is therefore not productivity. It is depth. The dividend is six weeks per year of relationship and intervention time the teacher could not previously afford. Spent on the same students. Inside the same room. Producing more of the work only she can do. That is the claim. Everything in the empirical record so far is consistent with it. None of the empirical record so far proves the student-outcome part of it. The chapter is going to land on that gap honestly in the closing section.

## The untrained-teacher gap — what compounds over a career

The 5.9-hour figure describes the thirty-two percent of teachers who use AI weekly. It does not describe the forty percent of teachers who do not use AI at all. Hold both populations in your head, because the gap between them is the chapter's hand-off to the equity argument the book runs in [Chapter 11](#).

Take a thirty-something teacher who used AI weekly throughout the 2024-25 school year. By the Walton survey's accounting, she generated six full school weeks per year of recovered time. If she sustains that across a ten-year career — and the persistence question is open, but treat the simplest version of it for now — that is sixty school weeks. Approximately one and a half years of recovered instructional time, compared to a colleague three doors down who is working the same total hours producing the same total output without the instrument. The colleague is not lazy. She is not less talented. She is — for whatever reason: no district training, no time to learn the tool, an early bad experience, a principal who told her not to bother — not using the instrument. The dividend is not being collected because the conditions to collect it are not in place. Over a career, the gap compounds. It is enormous. And it is invisible at any single point in time, because both teachers walk in at 7:15 and walk out at 4:30. The difference is what happened inside the hours.

The same gap exists at the school level. A school in a district that has invested in AI training collects the dividend. A school in a district that has not, does not. The gap compounds at the school level, the district level, the regional level. The students at the bottom of the equity distribution lose twice — first by attending schools less likely to train teachers in AI, and second by sitting in classrooms where the teacher has six fewer weeks per year to do the high-effect-size work the rest of the book argues is what changes outcomes.

This is the structural inverse of the equity dynamic [Chapter 1](#) named for the phone ban. There, the policy stripped a class of students of their home internet connection without replacing it. Here, the absence of training is stripping the same class of students of the dividend that would have flowed into their classrooms had their teacher been prepared to collect it. The mechanism is different. The pattern lands in the same place: a facially neutral arrangement — *AI is available to every teacher* — produces a sharply unequal outcome because the conditions for capturing the benefit are not equally distributed. The dividend, like the ban, is the policy that does not see the distribution it produces. [Chapter 11](#) is the chapter that asks what would.

The chapter's strategic-frame move is here, and worth saying explicitly. *Every empirical finding the pro-AI camp wields about productivity is, by symmetry, a finding about what trained teachers do and untrained teachers do not. The five-point-nine hours is not a finding about AI. It is a finding about the gap that opens between teachers who have been prepared to use the instrument and teachers who have not.* The 40 percent of teachers not using AI are working the same hours their trained colleagues are working. They are producing comparable outputs. They are also, silently, falling behind by six weeks of relationship time per year. The variable that determines whether the dividend exists for a given teacher is whether that teacher has been trained — formally, informally, by district investment or by autodidactic effort — to capture it. The 52 percent of teachers using AI who taught themselves are evidence that the dividend can be captured by individual heroics. The fact that they had to teach themselves is evidence the system has not done its part of the work.

## The chapter's turn

The AI dividend is the most plausible empirical finding the pro-teacher-AI argument currently has. The Gallup-Walton survey, drawn from the strongest available American teacher sampling frame, weighted to NCES national demographics and trimmed against outliers, reports that weekly-AI-using teachers save approximately six hours per week — about six full school weeks per year. The EEF randomized controlled trial gives the self-report causal cover for the lesson-preparation portion of the claim, showing a 31 percent reduction in prep time with no detected quality loss in blind-panel review. The Demszky M-Powering Teachers research provides a credible mechanism for converting recovered time into measurably better teaching practice through low-touch automated feedback. The Mollick-Dell'Acqua [BCG study with 758 consultants](https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/24-013_d9b45b68-9e74-42d6-a1c6-c72fb70c7282.pdf) — 25 percent faster, 40 percent higher quality on tasks inside AI's capability frontier, with the largest gains accruing to below-average performers — places the teacher-survey magnitudes inside the range that randomized field experiments outside K-12 have already shown for AI-assisted knowledge work. The four magnitudes do not all measure the same thing, and they cannot be averaged. They point in the same direction.

The dividend is real in magnitude. It is contested in how that magnitude was measured. The reinvestment story — that the recovered time goes into individualized feedback, intervention, enrichment, and the relational practices the effect-size literature identifies as highest-leverage — is the part most worth defending and most vulnerable to overclaim. The Walton self-report says reinvestment happens. The Demszky mechanism says reinvestment of the right kind can move measurable teacher behaviors. Neither of them yet shows that the chain closes on student outcomes at scale. That study has not been run. It is the study the field most needs.

The chapter's narrower claim is this. AI gives the trained teacher slack. The slack does not, by itself, fix burnout — Furze is right about the structural conditions that retain teachers, and the dividend does not pay for them. The slack does, however, create the bandwidth in which the high-effect-size practices the rest of the book has been building toward — feedback, individualized attention, responsive questioning, faculty-level coordination — become physically possible to run inside a normal teaching week. That is what the dividend can be defended as. Not a guarantee. A precondition. The slack is what makes the structural reforms able to land.

The untrained teacher does not get the slack. She works the same total hours producing the same total outputs, while her trained colleagues silently generate six extra weeks of relationship and intervention time per year. Multiply across a career, multiply across a school, multiply across a district, multiply across the distribution of districts by income, and the gap is the structural inequity the book has been describing in another vocabulary. The variable is whether the teacher has been trained to capture the dividend. The dividend itself is downstream of that variable. *Every empirical finding the pro-AI productivity camp wields is, by symmetry, a finding about the widening gap between teachers who have been prepared to use the instrument and teachers who have not.* The book is about closing that gap. The chapter is about the size of what is being lost while it stays open.

[Chapter 7](#) takes the same evidence apart from the student side: what happens when students get the tool without the teacher who knows how to channel it. The dividend story is what trained teachers do with AI. The cognitive-offloading story is what untrained students do with it. They are the same evidence read from two ends of the classroom. The conclusion in both cases routes back through the same variable. The teacher.

## What the research leaves open

Several things this chapter has not settled, and which the available evidence does not yet settle either. The persistence question is the largest one: does the 5.9-hour weekly savings hold across multi-year horizons, or does teacher work expand to absorb the recovered time, in the Lortie/Parkinson pattern the broader workload literature has documented for decades? The EEF ten-week trial cannot answer this; a multi-year time-audit study has not been published. The causal magnitude question is open in a parallel way: the 5.9-hour figure is a self-report; the EEF 25.3-minute figure is causally identified but covers only one task category; the multi-task, randomized, time-audit study with classroom observation does not yet exist for K-12 AI. The student-outcome question is the most important open one: no study has yet directly tested whether the reinvested AI dividend produces measurable student-achievement gains. The Demszky uptake studies measured teacher behavior, not student learning. The chain from saved time to reinvested time to better student outcomes has not been closed in a single study, and it should be. The burnout question is open in a different way: time savings on individual tasks and teacher retention are not the same construct, and the Furze critique is right that the structural drivers of burnout — workload realism, mentoring, autonomy, respect — are not addressed by an AI productivity gain. The distributional questions are under-reported: the Walton survey gives aggregate national figures and does not publish granular subgroup analyses by school income or district size that would let the equity argument be made directly from its data. And the AI-model-evolution question runs through everything: the 2025 survey captured 2024-25 tool capabilities, and by 2027 the tools will be substantially different, in ways that may grow, plateau, or shrink the dividend in ways the current evidence cannot anticipate.

## Still puzzling

If the Demszky M-Powering Teachers result holds — a 20 percent increase in focusing questions from a low-touch weekly email — then the bandwidth required to collect that gain is small enough that even a fraction of the AI dividend could fund it at scale. Why are no districts running the obvious follow-up: protect two of the recovered six hours per week for structured automated-feedback engagement, and measure both teacher behavior and student outcomes over an academic year? The instrument exists. The dividend that would fund it exists. The intervention is operationally light. What is missing is the institutional will to combine the two into a study large enough to settle whether the chain from saved time to student outcomes can be made to close.

## What would change my mind

A multi-year, large-sample time-audit study — not self-report — showing that the 5.9-hour weekly savings either does not survive past the first year of AI use, or that teachers reroute the recovered time entirely into more elaborate administrative work rather than into the relational practices the chapter has argued are the dividend's pedagogical justification. Either finding would substantially weaken the chapter's central claim. The first would mean the dividend is a short-window effect, not a durable feature of trained-teacher workflows. The second would mean the dividend exists but does not, in practice, cash into the effect-size literature the chapter has been routing it through. I think the second is the more likely failure mode, and the harder one to defend against without much better measurement than the field currently has. A well-designed three-year audit study could distinguish these worlds. The book's central argument would survive either; the AI-dividend chapter would not survive both.
