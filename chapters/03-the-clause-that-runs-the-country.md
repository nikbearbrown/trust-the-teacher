# The Clause That Runs the Country


The teacher has thirty-one seconds left in the lesson.

She's standing at the front of a seventh-grade science classroom in Broward County, Florida — late September, the AC running hard against the heat outside — and one of her students has just asked whether he can photograph the diagram on the whiteboard because he didn't finish copying it down. His phone is on his desk. It's been on his desk all morning, face-down and quiet, technically not in use. Now he's asking.

She knows what the law says. She passed a professional development module on it in August. Florida's HB 379, signed in 2023, updated in 2025, extended to cover the entire school day: students may not use wireless communications devices during instructional time *except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes*. Six words. She is the teacher. This is, she believes, instructional time. And the question she now has to answer — in thirty seconds, or twenty, while twenty-nine other students watch to see what she does — is whether photographing a whiteboard diagram counts.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — HB 379 (2023) statutory quote verbatim accurate. "Updated in 2025, extended to cover the entire school day" plausible (HB 1105 2025) but should cite the 2025 update bill. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


She says yes.

Two classrooms down, a different teacher says no to the same question.

Neither of them is wrong, exactly. Neither of them is right. They are both operating inside the same four-word hole at the center of the law: *for educational purposes*. The phrase is in thirty-three state phone ban statutes. It is the hinge between a technology ban and a technology policy. It does all of the work that distinguishes the device-as-distraction from the device-as-instrument. And in every one of those thirty-three states, the legislators who wrote those words declined to say what they mean.

That is what this chapter is about. Not the ban. The blank.

## Twenty-two states, one shared blank

By the close of 2025, the pace of state phone legislation had become unusual enough to attract attention from people who watch state legislation for a living and are not usually surprised by anything. Twenty-two states passed K-12 cell phone restrictions in 2025 alone. State legislatures do not typically agree on anything this fast.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED for August 2025 Ballotpedia framing. The 22-state figure was Ballotpedia's August 2025 snapshot of 2025 enactments; the count grew through Q4 2025. Recommend pinning to date or using the year-end total. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->
 Climate policy, education funding, healthcare — these are contentious, slow, fought over for years. Phone bans moved through chambers in weeks. The political coalition was, and remains, odd: progressive Democrats worried about the mental health of adolescent girls, conservative Republicans worried about social media and attention spans, school boards worried about liability, parents worried about everything at once. The coalition agreed on the ban. It agreed, with equal firmness, on the exemption.

Because every one of these laws contains one. Florida's exemption is "except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes." New York's, signed by Governor Hochul in May 2025, authorizes exceptions "for a specific educational purpose." Ohio's 2024 law requires district policies to allow phone use for "student learning as determined by school officials." Three states. Three formulations. One shared word: *educational*. Not one statute explains what that word is supposed to catch.

This is not careless legislative drafting. The statutes themselves are careful — they enumerate other exceptions with precision. Louisiana's 2024 Act 313 specifies, in fine detail, that phones may be used for medical accommodations under IEPs, 504 plans, and Individualized Health Plans, and during "an emergency," which it defines as "an actual or imminent threat to public health or safety which may result in loss of life, injury, or property damage." Louisiana's legislators knew how to write a specific exception when they wanted one. They chose not to write one for educational use. The blank is not an accident. It is a choice.

The choice has consequences that are currently being lived out in classrooms across the country — thirty-one seconds at a time.

## Thirty-five laws, six approaches, zero definitions

Lay the statutes next to each other and the variation reveals something the headlines about the "phone ban wave" mostly miss. These are not thirty-three versions of the same policy. They are thirty-three versions of thirty-five different guesses at the same unanswered question.

California's AB 3216, the Phone-Free School Act signed by Governor Newsom in September 2024, is what lawyers call a mandate-the-policy law. The state doesn't write the rule; it tells districts to write a rule by July 1, 2026. On the question of educational exceptions, it defers entirely to the teacher: exceptions are permitted "when a teacher or administrator of the school grants permission to a pupil." No criterion. The teacher is the rule.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — AB 3216 signed Sept 23, 2024 by Gov. Newsom; July 1, 2026 district policy deadline; teacher/administrator permission exception in Ed. Code § 48901.7 matches the chapter's quote. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


Virginia took the opposite position. Governor Youngkin's guidance, issued through an executive order in July 2024 and finalized in December, states the official position in five words: *cell phones are not an option for instructional use*. Virginia is the only state where the guidance explicitly refuses the educational-purposes carve-out as a category. No ambiguity. No teacher judgment required. No student is photographing the whiteboard.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: PARTIAL — EO 33 issued July 9, 2024 CONFIRMED. "Finalized in December" appears to be a date error — VDOE guidance was finalized by EO 33's January 1, 2025 deadline. Recommend citing the specific VDOE guidance source or rendering as paraphrase. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


South Carolina, working from a budget proviso and a September 2024 state board policy, came closest to writing what an operational definition actually looks like. "Classroom Use" is defined as phone use "explicitly approved by the District Superintendent or his/her designee in writing, and the approval must include alignment with a standards-aligned educational objective that cannot be reasonably achieved using district-owned devices." Two tests. Written prior approval from a named administrator. A demonstration that the school Chromebook cannot accomplish the same thing. It is the most precise formulation in the country.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — SC State Board of Education approved model policy September 3, 2024; quoted "standards-aligned educational objective that cannot be reasonably achieved using district-owned devices" matches the model policy verbatim. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


It is also, by any practical measure, unusable. A seventh-grader with thirty seconds left before the bell is not going to produce written superintendent authorization for a photograph of a whiteboard diagram.

Texas and Arkansas found a different solution: define the problem out of existence. Texas's HB 1481, signed in June 2025, and Arkansas's Bell to Bell, No Cell Act, signed in February 2025, both exempt school-issued devices from their bans entirely. The personal phone stays in the bag. The Chromebook from the cart is fine. The educational question is resolved structurally rather than by definition: whatever the district issues, the district evidently endorses. Whatever the student brings from home, the district cannot evaluate in real time.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — Texas HB 1481 signed June 20, 2025 (excludes "an electronic device provided to a student by the district or charter school" from "personal communication device"); Arkansas Act 122 (SB 142) signed Feb 20, 2025 by Gov. Indiana wrote a different kind of provisionality into SB 78. The statute permits teacher-directed educational use on either school-supplied *or* school-sanctioned personal devices — until July 1, 2028. After that date, instructional use must be on school-supplied devices only. The legislature did not abolish the educational exemption; it pre-committed to narrowing the device fleet on which the exemption can be exercised, on the theory that two years is enough time for districts to fund what the personal phones are currently substituting for. The pre-commitment is more thoughtful than any of the other state laws. It is also a forecast about district budgets that no Indiana school finance officer I can locate has publicly endorsed.

Ten states, six different approaches, zero shared definitions. The same lesson — say, using an AI writing tutor that requires students to argue against the model's suggestions before accepting them — is permissible in Florida if the teacher says so, permissible in New York if the principal authorizes, permissible in South Carolina if the superintendent signs written approval and the Chromebook demonstrably cannot do it, and flatly impermissible in Virginia. Move the classroom sixty miles in the right direction and the whole legal landscape shifts.

## What the Constitution calls a blank like this

There is a doctrine in constitutional law called void-for-vagueness, and it is worth meeting because it names what is happening here — even though, strictly speaking, it probably doesn't apply.

The doctrine's clearest statement comes from *Grayned v. City of Rockford*, decided by the Supreme Court in 1972. A law is impermissibly vague, the Court said, when it "impermissibly delegates basic policy matters to policemen, judges, and juries for resolution on an ad hoc and subjective basis, with the attendant dangers of arbitrary and discriminatory application." The principle is that if a rule cannot specify what it forbids, it hands the forbidding to whoever happens to be enforcing it — and that the law should not be in the business of creating that kind of arbitrary power.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — Grayned v. City of Rockford, 408 U.S. 104 (1972) at 108–109 contains the exact quoted phrasing in Justice Marshall's opinion. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


I do not think these phone ban statutes are unconstitutional. Two reasons. The vagueness doctrine has been most rigorously applied to criminal statutes, where the consequences for the defendant are severe and where the Sixth Amendment right to fair notice has maximum force. School technology policy is administrative. The bar is lower. And the school-discipline context has, for decades, explicitly tolerated significant discretion. *Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier*, decided in 1988, approved school regulation of student expression that was "reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns" without requiring precise advance definition of what those concerns were. The Supreme Court has approved an unspecified educational-purposes standard — deliberately, in writing — for close to forty years. The phone ban statutes are heirs to that tradition.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier, 484 U.S. 260 (1988) holds that educators may control school-sponsored student expression so long as their actions are "reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns." Quote and characterization accurate. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


No successful vagueness challenge is coming. The chapter is not arguing the statutes should fall.

It is arguing something more pedagogically important. The doctrine's underlying intuition — that a rule which cannot specify what it forbids hands the forbidding to whoever happens to be enforcing it — describes exactly what these statutes have done. They have written the definition of "educational purposes" as *whatever the enforcing actor decides*. That actor is a public-school teacher. She has, in the moment the question arrives, somewhere between thirty seconds and three minutes to decide whether the use in front of her qualifies. The legal system has decided this delegation is permissible. The pedagogical system has to live with it.

## The strongest argument for leaving it blank — and what it misses

The school-board associations have an argument for why leaving the term blank is actually correct. It is the strongest defense of the current design, and it deserves a fair statement before the chapter moves past it.

The argument: any state-level definition will be wrong for most classrooms. A statute that enumerates permitted uses will, by enumeration, exclude the legitimate use that arrives the following semester. A statute that enumerates prohibited uses will fail to anticipate the next form of evasion. The classroom is too varied, the technology too fast-moving, the pedagogical contexts too divergent across thirteen thousand school districts for any legislature to capture the distinction in statutory language. Better, the argument goes, to leave the term open and let local judgment fill it in case by case. The vagueness is not a bug. It is the only design that survives contact with the diversity of classrooms the statute has to govern.

This argument is not wrong. It is the same argument I would have made if I had been drafting these statutes under the time pressure that produced them. There is almost certainly no operational definition of "educational purposes" that would survive the variety of instructional contexts it would have to cover without either excluding something legitimate or authorizing something harmful.

But declining to write the definition is not the same as solving the problem. It moves the problem. If the definition must be filled in by the teacher in the room, then the teacher in the room has to be capable of filling it in well. She has to know the difference between a lesson that uses AI to extend the cognitive reach of the student and a lesson that uses AI to replace the thinking the student was supposed to do. She has to be able to make that call quickly, consistently, and defensibly — and to make it differently for the student with a processing disorder than for the student who is looking for a shortcut.

The legislatures wrote a statute that depends entirely on professional judgment at the exact point where it stops defining the rule. They did not, in the same legislative session or in any subsequent one, fund the professional development that would allow that judgment to be exercised competently. The exemption is the place the statute hands the question to the teacher. What the teacher knows when she catches it is a question the statute does not ask.

## How the districts passed it down

If the state statute leaves the term undefined, logic says the district fills it in. Districts are closer to classrooms. They have implementation staff. They have policy infrastructure. This is where vagueness is supposed to get resolved.

What the districts have done is repeat the vagueness one level down.

LAUSD implemented its all-day phone ban in February 2025. When reporters asked the Chief of School Operations what "emergency" meant as an exception, the answer was that "common sense applies." The district published no definition of educational use. New York City's Chancellor's Regulation A-413, updated in July 2025 to implement the new state law across the nation's largest school district, requires each of the city's approximately 1,600 principals to establish a written school-based policy consistent with the regulation — and then defers to the principal on what educational purposes means. There are roughly 1,600 implementations. Houston ISD's 2025–26 policy handles the question the way Texas handles it statutorily: district Chromebooks are fine, personal devices are not. The educational-purposes question is answered by changing the domain of the rule rather than by defining its terms.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — LAUSD all-day ban took effect Feb 18, 2025; LAUSD Chief of School Operations Andrés Chait said "When all else fails, apply common sense" re emergencies (the chapter's compressed "common sense applies" is a defensible paraphrase). NYC Chancellor's Regulation A-413 was amended July 24, 2025; ~1,600 NYC public schools is the canonical figure. Houston ISD characterization plausible but unverified directly. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


The pattern is the same across every large district with published implementation guidance. The phrase passes through the system unchanged. The legislature delegates to the district; the district delegates to the principal; the principal delegates to the teacher. Statutory vagueness becomes administrative vagueness becomes classroom-by-classroom inconsistency.

This is not malice. It is what happens when a rule cannot be specified at the level it actually operates. Nobody in the chain is being negligent. The legislature couldn't define educational purposes for thirteen thousand districts. The district can't define it for six hundred schools. The principal can't define it for forty teachers. And the teacher can't define it ahead of time for thirty students, because the determination is always contextual — it depends on what the student is actually doing in her head, which is not a fact the teacher can read off the face of the device.

Someone has to make the call in the moment. The chain has been built so that someone is the teacher. The chain has not been built so that she has been trained.

## Nobody else has solved it either

No major jurisdiction has solved this. That is worth saying plainly, because the American discussion has sometimes described the undefined exemption as American sloppiness. It isn't. The same gap appears everywhere.

France passed the original school phone ban in 2018 — the first major democracy to do so. The educational exemption in the French law is, in terms of structural requirements, the most sophisticated in the world: phones may be used for "*usages pédagogiques explicites et spécifiques, encadrés par les enseignants*" — pedagogical uses that are explicit, specific, and supervised by teachers. Three modifiers where U.S. statutes manage one or none. The French legislature required that educational use be *pre-declared* and *bounded*. It still did not define what counts as pedagogical. The structural conditions are genuinely stricter than anything in American law. The substantive definition remains unwritten.

The UK Department for Education issued guidance in February 2024, sharpened in January 2026, requiring that schools be "mobile phone-free environments by default." Schools write their own exception conditions. A survey by the Children's Commissioner in April 2025 found variation substantial enough to conclude that the guidance had produced dozens of policies rather than one. Australia implemented state-by-state bans from 2020 through 2023 with the same pattern: medical and educational exemptions with "exceptional circumstances" at school discretion, no central definition.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — UK DfE published "Mobile phones in schools" guidance February 2024; "mobile phone-free environments by default" phrasing matches the guidance. Three approaches across the countries that have tried this: pre-declare and supervise the use (France), let each school define it (UK, Australia, most of the U.S.), or refuse to recognize the device as an instructional tool at all (Virginia, effectively). Nobody — anywhere — has produced a substantive, operational definition of educational use that scales across schools with different resources, different student populations, different curriculum structures, different levels of technology infrastructure. The closest is France's structural framing, which adds procedural conditions without answering the underlying question. The problem is not American. It is in the kind of question being asked.

## What the frameworks get right, and where they stop

The research community has tried to build the framework the legislatures couldn't. Its attempts are worth examining because they are the best the field has produced — and because each one fails in the same instructive way.

The American Academy of Pediatrics developed the 5 Cs of Media Use through its Center of Excellence on Social Media and Youth Mental Health. In their current formulation: Child, Content, Calm, Crowding Out, Communication. Who is this child, what are their specific vulnerabilities? What content are they consuming? Is the device being used to regulate emotion, displacing other activities, being discussed openly at home? The framework is, by some distance, the most thoughtful single attempt to operationalize the question of when media use is helpful and when it is harmful for a young person.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONFIRMED — AAP Center of Excellence on Social Media and Youth Mental Health publishes the 5 Cs (Child, Content, Calm, Crowding Out, Communication). Framework is for pediatric clinicians. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


It was designed for a pediatrician conducting a twenty-minute well-child visit with a parent who has brought a specific concern. The output is a recommendation tailored to a family. A teacher cannot run a 5-C interview with each of thirty students mid-lesson. The framework operationalizes the right question at the wrong unit of analysis.

Richard Culatta, CEO of ISTE+ASCD, has argued for years that the question should not be how long the screen is on but what it is doing — whether technology is making students "knowledge constructors and problem solvers" or presenting content for passive consumption. This is useful as a direction of travel. It is not operational at the level of the thirty-second call in Broward County. "Is this screen use producing value?" is the same question as "is this educational?" restated.

A third attempt, scattered across the work of several researchers — Lisa Guernsey, Justin Reich, Rich Halverson — centers on what they call productive friction: the idea that technology use is educational when it requires the student to exert cognitive effort, and harmful when it removes effort. This connects to a striking 2025 paper in PNAS by Bastani and colleagues, who found that students who used AI tutoring without constraint showed worse long-term learning than control students — not because the AI was inaccurate, but because it was too helpful, removing the difficulty that learning requires. Productive friction is not a slogan. It points toward a real neurological distinction between cognition that builds capacity and cognition that outsources it.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: PARTIAL — All three researchers (Guernsey, Reich, Halverson) are real and in ed-tech research. "Productive friction" exists in education literature, but the specific attribution to these three as joint originators of the term is UNVERIFIED. The concept appears more prominently in WestEd's "Friction by Design" (2025) and other recent commentary. Bastani et al. PNAS 2025 CONFIRMED (see Ch 2 factcheck). Recommend grounding the productive-friction attribution to a specific paper. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


The trouble: "Is the student exerting cognitive effort?" is a question only the teacher in the room can answer in real time, watching the student's face, knowing the student's history, understanding the assignment's design. The productive-friction framework, properly applied, points at exactly the same conclusion this chapter has been approaching since the cold open. The distinction between educational use and distraction is real. It is not arbitrary. It can be specified — at the level of the individual student, in a particular moment, by someone with relevant training and presence. No statute can capture it. No district policy can resolve it in advance. The frameworks are useful as training material for the teacher who will make the call. They are not substitutes for her.

## The blank falls hardest where training is thinnest

The blank is the right blank. Say it plainly. No legislature can write a substantive definition of educational purposes that survives contact with the variety of classrooms, devices, students, lessons, and learning objectives it would have to cover. The school-board associations are correct about this. The delegates are correct about this. Anyone who has spent a week in a real classroom is correct about this. The exemption is open-ended because any closed version would be wrong.

But the blank only works if the person it delegates to can fill it in well.

Here is where the 2025 ban wave has failed, and here is where the failure is not random.

SETDA's November 2025 national research on professional learning found that fewer than forty percent of local education agencies use Title II-A federal funds — the primary federal funding stream for teacher professional development — for technology-related training. Only nine states prioritize these funds for technology instruction. The majority of professional development funded by Title II-A takes the form of short-term workshops rather than sustained, subject-specific development. *EdWeek* put the headline plainly: billions of federal dollars spent on teacher training, and less than half going to technology professional development. In the same month, the week before the FCC announced cuts to the E-Rate program that funds school internet connectivity, the country's phone ban infrastructure sat on top of a professional development infrastructure that was, by any serious measure, inadequate to the task the bans had created.

Seventy percent of teachers who are not using AI in their classrooms, according to OECD's 2024 TALIS data, report lacking the skills or knowledge to do so. A Gallup-Walton survey in 2025 found that teachers who use AI weekly save an average of 5.9 hours per week. Teachers in under-resourced schools who received no training save nothing. The training is correlated with district wealth. The districts that can pay for sustained, subject-specific AI training are the districts whose students were already advantaged.

The EdWeek coverage of the ban wave, published in July 2025, noted that implementation was "almost all unfunded and brought on rather quickly, placing the burden of implementation on teachers." Enforcement varies by classroom. And the variance is not random. It is sharper — the inconsistency is larger, the gaps are wider — in schools where teachers have less training, less administrative support, less curriculum infrastructure to anchor a defensible call in the moment it arrives.

The void-for-vagueness doctrine warns against rules that delegate basic policy matters to front-line enforcers, because delegation risks "arbitrary and discriminatory application." The equity data show that the risk has been realized. The application of the educational-purposes exemption is not random. It is patterned by the training the enforcer has had. The training is patterned by district wealth. The students in the schools with the least training are, in the majority of cases, the students who need the most help navigating the line between a device that extends their cognitive reach and a device that replaces their thinking. They are the students who cannot compensate at home when the classroom gets it wrong. They are the students the 2025 ban wave was, in part, designed to protect.

## The question the statute handed back

Four words. *For educational purposes.* Some version of them appears in thirty-three state statutes. They are the policy. Everything else — the storage requirements, the confiscation protocols, the parental notification procedures — is logistics. The four words are where the question lives.
<!-- FACT-CHECK FLAG: CONTRADICTED — see flag on line 14. Ballotpedia's March 2026 count is 33 states with enacted statewide K-12 cellphone bans. The "thirty-five" figure appears 4+ times in this chapter and should be reconciled across all occurrences. See factchecks/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country-assertions.md -->


What the legislatures did, in writing those words and leaving them blank, was correct. Any definition they could have written would have been wrong. They handed the question to the teacher and said: *you decide*. They were right to do it. The distinction between the device-as-instrument and the device-as-drain is real and contextual and irreducibly dependent on the professional judgment of the person in the room.

What the legislatures did not do — what the school-board associations did not do, what the districts did not do, what no state in the 2025 wave did — is treat the blank as a professional development problem. The exemption is a delegation to a workforce. The workforce has not been prepared for the delegation. The legal architecture says the delegation is permissible. The pedagogical architecture says it is the central problem: a rule whose entire force depends on the trained judgment of the person enforcing it requires that the person enforcing it be trained.

The teacher in Broward County said yes to the whiteboard photograph. Two classrooms down, a different teacher said no. Both decisions flowed through the same blank. Neither teacher is wrong, exactly. Neither has been given the tools to be consistently right. The statute handed them the question. Nobody has helped them learn to answer it.

That is the condition we have created. The ban took the phone. The app sold the dashboard. The exemption handed the question back to the teacher and called it policy.

It is the right blank. We have done almost nothing to fill it.
