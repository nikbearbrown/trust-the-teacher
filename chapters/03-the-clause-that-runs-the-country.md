# Chapter 3: The Clause That Runs the Country

*Four words in every phone ban law — and why no one has finished writing them*

---

By the close of 2025, the Kaiser Family Foundation's [September policy tracker](https://www.kff.org/affordable-care-act/a-look-at-state-efforts-to-ban-cellphones-in-schools-and-implications-for-youth-mental-health/) counted thirty-one states and the District of Columbia with statutes or board policies restricting student cell-phone use in K-12 schools. By spring 2026 the [ABC News tracker](https://abcnews.go.com/US/states-school-cellphone-bans/story?id=119906999) had it at thirty-five [verify current count at publication]. Twenty-two of these laws were passed in 2025 alone, according to a [Ballotpedia legislative summary](https://news.ballotpedia.org/2025/08/08/twenty-two-states-enacted-k-12-cellphone-bans-so-far-in-2025/) published that August. The pace is unusual. The convergence is unusual. State legislatures do not normally agree on anything this fast.

What every one of these laws contains is a carve-out. Florida's HB 379 (2023), now codified at [Fla. Stat. § 1006.07(2)(o)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379) [verify exact subsection], prohibits wireless device use "during instructional time, except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes." New York's [Education Law § 2803](https://www.governor.ny.gov/eliminating-distractions-new-york-schools/new-york-state-phone-free-schools-law), signed by Governor Hochul in May 2025, authorizes exceptions "for a specific educational purpose." Ohio's [HB 250](https://education.ohio.gov/Media/Ed-Connection/May-20-2024/Governor-DeWine-signs-bill-requiring-Ohio-schools), signed May 2024, requires district policies to allow phone use for "student learning as determined by school officials."

Three states, three formulations, one shared word: *educational*. Not one of these statutes defines what educational use means. Not one tells the teacher in the room how to recognize it when she sees it. Not one acknowledges that the same device, in the same classroom, can do two opposite things depending on what the student's mind is doing while her hands are on the keyboard.

The four words at the head of this chapter are "for educational purposes." Some version of them appears in nearly every state phone ban in the United States. They are doing all of the policy work that distinguishes the device-as-distraction from the device-as-instrument. And they have been left blank.

This chapter is about that blank. About why the legislatures left it that way, about why the leaving-it-blank may not be the error it looks like, and about why — even if it is the right place for the policy to stop — leaving it there without a plan for what happens next is the failure that defines the 2025 ban wave.

---

## The phrase, dropped into the law

Lay the statutes next to each other and the variance shows up immediately.

[Florida HB 379 (2023)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379), effective July 1, 2023, prohibits student use of a wireless communications device "during instructional time, except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes." The 2025 update extends the prohibition across the entire school day for elementary and middle schools. The operative phrase carries three modifiers — *expressly*, *solely*, *for educational purposes* — and none of the three is defined further in the bill text.

California's [AB 3216](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240AB3216), the Phone-Free School Act signed by Governor Newsom on September 23, 2024, is a mandate-the-policy law: every district must develop its own rule by July 1, 2026. The statute does not use the standalone phrase "educational purposes." It enumerates four exceptions: emergency, "when a teacher or administrator of the school grants permission to a pupil to possess or use a smartphone, subject to any reasonable limitation imposed by that teacher or administrator," physician determination, and IEP requirement. Exception (2) is the one doing the educational-purposes work — and it does so by handing the entire judgment to the teacher or administrator with no criterion at all. The teacher *is* the rule.

Louisiana's [Act 313 (2024)](https://www.legis.la.gov/legis/ViewDocument.aspx?d=1380785), codified at La. R.S. 17:239, is the strictest of the early adopters. The statute has *no* educational-purposes exemption. The only exceptions in the bill text are required use under an IEP, IAP, Section 504, or Individualized Health Plan; and "an emergency," defined as "an actual or imminent threat to public health or safety which may result in loss of life, injury, or property damage." When Louisiana teachers use student devices for instruction, they are operating outside the statute's enumerated exceptions. Parish-level implementation guidance has quietly assumed some teacher-directed use is permitted. The statute does not authorize it.

Virginia took the most explicit position. Governor Youngkin's [Executive Order 33](https://www.doe.virginia.gov/about-vdoe/cell-phone-free-education-in-virginia-k-12-public-schools/cell-phone-free-education-guidance-resources) (July 9, 2024) directed VDOE to issue "Cell Phone-Free Education" guidance for K-12 public schools. The final guidance, issued December 2024, states the position in five words: "Cell phones are not an option for instructional use." Virginia is the only state where the guidance explicitly *refuses* the educational-purposes carve-out as a category. The trade-off is honest. Virginia has chosen, in writing, that the device itself is not an instructional instrument. Clearer rule. Harder enforcement when teachers in fact need devices for translation, accessibility, or AI-assisted lessons.

Texas signed [HB 1481](https://tea.texas.gov/sites/default/files/taa-2025-07-31-implementation-of-texas-house-bill-1481student-use-of-personal-communication-devices.pdf) on June 20, 2025, with districts required to adopt implementing policies by September 18. The statute's exemptions, as relayed by TEA implementation guidance [verify against enrolled bill text], cover IEP and 504 plans, physician-directed medical need, and health-or-safety compliance. Texas, like Louisiana, does not include a standalone educational-purposes exemption. The pathway through which instruction enters the classroom is structural rather than discretionary: the statute's definition of "personal communication device" *excludes* devices the district issues. Bring the Chromebook, not the phone.

Ohio's [HB 250](https://education.ohio.gov/Media/Ed-Connection/May-20-2024/Governor-DeWine-signs-bill-requiring-Ohio-schools), signed May 15, 2024, took the opposite path. It is a mandate-the-policy law that requires districts to write rules "minimizing cell phone use during school hours." Required exceptions include students who need a phone for a health concern and students for whom phone use is needed for "student learning as determined by school officials." Ohio's formulation is the most explicit *delegation* in the country. The open-endedness is written into the statute itself.

Arkansas's [Act 122 of 2025](https://dese.ade.arkansas.gov/Files/BelltoBell_LegislationSummary_Final_Legal.pdf), the "Bell to Bell, No Cell Act" signed February 20, 2025, names its required exemptions narrowly: documented health reasons, IEPs, after-school activities, emergencies. No educational-purposes carve-out. The path for instructional use is the same as in Texas: *school-issued* devices are exempt from the ban. The personal device is removed from the instructional toolkit by definition.

South Carolina, working from a [budget proviso](https://ed.sc.gov/initiatives/free-to-focus/model-policy/) and a state board policy adopted in September 2024, wrote the most operational definition in the country. The SBE policy defines "Classroom Use" as: "The use of a Personal Electronic Communication Device that is explicitly approved by the District Superintendent or his/her designee in writing, and the approval must include alignment with a standards-aligned educational objective that cannot be reasonably achieved using district-owned devices." This is, to my reading, the only state-level definition that ties the educational exemption to two operational tests — written prior approval from a named administrator, and a demonstration that the district-owned device cannot reasonably accomplish the same objective. It is the closest thing in the country to a teachable rule. It is also administratively heavy enough that one wonders how often a teacher actually invokes it.

New York's [Education Law § 2803](https://www.governor.ny.gov/eliminating-distractions-new-york-schools/new-york-state-phone-free-schools-law), signed May 9, 2025, authorizes exceptions for use "for a specific educational purpose" — authorized by a teacher, principal, district, charter school, or BOCES. The phrase "specific educational purpose" is not further defined in the statute. New York City's [Chancellor's Regulation A-413](https://pwsblobprd.schools.nyc/prd-pws/docs/default-source/default-document-library/a-413-english.pdf), updated July 24, 2025 to implement the state law for the nation's largest school district, likewise authorizes principals or designees to grant exceptions "for an educational purpose" without defining the term.

Indiana's [SB 78](https://iga.in.gov/pdf-documents/124/2026/senate/bills/SB0078/SB0078.08.ENRH.pdf), signed by Governor Braun on March 5, 2026, takes effect July 1, 2026 [verify enrolled exemption text]. It permits students to use *home-brought* laptops or tablets "at a teacher's direction" — but that provision sunsets July 1, 2028. Indiana is the only state I have found that has written a sunset clause into a teacher-directed exemption. The sunset signals legislative awareness that the policy is provisional. It offers no plan for what replaces it.

Ten state approaches. Six distinct phrasings of the exemption. Zero shared definition. The same student moving across state lines hits a different rule. The same teacher moving across district lines hits a different application of the same rule. The same lesson — Claude as a writing tutor, with the student instructed to argue against the AI's response before accepting it — is permitted in Florida if the teacher says so, permitted in New York if the principal authorizes, permitted in South Carolina if the superintendent signs off in writing and the district Chromebook cannot reasonably do it, and impermissible in Virginia.

This is the variance the chapter wants you to see. The educational exemption is the hinge between a technology ban and a technology policy. Every law turns on it. Not one defines it.

## What the legislatures wrote when they wrote nothing

A statute that does not define its operative term has not failed to write the term. It has written the term as *whatever the enforcing actor decides*. That is the move, made by legislatures across the country, in the laws above. They wrote *what* the rule is. They left *what counts as an exception to the rule* to whoever happens to be standing at the front of the room.

The doctrinal name for this in constitutional law is the void-for-vagueness problem. The classic statement, from *[Grayned v. City of Rockford](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/vagueness_doctrine)* (1972) and a long line of subsequent cases: a law is impermissibly vague when it "impermissibly delegates basic policy matters to policemen, judges, and juries for resolution on an ad hoc and subjective basis, with the attendant dangers of arbitrary and discriminatory application." Due process, on this reading, requires that ordinary people can understand what is prohibited and that enforcement is not arbitrary. A rule that does not specify what it forbids hands the forbidding to whoever happens to be enforcing it.

I do not think these phone ban statutes are constitutionally vague in the technical sense, and I want to be clear about that before the chapter leans on the doctrine. Two reasons. First, vagueness doctrine has been most rigorously applied to *criminal* statutes, where the consequences for the defendant are severe. School technology policy is civil and administrative. The bar for vagueness invalidation is lower. Second, the school-discipline context has historically tolerated significant administrator discretion. *[Tinker v. Des Moines](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/393/503/)* (1969) and its progeny establish a regime in which school authorities have substantial leeway to make in-the-moment judgments about student conduct. *[Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/484/260/)* (1988) approved school regulation of speech "reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns" without precise prior definition. The Supreme Court has, deliberately, approved an unspecified educational-purposes standard for forty years, on the explicit grounds that the alternative — judges defining education — is worse. The phone ban statutes are an inheritance of that posture.

So no successful federal vagueness challenge is likely. The chapter is not arguing the statutes are unconstitutional. It is arguing something more pedagogically useful. The doctrine names what the educational-purposes exemption *is*: a delegation. Of policy authority. From the legislature. To the front-line enforcement actor — who, in this case, is a public-school teacher with thirty kids in front of her, six minutes left in the period, and a question to answer about whether the laptop open on the desk in front of her counts as instruction or as evasion. The doctrine's underlying intuition — that a rule which cannot specify what it forbids hands the forbidding to whoever happens to be enforcing it — applies precisely here. The legal system tolerates the delegation. The pedagogical system has to live with it.

The school-board associations have an argument for why the leaving-it-blank is correct. I want to take it seriously, because it is the strongest defense of the current design. The argument runs: any state-level definition will be wrong for most classrooms. A statute that lists permitted uses will, by enumeration, exclude the legitimate use that arrives next year. A statute that lists prohibited uses will, by enumeration, fail to anticipate the next form of evasion. Better to leave the term open, the argument goes, and let local judgment — district, school, teacher — fill it in case by case. The vagueness is not a bug. It is the only design that survives contact with the variety of classrooms the statute has to cover.

The argument is not stupid. It is the same argument I would have made if I were drafting one of these laws under time pressure. There is no possible operational definition of "educational purposes" that would not break the moment a teacher tried to apply it to a case the drafters did not foresee. The legislatures, in this sense, are doing the honest thing: they are declining to write what they cannot write.

But declining to write the definition is not the same as solving the problem. It just moves the problem. If the definition has to be filled in case by case by the teacher in the room, then the teacher in the room has to be capable of filling it in. The legislatures wrote a statute that depends on professional judgment at exactly the point where it stops defining the rule. They did not, in the same legislative session — they did not, in any subsequent legislative session — fund the professional development that would let that judgment be exercised competently. The exemption is the place the statute hands the question to the teacher. What the teacher knows when she catches it is a question the statute does not ask.

The absolutist counter-argument is the other direction. Don't write an exemption. Don't let the device in. Virginia's position. Arkansas's position, more or less. Louisiana's position by silence. If the educational-purposes carve-out is the soft spot the whole policy collapses through, just close it. The clearest rule wins. I am sympathetic to this. The Virginia guidance — "cell phones are not an option for instructional use" — is the most enforceable sentence in the country on this question. It also writes off a class of instructional uses that the device is, in fact, capable of supporting: the immigrant student using the translator app to follow a science lesson, the dyslexic student using text-to-speech, the AP physics student photographing a complicated derivation off the board because copying it by hand would consume the rest of the class. Each of these has a workaround through a school-issued device. The workaround is sometimes worse than the original. Pretending the device has no instructional value is a clean rule that purchases its clarity at the cost of telling part of the truth.

The honest position, I think, is that both the leave-it-blank states and the close-the-exemption states are responding to the same constraint. The constraint is that the distinction between instructional use and distraction is irreducibly contextual. The leave-it-blank states acknowledge this by handing the call to the teacher. The close-the-exemption states acknowledge this by refusing to make the call at all. Neither approach builds the capacity to make the call well. That is the gap. That is the chapter.

## What the district guidance does not add

If the state statute leaves the term undefined, you would expect the district to fill it in. The district is closer to the classroom, has implementation responsibility, and has the staff to write the operational language. This is the layer where, in principle, the vagueness gets resolved.

In practice, what districts have done is repeat the vagueness one level down.

LAUSD implemented its all-day phone ban on February 18, 2025, in response to California AB 3216. The board policy specifies that cellphones, smartwatches, and earbuds must be turned off and stored on campus during the school day, with exceptions for IEPs, 504s, and medical accommodation. On "emergencies," LAUSD Chief of School Operations Andrés Chait told [LAist](https://laist.com/news/education/los-angeles-unified-lausd-cellphone-social-media-policy-february) that "there isn't a formal definition of 'emergency'; common sense applies." On *educational purposes*, the district publishes no definition. The earlier (pre-2025) LAUSD policy limited social media access to "educational purposes" without defining what that meant. The new policy, by mostly removing the device, sidesteps the definition rather than supplying it.

NYC's [Chancellor's Regulation A-413](https://www.schools.nyc.gov/about-us/policies/cell-phone-and-electronic-device-policy), updated July 2025, takes the delegation one level further. It requires each principal to establish a written school-based policy "consistent with this Regulation" setting forth "the circumstances under which communication devices, computing devices, and portable music and entertainment systems may be used on school property." The state law passed the educational-purposes definition to the district. The district passed it to the principal. The principal, depending on school, passes it to the teacher. There are roughly 1,600 NYC public schools. There are roughly 1,600 implementations.

Houston ISD's [2025–26 policy](https://www.houstonisd.org/cell-phone-policy), built to implement Texas HB 1481, prohibits use of personal communication devices "during the instructional day." The phrase "educational purposes" does not appear in the published district guidance. The pathway through which classroom instruction operates is structural: district-issued Chromebooks are exempt; personal devices are not. Houston has effectively followed the Texas statute's choice to define the educational question out of the prohibition by limiting the prohibition's domain.

Chicago Public Schools operates without a statewide ban (Illinois had not passed one as of mid-2026; a [Pritzker-backed bill](https://www.chalkbeat.org/chicago/2026/04/28/illinois-bill-to-ban-school-cell-phone-use-moves-forward/) was advancing in spring 2026). CPS gives principal-level discretion. That means roughly six hundred schools have roughly six hundred implementations. The CPS Acceptable Use Policy governs district devices; personal devices are governed at the school level by policies that range from full bans to free-use cafeteria-only rules. This is the country's third-largest district operating as the natural-experiment version of what "principal discretion" looks like at scale. The variance is enormous. The published district-level definition of educational use is, in effect, absent.

Miami-Dade County Public Schools operates under Florida HB 379 and its 2025 update. The 2024–25 [district parent handbook](https://api.dadeschools.net/SMAv2/1761/Handbook/District%20Parent%20Handbook%202024-2025.pdf) references the statutory prohibition and the teacher-direction exemption verbatim. The district does not publish a more specific definition. The exemption sits at exactly the level of abstraction at which the legislature left it.

Five of the country's ten largest districts. The pattern is the same in all five. The district-level implementation does not add definitional clarity to the state statute. The phrase passes through. The administrator passes the judgment down to the principal. The principal passes it to the teacher. Statutory vagueness becomes administrative vagueness becomes classroom-by-classroom inconsistency.

This is not malice. It is what happens when a rule cannot be specified at the level the rule operates. The legislature could not define educational purposes for thirteen thousand districts. The district cannot define it for six hundred schools. The principal cannot define it for forty teachers. The teacher cannot define it ahead of time for thirty students. Someone in the chain has to make the call in the moment, with the student in front of her. The chain has been built so that the someone is the teacher. The chain has not been built so that she has been trained to make the call.

## The international comparison, briefly

The U.S. is not the only jurisdiction that has tried this. The international comparison is short and the lesson is sharp.

France passed the original phone ban in 2018 — [Law No. 2018-698](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2018-10-30/france-government-adopts-law-banning-cell-phone-use-at-school/) of August 3 of that year, banning smartphones and "other electronic communication devices" from kindergartens, elementary schools, and middle schools up to about age fifteen. The exemptions are the most teacherful of any major jurisdiction: students with disabilities, emergencies, and "within the framework of explicit and specific pedagogical use, supervised by the teachers" — *dans le cadre d'usages pédagogiques explicites et spécifiques, encadrés par les enseignants*. Three modifiers — **explicit**, **specific**, **teacher-supervised**. The French formulation does what no U.S. state statute does. It requires the educational use to be *pre-declared* (explicit) and *bounded* (specific). It still does not define what counts as pedagogical. It imposes structural conditions on the invocation that no U.S. law does. The internal rules of each *établissement* — the school institution — establish the exception conditions, which means the rule has a definite locus of accountability.

The UK Department for Education issued [non-statutory guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/mobile-phones-in-schools) in February 2024, sharpened in a January 2026 update to: "All schools should be mobile phone-free environments by default; anything other than this should be by exception only." The guidance does not centrally define the exception conditions. Schools write their own. The [Children's Commissioner's April 2025 survey](https://assets.childrenscommissioner.gov.uk/wpuploads/2025/04/cco-school-survey-smartphone-policies.pdf) on school phone policies found enormous variance.

Australia is the international comparison the U.S. most closely resembles — bans implemented state by state, no single national definition. Victoria (2020), Queensland (2021), South Australia (2023), [New South Wales (2023)](https://education.nsw.gov.au/news/latest-news/mobile-phones-now-banned-in-all-nsw-public-schools). Each state writes its own implementation. The exemption pattern is medical and educational reasons with "exceptional circumstances" at school discretion. NSW's Department of Education guidance does not centrally define "educational purpose" either.

Three approaches across the international cases. *Pre-declared and supervised* (France) — the use must be explicit, specific, supervised, with structural conditions short of substantive definition. *School-defined* (UK, Australia, U.S.) — each school writes its own. *Refused* (Virginia, partially) — the device is not an instructional tool.

No major jurisdiction has produced a substantive, operational definition of educational use that scales across schools. The closest is France's structural framing. No one — anywhere — has solved the problem. This is worth saying out loud because the U.S. policy conversation has sometimes treated the gap as American sloppiness. It is not. It is the same gap everywhere. The problem is not jurisdictional. It is in the kind of question that is being asked.

## What the pedagogical frameworks try to do, and why they don't finish

The research community has tried to build the framework the legislatures couldn't. Three attempts are worth naming because they are the best the field has, and because each fails in the same instructive way.

The American Academy of Pediatrics published its [5 Cs of Media Use](https://www.aap.org/en/patient-care/media-and-children/center-of-excellence-on-social-media-and-youth-mental-health/5cs-of-media-use/) through its Center of Excellence on Social Media and Youth Mental Health. In the 2024 formulation, the 5 Cs are **Child** (who is this child, what are their individual characteristics and vulnerabilities?), **Content** (what are they consuming?), **Calm** (is media being used to regulate emotion?), **Crowding out** (what other activities is media displacing?), and **Communication** (how is the family talking about media?). [verify formulation against the latest AAP publication — an earlier "Content, Context, Connections, Co-viewing, Communication" formulation associated with Lisa Guernsey's work circulated for years and is still cited in some literature.] The framework is the most thoughtful single attempt I have seen to operationalize the question of when media use is helpful and when it is harmful for a child.

It is not a classroom tool. It was not designed as one. The 5 Cs were built for pediatricians counseling individual families during well-child visits — a structured conversation, conducted by a trained clinician, with parents who have brought a specific concern. The output is a recommendation tailored to that family. A teacher cannot, mid-lesson, conduct a 5-C interview with each of thirty students about their device use. The framework operationalizes the question at the level of the appointment, not at the level of the lesson. It is the right tool for the wrong unit of analysis.

The second attempt is the "screen value, not screen time" framing developed by [Richard Culatta](https://www.nasbe.org/the-nasbe-interview-richard-culatta/), CEO of ISTE+ASCD. Culatta has argued for years that the question should not be how long the screen is on but what it is doing — whether the technology is being used to make students "knowledge constructors and problem solvers" or whether it is presenting content the student passively consumes. The framing is useful as a *direction*. It is not operational at the level of an in-the-moment classroom call. "Is this screen-time use producing value?" is the same question as "is this use educational?" — restated.

The third attempt is the "productive friction" concept that appears across the screen-quality research literature — work by Lisa Guernsey, Justin Reich, Rich Halverson, and others. The idea is that technology use is educational when it requires the student to exert cognitive effort, and harmful when it removes effort. This connects directly to [Bastani et al.'s PNAS 2025 finding](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122) on cognitive offloading, which Chapter 7 develops in detail [verify link to enrolled Bastani publication]. The trouble: "Is the student exerting cognitive effort?" is a question only the teacher in the room can answer in real time. The framework, properly applied, recapitulates the conclusion this chapter is moving toward — that the only mechanism that operationalizes the educational/distraction distinction is a trained teacher.

Every framework points the same direction. The educational-use distinction is real. It is not arbitrary. There is a meaningful difference between an AI tutor that requires the student to defend a position before accepting the AI's response, and a YouTube recommendation feed designed to drain attention. The distinction is fine-grained enough that it can only be made in context, by someone with relevant training, in the moment. No legislature can write the rule. No district can write the rule. The frameworks are useful as *training materials* for the teacher who will make the call. They are not enforcement instruments that substitute for her.

This is the affirmative claim of the chapter. The exemption is not under-defined because legislators were lazy. It is under-defined because the distinction it tries to capture is irreducibly contextual. The legislatures wrote the exemption blank because no rule can capture professional judgment. The chapter has been moving toward this point. Here it is.

The blank is the right blank. What was supposed to happen next — the building of the professional capacity that the blank assumes — has not.

## The equity tilt

The exemption works better in schools that have invested in teacher training. The schools that have invested in teacher training are, mostly, the wealthier ones. The undefined exemption does not create a level playing field. It tilts the field in exactly the direction equity requires it not to tilt.

The training gap is well-documented. SETDA's November 2025 [national research on professional learning](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf) found that fewer than forty percent of local education agencies use Title II-A federal funds for technology-related professional learning. [verify exact phrasing of the underlying statistic — whether this is LEAs spending any Title II-A funds or primary funds on tech PD.] Only nine states prioritize these funds for technology training. The majority of Title II-A PD funding goes to short-term workshops rather than sustained development. EdWeek's [November 2025 coverage](https://www.edweek.org/technology/billions-of-federal-dollars-are-spent-on-teacher-training-less-than-half-goes-to-tech-pd/2025/11) made the headline plain: billions of federal dollars are spent on teacher training; less than half goes to tech PD.

OECD's 2024 TALIS data, cited in the book's outline, finds that roughly seventy percent of teachers who are not using AI report lacking the skills or knowledge to do so [verify TALIS 2024 published figure]. The Gallup-Walton 2025 survey on AI in teaching found that teachers using AI weekly save 5.9 hours per week — but the savings accrue to teachers who received training. Teachers in under-resourced schools who received no training save nothing.

The pattern is consistent across every dataset I can find. Training is correlated with district wealth. The districts that can pay for sustained, subject-specific AI training are also the districts whose students arrived already advantaged. The 2025 ban wave was, [EdWeek reported in July 2025](https://www.edweek.org/technology/most-students-now-face-cellphone-limits-at-school-what-happens-next/2025/07), "almost all… unfunded and brought on rather quickly, placing the burden of implementation on teachers." Enforcement varies by classroom — "If I can do it in someone's classroom, why can't I do it here?" — and the variance is not random. It is sharper in schools where teachers have less training, less administrative support, less curriculum infrastructure to anchor a defensible call.

The exemption is the entire teacher-training question disguised as a legal-drafting problem. The legislatures wrote a clause they could not define, and handed it to the teacher to define in real time. In a district that has invested in her training, the exemption works — she can tell substantive AI use from substitution because she has been shown the difference, has practiced the call, has a department head she can ask. In a district that has not, the exemption defaults to one of two failure modes. Either the teacher is too cautious and the device stays out of the lesson where it might have helped, which leaves the kid in front of her on the wrong side of the digital divide that Chapter 1 documented. Or the teacher is too permissive and the device replaces the thinking the lesson was supposed to develop, which leaves the same kid on the wrong side of the cognitive offloading problem that Chapter 7 will document. Either failure mode falls hardest on the student whose home support cannot compensate. The exemption that was supposed to enable instructional flexibility becomes, for the under-resourced school, the place where the policy fails by default.

The doctrinal point and the equity point are the same point in different vocabularies. The vagueness doctrine says: a rule that delegates basic policy matters to the front-line enforcer risks "arbitrary and discriminatory application." The equity data say: in the absence of training, that risk has been realized. The application of the educational-purposes exemption is not random. It is patterned by the training the enforcer has had. The training is patterned by district wealth. The discriminatory application the doctrine worries about is the application that is occurring.

This is not an argument against the exemption. The exemption is the right blank. It is an argument for what has to be true if the blank is going to work — that the teacher reading it has been trained to fill it in well. Right now, in most districts, she has not been.

## The chapter's turn

The four-word phrase has been doing all of the work since 2023. It will keep doing all of the work for as long as the bans are in force. Every classroom AI lesson, every translation-app accommodation, every emergency call, every accessibility carve-out — they all flow through "for educational purposes" or one of its near-cousins. The phrase is the policy.

The phrase is also, structurally, an admission. The legislatures that wrote it knew they could not specify what they were authorizing. They knew the call would have to be made by the teacher in the room. They wrote the blank because no rule can capture professional judgment. They were right to write the blank.

What they did not write — what the school-board associations did not write, what the districts did not write, what the FCC did not write the week before it cut the hotspot money — is the professional development line item that the blank presumes. The exemption is a delegation to a workforce that has not been prepared for the delegation. The legal frame says the delegation is permissible. The pedagogical frame says it is the central problem: a rule whose enforcement depends entirely on the trained judgment of the person enforcing it requires that the person enforcing it be trained.

This is the philosophical claim Movement I of the book has been building toward. The ban (Chapter 1) is the right policy that subtracts. The app (Chapter 2) is the right tool that does not, by itself, do the work. The exemption (Chapter 3) is the right blank. All three of them — the device-removal, the platform purchase, the legislative carve-out — leave the same question on the table. The question is what the trained teacher does. Movement I has named the question by examining the three places it shows up: in the policy that removes the device, in the technology that promises to replace the teacher, and in the statute that quietly hands the call back to her. Movement II takes up the question directly. What does the evidence say about the teacher as the variable that determines whether learning happens?

The phrase "for educational purposes" is the legislature handing the question to the teacher and saying *you decide*. Whether she can decide well depends entirely on whether anyone has helped her learn how. The book is about what helping her would look like. The ban took the backup. The app sold the dashboard. The exemption asked her the question. None of the three trained her to answer it.

That is what Movement II is for.

## What the research leaves open

Several pieces of this chapter rest on evidence I would like firmer before the book goes to print. The exact enrolled-bill exemption text for Texas HB 1481 and Indiana SB 78 needs direct verification against the legislative PDFs; the chapter quotes from implementation guidance and legislative summaries rather than the enrolled statutes in those two cases. The Florida HB 379 codification location — I have it at § 1006.07(2)(o), the location at original codification — should be checked for any post-2023 recodification. The state count of phone-ban jurisdictions varies between twenty-six and thirty-five depending on source and definition; the chapter's thirty-one figure is the September 2025 KFF count and should be reconfirmed at publication. The "less than forty percent of LEAs" Title II-A figure from SETDA should be cross-checked against the underlying survey methodology. None of these flags change the chapter's argument. All of them would let the chapter make the argument with sharper edges.

I also have not found a state attorney general opinion formally interpreting "educational purposes" since the 2023 wave began. If one exists — Florida, Virginia, Louisiana, and Texas are the most likely candidates — it should be in the chapter. The absence of such an opinion would itself be evidence: that the term is so unspecified that nobody has had standing to ask for an interpretation, or so consistently applied at the teacher level that no conflict has surfaced. I suspect the former. I cannot prove it yet.

## Still puzzling

If the educational-purposes blank is the right blank — if no legislature can write a substantive definition that survives contact with the variety of classrooms it has to cover — then why has no state, in the 2024–2026 wave, written its phone ban *with* a defined professional-development pipeline attached? The teacher discretion the exemption requires is a known unknown at the time the law is written. Why is no legislature treating it as a known unknown that the law should help fund the answer to?

South Carolina is the closest thing to an exception — it imposes structural conditions on the invocation (written approval, demonstration that the district device is insufficient). The structural conditions are about *bureaucratic accountability*, not professional capacity. Why is no jurisdiction writing the conditions about capacity?

Why did the school-board associations, who would have been the natural drafters of an operational definition, choose the leave-it-blank position uniformly rather than the closer-to-South-Carolina position? Is the choice a defensible reading of the legal landscape, a reading of district capacity, or political risk aversion?

What would the exemption look like if it were written by the teachers who have to apply it — not consulted, written?

## What would change my mind

A jurisdiction that paired the educational-purposes exemption with a funded, sustained, subject-specific professional-development pipeline for the teachers who have to apply it, *and* that showed, two years later, no greater variance in application across high- and low-resourced schools than would have occurred under a uniform statutory rule with no exemption at all. Either the equity tilt closes, or it does not. If it closes, the affirmative case this chapter and the rest of the book are making gets stronger. If it does not — if even with training the application still patterns by district wealth — then the constraint I have located in the exemption is in the wrong place, and the rest of the book has to be rewritten.
