# Chapter 9 — Social Cognition and Theory of Mind

Atlanta, Georgia. James Burkett, in Larry Young's laboratory at Emory, has a pair of prairie voles in adjacent chambers. They are bonded — prairie voles form lifelong pair bonds, which makes them one of the few mammals where pairwise affiliative behavior is a real, measurable, structural thing rather than an interpretive overlay.

One of the voles is removed for a brief stressor — mild restraint, a small unfamiliar disturbance — then returned to the chamber with its partner. The partner approaches. The partner grooms — not the brief grooming that prairie voles do all the time, but extended, directed grooming, focused on the returned animal, scaling in duration and intensity with how stressed the returned animal appears. The unstressed partner is *consoling* the stressed one.

Now the experimental move that makes this something more than a touching anecdote.

Burkett injects an oxytocin-receptor antagonist directly into the anterior cingulate cortex of the would-be consoler before the partner is stressed. The drug blocks oxytocin signaling at a specific cortical target, locally, before the social situation begins. The partner is again stressed and returned. The consoler does not console.

It is not that the consoler is sedated. It is not that it is incapable of affiliative contact — it grooms an unstressed partner normally. And here is the precise thing: the consoler's own physiological arousal during the partner's distress is unchanged. It is still matching the partner's emotional state. What is gone, specifically, is the directed-helping behavior — approaching the distressed partner and grooming it longer than usual in response to that distress.

The affect-matching survives. The consolation does not.

That is the whole chapter in one pharmacological dissociation. Social cognition is *not* one capacity that comes in different amounts. It is a stack of distinct operations that can come apart — neurally, behaviorally, evolutionarily — and pulling them apart is the work.

---

There are three layers, and the boundaries between them matter more than people usually notice.

**Emotional contagion** is automatic affect-alignment. The observer's emotional state shifts to match the target's without any explicit modeling of who or what is producing the shift. A chick startles when its neighbor startles, before there is time for independent threat evaluation. A human in a theater laughs when those around her laugh, often without knowing what was funny. The rat freezes when it sees another rat freeze. Contagion is ancient and widespread across vertebrates. Its adaptive logic is transparent: a flock where every individual's alarm is instantly triggered by one neighbor's alarm is harder to surprise than a flock of independent evaluators.

**Empathy**, in the comparative literature's narrower usage, is contagion plus *self-other differentiation*. The empathic animal has an affective response to the target's state — the affect is real — but represents the target as a separate self rather than simply mirroring the state internally. The behavioral signature that most cleanly distinguishes this from raw contagion is consolation: an uninvolved bystander approaching a victim of distress and offering affiliative contact — grooming, touching, soft vocalization — without any personal stake in the matter.

Consolation has been documented in great apes, canids, elephants, corvids, and the prairie vole. It is notably absent in macaques, despite robust emotional contagion in those species. Macaques respond to conspecific distress — they startle, they show physiological arousal — but they do not go to the victim afterward and groom them. The difference is not affect. It is the self-other boundary that makes *directed* helping possible. A system that mirrors a state without locating the source of that state externally cannot direct help toward an external source, because from inside the system, the distress feels like its own.

The Burkett result is what makes this taxonomy molecular rather than descriptive. Knock out oxytocin signaling in the anterior cingulate and you lose consolation while contagion remains. Two layers. Different mechanisms.

**Theory of mind** is the cognitive representation of another agent's mental states: beliefs, intentions, knowledge, perspectives. It does not require sharing the agent's affect at all. The subordinate chimpanzee, on which the next section turns, is not feeling what the dominant feels. The subordinate is modeling what the dominant *can see*, and using that model to make a strategic choice.

---

There is a standard experiment on rats that illustrates why these distinctions matter operationally — and why behavior alone can never cleanly diagnose them.

In a laboratory chamber, behind a transparent wall, another rat is receiving mild electric shocks. The observer rat can press a lever that stops the shocks. It does nothing for the observer. The observer presses the lever.

This result has been cited as evidence for rat empathy, rat emotional contagion, and rat altruism. These are three different claims, and the experiment cannot distinguish between them. The observer might be feeling what the other rat feels — affect-matching — and pressing the lever to remove its own aversive state. From inside the observer, the distress feels like its own. Self-regulation, not empathy. Or the observer might represent the other rat as a separate self in distress and act to relieve that other's distress. Directed help, empathy proper. Or the observer might simply be suppressing an aversive auditory-visual pattern — the squeaking, the visible movement — with no model of the other rat's experience at all.

Each reading has a different neural architecture and a different evolutionary story. The same lever press is consistent with all three. A behavior consistent with empathy is not the same as a behavior that *requires* empathy. The work of comparative cognition is mostly in that gap.

---

Now to the case the rat does not establish: mental-state attribution in primates.

Two chimpanzees in a research enclosure. Between them, set out by a researcher, are two pieces of food. One piece is visible to the dominant animal. The other is hidden from the dominant's view by an opaque divider — only the subordinate can see around it. The barrier opens. Both chimps run. The subordinate goes for the hidden food. Every time. When the configuration is reversed so that both pieces are visible to the dominant, the subordinate retreats entirely.

Brian Hare, Josep Call, and Michael Tomasello published this in 2000. The subordinate is not just running for the closest food. The subordinate is modeling what the dominant *knows*, and acting on the gap between the dominant's information and its own. Follow-up work showed that chimpanzees track not just what a competitor currently sees but what the competitor has recently seen, and that they track gaze direction as an indicator of informational state.

What this establishes is *perceptual perspective-taking*: the ability to represent what another agent can and cannot see from their current position, and to predict behavior on the basis of that representation. It is one genuine component of theory of mind. It is not the whole of it.

The hard test is the *false-belief* task — the case where an agent's visual access and their knowledge come apart. The Hare paradigm cannot probe this. In it, what the dominant can see and what the dominant knows are the same thing. The subordinate tracks current perception. Whether it can represent the dominant as an agent who can hold a false belief about the world is a different question, and it requires a different design.

The philosopher Daniel Dennett identified this gap in 1978, reading Premack and Woodruff's original chimpanzee work. He argued that to test for belief attribution, you need a design in which the subject's knowledge of the world *differs* from the actor's knowledge, and the test is whether the subject predicts the actor's behavior based on the actor's *false* belief rather than on the truth.

Heinz Wimmer and Josef Perner provided the design in 1983. Simon Baron-Cohen, Alan Leslie, and Uta Frith made it canonical in 1985 with the Sally-Anne task.

Sally puts a marble in a basket. Sally leaves the room. Anne moves the marble to a box. Sally returns. Where will Sally look for her marble?

Four-year-old neurotypical children answer correctly: the basket, where Sally left it and where Sally *believes* it still is. Three-year-olds answer incorrectly: the box, where the marble actually is. The three-year-old's error is not a memory failure. Ask where the marble is and the three-year-old knows. Ask where Sally will look and the three-year-old says the box — their own accurate knowledge of the marble's location overrides the inference about Sally's belief.

What changes between age three and age four is the capacity to hold a representation of a belief *as a representation* — as a mental state that exists in a mind and that can be wrong about the world.

For forty years after Sally-Anne was established, great apes failed every version of it. The consensus hardened: great apes could attribute goals and perhaps desires, but not beliefs. They were perspective-takers but not false-belief reasoners. Then in 2016, the consensus broke.

---

In a primate research center in Japan, a chimpanzee is sitting in a quiet room. An infrared eye-tracker is watching her eyes. On the screen in front of her, a video plays.

A human-costumed "King Kong" character hides in one of two haystacks. An actor watches it hide, then leaves. While the actor is gone, King Kong moves to the other haystack. The actor returns. The actor will search for King Kong. Where?

A fraction of a second before the actor begins to move, the chimpanzee's eyes shift to the location of the *first* haystack. The place King Kong used to be. The place where the actor falsely believes King Kong still is. Not the place where King Kong now sits. The place the actor *thinks* he is.

The chimpanzee is anticipating a mistake. She is predicting that the actor will go to the wrong place, based on a belief she knows to be false — and she is doing this before the actor has made any movement she could simply follow. Across chimpanzees, bonobos, and orangutans, the anticipatory gaze went to the false-belief location, significantly above chance.

It was the first time any non-human animal had passed a version of the false-belief task that forty years of behavioral testing had ruled out. The ape looked at the wrong place. The wrong place was the right answer.

The difference between 2016 and the previous four decades is not the species. It is the measure. All previous false-belief tests used explicit behavioral responses — select a photograph, press a button, search in a location. Human children show the anticipatory-looking pattern on implicit false-belief tasks at fifteen months — more than two years before they can pass the explicit version. Krupenye and colleagues applied the same implicit methodology to apes.

The critic's response was immediate. Cecilia Heyes argued that the apes could be running *submentalizing*: simple learned associations about where objects last appeared in an actor's view, with no representation of belief as a mental state. The apes had extensive experience watching objects get moved; they might have learned a statistical regularity that actors tend to look where they last saw something.

The barrier-experience control answered this.

---

Fumihiro Kano and colleagues pre-exposed different apes to barriers of the type used in the false-belief video. One group experienced an opaque barrier — they put their faces against it and learned, from their own perception, that it blocked vision. A second group experienced a transparent barrier — visually similar in surface features, but see-through.

Later, both groups watched the same video, in which an actor stood behind a barrier of the type they had experienced while events occurred on the other side. The apes' anticipatory gaze depended on their *own* past experience with that specific barrier type.

Apes with opaque-barrier experience looked to the false-belief location: the actor stood behind something opaque, so the actor did not see the move, so the actor has a false belief. Apes with transparent-barrier experience looked to the true location: the actor stood behind something see-through, so the actor *did* see the move, so the actor knows where King Kong now is.

This is the dissociation the submentalizing account cannot explain. The two groups of apes saw identical on-screen events. The only difference between them was their own first-person experience of what that type of barrier permits or blocks. The apes were projecting their own perceptual experience onto the actor — using their knowledge of what that barrier allows one to perceive to infer what the actor had been able to see.

The floor of theory of mind has moved. The apes are not doing Sally-Anne explicitly — they cannot select a photograph or verbally answer the question. They are doing something structurally equivalent to it, implicitly, in a prediction system that runs before any deliberate response is made. And the Kano result shows the mechanism: self-projection. The ape uses its own perceptual machinery as a model for the actor's.

Self-projection in a different form shows up in corvids. Nathan Emery and Nicola Clayton showed that western scrub jays that had themselves been thieves — birds that had raided other birds' caches — would re-cache their own food when they had been observed, while naive birds that had never stolen did not. The experienced thief knows what *it* does when it watches another bird cache, and projects that behavioral tendency onto the observer. It takes a thief to know what a thief will do. This is not false belief. But it requires taking one's own mental states as a model for another agent's, which is the core computation self-projection demands.

---

A computational frame makes the comparative hierarchy clear and provides the bridge to AI.

In ordinary reinforcement learning — Chapter 8 — an agent is given a reward function and learns a policy that maximizes it. In *inverse reinforcement learning* (IRL), the situation reverses. An observer watches another agent behave and infers the reward function — and the agent's world model — that explains the behavior. When you watch someone walk toward the kitchen, you do not just represent their trajectory. You infer that they are hungry and believe food is in the kitchen. From observed behavior, you back-compute a compact set of latent variables — desires, beliefs, intentions — that explains everything the person is doing and predicts what they will do next in situations you have not yet observed.

Applied to the hierarchy in this chapter, each layer is adding a richer internal model of the target agent. Goal attribution infers only the target state of the agent's policy: *he is walking toward the kitchen*. Perspective-taking adds the agent's information state: *he is walking toward the kitchen because he can see food on the counter*. False-belief reasoning adds the possibility that the agent's world model is wrong: *he is walking toward the empty kitchen because he believes there is food there, though I know there is not*.

Each level generalizes differently. Goal attribution generalizes to any context where the goal is relevant. Perspective-taking adds predictions about what the agent knows versus what it does not. False-belief reasoning handles all of the above plus cases where the agent's information and the truth have diverged.

The IRL frame is what makes the AI test sharp. A system genuinely doing IRL on agent mental states should generalize when the scenario is modified, because the model is built on the agent's information state rather than on the surface form of the story. A system that pattern-matches on narrative templates will not generalize when the surface form changes, even when the underlying information-state computation is preserved.

---

In 2022, Michal Kosinski reported that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 passed a large battery of standard false-belief tasks — Sally-Anne and variants — with accuracy approaching that of human adults. Speculation followed that large language models had developed theory of mind as an emergent property of scale.

Tomer Ullman answered this in 2023 by making small modifications to the same tasks. Replace the opaque container in the Sally-Anne story with a transparent one, so that Sally could have seen the marble move even when she was present. Add a sentence explicitly granting the actor knowledge of the object's new location. Make the agent distracted. Negate the question. Make changes that preserve the logical structure of the false-belief situation while altering the surface features of the canonical story.

GPT-4, on the modified versions, continued to predict that Sally would look in the basket — the answer correct for the canonical version — even when the modification made it clear that Sally had seen the marble move and therefore knew where it was. The model was applying the learned template: *actor-absent-during-move → actor-has-false-belief*. It applied this template even when the story's explicit content contradicted it.

Humans do not do this. A human reading the transparent-container version immediately updates the prediction: Sally saw it happen, so Sally knows. The inference generalizes because humans are running an actual model of Sally's information state — taking the story's content as input and outputting a belief-state for Sally by applying something like the IRL computation to that content.

The pattern across Ullman's five variants — pass on canonical, fail on structurally-modified perturbations — is the same diagnostic structure this book has encountered in every domain. In Chapter 6, it was Geirhos showing that vision models match texture rather than shape when the two are decoupled. In Chapter 4, it was the blocking experiment showing that learning tracks prediction error rather than mere co-occurrence. Preserve the logical structure, alter the surface features. Genuine competence generalizes; surface template-matching does not.

The diagnostic is asymmetric. Failing Ullman's perturbations diagnoses template-matching cleanly — you know what the system is doing. Passing them does not diagnose genuine IRL on mental states — a more sophisticated template-matcher operating at higher abstraction might still pass. But the asymmetry is honest: *fail this and you are doing template-matching; pass this and you are doing something better, but not necessarily IRL on mental states.* The test is sharp on one side and soft on the other, and the honest stance is to say so.

---

Let me close with the convergent evolution evidence, because the comparative claim of this book lands here as hard as anywhere.

Bottlenose dolphins in Shark Bay, Western Australia, operate a three-tier alliance system. First-tier pairs or trios of males herd single females for mating access. Second-tier teams of four to fourteen males compete against other teams, stable for decades across lifetimes. Third-tier coalitions of coalitions form for specific competitive contests. A dolphin's reproductive success depends on knowing not just who his immediate allies are but which groups those allies' groups are currently aligned with — and updating that knowledge as alliances shift. The cognitive bookkeeping is comparable, in the number of distinct relationships and their higher-order dependencies, to chimpanzee politics in de Waal's Arnhem colony.

The dolphin lineage diverged from the primate lineage approximately a hundred million years ago. Their brains are anatomically distinct — heavily convoluted cortex, but with different cytoarchitecture, different laminar organization, different columnar structure. Two lineages arrived at comparable political complexity from entirely different anatomical starting points.

At the cellular level, what converges across primates, cetaceans, and elephants is the presence of Von Economo neurons in the anterior cingulate and frontoinsular cortex — large spindle-shaped cells found in socially complex mammals and absent or rare in species without complex sociality. They appear independently in three lineages facing the same computational demand. The convergent presence is well-supported. The specific functional hypothesis — that their sparse, high-speed projections allow rapid social decisions that cannot wait for full cortical processing — is consistent with their morphology and awaits direct functional confirmation. But independent arrival at the same cellular solution in the same cortical regions, across three distantly related lineages, is exactly what you would predict if the *problem*, not the *substrate*, determines the form of the solution.

This is the book's central thesis in its most socially complex form: what the environment demands is the determining variable. The substrate is the medium. The function is the thing.

What remains genuinely contested is whether non-human animals do explicit, second-order, recursive mentalizing — and the available evidence suggests they do not, in a way humans evidently do. The apes pass the implicit false-belief task. They do not yet pass the explicit version in the way a four-year-old does. The implicit-explicit gap is large and informative. Human children pass implicit at fifteen months and explicit at four years — a developmental gap of two and a half years. Apes appear to live in something like that gap: implicit yes, explicit not yet shown.

What closes the gap in human development is still debated, but language is almost certainly involved. The explicit Sally-Anne test requires holding a representation of a belief *as a representation* — holding the marble's true location and Sally's false belief simultaneously in working memory, and suppressing your own accurate knowledge to compute Sally's. That is a recursive, second-order operation, and whether it requires language or whether language is merely its most visible expression is an open question. It is the question the chapter would pursue if it had another section.

The worm at the copper line — the chapter we began with — was making a decision. A genuine, state-dependent, experience-informed decision, in 302 neurons. Somewhere between that worm and a four-year-old child answering a question about Sally's marble, the nervous system acquired the capacity to hold a model of another mind, and then to hold a model of a model that was *wrong*, and to reason from the wrongness rather than from the truth.

The capacity is real. Its phylogenetic and mechanistic boundaries are still being drawn. And the diagnostic for whether any system — animal or artificial — has it is not whether it passes the canonical version of the test. It is whether it passes the perturbations.
