## A. Conceptual foundations

The chapter rests on four claims that must be specified before they can do any work. Three of them are descriptive (what is the distribution of harm). One is normative (what follows). The descriptive claims are well-sourced. The normative claim is where the chapter earns its title — and where the obvious move ("AI is widening gaps, so use it less in poor schools") gets refused.

### 1. What "equity" actually means in this chapter

"Equity in education" is one of those phrases that does a great deal of work without being asked to specify itself. The chapter must say what it means.

**The specification move.** Three different senses are in active circulation:

- **Equality of access.** Same device, same software, same broadband. The 1:1 Chromebook program. This is the framing the EdTech industry prefers because it is what hardware budgets buy.
- **Equality of opportunity.** Same access plus same conditions for using it productively: a teacher who knows what she is doing with the tool, a classroom climate that lets the tool teach, a curriculum that uses the tool as instrument rather than babysitter. This is the framing this book defends.
- **Equality of outcome.** Same achievement. This is the strongest claim and the one the chapter explicitly does *not* make. Equal outcomes are the consequence of many things this book cannot fix on its own (housing, food, health, intergenerational wealth, neighborhood safety). The chapter argues for the second sense, not the third.

The chapter's normative argument is that the **second** sense — equality of meaningful opportunity, conditioned on trained teachers — is the only equity claim that survives the empirical literature in Chs 1, 2, 5, 6, and 7. Access-only equity is what the phone ban claimed to deliver and what the 1:1 Chromebook claimed to deliver. Both of those claims fail in the specific way Ch 1 documents.

**Common misconception.** That "equity" means "the same intervention applied uniformly." The Tutor CoPilot finding (Ch 5) shows the opposite. The same AI tool produces larger gains where the baseline is weakest. Uniform application produces non-uniform gain — which is what the chapter is going to argue for.

### 2. What "compounds at the bottom" means

The chapter's load-bearing structural claim is that the failures of Chs 1–7 are not randomly distributed across the student population. They concentrate, with measurable severity, in the same demographic categories that already absorb most of American education's existing inequities.

**The specification move.** "Compounds" here is not metaphor. It names a specific empirical pattern: when two independent harms strike the same population, the population's total harm is the sum, not the maximum, and the population's capacity to absorb the harm is lower than the population's mean. The Chapter 1 example — phone ban removes a device that was infrastructure for low-income students, FCC simultaneously removes the school-provided substitute (E-Rate hotspots) — is the canonical case. Two decisions made by different actors for different stated reasons land on the same students. Neither actor designed the combined outcome. Nobody is accountable for it. The students absorb both.

This is the structural shape the chapter is going to recover across every other harm vector in the book.

**Common misconception.** That naming compounding is the same as proving discriminatory intent. It is not. The chapter must be precise: the policies are facially neutral. The compounding is structural — it follows from the fact that the underlying conditions (home broadband, teacher training budgets, self-regulation skills, school disciplinary climates) are themselves unequally distributed before any policy lands. The chapter should name this without converting it into a more polemical claim than the evidence supports.

### 3. The "affirmative case" — what the chapter is *for*, not against

The easy move for an equity chapter in 2026 is to argue that AI is widening gaps and therefore should be deployed less in low-income schools. This is the move the chapter explicitly refuses.

**The specification move.** The affirmative case has three parts:

1. **The pattern of harm is real.** The chapter does not minimize it.
2. **The pattern of harm is overwhelmingly traceable to AI deployed without trained teachers** — unguided AI access, exemption without definition, hardware without PD, ban without alternative. Chs 1–7 document each.
3. **Therefore the response that fits the evidence is more — not less — trained-teacher-managed AI in low-income schools.** This is the Tutor CoPilot move (Ch 5): the largest gains accrued to students of the lowest-rated tutors. The tool levels the floor. Withdrawing the tool from under-resourced schools — the "let's wait until the technology is safe" move — guarantees that the lifting-from-the-bottom effect happens only in wealthy schools.

This is the book's full thesis collapsed into one chapter: trust the teacher means trust the *low-income* teacher with the same instruments and the same training that the well-resourced suburban teacher gets. Not as token gesture. As the only deployment pattern the evidence actually supports.

**Common misconception.** That "more AI in low-income schools" means "more Chromebooks in low-income schools." It does not. It means more *trained teachers* who use AI as an instrument under their direction. The hardware-without-training pattern is precisely what produces the harm. The chapter's argument is for the inverse ratio that Ch 8 develops: more dollars on people, fewer on devices.

### 4. Why this chapter sits in Movement IV (the affirmative move), not Movement I (the diagnosis)

The book's architecture matters here. The reader has spent ten chapters watching the policy landscape mishandle a real opportunity. The equity chapter is not a fresh diagnosis — it is a re-aiming. The data has already been presented. The chapter's contribution is to show that the harms in Chs 1, 2, 6, and 7 are not random — they cluster, and they cluster on the students whose case for *more* high-quality AI-supported teaching is strongest.

If the chapter were placed in Movement I, it would read as additional indictment. Placed in Movement IV, after the AI+1 model (Ch 8) and the teacher-this-book-is-arguing-for (Ch 12), it does its actual work: closing the gap between "AI plus a trained teacher works" and "this is where the country should be investing first."

## B. The four harm vectors — what the chapter has to lay out

The opening paragraph names four specific harms. The chapter needs to substantiate each, then explicitly note that all four land on overlapping populations.

### B.1 The phone ban and racially-patterned year-1 disciplinary costs

**The Figlio & Özek finding, what it actually says.** David N. Figlio (University of Rochester) and Umut Özek (RAND), *The Impact of Cellphone Bans in Schools on Student Outcomes: Evidence from Florida*, NBER Working Paper 34388 (October 2025). The study uses student-level administrative data from one of the ten largest U.S. school districts (Florida) that moved to bell-to-bell enforcement.

**The published numbers, as reported by the NBER Digest and the working paper itself:**

- Year 1 (immediately after enforcement): a **16 percent short-term increase in suspensions for Black students**, concentrated in the first few months of enforcement and dissipating by year 2.
- Year 2: significant **gains in test scores** in math and reading across racial/ethnic subgroups; **reduced unexcused absences** explaining roughly half of the test-score gain.
- Heterogeneity: effects larger in middle and high schools than elementary; concentrated in schools where pre-ban personal smartphone use was highest. ([NBER paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34388); [NBER Digest December 2025](https://www.nber.org/digest/202512/school-cell-phone-bans-and-student-achievement); [Hechinger Report October 2025](https://hechingerreport.org/proof-points-cellphone-bans/); [K-12 Dive coverage](https://www.k12dive.com/news/cellphone-ban-Florida-school-district-discipline-learning-attendance/819875/).)

**The TIKTOC framing — what's defensible.** The TIKTOC opening characterizes the disparity as "30% for Black boys, near zero for white/Hispanic." The 16% figure for Black students overall is the NBER-digest-confirmed number. The 30%-for-Black-boys specifically is consistent with secondary coverage and with the chapter-1 essay framing, but the precise subgroup table needs to be cross-checked before the chapter cites either number with confidence. **[verify the race × sex breakdown in Figlio & Özek's heterogeneity tables before drafting; the chapter should fall back to "16% increase for Black students, concentrated in male students per the heterogeneity analysis" if the precise 30%-for-Black-boys figure does not appear in the published paper].**

**The equity-chapter framing.** What matters for Ch 11 is not the exact figure (which Ch 1 already covers) but the structural pattern: **the benefits of the ban are population-wide and appear in year 2; the costs are concentrated in Black students and appear in year 1.** This is the canonical "front-loaded cost on the population least able to absorb it" pattern. A year of elevated suspensions costs an at-risk twelfth-grader the credit recovery she needed to graduate; a year of elevated suspensions costs an upper-middle-class ninth-grader an inconvenience. Same policy, very different consequences, predictably distributed.

**The pre-existing distribution.** The harms compound because the underlying distribution of school disciplinary practice is already heavily racialized. The U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights data has shown for over a decade that Black students are suspended at roughly three times the rate of white students for comparable infractions ([2014 Dear Colleague Letter on School Discipline (PDF)](https://www2.ed.gov/about/offices/list/ocr/letters/colleague-201401-title-vi.html); [GAO 2018 report on school discipline disparities](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-18-258)). The phone ban does not create this distribution. It interacts with it. A new enforcement category, applied through the same disciplinary apparatus, produces the same disparity at higher volume.

### B.2 Home internet access — the smartphone-only population

**The structural fact.** The Pew Research Center's most recent data (survey conducted February–June 2025) finds that **about a third of adults in households earning less than $30,000 are "smartphone-dependent"** — they have a smartphone but no home broadband subscription. The comparable figure for households earning $100,000 or more is **4 percent**. Only **57 percent** of households below $30,000 subscribe to home broadband, versus **95 percent** above $100,000. ([Pew Research Center, "Internet use, smartphone ownership, digital divides in the U.S." (January 8, 2026)](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2026/01/08/internet-use-smartphone-ownership-digital-divides-in-u-s/); see also [Americans' Use of Mobile Technology and Home Broadband (January 2024)](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2024/01/31/americans-use-of-mobile-technology-and-home-broadband/).)

**The NCES corollary for children specifically.** The National Center for Education Statistics' *Condition of Education* indicator on Children's Internet Access at Home, drawing on the American Community Survey, reports that in 2021 **97 percent of 3- to 18-year-olds had home internet access**: 93% via computer, **4 percent via smartphone only**, and **3 percent with no home internet at all**. The smartphone-only and no-access shares are heavily concentrated in low-income households — among children in households below the poverty line, the smartphone-only and no-access rates are several times the national average. ([NCES Condition of Education — Children's Internet Access at Home](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cch/home-internet-access); [NCES Fast Facts — Access to the internet](https://nces.ed.gov/fastfacts/display.asp?id=46); [NCES blog on internet access by socioeconomic status during the pandemic](https://nces.ed.gov/blogs/nces/post/students-internet-access-before-and-during-the-coronavirus-pandemic-by-household-socioeconomic-status).) **[verify the precise low-income smartphone-only and no-access figures from the most recent ACS release; the chapter should cite "≥X percent of children in households under the poverty line rely on a smartphone as their only home internet, vs. ≤Y percent of children in households above 200% of poverty," with X and Y pinned to the most current indicator update].**

The K-12 Dive corollary: **22 percent of low-income households with children lack home internet entirely**, according to recent Connected Nation analysis. ([K-12 Dive: "Digital divide persists as 22% of low-income households with children lack internet"](https://www.k12dive.com/news/digital-divide-households-children-no-internet/694596/).)

**The "phone as infrastructure" specification.** For roughly one in four students from low-income households, the phone is not a device. It is *the* device — the household's only path to the internet. The 1:1 school Chromebook the district sent home does not connect to the internet without a connection, and a connection is exactly what the phone was providing. The Pew survey item the chapter can use directly: among low-income adults who decline to subscribe to home broadband, **33% cite "I have broadband access elsewhere (work/school)"** and **32% cite "my smartphone allows me to do everything I need online"** as their top two reasons.

**The FCC compound.** Already covered in detail in `01-the-ban_notes.md` §B.4: the September 30, 2025 FCC 2–1 Order on Reconsideration removed off-premises Wi-Fi hotspots and school bus Wi-Fi from E-Rate eligibility. FY2025 schools and districts had requested **$42.6 million for hotspots and $15.3 million for school bus Wi-Fi**; 1,762 libraries had applied. ([FundsForLearning: "FCC Ends E-rate Support for Hotspot & Bus Wi-Fi Services"](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/); [K-12 Dive: "FCC removes school bus Wi-Fi, hotspots from E-rate"](https://www.k12dive.com/news/fcc-removes-school-bus-wi-fi-hotspots-from-e-rate/761481/); [ALA statement](https://www.ala.org/news/2025/09/ala-disappointed-fcc-takebacks-lack-due-process-decision-end-library-hotspots-school).)

**The Common Sense Media homework-gap scale.** Common Sense Media estimates that **15 to 16 million public school students** live in households without adequate internet access or computing devices to facilitate distance learning, and **about 400,000 public school teachers** are themselves caught in the gap. The federal Emergency Connectivity Fund, which had filled some of this gap during the pandemic, **expired in June 2024**. ([Common Sense Media: Connect All Students](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/kids-action/publications/closing-the-k-12-digital-divide-in-the-age-of-distance-learning); [Common Sense Media press release on K–12 digital divide analysis](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/k-12-student-digital-divide-much-larger-than-previously-estimated-and-affects-teachers-too-new-analysis).) The homework-gap coalition's joint letter to the FCC ahead of the September 2025 vote estimated **9 to 15 million students still lack broadband at home**, disproportionately in low-income and rural communities. ([Schools, Health & Libraries Broadband Coalition, joint letter](https://www.shlb.org/blogs/shlb-ala-edlinc-homework-gap-coalition-urge-fcc-dont-cut-school-bus-wi-fi-and-hotspot-lendin).)

**The equity-chapter framing.** Three independent policy currents (state phone bans, FCC E-Rate rescission, ECF expiration) arrived in the same eighteen-month window, all touching the same population — the smartphone-dependent low-income student. None of the actors coordinated. None designed the combined outcome. The chapter should name the structural shape and refuse to overclaim intent.

### B.3 Teacher AI training is unequally distributed across district income

**The SETDA finding.** SETDA's November 2025 report — *Improving Professional Learning Systems to Better Support Today's Educators: How Title II, Part A Offers a Model for State and Local Leadership* — is the cleanest available source on how federal teacher-PD dollars actually move through the system. The relevant numbers, drawn from research with 24 state education agencies and 76 local education agencies:

- **Over 60% of Title II-A funds** were spent on professional development in 2022–23, but much of it in short-term workshops rather than sustained development.
- Only **39% of districts** reported using Title II-A funds for technology-related professional development; only **5%** listed technology as one of their two largest PD expenditures.
- Only **nine states** formally prioritized Title II-A funds for educator training on integrating technology and AI.
- Title II-A is a roughly **$2.2 billion-per-year** federal grant program. ([SETDA PDF: Improving Professional Learning Systems (November 2025)](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf); [SETDA press release](https://www.setda.org/news/press-releases/press-release-2025/setda-releases-national-guide-on-strategic-professional-learning-investments-to-close-the-digital-design-divide/); [EdWeek: "Billions of Federal Dollars Are Spent on Teacher Training. Less Than Half Goes to Tech PD"](https://www.edweek.org/technology/billions-of-federal-dollars-are-spent-on-teacher-training-less-than-half-goes-to-tech-pd/2025/11); [GovTech coverage](https://www.govtech.com/education/k-12/setda-recommends-underutilized-federal-dollars-for-ed-tech-pd).)

**The "digital design divide" — SETDA's term.** SETDA names the central gap "the digital design divide": the gap between districts that give teachers the time and support to design lessons using digital tools and those that don't. The 2025 report is explicit that this gap correlates with district resources — high-resource districts can absorb the planning time, the substitute coverage, the consultant fees, the ongoing coaching cycles. Low-resource districts buy the device and call it done. ([EdTech Digest summary: "Closing the Digital Design Divide"](https://www.edtechdigest.com/2025/11/05/closing-the-digital-design-divide-setdas-new-framework-reimagines-professional-learning-for-the-ai-era/); [Tech & Learning: "Addressing The Digital Design Divide"](https://www.techlearning.com/technology/addressing-the-digital-design-divide).)

**The RAND corollary that the SETDA report cites.** A 2025 RAND study found that **half of all U.S. districts have not provided any AI-specific professional learning** to teachers — despite teachers explicitly asking for it. The chapter can cite this in the affirmative-case section to show that the bottleneck is supply, not demand. **[verify the precise RAND citation — it appears in the SETDA report's references and in the EdWeek coverage; the chapter should pull the original RAND report URL].**

**The teacher-quality-distribution layer.** Even before AI training, the distribution of effective teachers across schools is unequal. Goldhaber, Lavery, and Theobald (*Educational Researcher*, 2015), *Uneven Playing Field? Assessing the Teacher Quality Gap Between Advantaged and Disadvantaged Students*, found that across all school districts in Washington State, **virtually every measure of teacher quality examined** — experience, licensure exam scores, value-added — was inequitably distributed against disadvantaged students. ([Goldhaber et al. 2015 in *Educational Researcher*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0013189X15592622); [ERIC abstract](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1068119); [Advocacy Institute PDF](https://www.advocacyinstitute.org/ESSA/Uneven_AEFP-1.pdf).) The follow-on Goldhaber, Quince, and Theobald (*Educational Researcher*, 2018) traced this pattern's persistence over time. ([Goldhaber, Quince, & Theobald 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/0002831217733445).)

The Clotfelter, Ladd, and Vigdor literature on teacher sorting reaches the same conclusion. *High Poverty Schools and the Distribution of Teachers and Principals* (Duke Law and Policy Scholarship, 2007) and the follow-on NBER WP 12285, *Would Higher Salaries Keep Teachers in High-Poverty Schools?*, document the systematic migration of higher-credentialed teachers toward higher-achieving schools and the difficulty of using salary differentials alone to reverse it. ([Clotfelter, Ladd & Vigdor — High Poverty Schools (Duke Scholarship)](https://scholarship.law.duke.edu/faculty_scholarship/1915/); [NBER WP 12285 PDF](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w12285/w12285.pdf); [Urban Institute PDF](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/33266/1001429-Teacher-Mobility-School-Segregation-and-Pay-Based-Policies-to-Level-the-Playing-Field.PDF).)

**The equity-chapter framing.** AI training inequity does not arrive in a flat field. It arrives on top of a teacher-distribution pattern already tilted against low-income students. New training opportunities flow disproportionately to the districts that can fund the substitute coverage and the coaching infrastructure to absorb them. The chapter can name this compounding precisely — *Goldhaber + Clotfelter + SETDA* — without overclaiming any single source.

### B.4 Cognitive offloading falls hardest on students with the weakest self-regulation

**The Bastani finding, again carefully.** Hamsa Bastani, Osbert Bastani, Alp Sungu, Haosen Ge, Özge Kabakcı, and Rei Mariman (2025), *Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning: Evidence from high school mathematics*, *PNAS* 122(26): e2422633122. ([PNAS DOI](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422633122); [PubMed 40560616](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40560616/); [SSRN preprint](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4895486).) The pre-registered RCT: ~1,000 Turkish high school math students, three arms (Control, GPT Base, GPT Tutor). The headline numbers: **GPT Base practice gain +48%; GPT Tutor practice gain +127%; GPT Base exam decrement −17%** when AI access is removed.

**The honest caveat the chapter must hold.** Bastani et al. report that they looked for heterogeneous treatment effects on pre-registered moderators (ability, resources, effort) and **found limited to no statistically significant heterogeneity** in unassisted exam performance. The TIKTOC framing — that the −17% "falls hardest on students who lack the self-regulation skills to resist it, who are disproportionately from low-income and high-stress households" — is a *theoretical extension* of Bastani's finding, not a direct empirical result reported in the Bastani paper itself. **[verify: Bastani et al.'s heterogeneity analysis must be characterized as "limited" or "null" in the chapter; the moderator analysis the TIKTOC depends on must be supplied by other literature, not by Bastani directly].**

This is a serious accuracy constraint for the chapter. The honest move:

1. **Bastani shows the average harm.** −17% on exams when access is removed, in unguarded GPT use, across a population that on average had not been pre-trained in deliberate AI hygiene.
2. **The mechanism Bastani identifies — fluent in-tool performance producing illusion-of-competence and disengagement from effortful schema-construction** (see `07-the-cognitive-offloading-problem_notes.md` §C and §D) — is precisely the mechanism the cognitive-science literature predicts will fall hardest on students with weaker baseline self-regulation. *Bastani does not prove this; the cognitive-science literature does.*
3. **The cognitive-science literature on self-regulation by SES is independent and well-established.** This is what bridges Bastani's average finding to the equity claim.

**The SES → self-regulation literature.** Two pillars of this case are robust enough to anchor the chapter:

- **Evans & Schamberg (2009), *PNAS* 106(16): 6545–6549**, *"Childhood poverty, chronic stress, and adult working memory."* Childhood poverty is inversely related to working memory in young adults, and the relationship is mediated by elevated chronic stress (allostatic load) during childhood. The longer the childhood poverty exposure, the larger the working-memory deficit. ([Evans & Schamberg 2009, PNAS DOI](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0811910106); [PubMed 19332779](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19332779/); [PMC full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2662958/).)
- **Blair & Raver, multiple papers on executive function and SES.** Chronic exposure to poverty-related stressors (neighborhood violence, family chaos, racial discrimination, noise, pollution) alters the neurobiological systems supporting executive function. The plasticity of these systems also means that targeted interventions early in development can close meaningful portions of the gap. ([Blair & Raver, *New Directions for Child and Adolescent Development*, 2016, "Poverty, Stress, and Brain Development"](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1876285916000267); see also [Blair & Raver, *Closing the Achievement Gap through Modification of Neurocognitive and Neuroendocrine Function: Cluster RCT*](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Closing-the-Achievement-Gap-through-Modification-of-Blair-Raver/1aa7995d8a235afada77df1560aafe6c3a694cf2); UC Davis Center for Poverty Research [summary of the Blair-Raver Tools-of-the-Mind RCT](https://poverty.ucdavis.edu/post/boosting-executive-function-may-help-close-income-based-achievement-gap).)
- **Corroborating reviews.** "Executive function, self-regulation skills, behaviors, and socioeconomic status in early childhood" (PLOS ONE, 2022) confirms the well-established SES → executive-function gradient and locates it in the chronic-stress mechanism. ([PLOS ONE 2022](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0277013); [PMC version](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9629624/).)

**The cognitive-offloading-development literature is thinner.** The most recent review — Armitage (2025), *Child Development Perspectives*, "The nature and development of cognitive offloading in children" — observes that children as young as 4 can engage in effective offloading strategies, but that young children's strategies show bias, lack of selectivity, and lack of self-initiation. The review also explicitly notes that almost all developmental cognitive-offloading research has surveyed WEIRD samples, leaving us with limited evidence about how socioeconomic stress mediates the development of healthy offloading habits. ([Armitage 2025](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cdep.12532).) **[verify: the chapter should note that the cognitive-offloading-by-SES literature is preliminary; the inference goes through the better-established self-regulation-by-SES literature rather than through a direct offloading-by-SES study].**

**The synthesis the chapter can argue.** Bastani shows the average decrement when AI is deployed without scaffolds. Cognitive science shows that the self-regulation skills required to resist the AI's fluency-and-shortcut affordances are precisely the skills most disrupted by chronic poverty-related stress. The chain of inference is sound; the chapter must label it clearly as an inference rather than a direct empirical finding. The conclusion the chapter draws — that unguided AI access concentrates harm in exactly the students least equipped to navigate it — is supported but should be presented as the most reasonable reading of a chain of independent literatures, not as a single replicated experimental result.

## C. The affirmative case — what the evidence actually argues *for*

This is the chapter's spine. Three independent literatures converge on a single empirical pattern: **AI tools paired with trained operators produce their largest gains at the bottom of the operator skill distribution.** Each literature gets one paragraph in the chapter, and the chapter argues that the convergence is what makes the affirmative case defensible.

### C.1 The Tutor CoPilot finding — the in-domain proof

Already laid out at length in `05-the-professional-tool_notes.md` §5 and `02-the-app_notes.md`. The short version for Ch 11:

Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, and Demszky (2024), *Tutor CoPilot: A Human-AI Approach for Scaling Real-Time Expertise* (arXiv 2410.03017; Stanford EdWorkingPapers AI-24-1054). Cluster RCT, 900 tutors, 1,800 students, FEV Tutor platform. Headline: **+4 percentage points overall mastery (p < 0.01); +9 percentage points for students paired with lower-rated tutors.** Analysis of 350,000+ messages shows the tool increased the use of probing questions and reduced generic praise. The tool was invisible to the student; the tutor remained the decision-making professional. Cost: ~$20 per tutor per year. ([Wang et al. 2024 arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.03017).)

**The equity-chapter reading.** Lower-rated tutors disproportionately serve low-income students — they are the less-experienced, less-credentialed, lower-paid practitioners that under-resourced tutoring programs can afford. The Tutor CoPilot finding is the cleanest available demonstration of the leveling-the-floor pattern in K-12 academic context. The chapter does not need to invent the equity claim — it is encoded in the heterogeneity result the paper itself reports.

The mechanism is also visible: the AI did not replace the tutor. It augmented the tutor's *professional practice* in real time. The student's experience was of a better tutor, not of an AI. This is the AI+1 model in microcosm — and it is the affirmative case for what should happen in low-income classrooms, scaled.

### C.2 The Brynjolfsson finding — the professional-services analogue

Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023), *Generative AI at Work*, NBER WP 31161; published in *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 2025. ~5,200 customer-support agents. Generative AI assistant deployed in real-time call workflow. The headline: average productivity gain **14%; for the bottom quartile of pre-AI performance, gain ~34%; for the top quartile, ~minimal**. ([Brynjolfsson et al. NBER WP 31161](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [QJE 2025 published version](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/140/2/889/8011591) — *[verify exact pagination at draft time]*.) Laid out at length in `05-the-professional-tool_notes.md` §4.

**The equity-chapter reading.** The same pattern in a totally different professional context. The same shape — AI levels the floor by lifting the bottom, not by raising the top. The chapter wants to argue: this is not coincidence. It is what high-quality AI tools do when paired with trained professionals. The least-experienced practitioner gets the largest gain because the AI is doing pattern-recognition work that experienced practitioners had already internalized — and that is exactly what makes the tool an equity instrument in the right hands.

### C.3 The precision-agriculture analogue — the catch-up effect at scale

Lan and Ban (2025), *The Farm-Level Economic and Environmental Benefits of Precision Agriculture Technology Adoption: A Meta-Analysis of Global Evidence*, *Sustainability*. 85 empirical studies, 1,472 independent farm observations. Pooled finding: precision agriculture adoption raised average ROI by **22.3%**, net profit by **18.5%**, nitrogen-use efficiency by **15.1%**. Crucially: **the gains were concentrated in farms operating well below the efficiency frontier**, with already-efficient large-U.S.-farm gains near zero. The "Kansas Farm Efficiency Study" — frequently cited in TIKTOC summaries of this literature — names the same catch-up pattern in a Midwestern subsample. ([Lan & Ban 2025, *Sustainability*](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/17/17/8013) — *[verify exact DOI at draft time]*.) Laid out at length in `05-the-professional-tool_notes.md` §3.

**The equity-chapter reading.** A third independent literature, a fourth profession, the same shape: the trained low-baseline operator gains the most from the tool. The chapter is not arguing by analogy from agriculture to schools; it is arguing that we are looking at a general empirical pattern in how professionals absorb new instruments. The pattern is not "AI is good for the strong"; it is "AI is good for the trained, especially when the trained are starting from a weaker baseline."

### C.4 The AI dividend that does not show up where there is no training

From `06-the-ai-dividend_notes.md`: the most-cited estimate of the teacher AI time-savings effect is the Gallup/Walton "Walton Family Foundation–Gallup 2024–25 educators survey," which finds **teachers using AI weekly save about six hours a week** of administrative and prep work. ([Gallup/WFF 2024–25 report](https://www.gallup.com/analytics/645445/walton-aieducation-report.aspx); [Walton Family Foundation 2025 follow-up](https://www.waltonfamilyfoundation.org/learning/teachers-and-ai-2025) — *[verify exact URL at draft time]*.) The dividend exists where teachers received the training to use the tools fluently. In districts where no training was provided, the dividend is near zero — teachers who do not know how to use AI well do not, on average, use it well, and the time-savings effect that the trained teacher gets is not available to them.

**The equity-chapter reading.** The dividend itself is regressive when training is unequally distributed. The teacher in the well-resourced suburban district gets six hours a week back; the teacher in the under-resourced urban district who received no AI training gets nothing back. The chapter should name this directly: *the same federal Title II-A dollar that produces a six-hour dividend in the trained district produces no dividend in the untrained district. The two districts are not playing the same game.*

### C.5 The convergence

The chapter's load-bearing synthesis: three independent literatures (tutoring, customer support, agriculture) plus one in-domain corollary (the teacher AI dividend) all show the same shape — the gains are largest where the baseline is weakest, *conditional on training*. This is the empirical claim the affirmative case rests on. Without training, the same tool produces flat or negative effects (Bastani for students; the no-training condition in farm and customer-support studies for workers). With training, the bottom of the distribution catches up.

The policy implication: in low-income schools, the case for AI deployment is not weaker than in wealthy schools. It is stronger — *if and only if* the deployment includes the training. The intervention that fits the evidence is to invest disproportionately in PD for teachers in under-resourced districts, on the explicit expectation that the catch-up gains will be largest there.

## D. The critical literature — what the chapter has to engage seriously

This is the most important methodological section of the chapter. The book's commitment to engaging counter-evidence requires that the chapter not duck the critics. The honest counter-position is real and is gathering force; the chapter must present it fairly and then say what survives the engagement.

### D.1 The "AI will widen gaps" critical literature

**Brookings — Darrell West and colleagues.** The Brookings articles in this series — *AI and the next digital divide in education*, *AI is coming to schools, and if we're not careful, so will its biases*, *How to avoid past edtech pitfalls* — synthesize the case that AI deployment without active equity guardrails will recapitulate every past EdTech failure: hardware-without-training, dashboards-instead-of-pedagogy, predictive analytics that calcify low expectations. The Brookings Global Task Force on AI in Education (2025 final report) explicitly worries that "a new digital divide could emerge where the rich have access to technology, increasingly powered by artificial intelligence, and to teachers to help them use this technology as part of their learning, while poor kids just have access to the technology." ([Brookings: "AI and the next digital divide in education"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/ai-and-the-next-digital-divide-in-education/); [Brookings: "AI is coming to schools..."](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/ai-is-coming-to-schools-and-if-were-not-careful-so-will-its-biases/); [Brookings Global Task Force on AI in Education](https://www.brookings.edu/projects/brookings-global-task-force-on-ai-in-education/).)

**U.S. Commission on Civil Rights, November 2024 report on AI in K–12 education.** The report's headline conclusion: AI in K–12 has the potential to "reinforce patterns of discrimination and disparate impact faster, more efficiently, and in a way that is less transparent than previously seen." ([USCCR 2024 report PDF](https://www.usccr.gov/files/2024-12/2024-ai-in-education.pdf).)

**Stanford Center for Racial Justice.** *How will AI Impact Racial Disparities in Education?* (June 2024). The Stanford Law School analysis specifically names the predictive-analytics risk: models trained to minimize prediction errors overall may inadvertently over-identify African American and Hispanic students as "at-risk," reproducing historical biases embedded in disciplinary and attendance data. ([Stanford CRJ June 2024](https://law.stanford.edu/2024/06/29/how-will-ai-impact-racial-disparities-in-education/).)

**The algorithmic-bias-in-education literature.** Safiya Noble's *Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism* (2018), Ruha Benjamin's *Race After Technology* (2019, "the New Jim Code"), and Cathy O'Neil's *Weapons of Math Destruction* (2016) provide the conceptual scaffolding. The newer education-specific critique: *FairAIED: Navigating Fairness, Bias, and Ethics in Educational AI Applications* (arXiv 2407.18745, 2024) and *Algorithmic bias in educational systems* (*World Journal of Advanced Research and Reviews*, 2025) document specific risks in adaptive-learning and early-warning systems. ([Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression*, NYU Press 2018](https://nyupress.org/9781479837243/algorithms-of-oppression/); [Benjamin, *Race After Technology*, Polity 2019](https://politybooks.com/bookdetail/?isbn=9781509526406); [O'Neil, *Weapons of Math Destruction*, Crown 2016](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/241363/weapons-of-math-destruction-by-cathy-oneil/); [FairAIED arXiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.18745v2).)

**What this literature gets right.** It is not making things up. The mechanisms are real: training data encoded with historical bias produces biased predictions; adaptive systems trained on disengagement signals can route low-engagement students to easier content rather than to better instruction; "personalization" at scale without pedagogical scrutiny becomes another way to track students into low expectations.

**What the chapter argues in response.** Not that this literature is wrong. That it identifies *exactly* the failure mode of AI-without-trained-teachers and therefore is the strongest argument *for* the AI+1 deployment pattern the book defends. The bias-in-models story is what happens when the model is the decision-maker. The AI+1 model puts the trained teacher between the model and the student — explicitly to absorb the bias detection, the suitability judgment, the moment-to-moment pedagogical calibration that the model cannot do. The critical literature is correctly diagnosing the harm of deployment-by-algorithm. It is not, on its own, an argument against deployment-by-trained-teacher. The chapter's contribution is to refuse the false dichotomy: the alternative to algorithmic harm is not no-AI; it is teacher-managed AI with the training that lets the teacher catch what the model gets wrong.

### D.2 The "AI gap is already showing up in PISA" question

The OECD's PISA 2022 data, reanalyzed in OECD Education Working Papers and synthesized in OECD's 2024 *Students, Digital Devices and Success* report, finds:

- Socioeconomically advantaged students spend more time using digital resources in most contexts.
- Schools' capacity to use digital devices effectively is significantly higher in socio-economically advantaged schools.
- Adequate availability and high-quality use of digital media (especially inquiry-based learning) can **mitigate** the SES-digital-competence gradient.
- A significant majority of students from socioeconomically disadvantaged or rural backgrounds remain digitally excluded. ([OECD: Digital divide in education](https://www.oecd.org/en/topics/digital-divide-in-education.html); [OECD: Students, Digital Devices and Success (May 2024)](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/05/students-digital-devices-and-success_621829ff/9e4c0624-en.pdf); [OECD Working Paper 340: Using digital resources for learning (December 2025)](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2025/12/using-digital-resources-for-learning_4594bfc4/eb9453f3-en.pdf); [Teacher Magazine: "Equity lessons for the AI era from PISA 2022"](https://www.teachermagazine.com/au_en/articles/equity-lessons-for-the-ai-era-from-pisa-2022); [meta-analysis from PISA 2022 in *Computers & Education*, 2025](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0360131525001770).)

PISA's note on what comes next: **PISA 2025 will include "Learning in the Digital World"** as an innovative domain, explicitly measuring students' capacity to engage in self-regulated learning with digital tools. PISA 2029 will include "Media and Artificial Intelligence Literacy" as an innovative domain. ([OECD PISA programme](https://www.oecd.org/en/about/programmes/pisa.html).)

**The equity-chapter reading.** PISA 2022 cannot yet tell us whether generative AI specifically is widening or narrowing gaps in international comparison — the data predates the post-November-2022 GPT-era. PISA 2025 will be the first round to attempt a direct measurement, and the results will not be available until late 2026 or 2027. The chapter should report the OECD's existing findings (digital-competence gradient with SES, schools' capacity gap, mitigation potential of high-quality use) and explicitly forecast that PISA 2025 will be the first major test of the chapter's prediction. **[verify the PISA 2025 release date once the OECD's release calendar is updated].**

### D.3 The Alpha School / 2-Hour Learning / Unbound Academy debate

Alpha School (the founding Texas private school) and its associated 2-Hour Learning AI platform, plus the Unbound Academy charter expansion (Arizona, fall 2025), are the loudest 2025 instantiation of the AI-as-teacher-replacement model. The marketing claim: students master core academics in two hours using AI tutoring and spend the rest of the day on "passion projects," reportedly with 5:1 staff-to-student ratios and average performance "in the top 1–2 percent nationwide." ([Alpha School](https://alpha.school/); [Alpha School Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_School); [Hunt Institute: AI Tutoring in Schools (June 2025)](https://hunt-institute.org/resources/2025/06/ai-tutoring-alpha-school-personalized-learning-technology-k-12-education/); [Axios on the model's scaling](https://www.axios.com/local/charlotte/2025/08/27/alpha-schools-ai-artifical-intelligence-education).)

**The critiques the chapter should engage.**

- **Dan Meyer (former Desmos chief academic officer; one of the most thoughtful EdTech critics in the field).** *"The Truth About 2 Hour Learning, Unbound Academy, and Alpha School a/k/a The School Replacing Teachers with AI"* — Substack post, 2025. Meyer's reading: the marketing claim conflates the AI tool with the elite human infrastructure (5:1 guides, motivated families, $40K+ private school tuition, heavily selected student body). The replicable component is the small bundle. The marketing claim is that the replicable component is the AI. ([Dan Meyer Substack](https://danmeyer.substack.com/p/the-truth-about-2-hour-learning-and).)
- **Scott Alexander review, *Astral Codex Ten*.** A detailed quantitative review of Alpha's published MAP scores. ([ACX review of Alpha School](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/your-review-alpha-school).) The Alexander review is critical but not dismissive — it argues that the data supports *some* gain over baseline, but nothing like the marketing numbers, and that the gains are confounded with selection effects severe enough to make the underlying claim unverifiable.
- **Chalkdust & Silicon, *"Why Alpha Schools' AI Hype Doesn't Equal Equity"* (Medium, 2025).** The most direct equity critique. The argument: "If the AI isn't the engine, and the magic is in the motivation delivered by elite staff, then this isn't a scalable solution. It's a curated experience." ([Chalkdust & Silicon on Medium](https://medium.com/chalkdust-silicon/innovation-without-critique-isnt-reform-it-s-marketing-c6f3bcf5062f).)
- **State charter board rejections.** Pennsylvania, Utah, Arkansas, North Carolina, and Texas all rejected Unbound Academy's charter applications, in most cases citing the absence of measurable academic goals tied to state assessments and the lack of independent research on the instructional model. Arizona approved on a 4–3 vote with reduced enrollment caps. ([Chalkbeat on Pennsylvania rejection](https://www.chalkbeat.org/philadelphia/2025/01/24/ai-cyber-charter-school-pennsylvania-promises-2-hour-learning/); [Teacher Misery on the four-state rejection](https://teachermisery.com/unbound-academy-arizonas-ai-powered-virtual-academy-and-the-rejection-by-four-states).)

**The equity-chapter reading.** Alpha is the cleanest available case study of the model the chapter argues *against*: AI presented as teacher-replacement rather than as teacher-instrument. The Alpha critique reinforces, rather than undermines, the chapter's thesis. The mistake Alpha makes is the inverse of the mistake the chapter is correcting in low-income public schools: Alpha claims the *AI* is doing the work when actually the *trained guides plus selected students plus parental investment* are doing it; under-resourced public schools claim the *teacher* will do the work without giving her the AI training the work requires. Both errors are about misattributing where the lift comes from. In both cases, the answer is the trained teacher + AI bundle, not either alone.

The chapter should be careful not to over-attack Alpha — there is real signal in their results that personalization and pacing matter, and the model is genuinely interesting. The argument is narrower: *the version of this model that scales to under-resourced public schools is not "AI tutor without teacher." It is "trained teacher with AI as instrument." Alpha is selling the first; the evidence supports the second.*

### D.4 Selwyn and the EdTech-skepticism school

Neil Selwyn's *Should Robots Replace Teachers?* (2019) and *Education in a Digital World* (multiple editions) are the most sustained academic argument against the EdTech industry's framing of education. Selwyn's position, in compressed form: even when EdTech "works" by measured metrics, it shifts what counts as "learning" toward what can be measured and away from the broader pedagogical, social, and civic functions schools are supposed to serve.

**The equity-chapter reading.** Selwyn would argue that even the AI+1 model the book defends is committed to a measurement frame that may foreclose the larger pedagogical questions. The chapter should grant this — the AI+1 model is *a* model; it is not a complete theory of what schooling is for. But within the frame the chapter is operating in (test-score gains, retained learning, classroom climate), the AI+1 model survives the equity test, and the alternatives the chapter is being asked to consider (no AI; AI-as-tutor-without-teacher; AI mandated by district fiat without teacher PD) do not.

This is the chapter's deepest concession to the critical literature: the AI+1 frame is contingent. It is the best deployment pattern the current evidence supports for the goals current measurement systems track. The book is not claiming it is the final answer to "what is school for." The chapter should be willing to say this rather than pretending Selwyn's challenge can be answered inside the chapter's frame.

## E. Teaching considerations

### E.1 Who is the reader for this chapter?

Same as the book's primary audience: K-12 principals, district leaders, instructional coaches, education policymakers. But the equity chapter has a sharper audience-within-an-audience: the district leader who has just had her budget cut and is being asked to choose between a Yondr contract for $500K, a 1:1 Chromebook refresh for $1.2M, and a teacher PD investment for $300K. The chapter wants to be useful for that decision.

The reader is also, by 2026, exhausted with equity rhetoric. She has heard the gap-closing claim for every program she has ever bought. The chapter must be specific enough about the evidence to be useful, and honest enough about what the evidence does and does not show that it earns trust.

### E.2 Failure modes the chapter must anticipate

**Failure mode 1: "We can't afford AI training in our district."** This is the most common pushback the chapter will get. The chapter should anticipate it with the Title II-A math: the district probably already has Title II-A funds it is using for short workshops; the SETDA report explicitly identifies how to redirect those funds toward sustained AI PD. The barrier is not funding. It is allocation. ([SETDA November 2025 report](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf).)

**Failure mode 2: "AI is biased, so we should not deploy it to vulnerable students."** This is the Stanford/Brookings/USCCR-aligned reading. The chapter should engage it with the AI+1 framing: yes, AI-as-decision-maker is dangerous; the chapter is not arguing for AI-as-decision-maker. The chapter is arguing for AI-as-trained-teacher's-instrument, in which the bias-catching, the misuse-detection, and the suitability judgment are the teacher's professional responsibility, supported by training that includes explicit instruction in algorithmic bias and its mitigation. This is what the bias literature should be used for — to inform the training curriculum, not to refuse the deployment.

**Failure mode 3: "The Tutor CoPilot study is one study; you can't generalize."** True for any one study. The chapter's contribution is the convergence: Tutor CoPilot in tutoring; Brynjolfsson in customer service; Lan & Ban in agriculture; the AI-dividend literature in teaching. Four independent literatures, four different settings, four different professions. The same shape — gains largest where the baseline is weakest, conditional on training. The convergence is what survives the single-study critique.

**Failure mode 4: Reading the affirmative case as "AI vendors should sell more to poor districts."** This is the misread the chapter must most strenuously refuse. The argument is not for more EdTech-vendor spending in low-income districts. It is for **more PD spending on teachers in low-income districts**, with AI tools as the *occasion* for the PD investment rather than the destination. The hardware-only ratio — which is what most of the EdTech industry is selling — produces the harm the chapter is documenting. The chapter must be vigilant that its argument is not laundered into another rationalization for the hardware spend.

### E.3 Analogies that earn their place

**"The double subtraction."** Already developed in `01-the-ban_notes.md` §E and used implicitly throughout this chapter. Two independent policies, neither coordinated, both falling on the same population — the structural shape of harm compounding.

**"The catch-up effect."** This is the chapter's central affirmative analogy. From `05-the-professional-tool_notes.md`: precision agriculture's largest gains in below-frontier farms; Brynjolfsson's largest gains in bottom-quartile agents; Tutor CoPilot's largest gains in lower-rated tutors. The teacher analogue: the trained teacher in the under-resourced school is, on average, the operator at the lower end of the distribution. The catch-up effect predicts her gains from AI+training to be the largest in the system. This is the affirmative case in one phrase.

**"Leveling the floor, not flattening the ceiling."** From the Brynjolfsson and Tutor CoPilot studies. The data does not show the top-quartile practitioner getting worse. It shows the bottom-quartile getting much better. The chapter wants this distinction clearly: the equity case is not zero-sum. The wealthy district's trained teachers do not lose anything by the low-income district's trained teachers gaining. Both rise; the bottom rises more. This is what the term "equity" can defensibly mean when it is supported by data.

### E.4 Analogies to avoid

- **"AI is the great equalizer."** Both because it overstates the evidence (AI without training is the great regressor) and because it converts the chapter's nuanced claim into vendor marketing copy. Cut on sight.
- **"AI is like a calculator for math."** A common move. The chapter can use it sparingly to make the cognitive-offloading point (calculators eventually became standard once the curriculum integrated them), but the analogy breaks down because a calculator is a procedural tool with a closed answer space, and generative AI is an open-ended fluency engine. The break-down matters; if the chapter uses the analogy, it must name the break-down.
- **"The phone ban hurt low-income students."** Too sweeping. The chapter's claim is that the phone ban without conditions removed infrastructure from low-income students. The ban as a policy is right (the chapter must not contradict Ch 1). The harm is in the without-conditions clause.

### E.5 Exercises the chapter could end with

The book's Movement IV chapters end with concrete "what would you do tomorrow morning" moves. Candidates for Ch 11:

1. **Run the catch-up math for your district.** Estimate current teacher AI PD spending per teacher per year. Compare to the Title II-A maximum the district could reallocate. Identify the three highest-need schools. Calculate the cost of saturating those schools with AI PD at the WestEd/SETDA-recommended unit cost. Compare to the district's hardware/pouch contract spend.
2. **Audit the AI dividend distribution in your district.** Of teachers reporting they save time using AI, what is the demographic breakdown of the schools they teach in? If the dividend is concentrated in the wealthier schools, what would it take to close the gap?
3. **Pull the Tutor CoPilot replication question.** Identify a tutoring or coaching program in your district. Ask what an AI+1 layer would cost. Ask what a small comparison RCT would cost. Both are within the range of districts to fund out of existing budgets if they choose.
4. **Engage the FCC question directly.** Pull the home-broadband data for your district. Identify the families that lost connectivity in the FCC's E-Rate rescission. Decide whether the district will provide an alternative. If yes, at what cost; if no, what the equity audit will show.

## G. Gaps and flags

### Things to verify before draft goes to Nik

1. **The Figlio & Özek race × sex breakdown.** The chapter inherits this verification from Ch 1. If the published paper does not specifically report "Black boys 30% / white-Hispanic near zero," the chapter must use "16% short-term increase for Black students per the NBER Digest, concentrated in male students per the heterogeneity analysis." **[verify]**
2. **The NCES smartphone-only-by-poverty figure.** The Pew "one third of households below $30K are smartphone-dependent" figure is solid. The NCES child-specific smartphone-only-by-poverty figure should be pulled from the most recent ACS release of the *Children's Internet Access at Home* indicator and cited with the exact year. **[verify]**
3. **The SETDA "9 states / 39% / 5%" specific figures.** Pulled from the SETDA November 2025 report and EdWeek coverage; the chapter should triple-check the SETDA report's executive summary tables before citing. **[verify the report's executive summary for the specific n = 76 LEAs + 24 SEAs research basis].**
4. **The RAND "half of districts have no AI PD" finding.** Quoted in SETDA and EdWeek; the chapter should pull the original RAND citation rather than citing a secondary source. **[verify the RAND report title and URL].**
5. **The Bastani heterogeneity analysis.** Critical. The TIKTOC implies that Bastani's −17% "falls hardest on students who lack self-regulation skills," but Bastani's published heterogeneity analysis finds limited statistically significant moderation. The chapter must characterize this honestly: Bastani shows the average; the inference to "hardest on low-self-regulation students" goes through the Evans/Schamberg/Blair-Raver literature, not through Bastani directly. **[verify the precise language Bastani uses about heterogeneity in the published paper before drafting].**
6. **The Lan & Ban "Kansas Farm Efficiency" framing.** The TIKTOC's "Kansas Farm Efficiency Study" phrasing may be a working-paper-era characterization; the published Lan & Ban (2025) is the global meta-analysis. The chapter should cite Lan & Ban and the catch-up framing without committing to the specific "Kansas Farm Efficiency" name unless it can be verified. **[verify the Kansas-specific subsample analysis in Lan & Ban's appendix, or cite the broader catch-up finding without the geographic specificity].**
7. **The Title II-A annual dollar figure.** $2.2 billion is the SETDA / EdWeek-cited figure; the precise FY appropriation should be confirmed from the U.S. Department of Education's most recent annual table. **[verify the FY2025 or FY2026 figure once published].**
8. **The FCC dissent on the September 30 vote.** The 2–1 vote and the legal rationale (Section 254 statutory authority) are documented; the dissenting commissioner (presumably Anna Gomez) and the specific dissent rationale should be sourced cleanly. **[verify the dissent author and the substance of the dissent].**

### Things that are contested and the chapter must say so

1. **Whether AI deployment is widening or narrowing K-12 achievement gaps overall.** The empirical literature is in motion. PISA 2025 will be the first major test. The chapter must be honest that the prediction it makes — that AI + training narrows gaps — is a prediction the next two PISA rounds will test, not a settled finding.
2. **Whether the Tutor CoPilot result will replicate at scale.** Wang et al. is one large study, in one platform (FEV Tutor), with one design. Replication in different platforms and demographics is the obvious next step. The chapter should be explicit about this.
3. **Whether the bias-in-AI risk can be sufficiently mitigated by trained-teacher oversight.** This is the chapter's deepest empirical bet. The critical literature (Noble, Benjamin, O'Neil, FairAIED) names real harms; the AI+1 model proposes that trained-teacher oversight is the mitigation; the actual evidence that trained teachers can reliably catch the bias the model would otherwise enact is preliminary. The chapter should be willing to flag this as the most important research question the AI+1 model raises but cannot yet definitively answer.
4. **Whether the catch-up effect in agriculture / customer support / tutoring generalizes to K-12 classrooms with the specific demographic stresses of low-income urban and rural schools.** The pattern is strong across three independent literatures, but classroom education is not customer support or farming. The chapter's confidence in the affirmative case is bounded by this generalization step.

### Things that may need domain expertise Nik does not directly have

1. **The Bastani heterogeneity analysis.** Requires careful reading of the published paper's tables. Pull, don't summarize from memory.
2. **The cognitive-offloading-by-SES literature.** Thinner than the self-regulation-by-SES literature. The chapter's inferential chain should be exposed clearly so a developmental psychologist reviewing the chapter could flag any overreach.
3. **The bias-in-AI mitigation evidence.** This is moving fast and is mostly preliminary. The chapter should be willing to write "this is the open empirical question" rather than overclaiming.

### Things missing from the pantry that would help

1. **A specific district-level voice describing AI PD inequity.** The Yondr/$5.2M LAUSD case (Ch 1) is concrete. The chapter could benefit from a parallel case of a district that *did* invest disproportionately in AI PD in its lowest-income schools and what happened.
2. **A specific tutor or teacher whose practice changed.** The Tutor CoPilot study has aggregate data; a single tutor-level qualitative scene would help.
3. **Updated 2026 Pew or NCES figures on the homework-gap aftermath of the FCC rescission.** The chapter would benefit from any survey work that has measured the actual loss of home connectivity in the months following September 30, 2025.

### The one major flag for Nik

**The Bastani-to-self-regulation-to-low-income inference is the chapter's most consequential reasoning chain, and it is the chain most easily misread as a direct empirical finding.** The TIKTOC framing — that the −17% "falls hardest on students who lack the self-regulation skills to resist it, who are disproportionately from low-income and high-stress households" — is true as an inference from the union of (Bastani's average finding) + (Evans/Schamberg/Blair-Raver's documentation of the SES-self-regulation gradient) + (the cognitive-science account of how cognitive offloading interacts with executive function). It is not a direct empirical demonstration. The chapter must make the chain visible, label it as inference, and resist the temptation to compress it into "Bastani showed that the harm fell hardest on low-income students." Bastani did not show that. The combined literatures support that inference. The book's intellectual honesty requires the distinction.

If Nik wants the chapter to claim more than this, the right move is to commission a direct moderation-analysis re-examination of Bastani's data with explicit SES proxies — but that is a research project, not a textbook chapter.

- Does not introduce new empirical findings about teacher PD effectiveness — that is Ch 4.
- Does not re-derive the cross-profession catch-up effect — that is Ch 5.

**Cross-reference logic.** When the chapter wants to say more about a harm, hand to the original chapter. When it wants to say more about the affirmative case mechanics, hand to Ch 5 or Ch 6. When it wants to say more about how to do the training, hand to Ch 8. The equity chapter's job is *aiming*, not re-deriving.
