# Chapter 6 — The AI Dividend
## Research Notes

**Chapter:** Movement II, Chapter 6 — "The AI Dividend"
**Subtitle:** *Six weeks per year, and what trained teachers do with the time*
**Target length:** 6,000–8,000 words
**Notes status:** Draft research synthesis. The 5.9 hours / six weeks figure is the load-bearing empirical claim of the chapter and probably of the book. The notes give it disproportionate attention, including a serious engagement with the methodological objections from Leon Furze, Marc Watkins, CRPE, and the *Chalkdust & Silicon* critique. Bottom line: the headline number is a teacher self-estimate, not a time-audit; the underlying pattern (teachers say AI saves them time on the tasks they do most often) is robust; the "and they reinvest it in the students" claim depends on a second self-report layer that deserves to be flagged.

---

## 1. The chapter argument in one paragraph

In 2025, the largest nationally representative survey of US public school teachers ever conducted on AI use produced one number that has done more rhetorical work for the AI-in-education argument than any other: teachers who use AI weekly say it saves them 5.9 hours a week, or six full school weeks per year. The number is real in a specific, careful sense — it comes from a 2,232-teacher RAND American Teacher Panel sample, weighted to the national NCES teacher demographic frame, with outliers trimmed at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles, summed across nine non-overlapping task categories. It is also a self-estimate, not a time-audit, and the chapter has to say that. The defensible chapter claim is not "AI saves teachers six weeks per year, period." It is "the teachers who use AI weekly report saving roughly six hours a week, the time-savings pattern is corroborated by a high-quality UK randomized controlled trial that found a 31% reduction in lesson preparation time with no quality loss (EEF 2024), and what those teachers say they do with the recovered time — individualized feedback, intervention, parent communication, getting home for dinner — maps cleanly onto the high-effect-size practices Chapter 4 already laid out." The AI dividend is real in magnitude. It is contested in how that magnitude was measured. The reinvestment story is the part most worth defending and most vulnerable to overclaim.

## 2. The Gallup-Walton Family Foundation 2025 survey — primary source pull

### 2.1 What the study is

The full title is **"Teaching for Tomorrow: Unlocking Six Weeks a Year With AI."** It is the third wave of an ongoing Walton Family Foundation-Gallup partnership on K-12 teacher experience; the AI-focused wave was fielded in spring 2025 and released in late June 2025.[1][2][3] The Walton Family Foundation funded the study; Gallup conducted the fielding, analysis, and reporting.

- **Sample:** n = 2,232 US public K-12 teachers.
- **Sampling frame:** RAND American Teacher Panel — a probability-based, nationally representative standing panel of US public school teachers.
- **Mode:** Web survey.
- **Field dates:** March 18, 2025 – April 11, 2025.
- **Weighting:** Final sample weighted to NCES national K-12 teacher demographics across school level, student poverty, student race and Hispanic ethnicity, school size, school urbanicity, teacher gender, teacher race and Hispanic ethnicity, and teacher years of experience.[1][2]

The report PDF and the Gallup news writeup are the two primary citations the chapter should use; secondary coverage (EdSurge, The 74, Idaho Ed News, EdSource) is useful for paraphrasing teacher voices but not for methodology claims.[1][2][3][4][5]

### 2.2 The headline numbers, exactly as stated

From the Gallup report and the Walton "AI Dividend" microsite:[1][2]

- **Adoption.** 60% of US public school teachers used at least one AI tool for work tasks during the 2024–25 school year. 32% report using AI at least weekly. 28% use it monthly or less. 40% are not using AI at work at all.[1][2]
- **The 5.9-hour figure.** Among teachers who use AI **at least weekly**, the average self-reported time savings is **5.9 hours per week**. Gallup translates this to "roughly six weeks per school year" using a standard 36-week academic calendar and a 30–35 hour direct-work week.[1][2]
- **Where the savings appear.** When teachers use AI for one of nine specified task categories, "majorities — ranging from 60% to 84% — report that AI saves them time. Very few (7% or less) report that their work takes more time when they use AI."[1] The largest reported time-savings categories: preparing to teach (37% use AI at least monthly), making worksheets or activities (33%), modifying materials to meet student needs (28%), administrative work (28%), developing assessments (25%).[1][6]
- **Where the time goes — reinvestment.** Among weekly AI-using teachers: 60% report having more time for "improving teaching and learning"; 60% report more time for "individualized feedback and support" to students; 57% report "better insights about student learning data."[2][7] Open-ended responses surface specific reinvestment patterns: more nuanced feedback, individualized lesson design, more deliberate parent communication, and — repeatedly — leaving school at a reasonable hour.[2]
- **Quality.** Among weekly users, large majorities report that AI improves the **quality** of their work tasks, with weekly users roughly twice as likely as monthly users to report "much higher quality" output.[1] This is a self-perception claim, not a blind-review claim, and the chapter should distinguish it from the EEF blind-review finding (§4).

### 2.3 The methodology footnote that matters — how the 5.9 hours was constructed

This is the part most secondary coverage glosses and the part the chapter has to get right. The figure is not a single-question estimate. The construction has four steps:[1][2]

1. Each weekly-AI-using teacher was asked, **task by task across nine task categories**, to estimate in half-hour increments how many hours AI saves or adds for them each week on that task.
2. Categories are **non-overlapping by design** — the same time isn't counted twice across, say, "preparing to teach" and "developing assessments."
3. Per-teacher totals are summed across categories.
4. Outliers are **trimmed at the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles** before computing the average. The reported 5.9 hours is the trimmed weekly mean across weekly AI-using respondents.

The trimming step is the methodological choice that makes the headline number defensible against a "one teacher claimed 80 hours saved" objection. Trimming the top 2.5% and the bottom 2.5% is a standard winsorizing move for self-report time estimates; it discards both implausibly high claims and implausibly low (or negative) ones. The number is what survives that filter.

What the trimming does **not** do:

- It does not correct for general optimism bias in self-reported time estimates.
- It does not verify that the saved time actually exists outside the respondent's perception of it.
- It does not establish causality between AI use and time savings; weekly AI users may differ systematically from monthly users in ways that affect baseline workload.
- It does not address social desirability — teachers in a survey about whether AI helps may feel more comfortable saying it does.

These are the limits the chapter should name explicitly. The chapter should not pretend they aren't there.

### 2.4 What the Gallup data does and does not let you say

**Defensible claims for the chapter:**
- Weekly AI-using teachers in a nationally representative sample self-report 5.9 hours per week of time savings on a curated list of tasks, with outliers trimmed.
- The pattern is broadly consistent across task categories: between 60% and 84% of weekly users report some time savings on whichever task they use AI for.
- A clear majority (around 60% each) of weekly users say they reinvest at least some of the recovered time into individualized feedback and improving teaching and learning.

**Claims the chapter should *not* make from this data alone:**
- That 5.9 hours of time were objectively saved (this is a self-report).
- That those 5.9 hours produced 5.9 hours of additional instruction-improving work (the reinvestment percentages do not specify dose).
- That AI adoption *causes* time savings (the comparison is cross-sectional, between weekly users and others).
- That the 5.9 figure generalizes to teachers who do not currently use AI weekly. The same teachers who report saving time may be selecting in *because* AI works for their specific workflow; the 40% non-users may be filtering out for reasons that would also block the time savings.

The chapter should make the defensible claims, name the limits, and then add the EEF RCT as the strongest external corroboration.

## 3. The RAND American Teacher Panel — why the sampling frame matters

The chapter argument depends on this being a nationally representative survey, not a convenience sample of AI enthusiasts. That depends entirely on the RAND American Teacher Panel.

The RAND American Teacher Panel is one of three standing panels (teachers, school leaders, district leaders) under the RAND American Educator Panels (AEP) program. It is, per RAND, "the only three standing nationally representative samples of educators in the U.S. who regularly provide their feedback on important issues of educational policy and practice."[8][9]

**Methodology highlights:**

- The panel contains over 25,000 teachers across grades PK–12.
- Panel members are recruited via probability-based sampling from a commercially available list of US public school teachers, with the sampling frame acquired from MDR Education.[8]
- Initial recruitment uses FedEx invitations with a $10 prepaid gift card, followed by email reminders. The overall empanelment rate is approximately 30%.[8]
- Each panel member carries a design weight that accounts for selection probability; weights are further calibrated so the panel matches NCES national K-12 teacher demographics.[8][9]

For Chapter 6's purposes, three things matter:

1. **It is not an opt-in online panel.** The frame is teachers selected through probability sampling from a commercial list, not teachers who self-recruited into an AI-interest panel.
2. **It is weighted to NCES.** The published estimates are population-projected, not raw panel responses.
3. **The 30% empanelment rate is real and a known limitation.** Teachers who refuse to join may differ from those who join. This is a standard issue with any panel; it is also why RAND publishes its weighting methodology and recalibrates regularly.[8][9]

The chapter should treat RAND ATP as the strongest available US teacher sampling frame and note that "strongest available" is not the same as "perfect."

## 4. The EEF ChatGPT RCT — the strongest external corroboration

The Education Endowment Foundation's "ChatGPT in Lesson Preparation" trial is the chapter's most important non-self-report evidence. It is a randomized controlled trial, run in real schools, with blind-panel quality assessment. The methodology is exactly what the Gallup self-report lacks.

### 4.1 Study design[10][11][12][13]

- **Trial type:** Cluster-randomized controlled trial, school-level randomization.
- **Sample:** 259 teachers across 68 secondary schools in England.
- **Treatment:** 129 teachers in 34 schools were given access to ChatGPT plus a structured implementation guide (developed by Bain & Company's Social Impact practice with input from schools and education charities; funded by the Hg Foundation).
- **Control:** 130 teachers in 34 schools continued without ChatGPT or the guide.
- **Subject and grade:** Year 7 and Year 8 science (roughly equivalent to US grades 6–7).
- **Outcome 1 (preparation time):** Self-reported minutes per week on lesson and resource preparation.
- **Outcome 2 (quality):** Independent panel of 5 science teachers rated 30 lesson resources (15 ChatGPT, 15 non-ChatGPT) blinded to condition, on four dimensions — clarity, engagement, age/ability appropriateness, scientific accuracy.
- **Outcome 3 (workload sentiment):** Teacher-reported workload perceptions and well-being.
- **Year:** Fielded 2024; reported December 2024.

### 4.2 Headline findings[10][11][12][13]

- **Time savings.** ChatGPT teachers spent 56.2 minutes per week on lesson and resource preparation. Control teachers spent 81.5 minutes. Difference: 25.3 minutes per week. **Percentage reduction: 31%.** The effect was statistically significant.
- **Quality.** The blind panel found **no statistically significant difference** in lesson resource quality between the two groups across any of the four scoring dimensions.
- **Workload perception.** Teachers in the ChatGPT condition reported lower lesson preparation workload. No detectable effect on broader well-being measures within the trial window.

### 4.3 Why this matters for the chapter

The Gallup self-report headline is "5.9 hours saved per week, across all task categories, among weekly AI users." The EEF finding is "25.3 minutes saved per week on lesson preparation specifically, in a randomized trial with no quality loss." The two numbers are not directly commensurable — Gallup spans nine task categories, EEF spans one. But:

- The **direction** is the same.
- The **magnitude per task** is in the same neighborhood (25 minutes on one task scales plausibly into hours when summed across nine).
- The EEF result was generated under randomized conditions, so it gives the Gallup self-report **causal cover** for at least the lesson-prep portion of the time savings claim.
- The EEF quality finding is the critical defense against the most plausible objection to time savings: that teachers are simply producing worse lesson materials faster. Within the EEF trial window, with blind panel scoring, that objection does not survive.

### 4.4 Limits of the EEF trial the chapter should name

- **Subject specificity.** Year 7/8 science is one corner of the curriculum. Generalization to English literature, history, primary literacy is not established by this trial.
- **Quality measurement is limited.** Blind ratings of 30 lesson resources are a *sample* of output, not a measurement of student learning outcomes. The EEF report explicitly cautions that the quality finding is "promising" but "should be treated with caution."[10]
- **Implementation support.** Teachers received a structured guide. The trial measures "ChatGPT + structured guide vs. control," not "ChatGPT alone vs. control." This is actually a feature for the book's argument — *trained* AI use is the variable that produced the result — but it's a limit for any naive "give teachers ChatGPT" reading.
- **No persistence measurement.** The trial does not establish whether the time savings persist beyond the study window, or whether teachers gradually expand the scope of their lesson plans to consume the saved time (the well-known "Parkinson's law of teacher work").

The Aila/Oak National Academy follow-up RCT, also commissioned by EEF, is in field with 450 KS2 (Year 3–6) teachers across 86 primary schools and is scheduled to report in autumn 2026.[14] That trial is worth tracking — it tests a more structured, pedagogy-aware AI assistant rather than raw ChatGPT, and it pushes the evidence into primary school for the first time.

## 5. The Stanford M-Powering Teachers / Demszky research — what AI does to teaching quality, not teacher time

The chapter's argument is two-part: (1) AI saves teachers time, and (2) trained teachers reinvest that time in higher-quality instruction. The Demszky lab's work is the strongest evidence we have for what "higher-quality instruction" can mean concretely, and for what AI feedback can do to teacher practice itself. It belongs in the chapter as evidence for *what the reinvestment looks like*, not as time-savings evidence.

### 5.1 The conversational uptake foundation (Demszky et al. 2021)

Demszky, Liu, Mancenido, Cohen, Hill, Jurafsky, and Hashimoto published "Measuring Conversational Uptake: A Case Study on Student-Teacher Interactions" at ACL 2021.[15] The paper does something the education research literature had not previously been able to do at scale: it operationalizes the construct of **conversational uptake** — the degree to which a teacher's next utterance builds on what a student just said — as a computable quantity, using pointwise Jensen-Shannon Divergence estimated via next-utterance classification.

Uptake matters because it is the linguistic signature of responsive teaching. A teacher who responds "good job, now turn to page 47" is not taking up the student's contribution. A teacher who responds "so you're saying the variable X depends on Y — what would happen if X were negative?" is. Higher uptake at the classroom level is empirically associated with higher student achievement.[15]

This is the measurement infrastructure that makes the rest of the Demszky lab's work possible.

### 5.2 M-Powering Teachers — the online course RCT (Demszky, Liu, Hill, Jurafsky, Piech 2024)

The most-cited M-Powering Teachers result is the Code in Place RCT:[16][17][18]

- **Setting:** Code in Place, a large online introductory CS course at Stanford.
- **Sample:** N ≈ 1,136 instructors (the figure is 1,136 in the published 2024 paper; some early summaries cite different N's from preregistration drafts).
- **Intervention:** Weekly emails to treatment-condition instructors with personalized, automated feedback on their uptake of student ideas during 1:1 sessions, generated by the M-Powering Teachers NLP tool.
- **Control:** No feedback emails.
- **Result:** Treatment instructors who engaged with the feedback increased uptake of student contributions by approximately **13%** (some reports cite "roughly 10%" — both numbers come from different specifications in the published paper).[16][17]
- **Publication:** *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*, 2024.[17]

The mechanism is striking: a low-touch, automated, weekly email to a teacher about the linguistic quality of her interactions with students measurably moved that quality. The treatment didn't require a coach, didn't require release time, didn't require platform adoption. It required reading the email.

### 5.3 M-Powering Teachers — the brick-and-mortar RCT (Demszky et al. 2024, *Computers & Education*)

The Code in Place trial answered "does this work online with adult instructors?" The brick-and-mortar trial — published in *Computers & Education* — answered "does it work in K-12 classrooms?"[19][20]

- **Setting:** 224 mathematics and science teachers in Utah K-12 classrooms.
- **Partner:** TeachFX, the classroom audio-recording platform Demszky's group has collaborated with for years.
- **Intervention:** Automated email feedback on teacher questioning patterns, particularly the use of "focusing questions" (questions that press students for reasoning rather than recall).
- **Engagement rate:** Teachers opened feedback emails 53–65% of the time.
- **Result:** Treatment teachers showed a **20% increase in use of focusing questions** (p < 0.01) relative to control. No statistically significant effects on other measured discourse features. Effect size in week 5: roughly 10 additional focusing questions per hour, a 59% increase over control at that timepoint.[19][20]
- **Dose response:** The effect was dose-dependent — teachers who recorded their lessons more consistently received more feedback and showed larger effects.

### 5.4 What this means for Chapter 6

The Demszky work is not direct evidence of the AI dividend. It is direct evidence for the **content** of what the AI dividend could be reinvested in. If a six-hour-per-week recovery from administrative tasks lets a teacher record her lessons twice a week and read a feedback email Monday morning, the M-Powering trials say her questioning quality will measurably improve.

The chapter should pair the Gallup time-savings story with the Demszky quality story this way:

> *Time savings without quality reinvestment is a productivity story. Quality reinvestment without time savings is an aspiration. The two together — a documented six-hour-per-week recovery on one side, and a documented mechanism for converting that time into measurably better teacher questioning on the other — is the AI dividend the book is arguing for.*

That linkage is the chapter's spine.

### 5.5 What Demszky's work does NOT establish

- **Student achievement.** The brick-and-mortar trial measured teacher behavior, not student learning. The Code in Place trial measured uptake, not student CS course outcomes.
- **Generalization beyond questioning.** The feedback targeted one specific practice. Whether broader pedagogical feedback would scale similarly is open.
- **Cost-effectiveness at scale.** TeachFX is a real platform with real costs. The studies do not address whether automated feedback is cost-competitive with human coaching (Kraft, Blazar & Hogan 2018, which is in the Ch. 4 notes, sets the human-coaching benchmark at d = 0.49 for instruction and d = 0.18 for achievement).

## 6. The Arkansas anecdote and what teacher voices actually say

The TIKTOC opens Chapter 6 with the Arkansas secondary teacher quote: *"Now I've been able to implement daily intervention time for at-risk students, as well as enrichment time every day for all students."* The quote appears across Walton Family Foundation materials and Arkansas press coverage, attributed to a teacher participating in the AI dividend study.[2][21][22]

Two important things to flag:

1. The quote is **attributed to a Walton Family Foundation source** in their "Six Weeks: Giving Teachers Time Back with AI" microsite materials.[2] It also surfaces in Arkansas Democrat-Gazette and KUAF (NPR member station) coverage of Arkansas-specific results from the Gallup study.[21][22] The Arkansas wave of the study found that roughly 7 in 10 Arkansas teachers reported using AI for lesson preparation — a higher rate than the national 60%, which is unsurprising given Walton's Arkansas roots and the state's higher rate of public-private AI partnerships.[22] **[verify direct attribution and full context with the original Walton microsite or report PDF — quote should be cited from primary, not via secondary coverage]**
2. The chapter should treat the anecdote as one teacher's testimony, not as a generalizable claim. The pattern it illustrates — recovered time → intervention and enrichment slots — is *consistent with* the 60% reinvestment figures from the survey, not proven by them. The opening should signal the move from anecdote to data, not blur the two.

Other teacher voices the chapter could draw on — all from Walton materials or secondary coverage:[2][6][7]

- *"This year alone, I've used AI to help with lesson plans, differentiating materials, writing parts of IEPs [individualized education programs], communicating with families, and all of that adds up to an entire planning day that I get back."*
- Teachers reporting that the recovered time went into "getting home to their families at a more reasonable time."[2] (This is not a high-effect-size pedagogical reinvestment, but it is honest evidence that not all the dividend goes into the classroom — some of it goes into teacher sustainability, which the book's argument about working conditions in Ch. 11 should pick up.)

## 7. The Mollick / Dell'Acqua BCG study — the analog from knowledge work

The Mollick-Dell'Acqua "jagged frontier" study is the chapter's analog from outside K-12, and the chapter should use it carefully. It is *not* a study of teachers. It is a study of management consultants. But it is the cleanest available randomized test of what AI does to knowledge-worker productivity, and the magnitudes are in the same neighborhood as the teacher findings — which is, by itself, evidence that the teacher numbers are not anomalous.

### 7.1 Study design[23][24][25][26]

- **Title:** "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of the Effects of AI on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality."
- **Authors:** Dell'Acqua, McFowland, Mollick, Lifshitz, Kellogg, Rajendran, Krayer, Candelon, Lakhani (Harvard Business School, Wharton, MIT Sloan, Warwick Business School, BCG Henderson Institute).
- **Working paper:** HBS Working Paper 24-013, September 2023.
- **Sample:** 758 BCG consultants.
- **Design:** Randomized assignment to AI access (GPT-4) or control, across 18 realistic consulting tasks.
- **Two task sets:**
  - **Inside the frontier:** Tasks AI handles well.
  - **Outside the frontier:** Tasks deliberately chosen to be beyond GPT-4's reliable capability.

### 7.2 Results within the frontier[23][24][25][26]

- Consultants with AI access completed 12.2% more tasks.
- They completed tasks 25.1% faster.
- Their output was rated 40% higher quality on average.
- Below-median performers improved by 43%; top performers improved by 17%. AI compressed the performance distribution upward.

### 7.3 Results outside the frontier

- Consultants with AI access were 19 percentage points **less likely** to produce correct solutions than non-AI consultants.
- Mechanism: AI confidently produced wrong answers; consultants who deferred to it were misled. Consultants who tried to verify struggled to distinguish the AI's plausible-but-wrong reasoning from correct reasoning.

### 7.4 Centaurs vs. cyborgs[24][25]

The paper introduces two patterns of AI integration the chapter could use as a frame:

- **Centaur:** Clean division of labor — human does what humans are best at, AI does what AI is best at, with explicit handoffs.
- **Cyborg:** Constant interleaving — human and AI alternate at sub-task granularity, with the human treating AI as a continuous partner rather than a delegated worker.

The study observed most consultants defaulting to cyborg patterns. For the chapter, this matters because the *trained teacher* is exactly the professional who has the chance to choose centaur deliberately — and the EEF guide-plus-ChatGPT design is essentially a centaur intervention (the guide tells the teacher what to delegate to ChatGPT and what to keep).

### 7.5 What the chapter can claim from this study

**Defensible:**
- AI access produces large, measurable productivity gains on tasks within its capability range, in a randomized field experiment with knowledge workers.
- Magnitudes (25% faster, 40% higher quality) are large enough to make the teacher self-report magnitudes plausible rather than implausible.
- The largest gains accrue to below-average performers — consistent with the Tutor CoPilot finding from Ch. 5 that AI tools level the floor.

**Not defensible from this study:**
- That teachers will see the same gains. Consulting and teaching are different tasks; teachers operate under different constraints; the AI tools available to BCG (curated GPT-4 access) are not the AI tools available to most teachers (general ChatGPT, often without enterprise security).
- That AI productivity gains persist over longer time horizons than the trial measured.
- That the "outside the frontier" failure mode does not apply to teaching. Arguably it applies *more* — distinguishing AI-correct from AI-confident-but-wrong is the entire problem with student use of AI (Chapter 7, Bastani et al.) and a major challenge for teacher use as well.

## 8. The serious critique — what the AI dividend story gets wrong, or could be getting wrong

This is the section the chapter needs most, per the book's "engage the strongest counter-evidence" commitment. The Gallup-Walton headline number has drawn three distinct serious critiques, plus a fourth one that is more about framing than measurement.

### 8.1 The Leon Furze critique — "time saved" is the wrong measurement

Leon Furze, an Australian AI-in-education researcher who has written some of the most-read critical pieces on AI deployment in schools, published "'Time Saved' is the Wrong Measurement for Teacher Workload and AI" in June 2025 and a follow-up in July 2025.[27][28]

His argument, distilled:

1. The "time saved" framing is the metric tech companies prefer because it's the metric that makes their product look good. It is not the metric that predicts teacher retention or burnout.
2. The Walton headline (5.9 hours) and the Microsoft headline (which has separately reported even larger time savings of 6–9.5 hours/week in some of its studies) both treat the saved tasks as fungible. They aren't. Some of the tasks AI is best at — generating worksheets, drafting parent emails — are exactly the compliance-administrative tasks that, when stripped away, *leave the harder relational work behind*. The hard work doesn't get easier because the easy work is gone.
3. The teacher workload literature is clear that what drives burnout is *not* total hours worked but **realistic workloads, adequate materials, mentoring, autonomy, and respect**. Time-saving on individual tasks doesn't address any of these structural drivers.
4. Therefore, presenting "AI saves teachers time" as the headline finding risks crowding out the structural reforms (planning time protected by policy, mentorship infrastructure, decision authority over classroom technology) that actually retain teachers.

For Chapter 6, the Furze critique is not a refutation of the AI dividend. It is a sharpening of what the dividend has to be defended as: not "AI fixes teacher burnout" (it doesn't) but "AI returns the easy administrative time *so that the structural reforms about hard relational work have any chance of working*." The chapter should name this distinction explicitly.

### 8.2 The Marc Watkins / *Chalkdust & Silicon* critique — context, not refutation

Marc Watkins (University of Mississippi) and the *Chalkdust & Silicon* blog independently published more measured critiques making roughly parallel points:[29][30]

- The Walton-Gallup report's core claim is based on **self-estimation**, not observed workflows, time audits, or actual logged data. This is true and worth saying clearly.
- The report does not — and cannot — provide evidence on **instructional quality, student outcomes, or equity**. The 5.9-hour figure is a workload claim, not a learning claim.
- The report's framing makes it "easy for vendors and policymakers to weaponize" as justification for rapid AI rollouts. Watkins worries about the rhetorical effect of the headline more than the headline itself.
- Reported relationships are not necessarily causal. Weekly AI users may differ from non-users in ways that affect their baseline workload.

The *Chalkdust & Silicon* author notes that the report itself includes a finding the celebratory coverage glosses: **52% of teachers using AI taught themselves; only 31% received district-provided training**.[29] This is the chapter's hook into Chapter 8 (Train Like a Doctor) — the dividend is being collected by autodidacts, not by trained professionals. The structural training infrastructure the book argues for is still missing.

### 8.3 The CRPE critique — the dividend as marketing artifact[31]

The Center on Reinventing Public Education published a Substack piece in summer 2025 ("The 'AI Dividend' — Does AI Create More Time for Teachers?") asking the harder version of the question.[31] Their central observation: the survey doesn't measure whether the recovered time produces measurably better outcomes for students, and absent that link, the dividend is just productivity rhetoric.

This is a fair point and the chapter should respond to it directly. The response is the Demszky M-Powering Teachers literature (§5), which provides the missing link between teacher behavior change and at least some intermediate outcomes (questioning quality, uptake) plausibly connected to student learning. But the response is honest only to the extent it acknowledges that the **direct** chain — saved time → reinvested time → better student outcomes — is not yet established by any single study.

### 8.4 The Lortie / Parkinson critique — work expands to fill the time

The most empirically grounded objection comes from the broader teacher workload literature: teacher work is famously elastic. Dan Lortie's *Schoolteacher* (1975) and decades of follow-up research describe how teachers, when given more planning time, tend to spend it on more elaborate planning rather than on rest or relational work. This is a version of Parkinson's law specific to teaching.

The implication for the AI dividend: if teachers expand their lesson plans to consume the recovered time — adding more differentiation, more detailed assessments, more elaborate parent communication — the time isn't recovered for the high-effect-size relational practices the chapter wants to claim. It's just rerouted to different administrative work.

The EEF trial is the chapter's evidence against this objection: in a randomized condition, teachers actually *did* spend less total time on lesson preparation, not the same time on more elaborate plans. Quality was maintained. But the EEF trial ran ten weeks; the Lortie effect operates over years. The honest chapter language is "early evidence is consistent with real time savings, but the long-run elasticity question is open."

### 8.5 The chapter's synthesis of the critique

The Furze, Watkins, CRPE, and Lortie critiques together amount to a real boundary on what the AI dividend claim can carry:

- **Yes:** Weekly AI-using teachers self-report saving roughly 6 hours per week, in a nationally representative survey with reasonable methodology, corroborated by a UK RCT showing 31% reduction in lesson prep time with quality maintained.
- **Yes:** Most of those teachers say they reinvest at least some of the recovered time in higher-quality work.
- **Yes:** The Demszky research provides a credible mechanism for converting that reinvested time into measurably better teaching practice.
- **Not yet:** Direct evidence that the reinvested time produces measurably better student outcomes at scale.
- **Not addressed:** Whether AI time savings persist beyond short trial windows, or whether they alleviate teacher burnout (Furze's structural-burnout objection is well-supported; AI time savings on individual tasks probably do not).

The chapter's closing has to land on this honestly. The AI dividend is the most plausible empirical finding the pro-teacher-AI argument has. It is not a guarantee. It is a window — six hours per week the trained teacher has to do work the algorithm cannot do — and the question is what we build around that window.

## 9. The reinvestment thesis — why the time matters

If the chapter's central empirical claim is the six hours, its central conceptual claim is the reinvestment story: that the time matters because it goes into the highest-effect-size practices identified in Chapter 4.

### 9.1 The crosswalk

Chapter 4 established Hattie's ranked influences. The relevant ones for Chapter 6 are the teacher-mediated, relational practices that the AI dividend purportedly enables more of:

| Hattie influence | d (most-cited) | What the AI dividend reportedly enables |
|---|---|---|
| Collective teacher efficacy | 1.57 | Shared planning time recovered; PLC depth possible |
| Self-reported grades / formative awareness | 1.44 | Detailed individualized feedback (60% reinvestment claim) |
| Teacher credibility | 0.90 | More responsive in-class interaction, better-prepared explanations |
| Feedback | 0.50–0.73 | Direct: 60% report more time for individualized feedback |
| Teacher-student relationships | 0.72 | Time freed for 1:1 check-ins, intervention, enrichment (Arkansas anecdote) |
| Comprehensive teacher PD | 0.51–0.62 | Time freed for sustained, peer-supported development |

The chapter should report these alongside the reinvestment percentages and make the conditional claim cleanly: *if* the saved time is reinvested in these practices, the effect-size literature predicts substantial student-outcome improvements. The Walton survey gives us the "if" with reasonable evidence. The achievement-outcome literature gives us the "then." Closing the chain is the work the AI+1 training infrastructure (Chapter 8) is supposed to do.

### 9.2 The within-30 framing

The TIKTOC's "within-30 argument" is the chapter's distinctive rhetorical move. The standard productivity story would say "AI saves teachers time so they can teach more students." The chapter's argument inverts that: **AI saves teachers time so they can do more for the students they already have**.

This matters because:

- Hattie's effect sizes are *relational*. They live in the teacher-student dyad and the small-group classroom. They do not scale by adding students; they scale by adding depth per student.
- The optimal class size for the highest-effect practices is roughly 25–30 (Chapter 4). AI does not change that ceiling.
- The labor-market alternative — using AI productivity gains to teach more students per teacher — would push past the optimum and erode the practices the dividend is supposed to enable.

The chapter's closing argument is therefore not productivity but **depth**. The dividend is six weeks per year *of relationship and intervention time the teacher could not previously afford*. Spent on the same students. Inside the same room. Producing more of the work only she can do.

### 9.3 The untrained-teacher gap

The TIKTOC closes Chapter 6 with the compounding-gap argument: the 40% of teachers not using AI are working the same hours producing the same outputs, while their trained colleagues generate six extra weeks of relationship time per year. Over a career, the gap is enormous.

This is the chapter's hand-off to the equity argument (Chapter 11). The dividend is not equally distributed. Teachers in schools that have invested in AI training collect it; teachers in schools that haven't, don't. The gap compounds at the school level, the district level, the regional level. The students at the bottom of the equity distribution lose twice — first by attending schools less likely to train teachers in AI, and second by sitting in classrooms where the teacher has six fewer weeks per year to do high-effect-size work.

The chapter should preview this argument and route the resolution to Chapter 11.

## 10. Open questions the chapter should name

Per the *Visible Learning* "What the research leaves open" convention from the book's voice notes, the chapter should close honestly on what we don't yet know. The defensible list:

1. **Persistence.** Does the 5.9-hour weekly savings persist over multi-year horizons, or does teacher work expand to absorb it (Lortie/Parkinson effect)? The EEF 10-week trial cannot answer this.
2. **Causal magnitude.** The 5.9-hour figure is a self-report. The EEF 25-minute figure is causally identified but covers only one task category. A multi-task, randomized, time-audit study with classroom observation does not yet exist for AI in K-12.
3. **Student outcomes.** No study has yet directly tested whether the reinvested AI dividend produces measurable student-achievement gains. The Demszky uptake studies measured teacher behavior, not student learning. This is the most important open question and probably the most important next study.
4. **Burnout vs. workload.** The Furze critique is that time-saving and burnout-reduction are not the same thing. The well-being measurements in the Walton survey and in EEF are weak and short-window. The structural-conditions argument (Furze) is probably right that AI alone cannot resolve teacher burnout; what AI can do is create the slack in which structural reforms could matter.
5. **Distributional questions.** The Walton survey reports aggregate national figures. It does not report subgroup analyses by school income, district size, or teacher experience in granular form. The dividend may be very unevenly distributed. This is critical for the equity argument but undermeasured.
6. **AI model evolution.** The 2025 survey reflects 2024-25 AI tool capabilities. By 2027, the tools will be substantially different. Whether the dividend grows, plateaus, or shrinks (because saved tasks expand or quality concerns reduce adoption) is open.

## 11. Suggested chapter structure (8-section spine)

Per the book's chapter conventions (TIKTOC §405–410 and CLAUDE.md §8), Chapter 6 should run 5,500–7,500 words in eight sections:

1. **Cold open: the Arkansas teacher voice** — intervention time and enrichment time every day, named teacher (or anonymized if attribution can't be cleanly verified), specific dollar/hour estimates of what those slots used to cost the school in unmet need.
2. **Specification: what "the AI dividend" actually means** — defining the 5.9 hours, the methodology, the trimming, the reinvestment categories, the population the figure describes.
3. **The Gallup-Walton survey in detail** — sampling frame, weighting, task-by-task construction, the nine categories, the 60%/60%/57% reinvestment triad.
4. **The EEF RCT — the strongest external corroboration** — design, results, the quality-maintained finding, why randomization matters for the Gallup self-report.
5. **The mechanism: Stanford M-Powering Teachers** — Demszky's uptake construct, the Code in Place 13% finding, the brick-and-mortar 20% focusing-questions finding, what reinvestment looks like at the linguistic-feature level.
6. **The serious critique** — Furze's "time saved is the wrong metric," Watkins/Chalkdust on causality, CRPE on the missing student-outcome link, Lortie on work elasticity. The chapter must engage these, not paper over them.
7. **The within-30 argument** — the dividend is depth, not scale; the Hattie effect-size crosswalk; the untrained-teacher gap as preview of Chapter 11.
8. **Synthesis and hand to reader** — what the dividend is, what it isn't, what the trained teacher does with it, what would have to be measured next for the chain to close. **"What would change my mind"** and **"Still puzzling"** lines per CLAUDE.md §9.

## 12. Voice and pacing notes

- The chapter's headline number does heavy lifting. Resist the temptation to lead with it. Lead with the Arkansas voice; let the number arrive in §2 as the answer to a question the reader is already asking.
- The methodology footnote in §3 is the chapter's "explainer's move" per CLAUDE.md §3.3 — the deep-dive on one specific mechanism. Spend the words. Show the trimming. Show why winsorizing at 2.5/97.5 is reasonable. Show what it doesn't fix.
- The critique section in §6 is the chapter's test of intellectual honesty. The book's voice is "explanatory with a view, not argumentative in the debate sense." Furze and Watkins are right about important things. The chapter wins by acknowledging that and showing the dividend claim still survives the boundary they draw.
- The Demszky section in §5 should celebrate the mechanism — *look at what they built; you can measure conversational uptake with NLP and it turns out to predict student outcomes; that's beautiful* — without overclaiming the achievement linkage.
- The closing should be short, declarative, memorable. Probably one sentence that names what the trained teacher does with the dividend that the algorithm cannot do alone.
