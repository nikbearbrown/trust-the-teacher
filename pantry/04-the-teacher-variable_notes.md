# Chapter 4 — The Teacher Variable
## Research Notes

**Chapter:** Movement II, Chapter 4 — "The Teacher Variable"
**Subtitle:** *What forty years of education research shows about the one thing that actually predicts whether students learn*
**Target length:** 5,500–7,000 words
**Notes status:** Draft research synthesis. Many headline effect sizes are widely cited but methodologically contested. The chapter's job is not to dismiss Hattie or to ratify him — it is to walk the reader through what the evidence actually shows, what it cannot show, and what survives the most serious critiques.

---

## 1. The chapter argument in one paragraph

Forty years of education research, gathered into Hattie's *Visible Learning* synthesis and into the parallel value-added literature, points to the same place: the variable that does the most explaining of student learning is the teacher. Not the device. Not the platform. Not the class size. The teacher's practice, the teacher's relationships, the teacher's collective belief with colleagues that they can move kids forward. The headline numbers — collective teacher efficacy d = 1.57, feedback d ≈ 0.70, teacher-student relationships d ≈ 0.72 — are widely cited and methodologically contested in equal measure. This chapter takes both seriously: it shows what the ranked influences are saying, walks through the most substantive critiques (Slavin, Wecker, Simpson, Bergeron & Rivard), and lands on the conclusion the critics and the defenders actually share. The teacher is the largest moveable variable in the system. The disagreement is about how much larger, not about whether.

## 2. The headline numbers — what they are and where they come from

### 2.1 The corpus

Hattie's *Visible Learning* began in 2009 with a synthesis of "over 800 meta-analyses" covering roughly 52,637 underlying studies, 146,142 effect sizes, and on the order of 80 million students. *Visible Learning: The Sequel* (2023) expanded to "over 2,100 meta-analyses," approximately 130,000 underlying studies, and is cited variously as 300 million or 400 million students.[1][2][3] The TIKTOC's "1,800+ studies, 300M+ students" should be revised in the chapter: the corpus is **2,100+ meta-analyses, 130,000+ underlying studies, ~300 million students**. The "1,800+ studies" figure appears to be a confusion of meta-analyses with studies. **[verify final numbers against current visible-learning.org and Visible Learning: The Sequel].**

The live database, **Visible Learning MetaX**, is updated through 2024 (Version 1.3, November 2024). MetaX adds a "robustness index" of 1–5, signaling how stable the average effect size is across the underlying meta-analyses.[4]

### 2.2 The hinge point

Hattie's interpretive convention: the average effect size across 320+ influences is approximately d = 0.40. He calls this the "hinge point" and frames it as "approximately one year's growth per year of schooling."[5] Any influence above 0.40 is "above average." The hinge framing has done more rhetorical work in the educational consulting world than any other number in *Visible Learning*. It has also drawn the sharpest critiques — discussed in §5.

## 3. The collective teacher efficacy number — what 1.57 actually rests on

This is the chapter's most visible claim. It deserves the most careful unpacking.

### 3.1 The construct

Collective teacher efficacy (CTE) is the shared belief among a school's teachers that they can, together, move student outcomes regardless of student background. The construct comes out of Albert Bandura's self-efficacy work in the 1990s and was operationalized for schools by Goddard, Hoy, and Woolfolk Hoy (2000), who built a validated measurement instrument and showed that CTE predicted achievement differences across urban elementary schools after controlling for SES.[12][13]

### 3.2 The derivation of d = 1.57

The d = 1.57 figure comes almost entirely from a single source: Rachel Jean Eells's 2011 Loyola University Chicago dissertation, "Meta-Analysis of the Relationship Between Collective Teacher Efficacy and Student Achievement."[14] Eells synthesized 26 underlying studies (dissertations and published articles) reporting at least one correlation between CTE and school-level achievement. Her **weighted mean correlation was r ≈ 0.617** (outlier-removed). Hattie converts that correlation to Cohen's d using the standard formula d = 2r/√(1−r²), which yields d ≈ 1.57.[15]

Three honest observations about that derivation:

1. **A single meta-analysis dominates the influence ranking.** When the headline "biggest effect in education research" rests on one PhD dissertation, the marginal value of additional meta-analyses is high and uncertain.
2. **A correlation-to-d conversion is mathematically valid but interpretively fraught.** A school-level correlation between teacher attitudes and student achievement is not the same kind of quantity as a student-level treatment effect from a randomized trial. Converting them onto a common metric makes them comparable on paper. It does not make them comparable in mechanism.
3. **The MetaX value has moved.** When a second meta-analysis (Çoğaltay & Karadağ 2017, r = 0.52, 35 studies) was incorporated, the weighted estimate softened. As of late 2024 / early 2025, the published MetaX value was reported to have dropped to roughly 1.01–1.12 — Hattie's team described this as the result of one new very-low-effect meta-analysis being incorporated, and indicated the correction was in process.[6]

The chapter should report all three points and then make this judgment: **CTE is a real construct with a real association with achievement.** Even Eells's r ≈ 0.62 — taken at face value, without the d-conversion — is a large effect by any standard in the social sciences. The honest reading is *not* "d = 1.57, end of story" and *not* "the number is bogus, ignore." The honest reading is "the underlying signal is real and substantial; the precise number is unstable; the mechanism — coordinated teacher belief moving practice across a school — is exactly the thing the AI+1 argument depends on."

### 3.3 What CTE means for the AI+1 case

The CTE finding is *not* about individual heroics. It is about what happens when an entire faculty believes — and believes together — that their joint practice can move outcomes. That is precisely the variable that distinguishes successful EdTech implementations from unsuccessful ones in the RAND Europe synthesis (see §6). The mechanism is collective adoption, peer support, shared expectation. AI tools enter that mechanism the way any tool does: amplified where the collective is strong, ignored or misused where it isn't.

## 4. The other top-tier teacher-mediated influences

### 4.1 Feedback

The traditional Hattie number is d ≈ 0.70–0.73. The current MetaX weighted mean is **d = 0.50** across 10 meta-analyses, 1,116 underlying studies, and 13,396 students.[8] The Education Endowment Foundation's Teaching and Learning Toolkit, working from 155 studies meeting its inclusion criteria, reports the equivalent of about **+6 months of additional progress** from feedback (~+7 months primary, +5 months secondary).[16]

The chapter should report all three: the historical 0.73, the current MetaX 0.50, the EEF months-of-progress framing. The phrase "feedback works" survives every version. The phrase "feedback is the highest-effect classroom-level intervention" is true in the EEF framing but contested in the MetaX revision. The honest reading: **feedback is a strong teacher-mediated influence; the exact magnitude depends on which meta-analytic family you trust; it sits clearly above any technology-only intervention regardless of which value you take.**

### 4.2 Teacher-student relationships

The d = 0.72 figure traces primarily to Roorda et al.'s 2011 meta-analysis of 99 studies, which separated positive and negative relationships and engagement and achievement outcomes. Their actual finding: associations with **engagement** were medium-to-large (mean effect sizes in the 0.30–0.40 range when reported as correlations); associations with **achievement** were small-to-medium.[10] Hattie's d = 0.72 represents an aggregation across multiple meta-analyses and a conversion of correlational findings. The underlying primary-research signal is real, well-replicated, and longitudinal. The d = 0.72 number is, again, partly an artifact of correlation-to-d conversion.

### 4.3 Comprehensive teacher PD

Hattie's older lists place PD at d = 0.51–0.62. The Kraft, Blazar & Hogan (2018) meta-analysis of teacher *coaching* (60 causal studies) is the most rigorous causal estimate in the literature: **pooled effect on instruction d = 0.49 standard deviations; pooled effect on student achievement d = 0.18 standard deviations**.[17] Two implications:

1. Coaching changes teacher practice substantially.
2. The translation of practice change into student achievement gains is smaller and slower.
3. Effectiveness drops sharply when programs are scaled — small efficacy trials produce much larger effects than effectiveness trials of larger programs. This is exactly the "implementation gap" the book has been describing.

The chapter should report Hattie's number and Kraft et al.'s causal estimate alongside each other. The honest sentence: **professional development reliably changes teaching; the achievement effect is real but smaller than the practice effect; the gap closes when PD is sustained, content-specific, coaching-anchored, and not scaled past the conditions where it works.**

## 5. The critiques — engaged seriously

This is the most important section of the chapter, per the book's "engage seriously with the evidence that complicates it" rule. The CLAUDE.md rule from §7 is binding: every framework that does work in the chapter has to be shown working and have its limits acknowledged. The Hattie framework is no exception.

### 5.1 The Slavin critique — input quality

Robert Slavin (Johns Hopkins; founder of the Best Evidence Encyclopedia), in a widely cited 2018 blog post and elsewhere, argued that Hattie's meta-meta-analysis approach accepts the outputs of underlying meta-analyses **without filtering for study quality**. Many underlying meta-analyses include studies that are small-sample, brief-duration, researcher-developed-measure, pre-post-without-control, or quasi-experimental. Each of those design features is known to inflate effect sizes. By averaging across meta-analyses that contain such studies, Hattie's aggregates inherit and compound the inflation.[18]

Slavin's sharpest phrase: Hattie is "merely shoveling meta-analyses containing massive bias into meta-meta-analyses that reflect the same biases."

This is the most serious empirical critique. The chapter should report it directly and quote Slavin's filtering counter-proposal: when you restrict to randomized trials of at least 12 weeks' duration with independent measures and adequate sample sizes, the headline effect sizes typically drop to d ≈ 0.10–0.20. **Large, properly randomized classroom evaluations rarely produce effect sizes above d = 0.20.**[19]

### 5.2 The Wecker critique — overlapping studies

Wecker et al. (2017) showed that Hattie's meta-meta-analysis double-counts (and sometimes triple-counts) the same underlying studies across the meta-analyses it averages. Early meta-analytic practice excluded second-stage meta-analyses with overlaps as small as 25%; Hattie's synthesis has overlaps that are sometimes much larger and goes unflagged.[20] Wecker also questioned Hattie's "year's progress = d 0.40" framing: an effect size of d = 0.30 over six weeks of intervention cannot plausibly correspond to less than a full year of normal schooling growth. The metrics are not commensurable.

Wecker provided one worked example showing that **using weighted averages that correct for the overlap would move one of Hattie's top-26 influences to 98th in the ranking.** Hattie & Hamilton (2020), in "Real Gold vs. Fool's Gold," acknowledged overlapping samples as "a source of distortion when using a synthesis approach" — the closest Hattie has come to conceding the Wecker critique on the merits.[21]

### 5.3 The Simpson critique — what an effect size actually measures

Adrian Simpson (Durham, 2017–2020) made the most statistically technical critique. His argument: an effect size (Cohen's d) is not a property of an intervention. It is a function of the intervention **and** the experimental design — specifically the comparison group, the range of the participant sample, and the measurement design. Three studies of the same intervention with different sample compositions, different control conditions, or different outcome measures will produce different d's *without anything changing about what the intervention does.*[22]

Simpson's three correlated confounders:

1. **Unequal comparison groups.** Different counterfactuals produce different d's.
2. **Range restriction.** A homogeneous sample (a single school, a single ability band) inflates d by deflating the denominator.
3. **Measurement design.** Researcher-developed measures yield much larger d's than independent standardized measures.

The most important consequence: averaging d's from studies with different design features compounds the design noise. You cannot interpret an averaged d the way you would interpret a single-study d. Simpson's punch line: "while calculating an effect size may be simple enough for a first course in statistics, there are considerable subtleties in understanding it sufficiently well to ensure that the processes of combining effect sizes in meta-analyses allow valid conclusions to be drawn."[22]

Simpson's broader frame, often quoted: Hattie's meta-meta-analysis is "like blending apples, oranges, and maybe a few wrenches." [23]

### 5.4 The Bergeron & Rivard critique — mathematical impossibility

Bergeron and Rivard (2017, McGill Journal of Education), writing as statisticians, identified that Hattie's *Visible Learning* contains mathematically impossible Common Language Effect (CLE) probability values — values reported as ranging from −49% to 219%. Probabilities cannot be negative; probabilities cannot exceed 100%.[24] Their conclusion was unsparing: "John Hattie and his team have neither the knowledge nor the competencies required to conduct valid statistical analyses." They characterized the methodology as **pseudoscience.** The errors went undetected in the educational community for years until two statisticians outside the field surfaced them.

The Bergeron & Rivard critique is the one Hattie's defenders have the most difficulty addressing on the merits. Hattie has acknowledged some CLE calculation errors and corrected them in subsequent editions. He has not accepted the broader pseudoscience characterization.

### 5.5 What survives the critiques

Read carefully, the four critiques say four different things, not one:

- **Slavin:** the input studies are too weak; cleaner inputs would yield smaller effects.
- **Wecker:** the same studies are being counted multiple times; the aggregates are inflated by overlap.
- **Simpson:** d's are design-dependent; averaging them across designs is interpretively invalid.
- **Bergeron & Rivard:** some of the specific calculations are mathematically wrong.

Together they constitute a serious indictment of *Visible Learning* as a precise quantitative instrument. They do not constitute an indictment of the underlying ranking *direction*. The teacher-mediated influences (collective efficacy, relationships, feedback, expectations) consistently sit at the top of the ranking even after the critics' corrections; the structural and technology influences consistently sit lower. The ranking survives the critiques even though the magnitudes do not.

This is what the chapter must say plainly: **the precise numbers are not the message. The ranking direction is the message. The most-criticized number in education research and the cleaner causal estimates from independent literatures both point at the same variable.** That variable is the teacher.

## 6. The independent causal literature — value-added and RAND Europe

The book's argument does not need Hattie to stand alone. There is a second, methodologically cleaner literature pointing at the same place.

### 6.1 The value-added literature

Rivkin, Hanushek & Kain (2005, *Econometrica*) is the foundational paper. Using Texas panel data, they estimated a semiparametric lower bound on the variance of teacher quality and found that **variations in teacher quality account for at least 7.5% of the total variation in student achievement** — a lower bound that almost certainly understates the true effect. Their headline finding: *the effect of a costly ten-student class-size reduction is smaller than the benefit of moving one standard deviation up the teacher-quality distribution.*[25]

Chetty, Friedman & Rockoff (2014) used quasi-experimental teacher transitions and 2.5 million students linked to tax records to estimate that **a one-SD improvement in teacher value-added raises end-of-grade test scores by about 0.10 SD in English and 0.14 SD in math.** Long-run: a one-SD improvement in teacher VA in a single grade raises adult earnings by about 1% at age 28. Replacing a bottom-5% teacher with an average teacher raises the present value of students' lifetime income by **roughly $250,000 per classroom.**[26][27]

Boyd, Loeb, Grossman, Staiger and colleagues, working in New York City and elsewhere, found similar magnitudes and emphasized the variation among teachers within schools: **the gap between top-quartile and bottom-quartile elementary math teachers is roughly 0.33 SD of student achievement per year.**[28] That is a larger between-teacher gap than any platform intervention has produced in any randomized trial.

These are not Hattie numbers. They are independent, quasi-experimental, tax-record-linked, peer-reviewed, replicated. They land on the same conclusion: teacher quality is the largest moveable variable.

### 6.2 The RAND Europe January 2026 synthesis

Already used in Chapter 2. The Chapter 4 angle: RAND Europe synthesized six years of large-scale EdTech trials and found that **teacher training and support consistently made or broke** the success of EdTech interventions. Where teachers were prepared and supported, technology improved outcomes. Where they weren't, "technology was underused or used ineffectively," "impact evaporated," and the underlying approach — however promising — failed to deliver.[29]

The chapter 4 quote: "implementation fidelity emerged as a critical factor, as many EdTech interventions are only as effective as their implementation. When teachers struggled with training, resources, or integration into daily routines, impact evaporated — even when the underlying approach was promising."[29]

This is the modern, evidence-based version of the Hattie ranking. It does not depend on meta-meta-analytic conventions. It depends on actual trials of actual interventions. The conclusion is identical.

### 6.3 The What Works Clearinghouse and tutoring

IES's What Works Clearinghouse synthesizes interventions to WWC standards.[30] The tutoring literature is the cleanest example: average tutoring raises achievement by ~0.29 SD; tutoring for elementary and middle students from low-income families produces ~0.36 SD; **effects shrink sharply with scale** (0.44 SD for studies under 100 students; 0.30 SD for 100–399; 0.21 for 400–999; 0.16 for ≥1,000 students).[31] The Tutor CoPilot finding in Chapter 5 (4 percentage points overall, 9 percentage points for students of lower-rated tutors) sits inside this literature. Tutors are teachers operating one-on-one. The amplification effect is largest where the tutor's baseline practice was weakest.

## 7. The class-size paradox — the move the chapter has to make

### 7.1 The Tennessee STAR experiment

Project STAR (1985–1989) randomly assigned 11,600 Tennessee kindergarteners and their teachers to small classes (~15 students), regular classes (~22), or regular classes with a teacher aide. Students stayed in their assigned condition for four years. It is the largest randomized class-size experiment in U.S. history.[32]

Krueger's reanalysis (1999, *Quarterly Journal of Economics*) yielded effect sizes of **d ≈ 0.20 in kindergarten, 0.28 in first grade, 0.22 in second grade, 0.19 in third grade.** The kindergarten effect was 64% as large as the white-black test score gap; the third-grade effect was 82% as large. Effects were larger for minority students and for students on free lunch.[33]

### 7.2 Hanushek's reanalysis

Hanushek (1999, *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*) reviewed STAR and identified design and implementation issues — non-trivial attrition, teacher awareness of condition, contamination — that introduce uncertainty about the magnitude of the treatment effects. He argued the commonly cited results were biased upward and noted that the broader non-experimental class-size literature shows no consistent relationship between class size and achievement.[34][35]

The Hanushek-Krueger debate is the closest education economics has to a defining controversy. Both economists are still actively publishing on it. The honest reading:

- STAR shows a real, positive, durable effect of class-size reduction in the early grades.
- The magnitude is contested. d ≈ 0.20 is the most defensible point estimate.
- Effects are concentrated in the early grades and for disadvantaged subgroups.
- Most non-experimental class-size variation produces null or negligible effects.

### 7.3 Hattie's d = 0.21 — why teachers don't change methods

Hattie's d = 0.21 for class size sits squarely within the STAR-Krueger range. **The number is not in dispute.** What is in dispute is the *interpretation.*

The peer-review pushback on Hattie's class-size section: he treats the d = 0.21 as evidence that class size doesn't much matter. The underlying meta-analyses he averages mostly show "a strong connection between class reduction and student achievement" that *doesn't emerge* in Hattie's synthesis.[36]

But Hattie's interpretive move — separate from the magnitude — is the one this chapter wants. Hattie's framing: *if teachers do not change their methods when class sizes shrink, you would not expect smaller classes to produce large gains.* The Tennessee STAR teachers, working in small classes, mostly taught the way they had taught in large classes. They did not redesign instruction to exploit the small-group affordances. The structural variable changed; the practice variable did not. Hattie's literal sentence: *"If teachers were retrained to work with smaller class sizes then indeed many of these optimal strategies may take effect; but merely reducing the number of students in front of teachers appears to change little."*

The chapter's argument lands here. **Class size matters less than methods. Methods change through training.** Smaller classes without trained teachers produce d ≈ 0.20. Smaller classes with trained teachers — properly studied — would presumably produce more. The book does not need the second half of that sentence to be settled. It needs the first half to be visible: the gain from changing the *teacher's practice* is the gain that the structural change alone cannot produce.

This is also the AI+1 frame. AI in the classroom without a trained teacher is a class-size reduction without method change. The structural variable shifts. The practice variable doesn't. The gain doesn't materialize.

## 8. The SETDA evidence on what districts actually do

From Chapter 2/3 work, reframed for Chapter 4:

The SETDA November 2025 report "Improving Professional Learning Systems to Better Support Today's Educators: How Title II, Part A Offers a Model for State and Local Leadership" found that in 2022–23, **over 60% of Title II-A funds were spent on professional development, but much of it took the form of short-term workshops rather than sustained strategies. Only nine states prioritized these funds for technology training; fewer than 40% of local education agencies used them to advance technology-related professional learning.**[37]

The chapter's use: even the existing federal infrastructure designed to fund teacher PD is being used for the wrong kind of PD (short-form workshops, not the sustained, coaching-anchored work Kraft et al. show actually moves teaching). The investment that produces the high-effect interventions in the Hattie ranking — collective efficacy built through sustained collaborative work, feedback systems, deep PD — is the investment districts are not making.

## 9. What the chapter's "optimal class size" argument actually claims

The TIKTOC's framing: learning does not scale like other industries. The highest-effect interventions are relational. Those cannot scale past roughly 25–30 students per teacher regardless of how good the AI is. What AI does is free the teacher within that group of 30 to do more of the d ≈ 0.70 work she was never able to do enough of.

The defensible version of this argument:

1. The empirical ranking — even after every critique above — places teacher-mediated relational and instructional moves above any technology-only intervention.
2. Class-size reduction without method change produces ~d 0.20 (STAR-Krueger).
3. Teacher coaching producing method change produces ~d 0.18 on student achievement, ~d 0.49 on instruction (Kraft et al.).
4. Teacher value-added variation accounts for at least 7.5% of student-achievement variance (Rivkin-Hanushek-Kain); the within-school teacher gap (top quartile vs. bottom quartile) is ~0.33 SD per year (Boyd-Loeb-Grossman-Staiger; Chetty-Friedman-Rockoff).
5. Therefore: the highest-leverage investment is teacher practice change, not structural change, and not technology distribution.

What this argument cannot claim:

- It cannot claim AI cannot scale teachers beyond 30 students. The evidence on AI-assisted teaching of larger groups is too thin to support that claim. The defensible claim is narrower: *the relational and feedback components of effective teaching have not been shown to scale beyond roughly 25–30 students per teacher in the empirical literature available so far.*
- It cannot claim d = 1.57 for collective efficacy at face value. The CTE finding is real. The 1.57 specifically rests on one PhD meta-analysis, a contested correlation-to-d conversion, and a value that has subsequently moved in MetaX.
- It cannot claim the ranking is precise. The ranking is directional. Critics on the merits would mostly agree on the direction.

## 10. The chapter's central mechanism — picked

Per the workshop's four-move method (CLAUDE.md §3), the chapter needs one deep-dive mechanism unpacked honestly. The candidate is **collective teacher efficacy as a multiplier of any classroom intervention, including AI integration.**

The mechanism, traced step by step:

1. A teacher acquires a new practice (say, structured AI-assisted formative feedback).
2. In isolation, that teacher's practice moves her students some amount, call it δ.
3. When the practice spreads across a faculty — when teachers see colleagues succeeding with it, talk about it in PLCs, refine it together, hold each other to it — three things happen:
   - The practice gets debugged. Implementation errors get caught and shared.
   - Student expectations rise across the school. Students arriving in a colleague's class next year already understand the routine.
   - Teacher belief that the practice works compounds. Bandura's self-efficacy → collective efficacy mechanism.
4. The aggregate effect on a student is larger than δ because the student now encounters the practice from multiple teachers across multiple years.

This is the CTE mechanism in plain language. It is *exactly* the mechanism that the RAND Europe synthesis points at when it identifies teacher training and support as the variable that makes EdTech work. It is *exactly* what the SETDA report calls for when it says short workshops are not enough; sustained, coordinated PD is. And it is *exactly* the variable that distributing tablets and AI subscriptions, by itself, leaves untouched.

This is the chapter's deep-dive: the explanation of why "collective" matters in collective teacher efficacy, why a school as a unit moves outcomes more than any individual teacher in isolation, and why the AI dividend — six weeks per year of reclaimed time — only converts into student learning when the school's faculty is coordinating its use.

## 11. The chapter's central analogy — picked

The TIKTOC offers the "tractor" analogy in Chapter 2. Chapter 4 should not duplicate. The candidate analogy for Chapter 4:

**A surgical team is not a collection of surgeons.** A hospital with one excellent surgeon and a poor anesthesiologist, poor nurses, poor pre-op protocols produces worse outcomes than a hospital with a competent surgeon embedded in a high-performing team. Surgical outcomes correlate more strongly with *team* effects than with individual surgeon effects (the variance in patient outcomes attributable to the surgical team as a unit exceeds the variance attributable to the surgeon alone). **[verify: surgical-team variance literature, possibly Ghaferi, Birkmeyer, Dimick]**.

This is collective efficacy made concrete. The school is the team. The student is the patient. The faculty's coordinated belief and practice is the surgical-team-level variable that explains outcomes better than any individual.

Where the analogy breaks down: students are not patients receiving a single procedure. A student encounters a school for 1,000+ hours per year over many years; a patient encounters a surgical team for 4 hours once. The accumulation effect over years is much larger for schools. **The analogy under-states the effect rather than over-states it.** That is the kind of named break the chapter should make visible (per CLAUDE.md §6 "Analogy").

## 12. Drafting decisions — what the chapter should not do

- **Don't lead with the headline numbers as if they were settled.** Lead with what they are evidence *of*, then unpack the contested magnitudes.
- **Don't dismiss the critiques.** Walk through Slavin, Wecker, Simpson, and Bergeron & Rivard with quotes and citations. The reader has to see that the book is willing to lose the precise number in order to hold the direction.
- **Don't claim AI is unique in this argument.** The teacher-variable case predates AI by decades. The argument lands harder *because* it doesn't depend on AI being the special instrument. AI is the latest tractor.
- **Don't over-specify the d = 1.57.** Use it. Cite it. Note that it has moved in MetaX. Note that even with the move, CTE remains a large and well-replicated finding.
- **Don't bury Hanushek.** The class-size paradox needs both the Krueger result (effect is real, ~d 0.20) and the Hanushek critique (magnitude is contested, design issues remain). Both serve the chapter's argument.
- **Don't forget the equity dimension already promised in Ch 11.** Note in passing that the Chetty et al. lifetime-earnings finding ($250K per classroom for replacing a bottom-5% teacher with an average teacher) lands disproportionately on the students who absorb the worst teachers — disproportionately Black, Hispanic, low-income.
