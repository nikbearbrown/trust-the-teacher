## Section 1 — The concept being specified

The chapter argues that the phone-ban debate has presented exactly two governance models — total prohibition and total permissiveness — and that the right model is a third thing the debate has not named clearly: **professional discretion within a trained framework.** Before this argument can do any work, the concept has to be forced specific. "Discretion" is one of those words wearing several jobs.

**Discretion in three different senses, all of them in circulation.**

1. *Lipsky's street-level bureaucrat discretion* — the on-the-ground judgment a public-service worker has to exercise because the policy as written cannot anticipate every case. Police officers deciding whether to issue a citation. Social workers deciding what counts as an emergency. Teachers deciding which student needs the next minute of attention. Discretion in this sense is descriptive and unavoidable — it exists whether or not the system authorizes it.[^lipsky]
2. *Professional discretion as it exists in licensed professions* — judgment authorized by training, accredited by an external body, and revisable through continuing competence. A physician decides which treatment to prescribe inside a scope of practice defined by her training and her license. The discretion is constrained by what she has been trained to know. This is the form of discretion the chapter is arguing for.
3. *Administrative discretion as schools currently use it* — the principal exempts a teacher's class from the rule, or the AUP says "subject to teacher approval." This is discretion in name only — it confers permission without the training that makes the permission meaningful.

The chapter wants sense #2 — the professional kind — and is using "Phone Fridays" as a concrete structural example of what it looks like when a school designs around it. The current phone-ban debate is operating with sense #3 (administrative permission, no training) or denying discretion entirely.

**The vague terms that need specification before the chapter can run.**

- *Total prohibition* — what people commonly call a "phone ban." Specifically: bell-to-bell, with administrative exceptions for medical or IEP accommodation. The current default in most state-level laws passed 2023–2025.
- *Total permissiveness* — phones permitted unless explicitly disallowed by a teacher in a given moment. The default in most US schools before 2022 and still the default in many districts that have not passed local restriction policies.
- *Professional discretion within a trained framework* — a third model in which devices are presumptively absent (locker, pouch, drawer) and the teacher makes contextual decisions about when devices come out, what they are used for, and how the use is structured, *because the teacher has been trained to make those decisions and the institution has built infrastructure to support them.*
- *Phone Fridays* — one structural instantiation of the third model. Not a name with established literature behind it; the term is Brown's framing for a pattern that exists in many districts under various names ("Tech Tuesday," "Digital Day," "BYOD Friday," "1:1 day"). The chapter should be honest that the term is doing prescriptive work — naming a design pattern, not reporting on a widely-adopted branded program.
- *Within-30* — Brown's shorthand from earlier chapters for "what one trained teacher can do for one class of roughly thirty students." The number is not arbitrary — it comes from the optimal-class-size argument in Chapter 4. *Within-30* names the unit at which the chapter is arguing pedagogy lives.
- *Tier 1 cognitive work / Tier 4 cognitive work* — Brown's Irreducibly Human taxonomy. Tier 1 = pattern recognition, recall, content delivery, practice generation, basic feedback (the work AI does well). Tier 4 = supervisory capacities — problem formulation, plausibility auditing, interpretive judgment, tool orchestration, executive integration (the work AI does not do).[^irreducible] The argument is not that AI handles "low-level" work — that condescends to the work and misstates the taxonomy. The argument is that the work AI handles is structurally different from the work the teacher does, and the teacher's time should be reorganized around that difference.

The chapter cannot use these terms without doing the specification move first. If a reader walks away thinking "discretion = permission" or "Tier 1 = boring," the argument has not landed.

## Section 2 — The "two failed models" claim, and what the evidence actually shows

The chapter opens on a binary the public debate has accepted: ban the phone, or trust the teacher to handle it informally. Both have been tried at scale. Both have failed in characteristic ways. The chapter has to support this with specifics, not assertion.

### 2.1 The failure of total prohibition (the New York 2015 lesson)

New York City's first phone ban, in effect from 2006 until Mayor Bill de Blasio rescinded it in March 2015, is the cleanest historical case study of total prohibition. The ban was bell-to-bell, district-wide, no educational exemption, enforced by school safety agents. The reasons for rescission, in the city's own statement: enforcement was inequitable (security-screening schools, disproportionately serving Black and Latino students, enforced the ban; schools without metal detectors didn't), the device-storage industry that grew up around it (paid lockers outside schools) effectively transferred a tax onto low-income families, and parents wanted communication access with their children.[^nyc2015] The ban was not wrong about the harm — every research review of the intervening decade found that smartphones in classrooms degrade attention and learning. The ban was wrong about the implementation.

What 2015 NYC demonstrated is that *prohibition without infrastructure* gets unwound by its own enforcement asymmetries. The new generation of state bans (2023–2025, covered in Chapter 1) are partial corrections — most now include educational exemptions and most leave storage to schools rather than to private lockers outside the door. But the central problem persists: a rule passed at the state level is being implemented in 13,000 districts, and the implementation falls hardest on the schools with the least administrative capacity to do it well. (See Chapter 1, Figlio & Özek 2025; Chapter 3, the educational exemption analysis.)

This is the chapter's first failure: total prohibition is implementable as a statute and not as a system. The statute lands evenly. The system that has to follow does not.

### 2.2 The failure of total permissiveness (the AAP 5 Cs problem)

The opposite model — phones permitted, teachers manage it case by case in the moment — is what most US schools were running between roughly 2010 and 2022. The evidence on this period is well-documented and forms the prosecution case in Chapter 1: declining attention spans, rising classroom disruption, displaced sleep and homework time, the documented downstream mental-health effects (Twenge, Haidt, the Common Sense Media surveys). The 2020–2022 surveys of teachers showed the model collapsing — teachers reported devoting 20–30% of class time to phone-related redirection, and most reported that they had no institutional support for enforcement decisions.[^commonsense]

The American Academy of Pediatrics' Media and Children framework — the "5 Cs" (Child, Content, Calm, Crowding-out, Communication) — was designed as a pediatrician-counseling-a-family framework. It is excellent for that purpose. Applied as a real-time classroom-management framework for thirty students by one teacher with no training in how to operationalize it, it is unworkable.[^aap5cs] The 5 Cs are not wrong. They are the wrong abstraction layer.

What total permissiveness demonstrated is that *discretion without training is just inconsistency.* The decision-load on the teacher is too high, the institutional backup is too thin, and the result over time is that classroom norms erode unevenly across teachers and classrooms in the same building. Students learn quickly which teacher will enforce and which won't.

### 2.3 The third model — professional discretion within a trained framework

The chapter's contribution is to name a third model that has been emerging in scattered practice but not consolidated as a category in the policy debate. Components:

- *Default-off, designated-on.* Devices are presumed absent (in lockers, pouches, drawers, or simply turned off in bags). Use is exception, not default. This handles the attention-protection problem the ban was right about.
- *Designated structured-technology time.* A scheduled block — a class period, a day a week, a thematic unit — when devices are deliberately part of the lesson. This handles the equity problem (low-income students need supervised access to the tools, see Chapter 11) and the training problem (you cannot teach digital literacy without a context in which to teach it).
- *Teacher discretion over the contextual decisions.* On the designated technology block, the teacher decides what the device is being used for, in what configuration, with what scaffolding, and which students need the alternative track. This is the discretion the educational exemption in every phone-ban law is trying to give and failing to support.
- *Trained discretion.* The teacher has been trained to make this decision — has working knowledge of what genuine AI-assisted learning looks like (Chapter 7's GPT Tutor vs. GPT Base distinction), what cognitive offloading looks like, and what kinds of tasks AI handles well vs. badly. This is the AI+1 training the book is arguing for as a system-level investment (Chapter 8).
- *Institutional backup.* Administrative support for teachers' enforcement and integration decisions. AUPs written to define the educational exemption with enough specificity to be teachable, not just enough generality to be defensible.

This is not a new idea. It is the structure that exists in every other licensed profession. A physician practices inside a hospital's protocols *and* within her own clinical judgment. The protocol exists to handle the modal case; the judgment exists for the cases the protocol cannot anticipate. The phone-ban debate has tried to do everything with the protocol and nothing with the judgment, or everything with the judgment and nothing with the protocol. Neither half works alone.

## Section 3 — Named programs and case studies (what exists in practice)

The TIKTOC asked for "at least 2 named programs with documented design" of designated structured technology time. The honest answer from the research: the **named** programs called "Phone Fridays" or "Tech Tuesdays" with widely-documented designs do not yet exist as a recognized policy category in the literature. What does exist is a pattern — designated 1:1 days, BYOD days, structured technology blocks — implemented in scattered districts. The chapter should be honest about this and present the pattern with the examples that do exist, not invent the literature it would prefer.

### 3.1 The Colorado picture — HB 25-1135 and the discretion-built-into-statute approach

Colorado House Bill 25-1135 (passed 2025, effective July 1, 2026) requires every public school in the state — local board, district charter, institute charter, and the Colorado School for the Deaf and the Blind — to adopt, implement, and publish a policy on student "communication device" possession and use during the school day.[^cohb1135] The statute does not impose a uniform ban. It mandates that every school *have* a policy and make it public. The discretion is delegated to the local level.

The Colorado approach is interesting for the chapter precisely because it does not legislate the answer. It legislates the obligation to specify. This is a closer match to the professional-discretion model than the bell-to-bell statutes that dominate the press cycle. Whether the local policies that emerge from this requirement are actually trained-discretion policies or just rebranded total-prohibition policies is an open empirical question — the implementation window only opens in July 2026.

The middle-school example used in the chapter opening should be presented as **illustrative** rather than as a verified named program. *"A middle school in Colorado"* is fine if the chapter is making the design point in the abstract; *"X Middle School in Y district has run Phone Fridays since 20XX"* is a claim that requires direct verification with the school before it can be made. **[verify]** required before any specific district is named.

### 3.2 Stillwater Area Public Schools (Minnesota, ISD 834) — phone-free with principal-level discretion

Stillwater's Policy 524.5, "Personal Electronic Communication Devices," took effect for the 2025–2026 school year in response to Minnesota's 2024 statewide statute requiring all districts to adopt cellphone policies by March 15, 2025.[^stillwater] The Stillwater model is *not* a trained-teacher-discretion model — it is closer to total prohibition with principal-level administrative exceptions. Specifics worth using:

- Students preK–12 are prohibited from using personal electronic communication devices on school premises from first bell to last, including instructional time, lunch, recess, and school-sponsored events.
- Devices must be kept in designated areas and turned off.
- Exceptions are written for: medical use, IEP-accommodation use, and "other exceptions at the discretion of the school principal."
- High school students hand devices to their last-period teacher; teachers maintain cellphone lockboxes.

Stillwater is useful in the chapter as a *contrast case*. It is a well-implemented version of the total-prohibition model. The exception language ("at the discretion of the school principal") is the place where the model concedes that judgment is unavoidable — but it routes the judgment to the principal rather than to the teacher, and provides no training to make that judgment systematically rather than ad-hoc. This is the institutional version of the same problem the educational exemption has at the state level.

### 3.3 Chatham County Schools (North Carolina) — the Yondr-pilot evidence

Chatham County Schools, NC, ran a Yondr-pouch pilot in six schools during the 2024–25 school year and presented findings to the governor's advisory council in November 2025.[^chatham] The pilot is useful for the chapter because the survey data is honest about implementation friction:

- Teachers and administrators broadly supportive.
- Parents mixed.
- Students least supportive — only 77 of 649 student responses "strongly agreed" that Yondr pouches had improved classroom engagement.
- Notable equity-relevant finding: the pilot was implemented at six schools simultaneously, and outcomes varied by school in ways the district attributed to implementation quality rather than to student demographics.

The Chatham case suggests that even with a well-designed prohibition mechanism, the success rests on implementation infrastructure that the mechanism alone does not provide. This is the chapter's argument: a pouch is not a system; a system requires a teacher who has been trained to do the work the pouch made possible.

### 3.4 LAUSD — the $7M Yondr failure as the cautionary case

Los Angeles Unified School District spent approximately $7 million on Yondr pouches and encountered immediate workarounds — students broke pouches, brought decoy phones, used the pouches as a compliance theater while using devices anyway.[^lausd] The LAUSD experience is widely cited in the trade press and is worth one paragraph in the chapter as the cautionary case: technology-by-itself does not solve a behavioral problem. The pouch is the same shape of intervention as the ban — a structural removal that bets on the structure replacing the judgment. When students discover the structure can be defeated, the judgment has not been built underneath it. The chapter should be precise: this is not an argument against pouches; it is an argument that pouches without a parallel investment in teacher capacity to design what happens during pouch-locked time will not produce durable improvement.

### 3.5 San Mateo High School — the longer time horizon

San Mateo High School (California) implemented bell-to-bell phone-free policies via Yondr in 2019, making it one of the earliest large-scale US implementations. A 2024 study commissioned by Yondr reported a 15 percentage-point increase in the likelihood of students receiving a passing grade after pouch implementation.[^sanmateo] **[verify]** — the study was commissioned by the vendor; the chapter should cite it with the conflict-of-interest disclosure visible, not bury it. Vendor-commissioned research is not automatically wrong, but it carries the same caveat the pharmaceutical industry's company-funded efficacy studies carry: the chapter has to flag the funding source.

### 3.6 The honest synthesis of the named-programs question

The chapter should not pretend that "Phone Fridays" is a verified branded program that fifty districts run. The chapter's contribution is to *name* a design pattern that exists in fragmentary practice — the BYOD day, the 1:1 day, the structured-technology block, the teacher-discretion exemption inside a default-off policy — and to argue that this pattern is the third model the policy debate has not articulated. The chapter is doing prescriptive design work as much as descriptive case-study work. The framing should make this honest.

## Section 4 — The professional-discretion literature in K-12 governance

The chapter wants to argue that professional discretion is a defensible governance model for technology decisions in classrooms. This is a substantive normative claim, not a self-evident truth. The K-12 literature has well-developed arguments on both sides.

### 4.1 Linda Darling-Hammond — the case for teacher professionalism

Darling-Hammond's work, spanning four decades, makes the structural case that teaching should be organized as a profession in the strong sense — with specialized training, performance standards developed by the profession, professional ethics, and meaningful autonomy within those standards. The 1996 National Commission on Teaching and America's Future report *What Matters Most: Teaching for America's Future*, which she directed, framed the argument and was named one of the decade's most influential education reports.[^ndcommission]

The core Darling-Hammond claim relevant to Chapter 9: *teacher quality is the most important school-based variable in student outcomes, and teacher quality is itself the product of professional preparation and institutional conditions that support continuing growth.* The conclusion: weakening teacher autonomy and routinizing the work both lower the quality of the teaching and accelerate teacher attrition. The implication for Chapter 9: a phone-ban statute that strips teachers of technology-decision authority is doing the opposite of what the research on teacher quality suggests.[^dhprof]

The more recent Learning Policy Institute work on effective teacher professional development (Darling-Hammond, Hyler, Gardner 2017) is what the chapter needs for the training-claim side of the discretion argument.[^dh2017] The features of effective professional development — content focus, active learning, collaboration, expert support, sustained duration, coaching, models, feedback, reflection — are exactly the features that AI+1 teacher training would need. Discretion-with-training is the model the PD literature supports; discretion-without-training is not, and prohibition-without-discretion is also not.

### 4.2 Susan Moore Johnson — schools as the organizational variable

Johnson's Project on the Next Generation of Teachers (Harvard Graduate School of Education, since 1998) has produced the most influential US body of work on the organizational conditions that make teaching sustainable and effective.[^jngt] *Where Teachers Thrive* (2019, Harvard Education Press) is the book-length synthesis — 257 interviews across 14 high-poverty Massachusetts schools.[^wherethrive]

The Johnson finding directly relevant to Chapter 9: a lot of what determines whether a teacher does good work depends on how the school is *organized* around her. Hiring as an interactive process; team-based curriculum planning; informative feedback on instruction; ongoing professional learning; principals who treat teachers as partners in school improvement — when these systems are in place, teachers can exercise discretion well. When they are absent, the same teacher cannot.

Johnson explicitly invokes the street-level bureaucrat framing — teachers are functionally "frontline bureaucrats" whose discretion is the policy as it lands. Her contribution is to show that discretion *quality* is school-level, not individual-level. A teacher in a well-organized school exercises better discretion than the same teacher in a poorly-organized school. This is the Chapter 9 mechanism: discretion is not an individual trait but a system output.

### 4.3 Andy Hargreaves — collaborative professionalism

Hargreaves and O'Connor's *Collaborative Professionalism: When Teaching Together Means Learning for All* (Corwin, 2018) develops the framework the chapter needs for the third-way governance claim.[^hargreaves] The ten tenets include "collective autonomy" — teachers having less independence from each other and more independence from top-down bureaucratic authority. The book's five country cases (China, Norway, Colombia, Ontario, US) demonstrate that the highest-performing systems all combine teacher autonomy with collaborative accountability *within* the teaching profession, rather than vertical accountability from external authorities.

The Hargreaves move that matters for Chapter 9: the binary in the phone-ban debate ("ban with no discretion" vs. "no ban with unlimited individual discretion") is a false binary because it misses the third axis of collaborative discretion — teams of teachers in the same school making coherent decisions together, with norms developed inside the profession rather than imposed from outside. The Phone Fridays design pattern, properly implemented, is a collaborative-professionalism artifact: it works because the building's teachers have collectively decided what the structured time will be for and how it will be supported, not because each teacher independently improvises.

### 4.4 Lipsky and the street-level bureaucrat tradition — what discretion is *for*

Michael Lipsky's *Street-Level Bureaucracy: Dilemmas of the Individual in Public Service* (1980; 30th-anniversary edition 2010) is the foundational text for understanding why discretion in public-service professions is unavoidable rather than just permitted.[^lipsky] The argument: street-level workers — police, social workers, public defenders, teachers — interact with the public under conditions that make rule-based decision-making impossible. They face high information demands, limited resources, contested goals, and standardized procedures that cannot anticipate the cases they actually meet. They exercise discretion not as a privilege but as a structural necessity.

Two corollaries that matter for Chapter 9:

1. *Discretion will be exercised whether or not it is authorized.* A statute that strips teachers of technology-decision authority does not eliminate the discretion; it just makes the discretion unsanctioned and inconsistent. Teachers will still decide which student gets the device for which task; they will just decide it without institutional backing.
2. *Discretion quality depends on training and support.* Unsupported discretion produces what Lipsky calls "people processing" — coping strategies that prioritize the worker's survival over the client's outcome. Supported discretion produces professional judgment. The difference is institutional.

The recent revival of this literature in the context of generative AI (Frontiers in Education 2025) extends the framework explicitly to faculty and teacher discretionary decisions about AI tools — *what counts as legitimate use of AI in an assignment* is the same kind of street-level decision that Lipsky's framework is built to analyze.[^frontiers] This is exactly the territory the chapter is operating in.

### 4.5 The counter-case — discretion as the problem, not the solution

The chapter has to engage the counter-evidence honestly. The strongest critique of professional discretion in teaching comes from the standards-and-accountability literature: when teachers exercise discretion idiosyncratically, the result is wildly different educational experiences for similar students in the same building, often correlated with student demographics in unjust ways. The teacher who decides that "this group can't handle the AI exemption" or "this group is ready for it" is making a judgment that has been shown empirically to track bias.

The street-level bureaucrat scholarship has documented this directly. The 2007 Ian Taylor review of teacher discretion in the post-NCLB era argued that the high regulation and accountability infrastructure of the 1990s–2000s eroded teacher discretion to the point that Lipsky's framework barely applied anymore — and that this erosion was a *response to* documented discretion failures, not just bureaucratic overreach.[^taylor] The chapter cannot pretend professional discretion has a clean record.

The honest move in the chapter: professional discretion is the right model *when paired with the training and accountability infrastructure that makes its quality visible.* Discretion without training is what the prohibition advocates rightly fear. The chapter's argument has to be that the alternative to bad discretion is not no discretion; it is trained, supported, collectively-accountable discretion.

## Section 5 — The within-30 design principles (the chapter's pedagogical claim)

This is the chapter's prescriptive section. The TIKTOC lists five design principles for what a trained teacher does differently with AI in a class of roughly thirty students. Each principle needs an evidence base and a specific mechanism the chapter can show.

### 5.1 Real-time peer grouping suggestions

The pedagogical claim: a trained teacher with AI tools can group students based on *what they are actually producing in the current moment* rather than on prior assessments or static "ability tracks."

The evidence base: Carol Ann Tomlinson's three-decade body of work on differentiated instruction documents that flexible grouping — groups that re-form every 2–3 weeks based on formative data, not fixed tracks — is more effective than static grouping for diverse learners.[^tomlinson] The Tomlinson framework explicitly includes like-readiness groups, mixed-readiness groups, interest groups, learning-profile groups, and whole-class configurations, with the teacher deciding which configuration the current lesson needs based on continuous assessment.

What AI changes: the time cost of "continuous assessment" drops by an order of magnitude. A teacher who used to look at exit-ticket responses overnight and regroup students for the next day can now look at student work in progress (with AI parsing the pattern of errors across thirty submissions) and regroup the class for the next ten minutes of the same lesson. The granularity of differentiation moves from week-to-week to within-lesson.

Honest limit: the Tomlinson literature is correlational and quasi-experimental — there is not yet a randomized controlled trial of *AI-enabled real-time peer grouping vs. teacher-managed peer grouping without AI*. The chapter should make the inferential claim ("AI compresses the time cost of differentiation, which extends the Tomlinson framework's reach") without claiming a causally identified effect we do not yet have.

### 5.2 Differentiated materials generated in minutes rather than hours

The pedagogical claim: AI lets a trained teacher produce three reading-level versions of the same primary source, or three problem-set difficulty levels, in eight minutes — a job that used to take three hours and therefore was never done weekly.

The evidence base: the EEF ChatGPT RCT (covered in Chapter 6) — 259 teachers across 68 schools, lesson preparation time reduced by 31%, output quality maintained by blind review.[^eef] The Gallup-Walton survey — 5.9 hours per week reclaimed by teachers using AI weekly, with 60% reporting more time for "improving teaching and learning" and 57% reporting better insights about student learning data.[^gallup]

What this enables in the within-30 unit: the differentiation work that Tomlinson's framework prescribes but most teachers cannot find time to do is suddenly tractable. The teacher who is differentiating Tuesday's history reading at three levels Monday night used to spend two hours on it and skipped the differentiation more often than not. With AI, the same task is twelve minutes, and the teacher does it for every lesson.

Honest limit: the time saved on materials production has to be reinvested in something. The Gallup self-report says teachers report reinvesting it in feedback and individualization, but no direct causal study yet shows that the reinvested time produces measurably better student outcomes at scale (see Ch. 6 §5 on the Demszky mechanism evidence and §8 on what is not yet established).

### 5.3 Formative questions specific to what this student actually wrote

The pedagogical claim: the trained teacher with AI can read student work in real time and generate a probing question calibrated to what the student is actually thinking, not a generic prompt that ignores the specifics of the student's reasoning.

The evidence base: the Tutor CoPilot study (Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, Demszky 2024) is the strongest direct evidence in the literature.[^tutorcopilot] The headline result the chapter has already discussed in Chapter 5: 4 percentage point mastery improvement overall, 9 percentage points for students of lower-rated tutors. The mechanism finding the chapter needs to re-pull here: analysis of over 350,000 tutor-student message exchanges showed Tutor CoPilot *increased the use of probing questions and reduced generic praise.* Tutors with the AI in their corner asked more cognitively-demanding questions and gave fewer direct answers.

This is the load-bearing finding for Chapter 9's third principle. The AI is not generating the formative question and pushing it to the student. The AI is changing what the *human* asks. The student's experience is one of better human attention. The mechanism by which the better attention happens is AI-augmented pedagogical practice in the moment.

The chapter should be explicit about what this is and is not:

- It is direct empirical evidence that AI-augmented tutoring produces measurably better human pedagogy in the moment, not just measurably better student outcomes downstream.
- It is *not* yet evidence that the same effect would generalize from one-on-one chat-based tutoring to classroom teaching. The mechanism is plausible; the studies in classroom settings are still in progress.

### 5.4 Self-regulation scaffolding calibrated to individual student needs

The pedagogical claim: a trained teacher can use AI to give different students different versions of metacognitive scaffolding — explicit prompts to plan, monitor, and reflect — calibrated to what each student needs.

The evidence base: the M-Powering Teachers brick-and-mortar RCT (Demszky et al. 2024, *Computers & Education*) is the closest empirical match.[^mpowering] 224 mathematics and science teachers in Utah K-12 classrooms; automated email feedback on teacher questioning patterns, particularly use of "focusing questions" (questions that press for student reasoning rather than recall). Teachers opened feedback emails 53–65% of the time. Result: 20% increase in use of focusing questions (p < 0.01), with no significant impact on other measured discourse features. By week five, treatment teachers were asking roughly 10 additional focusing questions per hour — a 59% increase over control.

The mechanism extension to Chapter 9: if AI feedback on the teacher's own questioning practice measurably moves that practice, AI feedback on the *student's* self-regulation could plausibly move that as well — but the chapter should not claim what the literature has not yet shown. The conservative move: the M-Powering evidence demonstrates that AI feedback can change teacher behavior at the discourse level in K-12 classrooms; the further claim about scaffolded self-regulation for students is an extension the chapter is making by analogy, not a finding it can cite directly.

The PERTS Elevate program (Stanford, since 2017) provides the closest large-scale evidence on student-survey-feedback-loop interventions.[^perts] During 2023–24, Elevate was implemented by 2,300 educators across 63,000 students in 271 middle and high schools across 94 districts. Reported outcomes: more than 75% of teachers improved classroom learning conditions over multiple cycles; students were 86% more likely to report a higher sense of belonging when conditions improved; in a 2022 cohort, students were 2x more likely to earn a B or better in math when they rated learning conditions most positively. The Elevate evidence is *not* causally identified at the same level as the Tutor CoPilot RCT, but it documents a large-scale implementation in which structured feedback loops measurably moved classroom conditions — which is the precondition for the self-regulation scaffolding the chapter is arguing for.

### 5.5 AI handling Tier 1 cognitive work; teacher handling Tier 4 cognitive work

This is the principle that the chapter needs to handle most carefully because it is also the one that risks sounding condescending if specified badly. The mechanism is the division-of-labor argument the book has been building since Chapter 5.

*What Tier 1 cognitive work is:* the work AI handles well — pattern recognition, recall, factual content delivery, basic problem-set generation, first-pass feedback on a routine problem set, summarization of a known source, translation, generating practice items at a specified difficulty. The work is structurally cognitive — it requires thinking — but it requires the kind of thinking machines do at scale.

*What Tier 4 cognitive work is:* the supervisory work AI does not do — plausibility auditing (does the AI's output actually answer the question correctly, in the context the student is working in?), interpretive judgment (what does this student's confused answer reveal about what she is actually thinking?), problem formulation (what is the right question to ask this student right now?), tool orchestration (which AI tool should be running for which task?), relational teaching (the hour-by-hour adjustment that comes from knowing this specific student).

The taxonomy is Brown's own — the Irreducibly Human seven-tier framework — and the chapter should cite it as such with the skepticism.ai URL.[^irreducible] The framework is load-bearing for several arguments in the book, and Chapter 9 is the place where it gets operationalized at the classroom level. The Tier 4 capacities are the *educational exemption* that the phone-ban laws are reaching for and have not been able to specify. The taxonomy is the framework that lets a school distinguish between AI use that develops these capacities (legitimate educational use) and AI use that substitutes for them (cognitive offloading, the thing Chapter 7 documents as harmful).

The chapter should also acknowledge the related-but-different frameworks it is not using and why:

- **Bloom's revised taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl 2001)** organizes cognitive processes from remember → understand → apply → analyze → evaluate → create. Useful for lesson-objective writing. Not designed to distinguish what AI does well from what humans do well — Bloom's was developed in the 1950s and revised before LLMs existed.
- **Webb's Depth of Knowledge (DOK) framework (1997)** organizes tasks by cognitive complexity at four levels: recall, skill/concept, strategic thinking, extended thinking.[^webb] Useful for curriculum and assessment alignment, and important for the chapter to mention because principals know it. The chapter should note that DOK Level 4 (extended thinking) overlaps substantially with Tier 4 supervisory capacities, but DOK was designed to evaluate curriculum demand, not to allocate work between humans and AI tools.
- **Hess's Cognitive Rigor Matrix (2005)** combines Bloom's taxonomy and Webb's DOK into a 6x4 grid for analyzing instructional tasks.[^hess] The most operationally useful of the older frameworks. The chapter can position the Irreducibly Human taxonomy as the AI-era extension that Hess's matrix prefigures: same shape of analytic move, updated for the question of what work AI does and does not do.

The honest move: the chapter should present the Irreducibly Human taxonomy as Brown's contribution, not as established curriculum scholarship. The framework is real and substantively well-developed; it is also Brown's own work and should be cited as such, with the prior frameworks acknowledged as the lineage.

### 5.6 The within-30 frame — why thirty matters

The chapter's design principles all operate at the unit of one teacher with roughly thirty students. The number is not arbitrary. It comes from the Hattie effect-size argument in Chapter 4: the highest-d interventions are relational (teacher-student relationships d = 0.72, feedback d = 0.73, collective efficacy d = 1.57) and these cannot scale past roughly 25–30 students per teacher regardless of AI tooling. AI does not let one teacher serve five hundred; it lets one teacher do more for the thirty she already has.

The within-30 frame is the chapter's protection against the EdTech industry's preferred reading of AI-in-education, which is that AI scales good teaching. The book's reading is that AI deepens good teaching within the optimal unit. Chapter 9 is where this becomes operational at the level of classroom design.

## Section 6 — International comparisons (what to cite, what to handle carefully)

The TIKTOC asked specifically about Finland, Japan, and Estonia. The honest summary: all three are interesting but none of them maps cleanly onto the trained-teacher-discretion model the chapter is arguing for. They are useful as comparison points that illuminate the design space, not as positive examples to import wholesale.

### 6.1 Finland — recent restriction with teacher-discretion exception built in

Finland passed legislation in 2025 (effective August 2025) restricting student smartphone use during the school day. The mechanism is interesting for the chapter because *the discretion is built into the statute itself*: students may use phones during lessons "for learning purposes or for personal healthcare reasons with permission from their teacher or principal."[^finland] The teacher has explicit statutory authority to allow educational use. Principals and teachers have explicit authority to confiscate devices that disrupt instruction. Schools determine in their codes of conduct how to handle device use at recess, lunch breaks, and storage during the day.

The contrast with most US state laws: Finland built the educational exemption into the law as a teacher-empowering provision; most US state laws either omit the exemption or render it administratively (principal-approved). Finland's model is closer to the third-way governance Chapter 9 is arguing for, embedded in a national-level statute.

The contextual caveat that has to come with this: Finland's teachers are uniformly master's-degree trained, with substantial institutional time for professional development, and the Finnish DigiErko program (covered in Chapter 8) provides ongoing in-school coaching. The discretion Finnish law confers operates on top of an infrastructure US schools mostly lack. Importing the law without the infrastructure would not produce the same result.

The recent press coverage about Finland "going back to books" — the *School Transparency* and *Hacker News* discussions of Finnish concerns about over-digitization — should be acknowledged but not overweighted. The chapter is not arguing for unlimited screen time; it is arguing for trained-teacher discretion *within* a default-off environment. Finland's policy direction is broadly consistent with that argument.

### 6.2 Japan — prohibition with very limited teacher discretion

Japan's policy direction is closer to total prohibition. Elementary and junior high schools have prohibited cellphone use under a 2009 Ministry of Education notice; junior high schools have recently been allowed conditional carry of phones for emergency communication during commutes, but in-class use remains broadly prohibited.[^japan] Cellphone use in classrooms is forbidden; phones must be turned off and stored at the door. Toyoake City in Aichi Prefecture introduced a voluntary 2-hour daily cap in October 2025, with no enforcement mechanism.

Japan's model is useful for the chapter as a *contrast case*: very high cultural-compliance norms make prohibition operationally tractable in Japan in a way it is not in the US. The US debate often imports Japanese (or East Asian) prohibition success stories without acknowledging the very different baseline of family and school norm-enforcement. A US district trying to implement Japan's policy without Japan's compliance infrastructure is running the policy in a different environment with different consequences.

### 6.3 Estonia — digital-first with the integration model

Estonia is the most interesting international case for Chapter 9 because it is the inverse of the prohibition model and has been running it longest. Estonia adopted BYOD as national policy in 2014; digital literacy is a curriculum strand from grade 1 across all subject areas; most schools employ a full-time educational technologist (a teacher with a second master's in educational technology) whose job is to support colleague integration of technology into teaching.[^estonia]

The 2025 AI Leap initiative — 20,000 high school students (grades 10–11) and 3,000 teachers given access to AI tools with training on effective use — is the closest national-level analogue to the AI+1 training the book is arguing for.[^aileap] The training-first sequencing (teachers and students receive the tools and the training together; the tools do not go in without the training) is the design principle Chapter 9 is operationalizing at the school level.

Estonia is useful for the chapter as a *positive comparison case* — the integration-with-training model exists at national scale and is producing measured results. The contextual caveats: Estonia is a country of 1.3 million people with a uniquely centralized education ministry; the implementation infrastructure is far easier to coordinate than across 13,000 US districts. The model is import-relevant for principles, not for one-to-one policy translation.

### 6.4 The international comparison framing for the chapter

The honest synthesis the chapter should make: no country has yet implemented the trained-teacher-discretion model at scale in a form that maps directly onto US K-12 conditions. Finland is the closest analogue on the statutory side; Estonia is the closest analogue on the infrastructure-and-training side; Japan is the cleanest cultural contrast that explains why US prohibition policies do not work the way Japanese ones do. The chapter is proposing a US-specific synthesis — Finland's statutory discretion plus Estonia's training infrastructure, implemented within the US's decentralized district structure.

## Section 7 — What is uncertain, contested, and load-bearing

### 7.1 The chapter's strongest claim and what supports it

Claim: a trained teacher with AI tools, exercising professional discretion within a default-off device environment and a structured-technology-time framework, will produce better student outcomes than either total prohibition or total permissiveness.

Strongest evidence:
- Tutor CoPilot RCT — AI-augmented one-on-one tutoring produces measurably better pedagogy (more probing questions, less direct-answer-giving) and better student outcomes (4 pp / 9 pp mastery improvement, larger for lower-baseline tutors).[^tutorcopilot]
- M-Powering Teachers Utah RCT — AI feedback on teacher questioning produces measurably better classroom questioning practice (20% increase in focusing questions, p < 0.01) at the K-12 classroom scale.[^mpowering]
- Gallup-Walton AI dividend — teachers using AI weekly report saving 5.9 hours/week, with majorities reporting they reinvest the time in feedback, individualization, and learning insights.[^gallup]
- EEF ChatGPT RCT — 31% reduction in lesson prep time with output quality maintained.[^eef]
- Effective PD literature (Darling-Hammond et al. 2017) — the features of effective teacher professional development match the features AI+1 training would need.[^dh2017]
- Where Teachers Thrive (Johnson 2019) — school organizational conditions determine whether teacher discretion produces good outcomes.[^wherethrive]

### 7.2 What the chapter's argument does not yet have

Honest limits the chapter must acknowledge:

1. **No direct RCT of "Phone Fridays" as a structural design.** The chapter is arguing for a design pattern that does not have a randomized evaluation behind it. The components (default-off devices; structured technology time; trained teacher discretion; institutional support) each have evidence; the assembly does not yet.
2. **The within-30 design principles have asymmetric evidence.** Real-time peer grouping (Tomlinson framework, no AI-specific RCT). Differentiated materials in minutes (EEF on prep time, plus first-principles inference about what the time enables). Formative questions specific to student writing (Tutor CoPilot, strong evidence for one-on-one chat). Self-regulation scaffolding (M-Powering for teacher behavior; analogy for student behavior). Tier 1 vs Tier 4 work allocation (Brown's own framework; theoretical lineage in Bloom/Webb/Hess but not in EdTech-RCT literature).
3. **The Brown-frameworks question.** Both the Irreducibly Human taxonomy (Tier 1/Tier 4 specifically) and the GLP/Frictional framework are Brown's own contributions, not established curriculum scholarship. The chapter should cite them as such with the skepticism.ai URL and the irreducibly.xyz pointer. Nik may want to clarify whether the chapter should treat these as "Brown's framework, presented for the first time at scale in this book" or as "Brown's framework, building on Bloom/Webb/Hess in the named lineage." Either is defensible; the chapter should make the choice explicitly. **[verify with Nik]**
4. **The named-programs gap.** "Phone Fridays" is not a verified branded program. The chapter is doing prescriptive design work. The case studies that exist (Stillwater, Chatham, San Mateo, LAUSD) are mostly prohibition-with-Yondr models, not discretion-with-training models. The chapter should be honest about this rather than borrow the names of programs that do not run the way the chapter is describing.
5. **The discretion-quality risk.** The strongest critique of teacher discretion is that it tracks teacher bias and produces inequitable outcomes for similar students. The chapter has to engage this — the Lipsky framework predicts it, the standards-and-accountability literature documents it, and the equity chapter (Chapter 11) is the place where the book makes the affirmative case for trained discretion as the way to *reduce* this risk rather than exacerbate it.

### 7.3 What would change the chapter's argument

The "What would change my mind" line:
- A well-designed RCT showing that a school implementing the third-way model (default-off + structured time + trained teacher discretion + institutional support) does *not* outperform a school implementing total prohibition on either student outcomes, teacher retention, or equity measures.
- Evidence that trained-discretion implementations track bias and produce worse equity outcomes than the rule-based prohibitions they replace.

The "Still puzzling" line:
- The honest uncertainty: the chapter is arguing that trained-teacher discretion is the correct governance model, and the evidence for the components is strong, but the integrated model has not yet been tested at scale. The chapter is doing prescriptive design as much as descriptive synthesis. That is the right move when the policy debate is bad and the alternative needs naming — but it is also the move that exposes the argument to being wrong about implementation details we cannot yet test.

## Section 9 — Notes for the drafter

- **The chapter has a strong invented framing ("Phone Fridays") that does not have established literature behind it.** This is the chapter's intellectual contribution. The book's answer — trained discretion plus institutional accountability — has to be made affirmatively, not by waving the problem away. Chapter 11 will pick up the equity thread; Chapter 9 should hand it off cleanly.
- **The closing should be short, declarative, memorable.** Probably one sentence about what the trained teacher in the room does that the legislator at the desk and the algorithm in the cloud both cannot.
