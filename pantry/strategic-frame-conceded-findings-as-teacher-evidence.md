# Strategic frame: conceded findings as evidence FOR the trained teacher

**Date:** 2026-05-16
**Author:** Nik Bear Brown
**Type:** Strategic positioning note for trust-the-teacher

---

## The move

Horvath gets three findings right. The "ban screens" crowd glob onto them. The book takes them seriously *and* turns them into the strongest case it has for trained teacher judgment.

The pattern:

> *Finding* (real) → *Blanket conclusion* (wrong) → *What it actually requires* (the trained teacher).

This is the same move the book already makes in Chapter 3 with the educational-exemption clause: a rule cannot adjust for the classroom and the learner in front of you. Only a trained human can. The book should make this move three more times — once for each of the findings below — and once *as a stated method* in the front matter so the reader sees the pattern.

## The three findings, each as a teacher-judgment argument

### 1. The PISA/TIMSS/PIRLS screen-time correlation is real

- **Finding:** Students using computers 6+ hours daily on PISA score 66 points lower. TIMSS daily-use math drops 41 points. PIRLS paper→digital drops 27.
- **The clickbait argument:** "Screens hurt kids. Ban them."
- **What the data actually show:** *Unstructured, high-dosage, recreational-grade screen use* correlates with worse outcomes. The data do not measure "screens in a classroom with a trained teacher who decides when and how." That study mostly hasn't been run. The data we have are dominated by ambient screen exposure, not pedagogical screen deployment.
- **What the finding actually requires:** Someone in the room deciding when this lesson, with this class, with this student, calls for a screen and when it does not. That someone is the teacher. A statute cannot make this call. A vendor algorithm cannot make this call. The PISA correlation is evidence *against* leaving the call to ambient exposure — which means evidence *for* placing it with the trained professional.

### 2. The handwriting/typing mode effect is neurologically grounded

- **Finding:** Handwriting produces deeper encoding than typing for note-taking and early literacy. Effect sizes around -0.20 to -0.40 favoring handwriting (Mueller & Oppenheimer 2014, Mangen & Velay 2010, more recent replications mixed but directionally consistent).
- **The clickbait argument:** "Keyboards damage children's brains. Ban devices in K-8."
- **What the data actually show:** Handwriting is better *for the specific cognitive task of encoding new material into durable memory.* It is not better for revising a draft, for accessibility for students with motor or processing disabilities, for collaborative editing, for production at length, or for any task where the goal is the artifact rather than the encoding.
- **What the finding actually requires:** A teacher who knows *this student* is still building foundational literacy and needs the pen. A teacher who knows *this student* has dysgraphia and needs the keyboard or speech-to-text. A teacher who knows *this lesson* is encoding-heavy and reaches for paper, and *this other lesson* is production-heavy and reaches for the laptop. The mode-effect literature is evidence that the choice matters. It is not evidence that the choice can be pre-made for every classroom by statute.

### 3. Reading on screen is worse than on paper

- **Finding:** Screen inferiority for expository reading: ES ≈ -0.15 overall, -0.29 for informational text (Delgado et al. 2018 meta-analysis of 38,000+ readers).
- **The clickbait argument:** "Print is sacred. Screens destroy reading."
- **What the data actually show:** The screen-inferiority effect is largest for *long informational text under time pressure*. It shrinks or disappears for short text, for fiction, for self-paced reading, and for screens designed for reading (e-ink, lower-distraction interfaces). The effect is consistent and real, but it is not a property of screens — it is a property of how attention is captured when text appears on a device that also delivers everything else.
- **What the finding actually requires:** A teacher who decides that today's primary-source analysis goes on paper because it's long, dense, and under time pressure — and that tomorrow's vocabulary practice goes on the tablet because it's short, paced, and benefits from instant feedback. The same teacher who, when using AI to generate three differentiated versions of a Civil War primary source, *prints them* before handing them out. The dividend the AI delivers (six weeks per year of reclaimed time) does not require that students read AI output on a screen. The teacher decides delivery format the same way she decides everything else.

## Why this is the right frame for the whole book

The "blind screen time is bad" argument is bait. It works on people who want a rule that absolves them of judgment. It loses to anyone who notices that the same screen, in two different classrooms, produces opposite outcomes — and that the difference is the teacher.

Every empirical finding the ban-everything camp brandishes has this same structure:

- The finding describes what happens when the technology is deployed *without* skilled human judgment in the loop.
- The blanket conclusion ("ban it") presumes there is no skilled human in the loop, or that the skilled human can be replaced by a rule.
- The actual policy implication is the opposite: the finding is evidence that skilled human judgment is the variable, which is exactly what the book argues we have systematically failed to develop, train, and trust.

This is recursive. It is the book's central argument applied to the data the book's central argument has to engage with.

**The line:** *Every finding that says "screens hurt learners on average" is a finding about what happens when nobody is deciding. The book is about who should be deciding.*

## Where this lands in the chapters

- **Front matter / Preface:** State the method explicitly. The book will repeatedly meet the strongest empirical case against EdTech, accept it, and show why it argues for trained teachers rather than against tools.
- **Ch 1 (The Ban):** Already does this for smartphones. Make the pattern visible — "the ban is right because the call cannot be made without judgment, and we have not built the judgment."
- **Ch 2 (The App):** Use the PISA/TIMSS finding here. Accept Horvath's correlation. Note what it cannot tell us. Turn it into the case for teacher-mediated deployment.
- **Ch 4 (The Teacher Variable):** This is the home chapter for the move. The mode-effect data and the screen-inferiority data both belong here — both are evidence that what the teacher decides is the high-effect-size variable.
- **Ch 9 (Phone Fridays):** This is where the move becomes concrete. "Adjusted for classroom and learner" is the operating principle. The chapter shows what that adjustment looks like in practice.
- **Ch 12 (Capstone):** The closing has to land here. The data say tools without judgment fail. The book says we should invest in the judgment.
