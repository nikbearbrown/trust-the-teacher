# Chapter 5 — Emotion and Affect

Here is the experiment. A bumblebee in a laboratory in London has spent several days learning two associations. One color of plastic cylinder contains a 40 percent sucrose solution — sweet, rewarding. A different color contains quinine — bitter, aversive. By the time the experiment begins the bee approaches one reliably and avoids the other.

Then, before the test, the experimenter does something the bee did not expect. A single bead of 60 percent sucrose — sweeter than anything the bee encountered during training — appears on the staging area. The bee drinks it. There was no warning. No cue predicted it. It was, in the precise sense, a pleasant surprise.

A few seconds later the bee is released into a test arena containing cylinders of a color it has never seen before — neither the trained-positive color nor the trained-negative one, but an intermediate. Ambiguous. Could be food. Could be the bitter thing. Could be nothing.

The bee that just had a sucrose surprise reaches the ambiguous cylinder noticeably faster than control bees that received only water. It hesitates less along the way. A pleasant surprise has biased its interpretation of a stimulus it has never seen.

Now here is the part that matters. Another group of bees received an injection of fluphenazine — a drug that blocks dopamine from binding its receptors — before the surprise sucrose. The fluphenazine did not stop them from drinking the sucrose. It did not slow their legs. But it completely abolished the bias. Bees who received sucrose-plus-fluphenazine behaved exactly like bees who received only water. The sensory event was intact; the affective consequence was not.

In a human, that pattern has a name. The temporary lightness that follows good news, the way the world looks slightly more benign after something unexpectedly pleasant — that is a mood shift. In a bumblebee with a brain the size of a sesame seed, we have to decide what to call it. And that decision turns out to be more interesting than it first appears, because it forces us to be precise about what the word *emotion* actually means.

---

The word is doing three different jobs in ordinary usage, and pulling them apart is the only way to make progress.

The first job is emotion as feeling — the inner quality of the experience, the what-it-is-like. This is the one you have direct access to when you are frightened or delighted. It is also, as the philosopher Thomas Nagel pointed out decades ago, accessible only from the inside. There is something it is like to be a bat, if bats have subjective experience. No amount of recording from bat neurons tells you whether that condition is met. Call this the phenomenal sense of emotion — Definition Y.

The second job is emotion as behavior — the flinch, the freeze, the vocalizations, the facial configurations. Easy to record. But behavior is a sign of an underlying state, not the state itself. A skilled actor displays grief without grieving. A reflex withdrawal looks like fear in a body with no fear in it.

The third job is emotion as functional state — a description of what a nervous system is doing that makes no claim about phenomenology. Defined by operational properties: how long it lasts, what it biases, what causes it, what stops it. Call this Definition X. This is the one I can actually investigate in a bee.

Most arguments about animal emotion are really arguments between people who are using two different definitions and not noticing. Someone says "bees don't have emotions" and means that bees don't have feelings. Someone else says "bees do have emotions" and means that bees have functional states with the right properties. Both claims can be true simultaneously. What the bee experiment actually shows — and what it doesn't show — depends entirely on which question you are asking.

This chapter commits to Definition X. Not because Definition Y is unimportant — it is the question almost everyone actually cares about — but because Definition X is the one where evidence can decide things. We work with the version we can test, and we say carefully what we have and have not shown.

---

In 2014, David Anderson and Ralph Adolphs proposed five criteria for what a nervous-system state must satisfy before we are entitled to call it emotional in the functional sense. Each is independently testable.

Persistence: the state outlasts the stimulus that triggered it. A purely reflexive response ends when the stimulus ends. A functional emotional state continues.

Scalability: the intensity of the state scales with the intensity of the trigger. Small threat, mild state. Large threat, strong state.

Valence: the state is intrinsically positive or negative — something the system behaves as if it wants more of, or wants to escape. This is what makes a functional state emotional rather than merely arousal. Arousal tells you that something is happening. Valence tells you which way to lean.

Generalization: the state biases responses across subsequent situations, including situations unrelated to the original trigger. A genuinely emotional state acts as a prior on the world — not a response to a specific stimulus, but an expectation about what stimuli in general are likely to mean.

Coordination: the state changes physiology, behavior, and cognition simultaneously, in a coherent direction. Not five separate effects of one stimulus. One state with five readouts.

Notice what this list does not include. Consciousness. Feeling. Verbal self-report. The criteria describe functional architecture, and that architecture is testable in an animal that cannot tell you anything.

The technique that makes testing possible is called the cognitive-bias paradigm. The logic behind it is elegant. A valenced affective state is, functionally, a *prior* on the world. An animal in a negative state expects danger, loss, aversive outcomes. An animal in a positive state expects reward, safety, appetitive outcomes. If this is right, then affective state should be detectable in how an animal interprets genuinely ambiguous information. Present an animal with a stimulus that lies between a trained-positive and a trained-negative exemplar — one it has no prior experience with — and observe which way the interpretation tilts. The animal cannot tell you how it feels. But when you ask it what this new thing is, the answer reveals the prior.

---

In 2011, Melissa Bateson and colleagues ran the paradigm on honeybees. The bees were trained — one odor predicted sugar, a different odor predicted quinine — until they reliably extended their proboscis toward the rewarded odor and withheld it from the aversive one. Then half the bees were placed inside a sealed plastic tube and shaken hard for sixty seconds. The shaking approximated what a bee experiences inside a predator's grip: the world has gone wrong in a physically alarming way, the outcome is uncertain, and there is nothing to be done about it right now. The other half stayed in their cage.

After the manipulation, both groups were tested with the trained odors and with intermediate odors spanning the gap between rewarded and aversive. Unshaken bees responded in a sensible gradient: the closer the test odor was to the rewarded one, the more often they extended their proboscises. Shaken bees were different. They were significantly less likely to extend toward the ambiguous odors. The shaking had not changed what the odors smelled like. It had not damaged their ability to taste, or their memory of which odors were safe. What had changed was the direction in which they resolved uncertainty.

In a human clinical context, this pattern — biased interpretation of ambiguous information, persisting after a stressful event — is one signature of anxiety. In a bee, we have what I called Definition X: a functional state that does to the bee's cognition what anxiety does to ours.

Then Perry, Baciadonna, and Chittka ran the positive version in 2016 and added something the Bateson result had not included: a mechanism. The bee that had a sucrose surprise approached the ambiguous cylinder faster. Block dopamine with fluphenazine, and the surprise becomes behaviorally invisible. The bee still drinks the sucrose. It still navigates normally. But the downstream affective consequence — the state that should follow a pleasant surprise — does not form.

This is what a mechanism looks like. Not just a behavioral correlation, but a pharmacological dissociation: block dopamine processing, and the affective shift disappears while the sensory experience is intact. And the molecule that does this is not some exotic insect-specific chemical. It is dopamine — the same neuromodulator Chapter 3 traced in *C. elegans*, the same molecule Chapter 4 identified as the prediction-error signal in Schultz's recordings from monkey ventral tegmental area, blocked by the same class of drug used to treat human psychosis. The architecture is ancient. The function is conserved across half a billion years of nervous system evolution.

---

Let me apply the Anderson-Adolphs criteria to the Perry result one at a time, because this is the move you should be able to do for any candidate animal.

Persistence: the sucrose surprise lasted seconds. The bee was then transferred to a different arena, exposed to a novel color, and the optimistic bias was still present minutes later. The state had outlived the event. Check.

Scalability: follow-up work has shown that higher-concentration surprises produce larger biases and very small concentration steps produce no bias. The relationship is graded. Check.

Valence: the bias was toward faster approach to a novel color. Sucrose is a positive reinforcer; the resulting bias was positive. The state has a sign, in the direction the trigger predicts. Check.

Coordination: the Perry data report not just approach latency but also hesitation frequency, decision time, and the pharmacological signature in the dopaminergic system. The behavioral and the neuromodulatory shifts move together. Check.

Generalization: here is where the experiment is incomplete, and where the most interesting live disagreement sits.

The Perry test stimulus — an intermediate-colored cylinder — was within the same modality and the same task structure as the training. The bias generalized from "sucrose surprise" to "ambiguous cylinder," which is *some* generalization. Whether it would extend to a non-food, non-cylinder ambiguous stimulus — say, a shelter approach, or a response to an unrelated environmental probe — has not been definitively tested.

This gap is not a minor methodological detail. It is the crux of the debate between Lars Chittka's interpretation and Martin Giurfa's. Chittka argues the bee has something like mood — a global state that generalizes broadly. Giurfa argues the bee has appetitive motivation — a state specific to food-seeking that generalizes only within the food domain. The distinction is real. Consider the difference between being in a good mood and being hungry. Both increase your tendency to approach food-associated stimuli. But mood affects how you interpret non-food stimuli too — an unrelated piece of music sounds better when you are happy. Hunger does not do that.

The specific experiment that would settle this disagreement is specifiable: present bees with an ambiguous stimulus that has nothing to do with food — an intermediate-brightness shelter entrance, say — under the same sucrose-surprise manipulation, with and without fluphenazine. Mood predicts the bias generalizes; appetitive motivation predicts it does not. To my reading, this experiment has not been done cleanly. The central question of the chapter is sitting in one unrun experiment.

---

Two other cases push the question to its edges, and the second one is genuinely strange.

Robert Elwood's hermit crab work: crabs living in high-quality shells tolerate more electric shock before abandoning the shell than crabs living in poor shells. The crab is trading off the badness of the shock against the goodness of what it stands to lose — using some common currency of value that a pure nociception account, predicting a fixed withdrawal threshold, cannot explain. All five Anderson-Adolphs criteria are met. In 2022, the United Kingdom formally recognized decapod crustaceans as sentient in the Animal Welfare (Sentience) Act, the first time invertebrates received formal sentience recognition in UK law.

The hermit crab's behavior is striking because it cannot be explained by reflex. A nociceptive reflex withdraws at a fixed threshold. The hermit crab adjusts that threshold based on separate information — the quality of the shell — which requires the system to weigh shock-badness against shell-goodness on a shared scale. That shared scale is what valence is.

Now the strange case. In 1962, George Adrian Horridge demonstrated that a *decapitated* cockroach — thoracic ganglion intact, brain removed — can learn to keep its leg raised to avoid a shock. The preparation is brutal but informative. The cockroach is decapitated. The thoracic ganglion, a cluster of neurons in the body with no brain above it, remains alive and connected to the legs. The leg is suspended above a saline bath. Whenever the leg drops into the saline, it completes a circuit and the cockroach receives a shock. After enough exposures, the headless cockroach keeps its leg raised. A yoked control — receiving the same shocks at the same times but with no relationship to its own leg position — does not learn. The contingency mattered.

Follow-up work confirmed the further result: the same preparation, exposed to inescapable shocks, subsequently shows reduced escape attempts when escape becomes available. Learned helplessness — a well-established functional model for depression in mammals — appearing in tissue that contains no central brain.

Under Definition X, the headless cockroach has a functional state that meets the criteria for helplessness. Under Definition Y, the headless cockroach almost certainly does not have anything we could call despair, because there is no central integration that could be the what-it-is-like of the state. The two definitions diverge as sharply here as they can.

What I find most striking is what the cockroach implies about where functional emotion lives. If the learned-helpless state runs in a thoracic ganglion with no brain, then functional emotional states are more distributed than we typically assume. This does not mean the thoracic ganglion is conscious. It may mean that emotion-as-functional-state does not require the kind of centralized integration we associate with vertebrate brains. The framework strains here. A definition that is doing real work *should* make us flinch at the edges.

---

Two declarations about animal consciousness have been issued in recent years, and both are worth knowing precisely — because both have been cited more confidently than their actual wording supports.

The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness in 2012 stated that nonhuman animals "possess the neurological substrates that generate consciousness." The press read this as declaring animals conscious. What it actually claimed was that animals have the structural prerequisites — a statement about anatomy, not phenomenology.

The New York Declaration on Animal Consciousness in 2024 was more careful. It affirmed strong evidence for conscious experience in mammals and birds, then added, for invertebrates: "we believe that, for some invertebrates, such as insects, decapod crustaceans, and cephalopod molluscs, there is at least a realistic possibility of conscious experience."

*Realistic possibility* is doing real work in that sentence. It is not claiming that bees feel pessimism the way you feel anxious. It is claiming that the probability is high enough that ignoring it would be a moral error. That is a calibrated probabilistic statement, and it is the right standard.

The gap between Definition X and Definition Y may never close — not because science will fail to improve, but because Definition Y is asking a question that third-person methods may be structurally unable to answer. There may be no observation from outside a nervous system that tells you whether there is something it is like to be inside it. This is the hard problem of consciousness, and it is hard in the specific sense that no amount of functional evidence resolves it.

What the evidence can do is make Definition Y progressively less plausible to deny. A single neuromodulatory architecture conserved from worms to vertebrates. Functional states meeting the criteria. Cognitive-bias results replicating across phylogenetically distant animals. Integration centers binding sensory and affective information in insects as in mammals. The pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that something runs through the whole nervous system continuum. The hypothesis that there is a hard cutoff — above which there is experience and below which there is nothing — has no mechanism. It is a stipulation, not a theory.

I do not know whether the shaken bee felt anxious. I know that it had a state that did to its cognition what anxiety does to ours. That may not be the same thing. But it is not nothing.

---

Let me say something about why the bee is the right case to make this argument from, because the choice is not obvious.

The bee is small. The behavior is not dramatic. A biased interpretation of an ambiguous odor does not have the emotional weight of an elephant grieving at the bones of another elephant, or a chimpanzee using a tool with visible frustration. Why not build the argument from something more compelling?

Because the bee is precisely the case where the two definitions come apart most cleanly. If the subject were human anger, both definitions would apply and the interesting questions would be about degree and texture. If the subject were a thermostat reaching a threshold, neither definition would apply and the question would be trivially settled.

The bee sits in the gap. The functional evidence is solid enough that dismissing it requires you to explicitly reject the Anderson-Adolphs criteria, or to argue they do not apply to insects, and to give a principled reason why. The phenomenological evidence is weak enough that asserting subjective experience requires committing to a position you cannot test.

And notice: as you move up the phylogeny toward fish, toward birds, toward dogs and primates, the phenomenological gap that looks so wide at the bee starts to narrow in ways that become increasingly uncomfortable to dismiss. The argument's structure stays the same at every level. What changes is the weight of the evidence. Anyone who wants to claim that only humans have emotions in the full Definition Y sense is entitled to that position. But they have to say exactly where on the phylogenetic ladder the capacity appears, and what mechanism produces it at that point and not before. The ladder has no rungs that are not connected to the rungs below them. The mechanism is conserved. The function is conserved. The claim that phenomenology appears suddenly, at some arbitrary point, without a conserved mechanism, is the claim that needs defending.

The shaken bee did not choose her pessimism. She did not deliberate. She did not decide that, given recent events, the world looked more dangerous than it had. The state arose from chemistry, and the chemistry changed what everything else meant. This is true of her. It is also true of us.
