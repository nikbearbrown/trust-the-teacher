# The AI+1 Education Series — Framework

## The Central Claim

A great teacher trained in AI+1 outperforms any expensive educational technology platform.

The phone ban debate is the entry point. AI+1 is the destination. Every article enters through a different door and arrives at the same place.

---

## The Touch Points

These are the modular building blocks. Every article draws from several of them. No article owns any one of them exclusively.

---

### T1 — The Phone Ban as Entry Point
The ban is right. Personal smartphones in schools are doing documented harm — Figlio (NBER 2025) proves it with the most rigorous causal evidence yet published. But the ban alone is an incomplete policy. It removes a device without replacing what it was doing. In low-income schools, that device was often the primary internet access point. The ban without conditions is regressive.

*Key evidence:* Figlio & Özek NBER WP 34388 (Oct 2025) — Florida statewide ban, one large urban district, 3.5M student-observations. Year 1: suspensions +30% for Black boys, near zero for White/Hispanic. Year 2: test scores improve across groups. Disciplinary costs are front-loaded; benefits are delayed.

---

### T2 — The Undefined Exemption
Every one of the 31 state phone ban laws contains the same four words: *except for educational purposes.* Not one defines them. The exemption delegates an impossible real-time judgment to teachers who have received no guidance, no training, and no criteria. In wealthy schools the vacuum fills with professional judgment built over years of support. In under-resourced schools it fills with inconsistency. The exemption doesn't just leave the question open — it transfers advantage to schools that already have it.

*Key evidence:* 31 state laws reviewed — all contain undefined educational exemption.

---

### T3 — Screen Value, Not Screen Time
Richard Culatta (ISTE CEO): "limit based on screen value, not screen time." Correct — and not enough. "Screen value" as a principle is not a law, not a training module, and not a classroom criterion.

Screen value, operationalized: technology qualifies as educational when it requires genuine cognitive effort from the student — when it extends thinking rather than replaces it. This is specifiable, teachable, and connects directly to what the labor market is rewarding.

---

### T4 — What the Labor Market Knows
Thirteen years of BLS OEWS data mapped against O*NET cognitive profiles shows the pattern clearly: jobs requiring pattern recognition, fact retrieval, and syntactic correctness — everything curricula currently optimize for — are declining or being automated. Jobs requiring supervisory judgment, causal reasoning, and interpretive skill are growing and commanding wage premiums.

Software Developers are up. Computer Programmers are down. Claude implements specifications now. Schools are still teaching the curriculum that prepared students for the jobs Claude does.

*Tool:* irreduciblyhuman.xyz/onet
*Visualization:* Brutalist D3 employment index — Programmers vs. Developers, 2012–2024, ChatGPT as inflection point.

---

### T5 — The Teacher Is the Variable
Teacher training and support was the variable that determined whether ed tech worked at all. Not the platform. Not the device. The teacher. RAND (2026): where teachers were unprepared or unsupported, technology was underused or used ineffectively.

A great teacher with Claude outperforms any expensive educational technology platform — because the teacher provides what no platform has: interpretive judgment, plausibility auditing, the relationship that makes a student push past the first wrong answer.

The phone ban written into law is a statement that teachers cannot be trusted to make technology decisions. If we reduce teachers to rule-followers, we have made them more replaceable. The rule-follower is the job AI takes.

*RAND finding:* "Teacher training and support consistently made or broke the success of EdTech interventions."

---

### T6 — Train Teachers Like Doctors
Doctors have mandatory continuing medical education — 50 hours per year, license renewal tied to demonstrated learning, specialization updates when practice changes. Nobody argues that doctors should figure out AI on their own.

Teachers need the same infrastructure: sustained, subject-specific, grade-level-specific AI+1 professional development. Not one-shot workshops. Online certification. Masters-level programs. Continuous learning, like medicine.

AI is so new that very few teachers can keep up on their own. This is where the money should go — not hardware.

*The "Phone Fridays" model:* Give teachers lockers and give teachers training. Class A needs devices — devices are out. Class B doesn't — devices in the locker. Teacher decides. Teacher knows the room.

*The spending gap:* Districts spend $847/teacher/year on EdTech PD; 62% of teachers feel unprepared to use their assigned tools. Less than 40% of districts use federal PD funds for technology-enabled professional learning. Hardware absorbs the budget. Training gets the remainder.

---

### T7 — Three Honest Measures
Current teacher evaluation uses one measure: student satisfaction. Insufficient — not because satisfaction doesn't matter, but because it is the sole instrument for a multi-dimensional outcome.

**1. Student wellbeing** — current TRACE or equivalent. Keep it. Happy students in safe environments learn better. Valid signal. Do not overweight it.

**2. Student learning gains** — pre/post standardized assessment, controlled condition, not graded by the same teacher. Missing almost everywhere.

**3. Teacher learning and improvement** — is this teacher getting better? Evidence of AI+1 professional development, certification, demonstrated growth. Doctors must show this annually. Teachers do not have to show it at all.

The third measure is the most important. You cannot reward teachers for improving if you don't measure whether they're improving.

---

### T8 — Student Evaluations Are Biased
- Correlation between student evaluations and subsequent student performance: **r = 0.04** (near zero). 89 studies, 5.4 million students.
- Departments that tied renewal to minimum evaluation thresholds: **+0.27 GPA-point grade inflation**.
- With all course elements held constant — identical content, assignments, schedule — **women and faculty of color received lower scores than white men** (Cambridge study, 14 identical online courses).

The instrument fails to measure learning, incentivizes grade inflation, and is structurally biased against rigorous teachers. Using it as the sole measure selects against great teaching.

---

### T9 — The Equity Argument
The equity case runs through every piece:

- Low-income students disproportionately rely on personal phones as their only internet access. The ban removes this without replacing it.
- FCC E-Rate reversal (2025): school-provided home internet access removed simultaneously with phone bans. Two rungs removed at once from students at the bottom.
- Figlio: Black boys absorb disproportionate disciplinary costs in year 1. Benefits arrive in year 2. The students who can least afford a year of elevated suspensions absorb it first.
- Teacher training investment: low-income districts receive less professional development support, compounding every other disadvantage.

The equity argument is not: don't ban phones. It is: the ban without investment is regressive. The investment is teacher training.

---

### T10 — AI+1 Is the Destination
All of this is one argument at different scales.

If schools exist to transmit facts and train pattern recognition, the algorithm does it cheaper. If they exist to develop the capacities that AI augments rather than replaces — judgment, causal reasoning, plausibility auditing, collective intelligence — then the teacher is irreplaceable.

AI+1: domain expertise plus the human capacities that make expertise genuinely useful. The great teacher with Claude is the AI+1 practitioner in the classroom — using Claude to extend her reach so she can do what only she can: hear the wrong note, ask the question that unlocks the student, hold the room.

We wrote laws about phones. We should have written laws about teacher development.
