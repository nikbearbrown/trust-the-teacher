## Section 1 — The concept being specified

The chapter argues by analogy. The argument has three moves that need to be specified before they can be argued.

**Move 1: Medicine has built a continuous professional development infrastructure.** What this actually means: every physician in the United States, in order to keep practicing medicine, must demonstrate continuous learning to a state medical board, an accrediting body, and (for board-certified specialists) a specialty board. The system has three layered requirements — state CME mandates for licensure, ACCME-accredited activity infrastructure that produces the credits, and specialty board Maintenance of Certification (MOC) that adds higher-stakes content requirements. The system is not optional. It is not "encouraged best practice." It is the condition of being a physician.

**Move 2: Teaching has not.** What this actually means: in most US states, teachers have license renewal requirements expressed in clock hours, but the requirements are far smaller, less content-aligned, more compliance-driven, and almost entirely uncoupled from the actual evolution of the field. There is no ACCME for teaching. There is no specialty board enforcing demonstrated competence in current methods. The infrastructure does not exist.

**Move 3: AI is forcing the gap into the open.** What this actually means: a teacher who learned about AI in 2023 has a knowledge base that is at minimum two model generations stale by 2026. The half-life of "what a teacher needs to know about classroom AI" is shorter than the half-life of "what a physician needs to know about depression treatment." The mismatch between the speed of the field and the cadence of professional learning is no longer tolerable.

The vague terms that need specification:

- *Continuing education* — not a unit of time. Three different things wear this name: clock-hour compliance (state mandates), credit-bearing learning activity (the ACCME credit), and demonstrated competence (specialty board MOC). Three layers, three purposes, conflated in popular usage.
- *Professional development* in K-12 — also three things conflated: in-service workshop hours required for license renewal, sustained coaching tied to an instructional initiative, and content-specific deep training tied to a credential. The literature shows the first does almost nothing for student outcomes; the latter two do.
- *Tutor teacher* / *coach* — the analog used by Finland and increasingly in US instructional coaching literature. Specifies: a peer practitioner with additional training whose job is to support colleagues' practice change, not to deliver content top-down.

Worth flagging because they show that the regulatory infrastructure can be updated as the field changes — exactly the move the chapter argues teaching needs.

### Medical CME — ACCME accreditation infrastructure

**ACCME, "2024 Annual Data Report" (released August 2025).**

The 2024 report covers calendar year 2024 activity. Key figures:

- 95% of CME/CE activities assess competence; 46% assess performance; 18% assess patient health outcomes.
- This is the 27th year of the ACCME accreditation system (founded 1981, ACCME formed 1981, current structure mid-1980s onward).

**ACCME, "2023 Annual Data Report" (released August 2024).**
URL: https://accme.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/2023-ACCME-Annual-Data-Report-1045_20240815.pdf
Cleaner numbers for the chapter:

### Specialty board MOC — the higher-stakes layer

**American Board of Internal Medicine, "Maintenance of Certification Requirements."**
URL: https://www.abim.org/maintenance-of-certification/moc-requirements/
- 100 MOC points every 5 years.
- Pass a knowledge assessment (longitudinal or one-day) on the indicated schedule.
- 2-year point requirement was eliminated (recent change, ~2024). Now smoother but still required across the 5-year cycle.

**American Board of Family Medicine, "5-Year Cycle" (effective January 2025 onward).**
URL: https://www.theabfm.org/continue-certification/5-year-cycle/
- 60 certification points (Self-Assessment + Performance Improvement) per 5-year cycle.
- 200 CME credits per 5-year cycle (= 40/year average).
- 25 quarterly exam questions OR one-day exam.
- Ongoing professionalism, licensure, and personal conduct compliance.
- Annual fee.

The pedagogical move for the chapter: medicine demands *both* clock hours (state CME) *and* demonstrated competence (specialty MOC). The two-layer system handles a problem teaching does not: how do you ensure that the time spent learning was time spent learning the *right* things, and how do you know the learning stuck? The MOC layer is the answer. The longitudinal assessment (25 questions per quarter) is in particular an interesting mechanism — low-stakes per question, embedded in normal practice, designed by content experts. The exact opposite of the half-day workshop.

### Finnish DigiErko — primary sources

**Universities of Eastern Finland, Helsinki, and Turku — DigiErko program pages.**
URLs:
- https://uefconnect.uef.fi/en/digierko-learning-and-teaching-in-digital-environments/ (University of Eastern Finland, English)
- https://www.helsinki.fi/fi/kasvatustieteellinen-tiedekunta/opiskelu-ja-opetus/tutkintoa-taydentavat-ja-erilliset-koulutukset-ja-opinnot/oppiminen-ja-opettaminen-digitaalisissa-ymparistoissa-erikoistumiskoulutus (University of Helsinki, Finnish)

- https://www.utu.fi/fi/yliopisto/kasvatustieteiden-tiedekunta/ope-erko/digiymparisto (University of Turku, Finnish)

**DigiErko national network site.**
URL: https://digierko.fi/

**Program design (confirmed across all three university sources):**
- Full name in Finnish: *Oppiminen ja opettaminen digitaalisissa ympäristöissä* ("Learning and Teaching in Digital Environments").
- 60 ECTS (European Credit Transfer System) credits. For reference, a full academic year of university study in the European system is 60 ECTS. So DigiErko is roughly a year of full-time study, delivered part-time alongside working teaching.
- Open to working teachers across all levels: early childhood through university.
- Prerequisites: a teacher's pedagogical qualification *plus* three years of teaching experience. This is deliberate — DigiErko is post-experience training for teachers who already know the classroom, not initial preparation.
- Research-based — built around participants conducting and applying education research in their own settings.
- Multi-modal delivery (some on-site, mostly remote/blended).
- The training is delivered through a consortium of three universities (Eastern Finland, Helsinki, Turku) and the Innokas Network of specialist teachers (https://www.innokas.fi/en/training/).

**Crucial honest framing for the chapter: DigiErko's most recent cohort ended December 2024.** Per the University of Eastern Finland site, "The last DigiErko program ended in December 2024." This is a vital qualifier. The chapter should not describe DigiErko as a thriving ongoing program in the present tense. It is a *completed Finnish experiment* in specialized digital pedagogy training, with cohorts who have since dispersed back into Finnish schools as tutor teachers. Whether the model will be revived (perhaps in an AI-specific form) is open. [verify] current status before publication.

**The broader Finnish tutoropettaja (tutor teacher) system that DigiErko fed into:**
URL: https://www.oph.fi/sites/default/files/documents/195451_oph_faktaa_express_3c_2018_englanti_sivut.pdf (Finnish National Agency for Education, "Tutor Teacher Activities in Basic Education in Finland," 2018, English)

- Launched ~2017 by the Finnish National Agency for Education (Opetushallitus / OPH).
- 7.5 million EUR allocated in initial year; ~23 million EUR across the government period.
- Target: one tutor teacher for every 220 students; coverage of all ~2,500 Finnish basic schools.
- A tutor teacher is defined as a *peer practitioner* — a teacher with additional training whose role is to support colleagues in their school in adopting new pedagogy and digital practices.
- The training is in-service, ongoing, and embedded in school life rather than delivered as detached workshops.

The DigiErko program was the higher-end specialization track that fed expert tutor teachers into this national system. So the analogy stack is:

| Medical layer | Teaching analog (Finnish version) |
|---|---|
| State CME hours (licensure baseline) | Per-school in-service training time |
| ACCME accredited activity infrastructure | Tutor-teacher system, peer-led, OPH-funded |
| Specialty board MOC (specialization + competence) | DigiErko, 60-ECTS university-credentialed specialization |

This is the move the chapter is making, and Finland is the closest existing implementation of it. [verify] that DigiErko maps to MOC and tutoropettaja to ACCME-accredited-activity-volume in the way I'm describing — this is my synthesis, not a Finnish source's framing.

**Language note for the chapter.** "DigiErko" is shorthand. The full Finnish name translates as "Learning and Teaching in Digital Environments." "Erko" comes from *erikoistumiskoulutus* ("specialization training"), which is a distinct credential type in the Finnish higher education system — formal post-master's specialization for working professionals. So DigiErko is to teaching what board specialty certification is to medicine: a formal credentialing pathway, university-issued, outside the basic licensure track.

### Teacher PD efficacy literature — what the research actually says

**Kraft, Blazar & Hogan (2018), "The Effect of Teacher Coaching on Instruction and Achievement: A Meta-Analysis of the Causal Evidence." *Review of Educational Research*, 88(4), 547–588.**
URL: https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/mkraft/files/kraft_blazar_hogan_2018_teacher_coaching.pdf

Confirmed findings:
- Pooled effect sizes across 60 causal studies: **+0.49 SD on instruction, +0.18 SD on achievement**.
- Coaching paired with group training was more effective than either alone.
- **No clear evidence that more coaching hours = larger impacts.** Quality and focus matter more than quantity. This is a Feynman-flavored finding — the cleanest stat is not what you'd expect.
- Most studies were of literacy coaching for pre-K and elementary teachers. The evidence base for secondary and for AI-specific coaching is thinner.

This is the cleanest empirical anchor for the chapter's argument. Coaching — peer-practitioner, classroom-embedded, sustained — works. It works in the way Finland's tutoropettaja model is built. It does *not* work because of clock hours; it works because of structure.

**Yoon, Duncan, Lee, Scarloss & Shapley (2007), "Reviewing the Evidence on How Teacher Professional Development Affects Student Achievement." Issues & Answers Report, REL 2007–No. 033. US Department of Education, Institute of Education Sciences, Regional Educational Laboratory Southwest.**
URL: https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED498548.pdf

Confirmed findings:
- Of more than 1,300 candidate manuscripts, only **9 studies met What Works Clearinghouse evidence standards**. (This itself is a damning fact about the field's evidence base.)
- Across those 9 studies, the average effect size of teacher PD on student achievement was **+0.54 SD** (moderate).
- The authors emphasized that the small N prevented conclusions about *form* of PD (workshop vs. coaching vs. credential program) or content.
- The studies that produced positive effects involved an average of ~49 hours of contact, spread over 6–12 months. PD lasting less than 14 hours showed no significant effect on student achievement. (This is the specific number that supports the "one-shot workshop is not a training model" claim.)

The Yoon 2007 review is the single most cited piece of evidence for the threshold-of-effectiveness claim. It is also old. The chapter should note both: it's old, but newer evidence (Kraft et al. 2018) has not contradicted its central finding.

**Desimone (2009), "Improving Impact Studies of Teachers' Professional Development: Toward Better Conceptualizations and Measures." *Educational Researcher*, 38(3), 181–199.**
URL: https://isidore.udayton.edu/access/content/group/48d85ee6-68d7-4a63-ac4e-db6c0e01d494/EDT650/readings/Desimone_Laura_M.pdf

Confirmed five core features of effective PD:
1. **Content focus** — grounded in subject-matter expertise, not generic strategy.
2. **Active learning** — not passive lecture; involves teachers doing the work.
3. **Coherence** — aligned with curricula, standards, school priorities.
4. **Sufficient duration** — sustained over time, not single sessions.
5. **Collective participation** — teachers from same school/grade/department learning together.

These five features map almost perfectly onto what medical CME does at its best (content-specific, active-learning case studies, coherent with practice areas, sustained over years, often delivered in cohorts of peer specialists). They map almost perfectly onto Finland's tutoropettaja model. They map almost perfectly onto what the typical US district one-shot workshop on AI does *not* do.

**Learning Policy Institute, "Effective Teacher Professional Development" (Darling-Hammond, Hyler, Gardner, 2017).**
URL: https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/sites/default/files/product-files/Effective_Teacher_Professional_Development_REPORT.pdf
Adds: 7 elements (Desimone's 5 plus models/modeling of effective practice, coaching/expert support, and feedback/reflection). Reviewed 35 rigorous studies of PD that produced positive student outcomes; identified the common structural features. Useful as a more recent corroboration of Desimone.

## Section 4 — The mechanism for the deep-dive

The chapter has three candidate mechanisms to unpack. Pick one for the deep-dive section; treat the others briefly.

**Candidate A: Why coaching works when workshops don't (Kraft et al. mechanism).**
The Kraft meta-analysis finding — that coaching produces +0.49 SD on instruction but coaching hours don't scale linearly with effect — has a clean mechanism. Coaching works because it changes *what teachers do tomorrow morning*, in their actual classroom, with their actual students, in response to feedback they can act on immediately. Workshop hours don't transfer to classroom practice because the cognitive distance from "I learned a thing in a session" to "I do the thing with these 27 kids" is enormous. Coaching collapses the distance.

This is the most Feynman-friendly mechanism — small, specific, falsifiable, and surprising. It also makes the chapter's recommendation (build the Finnish tutoropettaja model) follow from the mechanism rather than from analogy.

**Candidate B: Why the medical CME system has the structure it has (institutional design).**
Trace why state medical boards, ACCME, and specialty boards each exist; what failure mode each was responding to historically; how the three layers cohere. This is interesting but feels more like a history-of-institutions move than a mechanism move. Better suited for a section than a deep-dive.

**Candidate C: The arithmetic of obsolescence (why AI specifically demands continuous training).**
Take a teacher who learned about AI in 2023. List what was true then that is no longer true now. (GPT-4 was new and expensive; image generation was novel; Claude was unknown; Khanmigo had just launched; AI detectors were considered reliable; "prompt engineering" was a job title; agentic AI was sci-fi; multi-model orchestration didn't exist.) Then ask: what fraction of what she learned still applies? Then generalize: at this rate of change, a non-continuous-learning model produces teachers whose competence depreciates faster than they accumulate it.

**Recommendation: Lead with Candidate A (Kraft mechanism), use Candidate C as a hook, fold Candidate B into the Finnish DigiErko section.** This is the cleanest structure: open with the puzzle (why doesn't a one-shot AI workshop work?), unfold the specification (what "PD" actually means), deep-dive on the mechanism (coaching changes tomorrow's classroom; workshops change Friday's vocabulary), then synthesize through the Finnish model + medical analogy.

## Section 5 — The central analogy and where it breaks

The medicine-teaching analogy is the spine of the chapter. It needs to be tested.

**Where the analogy works:**
- Both are practice-based professions where the practitioner's individual judgment matters in each encounter.
- Both face fields that evolve faster than initial training can keep up with.
- Both have public-trust dimensions — failure has consequences for vulnerable people.
- Both have existing licensure systems that *could* be the vehicle for continuous-learning requirements.
- Both are populated by professionals capable of self-directed learning if given time, structure, and credit.

**Where the analogy breaks (must be acknowledged in chapter):**
- *Stakes per error.* A physician making a drug interaction mistake can kill someone in an afternoon. A teacher making a pedagogical mistake produces gradual cumulative harm that is harder to detect and attribute. The acuity gap explains why medical regulation is more aggressive and better funded.
- *Patient pays vs. taxpayer pays.* The financial structure is different. Medical CME is funded substantially by registration fees ($1.8B/year of the $3.7B in ACCME-system income); physicians pay for their own learning (often reimbursed by employers). Teacher PD is funded by school districts from state and federal allocations. Asking individual teachers to pay $2,000/year out of pocket for credentialed AI training is a non-starter at current teacher salaries.
- *Specialization scaffolding.* Medicine has ~150 board-certified specialties, each with its own credentialing pathway. Teaching has at best a coarse distinction between elementary/secondary and a handful of content areas. The infrastructure for *specialization tracks* (AI+1 for elementary literacy, AI+1 for secondary math, etc.) doesn't exist in teacher credentialing the way it does in medicine.
- *Adversarial / commercial pressure.* Pharmaceutical money distorts medical CME (commercial support was 24% of ACCME income in 2023 — the system has had to build elaborate conflict-of-interest disclosure machinery in response). Edtech vendor money would distort teacher PD in exactly the same way if a parallel system were built without those guardrails. The chapter should flag this. A district that lets MagicSchool or Khanmigo or Anthropic certify "AI+1 teachers" reproduces the worst pathology of pharma-funded CME from the 1990s.
- *Practice unit.* A physician sees patients one at a time, in encounters of finite length, with documentation. A teacher works with a room of 27 students simultaneously over a school year. The unit of "applying what you learned" is different in ways that affect what continuous learning even looks like.

The honest reading is: medicine is the *closest available analog* for what a continuous-learning teaching profession would look like. It is not a perfect template. The chapter should treat the analogy as a productive instrument to be tested, not as a justification.

## Section 6 — Honest counter-arguments

**Counter-argument 1: Teachers don't have time.**
The US teacher workday is already at saturation. Adding mandatory CME-equivalent hours without subtracting other duties is a salary cut and a path to attrition. *Response:* This is correct and important. The chapter cannot honestly call for the medical CME model without also calling for the time-and-cost structure that makes physician CME possible — paid time, employer-supported, with the cost rolled into compensation. The Finnish model worked because Finland *funded* it. The 7.5M EUR allocation was a real commitment.

**Counter-argument 2: Teachers already do continuous PD; it's just badly designed.**
The 60% Title II-A figure for "spent on professional development" shows the money exists; it's just spent on short workshops. The fix is not more requirements but better requirements. *Response:* True. The chapter's argument is structural — change what counts as fulfilling the requirement (sustained coaching, cohort-based credentialing) rather than add more required hours.

**Counter-argument 3: The medical CME system has documented quality and capture problems.**
ACCME has had to defend its independence from pharma for decades. Many physicians describe their CME as performative compliance. Replicating that system for teaching might just create a new compliance theater. *Response:* This is the most serious objection. The chapter should acknowledge it explicitly. The recommendation is not to copy medical CME wholesale but to copy the *structural commitments* it embodies (mandatory, ongoing, cohort-based, content-evolving, audited for outcomes) while building it with awareness of the failure modes the medical system displays.

**Counter-argument 4: AI in classrooms might be a passing fad; building a credentialing infrastructure around it is premature.**
*Response:* Possible. But the broader argument — that teachers need continuous, structured, mechanism-focused training on the tools their students are using — survives whether the specific tool is generative AI or its successor. Build the infrastructure for *continuous adaptation*, not for any specific tool.

**Counter-argument 5: Finland is small and homogeneous; the model doesn't scale to the US.**
*Response:* Finland has ~5.5 million people, ~2,500 basic schools, ~50,000 teachers. The US has ~50 million K-12 students, ~3.5 million teachers. The Finnish model would need adaptation, not direct transplant. The interesting move is not "do exactly what Finland does" but "ask what infrastructure design Finland's experiment validates" — the answers are: peer-practitioner training, government-funded mandate, university-credentialed specialization. Those design principles scale.

## Section 7 — Draft scaffolding for the chapter

**Opening (the puzzle).**
A specific scene. A teacher in 2026 attends the district's annual AI workshop. It's 2.5 hours. The presenter shows ChatGPT, Khanmigo, and a tool the district licensed last spring. The teacher takes notes. She goes back to her classroom Monday morning where 27 students have already been using Claude, Gemini, and three apps the presenter didn't mention. What just happened? *(The puzzle: we treat 2.5 hours of vendor-led demo as professional development for managing the most rapidly evolving educational technology in 50 years. No other professional field would call this training.)*

**Move 1: Specify what we mean by "training" — three things wearing one name.**
- Clock hours / compliance.
- Sustained coaching.
- Credentialed competence.
Show that the literature (Yoon, Kraft, Desimone) supports the second and third and shows the first does little.

**Move 2: The medical infrastructure as concrete example.**
- State CME (the compliance layer): 20-50 hrs/year, varies by state.
- ACCME (the activity infrastructure): $3.7B/year, 68M learner interactions, audited for competence and performance and patient outcomes.
- MOC (the specialty competence layer): 100 points / 5 years (ABIM), longitudinal assessment, demonstrated competence.
Show the three layers. Note: medicine did not start with this. It built it incrementally over 50 years in response to specific failure modes.

**Move 3: The deep-dive on the mechanism.**
Why coaching works and workshops don't. The cognitive distance between "I learned a thing" and "I do the thing in front of 27 kids on Monday." The role of feedback you can act on tomorrow. The Kraft +0.49 SD effect on instruction. The non-linearity of coaching hours.

**Move 4: Finland — the closest existing analog.**
Tutoropettaja system. DigiErko specialization. Note: DigiErko's most recent cohort ended December 2024. The Finnish experiment ran for ~7 years; the principles are validated; what comes next for Finland is open.

**Move 5: The cost argument.**
SETDA Nov 2025: <40% of US districts use Title II-A for tech-enabled PD. Only 9 states prioritize it. The $2.2B/year exists. The infrastructure to use it does not.
WestEd's true-cost argument: hardware is the cheap part. (Drop or reframe the 30/70 attribution per Section 2.)

**Move 6: International comparison.**
Singapore embeds AI competency in NIE pre-service training. Estonia launched AI Leap September 2025 with national partnerships. Australia requires 100 hours / 5 years for all teachers. The US is the outlier.

**Move 7: The honest counter-arguments.**
Time. Money. Capture risk. Premature for AI specifically. Scaling Finland.

**Move 8: What a serious AI+1 teacher credential program looks like.**
Specialization tracks (AI+1 elementary literacy, AI+1 secondary math, AI+1 special education). Graduate-level credential issued by university (not vendor). Cohort-based, sustained over time, coaching-embedded. Re-credentialed as the field evolves. Funded by the same mechanism Finland used — government commitment, time built into the workload, not a side hustle.

**Closing.**
Return to the teacher in the 2.5-hour workshop. The honest reading: she is being asked to do a job we have not equipped her to do. Building the infrastructure to equip her is the only path that doesn't end in either banning AI from classrooms (which won't work — students are already using it) or pretending the workshop counts as training (which is what we're doing now, and the results are predictable).

**What would change my mind.** A serious longitudinal study showing that one-shot vendor-led workshops produce sustained changes in teacher AI practice and student outcomes — currently the evidence runs the other way (Yoon, Kraft).

**Still puzzling.** Why the medical analog held for so long with pharmaceutical capture as bad as it was, and what features of the design kept it from collapsing under that pressure. If we build a teaching version, we should know what kept medicine's version (mostly) honest.
