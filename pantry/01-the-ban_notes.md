## Chapter summary (from TIKTOC.md)

> ### Chapter 1: The Ban
> **Subtitle:** *Why removing the phone was right, what removing the phone alone cannot do, and who pays when we stop there*
>
> **Opening:** The Figlio finding. In year one of Florida's phone ban, suspensions increased 30 percent for Black boys. For white and Hispanic students, near zero. Test scores improved for all groups in year two. The ban is right. The disciplinary costs are front-loaded. The students who can least afford a year of elevated suspensions absorb those suspensions while they wait for benefits that arrive later.
>
> **The argument:**
>
> The phone ban is the correct response to a documented harm. Personal smartphones, specifically the social media applications and notification architectures built into them, are doing measurable damage to adolescent attention, anxiety, and academic performance. The bipartisan consensus is right. Thirty-one states were right to act.
>
> But the ban is a policy that lands on top of a system that was already failing the students it was most supposed to help. It removes the device. It does not build the replacement. And for low-income students whose phones were already doing the work the school couldn't afford to do — providing internet access, providing access to educational tools, providing the after-school connection to learning — the ban is a double removal.
>
> The FCC made it worse: E-Rate funding for off-premises hotspots rescinded in 2025, in the same policy window that states removed the personal device. Two rungs removed at once. Both from the students at the bottom.
>
> The phone ban is also a statement about teachers. A ban written into statute is a statement that teachers cannot be trusted to make technology decisions. Route around the professional. Write a rule. The chapter explores why this statement is both understandable — teacher technology decisions have been inconsistent, inequitable, and occasionally disastrous — and ultimately self-defeating. The exemption for educational purposes reinstates the judgment call the ban was supposed to eliminate, hands it back to the same teacher, and does so without any additional training, framework, or investment in her capacity to make it well.
>
> **Key evidence:** Figlio & Özek NBER WP 34388 (2025); NY 2015 ban reversal; FCC E-Rate rescission; NCES smartphone internet access data
>
> **The chapter's turn:** The ban is not the problem. What we did instead of training the teacher is the problem.

## A. Conceptual foundations

The chapter rests on five conceptual moves that must be specified before they can do any work. Each of them gets misused in the public conversation. Each of them is doing work the legislator did not specify.

### 1. What "the phone ban" actually is — and is not

The phrase "phone ban" is used as if it names one policy. It names a family of policies that vary on at least four axes: which devices are covered, when the prohibition is in effect, where the device must be stored, and what counts as an exception.

**The specification move.** A 2026 K-12 "cell phone ban" can mean any of:

- **Bell-to-bell prohibition with physical storage.** Personal smartphones, smartwatches, and earbuds locked in a Yondr pouch, a school-provided locker, or a centralized cart from the first bell to dismissal. Florida's HB 1105 (2025) and New York's Phone-Free Schools Law (2025) approximate this model. ([Florida HB 379 history](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379); [NY Phone-Free Schools](https://www.governor.ny.gov/eliminating-distractions-new-york-schools/new-york-state-phone-free-schools-law).)
- **Instructional-time prohibition.** Phones banned during class but permitted at lunch, in hallways, and between periods. Florida's original HB 379 (2023) before its 2025 expansion. ([Florida Phoenix coverage](https://floridaphoenix.com/2025/01/22/phone-ban-in-school-gets-mostly-positive-feedback-from-administrators/).)
- **State guidance encouraging district policy.** A non-binding recommendation that districts adopt some restriction. Connecticut, Maine, Minnesota, New Mexico, Washington — described as "guidance" or "resolution" states by Ballotpedia. ([Ballotpedia tracker](https://ballotpedia.org/State_policies_on_cellphone_use_in_K-12_public_schools).)
- **Local discretion under a state mandate to act.** A state law that requires every district to have *some* policy, with the substance left to school boards. New Jersey's 2025 legislation operates partly this way. ([New Jersey law coverage](https://6abc.com/post/governor-phil-murphy-signs-legislation-establishing-phone-free-schools-new-jersey/18372633/).)

**The 31-vs-26-vs-33 count problem.** The TIKTOC opening claims "thirty-one states." The actual number depends on the date and the definition. As of January 2026, civic trackers were reporting 26 states with statutory "bell-to-bell" requirements and another 7–9 with weaker guidance. By March 2026, Ballotpedia News reported Kansas as "the 33rd state to enact a K-12 cellphone ban." The number is moving fast; the working number for the chapter should be "more than 30, climbing." [verify the precise number at draft date]. ([Civic IQ 2026 tracker](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/10/26-states-now-require-school-cell-phone-bans-2026-k-12-policy-tracker-implementation-guide/); [Ballotpedia News Kansas](https://news.ballotpedia.org/2026/03/26/kansas-becomes-thirty-third-state-to-enact-a-k-12-cellphone-ban/).)

**The common misconception.** Reporters and parents use "phone ban" to mean "phones are not allowed at school." This is almost never what the law actually says. Almost every law contains a phrase like "except for educational purposes," "except when authorized by a teacher," or "except for documented medical or IEP-related need." The exemption is structural, not incidental. (See §A.3.)

**Source.** [Ballotpedia state-by-state policy tracker](https://ballotpedia.org/State_policies_on_cellphone_use_in_K-12_public_schools); [KFF policy review](https://www.kff.org/affordable-care-act/a-look-at-state-efforts-to-ban-cellphones-in-schools-and-implications-for-youth-mental-health/).

### 2. What the ban is responding to — the documented harm

The ban's premise is that personal smartphone use during school hours is doing measurable harm. This premise is largely supported, but the support is not as clean as the legislative coalition implies.

**The specification move.** What harm, exactly?

- **Attention and time-on-task harm.** Common Sense Media's 2023 "Constant Companion" study tracked smartphone use in a sample of teens for one week. The median teen picked up the phone 51 times per day, with the high end exceeding 498 pickups. About 23% of all notifications arrived during school hours. The median time spent on the phone during the school day was 43 minutes; the high end reached six and a half hours. ([Common Sense Media report](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/2023-cs-smartphone-research-report_final-for-web.pdf); [K-12 Dive coverage](https://www.k12dive.com/news/teen-cell-phone-use-schools/694901/).)
- **Academic harm.** Beland and Murphy (2016), studying 91 schools across four English cities, found that test scores for 16-year-olds rose by 6.4% of a standard deviation after phone bans were enacted, with lowest-quintile students gaining 14.23% — more than twice the average effect. ([Beland & Murphy, *Labour Economics* 2016, PDF](https://www.lpbeland.com/uploads/7/8/7/5/7875420/lpblabour_1-s2.0-s0927537116300136-main.pdf).)
- **Test-score harm reversed.** Figlio & Özek (NBER 34388, 2025) found significant test-score *gains* in year 2 of a Florida county-level bell-to-bell ban — see §B.1 for full treatment.

**The contested harm.** Whether smartphones *cause* the adolescent mental health crisis is genuinely contested — see §D, where Haidt, Twenge, Odgers, Orben, and Przybylski disagree on whether the evidence is sufficient to support causal claims. The chapter should be careful to distinguish "phones harm classroom attention and academic performance" (well-supported) from "phones cause the teen mental health crisis" (contested, mixed effect sizes, ongoing scientific dispute).

**Common misconception.** That UNESCO's 2023 call for phone bans settles the question. UNESCO's Global Education Monitoring Report does recommend restrictions, citing the Beland & Murphy study and PISA-derived associations. But UNESCO's own report acknowledges that the evidence is largely correlational at the international level and that "appropriate use" is the framing, not "ban." Reading UNESCO as a clean endorsement of bell-to-bell prohibition reads more into the report than the report itself. ([UNESCO Phone bans article](https://www.unesco.org/gem-report/en/articles/phone-bans-schools-are-spreading-worldwide-policy-debate-rages); [UNESCO "to ban or not"](https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/smartphones-school-only-when-they-clearly-support-learning).)

### 3. The educational exemption — the clause that runs the country

Every phone ban law in America contains an exemption for "educational purposes" (or some near-paraphrase). Not one law defines the term operationally.

**Examples of the language across states.**

- **Florida HB 379 (2023):** prohibits use during instructional time *"unless directed to do so by a teacher for educational purposes."* ([Florida Senate bill page](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379); [Florida bill analysis PDF](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379/Analyses/h0379z.CIS.PDF).)
- **Indiana:** *"Teachers can make exceptions to the rule for educational purposes or emergencies."* Indiana's pending SB 78 (2025–26 session) would tighten this by restricting "educational use" to school-issued devices only. ([Indiana Capital Chronicle](https://indianacapitalchronicle.com/2025/12/10/away-for-the-day-indiana-lawmakers-take-up-bill-expanding-cellphone-ban-to-entire-school-day/).)
- **New York:** authorizes use *"by a teacher, principal, or the school district for a specific educational purpose."* ([NY Phone-Free Schools page](https://www.governor.ny.gov/eliminating-distractions-new-york-schools/new-york-state-phone-free-schools-law).)
- **Arizona:** teachers may permit access to social media *"to the extent necessary for educational purposes."* ([Edweek state policy roundup](https://www.edweek.org/technology/most-students-now-face-cellphone-limits-at-school-what-happens-next/2025/07).)
- **California AB 3216 (2024) and follow-on:** exempts devices used *"with faculty permission"* and during emergencies. [verify exact statutory text at draft date].

**The common misconception.** That the exemption is a minor carve-out. It is not. The exemption is the hinge between a *device* ban and a *technology* ban. The same iPad that runs YouTube Shorts can run an AI tutor that refuses to give the student the answer until the student has demonstrated reasoning. The legislature cannot distinguish these two uses in statutory text. The teacher must distinguish them in real time, with no training to do so, and no guidance about what "educational purposes" actually means.

**Source.** [Edweek state policy summary](https://www.edweek.org/technology/most-students-now-face-cellphone-limits-at-school-what-happens-next/2025/07); [Parents Television Council July 2025 update PDF](https://www.parentstv.org/resources/PDF/Studies/2025-School-Phone-Policies-Update.pdf).

### 4. The equity layer — what the ban actually removes

The phone ban is facially neutral. Every student surrenders the device. The structural effect is not neutral. For students whose phones were *the* primary internet connection at home, the ban removes infrastructure, not just a distraction.

**The specification move.** What does "smartphone-only internet access" mean and how common is it?

- NCES, drawing on the American Community Survey, reports that roughly 6% of 3- to 18-year-olds have no home internet of any kind. ([NCES COE — Children's Internet Access at Home](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cch/home-internet-access).)
- Among adults in households earning under $30,000, just 57% report home broadband, and 27% are "smartphone-only" — they reach the internet only through a phone. Higher-income households (>$75,000) are 96% broadband. ([Pew Research mobile fact sheet — verify current year](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/teens-and-internet-device-access-fact-sheet/).)
- Translation: for roughly a quarter of low-income students, the phone is not a device. It is *the* device. The 1:1 Chromebook the school sent home with them does not connect to the internet without a connection — which the phone was providing.

**The common misconception.** That 1:1 device programs solve the equity problem. They don't. A school-issued Chromebook without a broadband connection at home is a piece of plastic. It runs nothing. The phone the student was using as a hotspot was the connection. (See §B.4 for the FCC E-Rate reversal that compounds this.)

**Source.** [NCES Condition of Education — Children's Internet Access at Home](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cch/home-internet-access); [NCES Fast Facts: Access to the internet](https://nces.ed.gov/fastfacts/display.asp?id=46); [K-12 Dive digital divide coverage](https://www.k12dive.com/news/digital-divide-households-children-no-internet/694596/).

### 5. The teacher-trust subtext

A ban written into statute is, among other things, a statement that the people closest to the decision cannot be trusted to make it.

This claim deserves to be specified, because it cuts in both directions.

- **The defensible reading.** Teacher technology decisions have been historically inconsistent. The kindergarten teacher who lets students watch CoComelon during morning meeting and the AP chemistry teacher who treats Khan Academy as the curriculum are both "exercising teacher discretion" over technology — and the variance is enormous. Some students get scaffolded use. Some get cognitive babysitting. Some get neither. A statewide rule corrects this by removing the variance.
- **The self-defeating reading.** The exemption reinstates the judgment call the ban was supposed to eliminate. The teacher who could not be trusted to keep phones away from her students at 9:15 a.m. is now trusted, with no additional training, to decide what counts as "an educational purpose" justifying their reintroduction at 10:30. The statutory architecture removes professional judgment in the abstract and reinstates it in the particulars — without investing in the professional's capacity to exercise it.

**The common misconception.** That the trust gap is permanent. The medical model demonstrates otherwise (Chapter 8 of the book). Doctors are also closest to the decision, and they are also subject to standardized rules — but the rules are written *with* the profession, training is required to make exemptions safely, and continuing education is mandatory. The phone ban borrowed the "rule" half of medicine's regulatory model and skipped the "professional training" half.

**Source.** This is the book's central claim. The argument is developed across chapters 1–3 and revisited in 8–9. Source: TIKTOC.md outline; [SETDA professional development analysis — November 2025 — verify URL at draft time].

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case 1: Florida — the implementation and the Figlio finding

**Florida HB 379 (2023).** Signed by Governor Ron DeSantis in May 2023; effective July 1, 2023. The law prohibited student use of "wireless communication devices" during instructional time and required districts to block social media on school-owned networks. Districts retained discretion over what to do during lunch and passing periods. Orange County and several others moved to full bell-to-bell prohibition; others permitted lunchtime use. ([Florida Senate HB 379 page](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379); [Yondr Florida overview](https://www.overyondr.com/blog/phone-free-legislation/florida).)

**The 2025 expansion.** Florida HB 1105 (signed 2025) extended bell-to-bell prohibition statewide for elementary and middle school students; high schools retained some district-level discretion. ([Click Orlando coverage](https://www.clickorlando.com/news/local/2025/08/06/new-florida-law-targets-cell-phone-use-in-schools/); [Florida Phoenix](https://floridaphoenix.com/2025/01/22/phone-ban-in-school-gets-mostly-positive-feedback-from-administrators/).)

**The Figlio & Özek paper (NBER WP 34388, 2025).** This is the single most rigorous causal analysis of a U.S. state phone ban published to date.

- **Authors:** David N. Figlio (University of Rochester) and Umut Özek (RAND).
- **Setting:** One of the 10 largest school districts in the United States — a large urban county-level Florida district that moved to bell-to-bell enforcement (phones silenced and stored in backpacks the entire school day, including lunch and transitions). The district is not named in the abstract; the methods section identifies it as Miami-Dade or Hillsborough-class [verify which district at draft time].
- **Methodology:** Difference-in-differences exploiting pre-ban variation in student smartphone ownership and use. The identification strategy uses student-level panel data and within-school variation in exposure to the policy.
- **Sample:** Student-level administrative records. The "3.5 million student-observations" figure from the TIKTOC opening appears in secondary coverage but is not the abstract figure; the abstract emphasizes the district's size (top-10) rather than a single sample-N. [verify exact sample at draft time].
- **Key finding 1 (suspensions).** Suspensions rose sharply in the months immediately after enforcement began. Secondary coverage (NBER digest; Chalkbeat; Boston Globe) describes the increase as roughly *doubling* in the month after enforcement began, with the rate 25% higher than the equivalent month the prior year. The increase was concentrated in Black students; the digest reports "a short-term 16 percent increase in suspensions for Black students."
- **The TIKTOC "30% for Black boys, near zero for white/Hispanic" framing.** This is the sharpest published characterization of the racial disparity. The 30% figure aligns with the *Boston Globe* coverage ("Black students are getting suspended more as the number of cell phone bans rise"), which appears to draw on the paper's subgroup analysis. The exact effect by race-and-sex (Black boys specifically vs. Black students overall) needs to be sourced to the paper's tables rather than the abstract. **[verify the specific 30% for Black boys figure against the paper's race × sex subgroup tables — the digest cites the all-Black-students 16% figure].**
- **Key finding 2 (test scores).** Significant improvements in test scores in year 2 of the ban. The effect appears in math and reading and is significant across racial/ethnic subgroups. ([NBER digest](https://www.nber.org/digest/202512/school-cell-phone-bans-and-student-achievement); [The 74 coverage](https://www.the74million.org/article/florida-study-cellphone-bans-promote-academic-gains-after-a-year-or-so/).)
- **Key finding 3 (absences).** Unexcused absences decreased significantly. The authors argue that reduced absences explain a large fraction of the test-score gains — students are physically in class more often, and the disciplinary surge in year 1 dissipates by year 2.
- **Key finding 4 (heterogeneity).** Effects are larger in middle and high schools than in elementary, where personal smartphone ownership is rarer.

**Sources for the paper.** [NBER paper landing page](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34388); [SSRN abstract](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5629464); [NBER Digest December 2025](https://www.nber.org/digest/202512/school-cell-phone-bans-and-student-achievement); [Chalkbeat](https://www.chalkbeat.org/2025/10/20/new-study-finds-cell-phone-ban-benefits-to-test-scores/); [Fordham Institute](https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/cutting-cord-early-evidence-cellphone-policy-implementation); [Boston Globe](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2025/10/23/nation/cellphone-use-in-schools-limited-without-academic-gains/); [The 74](https://www.the74million.org/article/florida-study-cellphone-bans-promote-academic-gains-after-a-year-or-so/).

**The framing the chapter wants.** "The ban works. The disciplinary costs are real, racially patterned, and front-loaded. The students least able to afford a year of elevated suspensions absorb those suspensions while they wait for benefits that arrive later." This framing is defensible from the published findings. The TIKTOC's 30% figure for Black boys specifically should be cross-checked against the paper's race × sex table before going to print.

### Case 2: New York City — the 2015 reversal that the 2025 wave forgot

**The 2006 ban.** Under Mayor Michael Bloomberg, NYC public schools prohibited personal cell phones on school grounds. Enforcement was conducted primarily through metal detectors, which were standard equipment in low-income high schools and rare in wealthy ones. Students at metal-detector schools either left their phones at home, paid bodegas $1/day to store them, or risked confiscation.

**The 2015 reversal.** Mayor Bill de Blasio rescinded the ban effective March 2, 2015. The stated rationale combined three threads:

1. **Equity.** The ban fell harder on low-income schools because enforcement mechanisms (metal detectors, scanners) were concentrated there. Wealthy students hid their phones in expensive bags or used schools without metal detectors. The policy was facially neutral; the application was sharply unequal. ([NYC Mayor's office press release Jan 2015](https://www.nyc.gov/office-of-the-mayor/news/013-15/mayor-de-blasio-chancellor-fari-a-lift-school-cell-phone-ban); [WNYC](https://www.wnyc.org/story/city-lift-cell-phone-ban-schools/).)
2. **Parental communication.** De Blasio cited emergency contact between parents and students — explicitly a post-Columbine, post-Sandy Hook concern. ([TIME magazine](https://time.com/3657860/new-york-city-schools-cellphones/).)
3. **"Out of touch with modern parenting."** De Blasio's son was in the NYC public schools at the time; the mayor acknowledged that his own son carried a phone to school.

**The 2025 return.** Governor Kathy Hochul signed New York's statewide Phone-Free Schools Law in 2025. NYC committed $29 million to implementation, much of it for Yondr-style pouches. The 2025 legislation does not address the structural inequity that brought down the 2006 ban. ([Hochul press release](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/distraction-free-schools-governor-hochul-and-new-york-city-public-schools-chancellor-aviles); [Chalkbeat $29M coverage](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2025/07/16/nyc-school-cell-phone-ban-funding-kathy-hochul-yondr/).)

**The teaching move.** The 2015 reversal is a natural experiment the 2025 legislators chose not to consult. The historical record is available, well-documented, and on point. The decision not to address it — not to require equity audits of enforcement, not to mandate alternative communication channels for parents, not to fund the supervised technology access the ban removes from low-income students — is itself a choice the chapter should name.

### Case 3: A specific district story — Yondr, Cincinnati, Los Angeles

The chapter benefits from a concrete district-level scene. Several are available; the strongest are LA and Cincinnati.

**Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD).** Contracted with Yondr for $5.2 million in 2023–24 to roll out phone pouches across middle and high schools. Implementation problems emerged within months: students used magnets purchased on Amazon to defeat the pouches, carried "burner" phones that went into the pouch while their real phone stayed in a pocket, or simply broke the pouch with sustained banging against a desk. EdSource reported widely on the workarounds. ([NBC News Yondr cost coverage](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/consumer/schools-spend-millions-yondr-pouches-store-cellphones-rcna230454); [EdSource on LAUSD subversion](https://edsource.org/2025/students-easily-subvert-lausd-phone-ban/730127); [Civic IQ Yondr pricing guide 2026](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/10/yondr-pouch-pricing-guide-2026-school-contracts-alternatives-what-districts-are-actually-paying/).)

**Cincinnati Public Schools.** Spent ~$500,000 to provide pouches to all students in grades 7–12. Reported "durability issues" within the first year; Garfield Heights (Ohio) transitioned to a different vendor.

**Houston ISD.** Allocated $800,000 for phone storage solutions in 2025. ([Houston Public Media](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/education/2025/08/01/527760/houston-isd-announces-student-cell-phone-policy-to-comply-with-new-state-law/).)

**The teaching move.** The chapter does not need to lean hard on the pouch-defeat stories — they will date quickly, and "students find workarounds" is not a strong argument against the ban itself. The more useful framing is what the pouch budget *displaces*. LAUSD's $5.2 million for pouches is approximately equivalent to the cost of providing sustained AI professional development for ~2,000 teachers at the WestEd-recommended unit cost [verify the per-teacher PD figure from SETDA/WestEd at draft time]. The pouch budget is the teacher-training budget that was not spent.

### Failure case: The FCC E-Rate hotspot rescission (2025)

**The program before the rescission.** E-Rate is the FCC's federal program that subsidizes internet connectivity for schools and libraries, funded through the Universal Service Fund and authorized under Section 254 of the Communications Act. In July 2024, the FCC under Chair Jessica Rosenworcel adopted an order expanding E-Rate eligibility to cover off-premises Wi-Fi hotspots (for use at students' homes) and school-bus Wi-Fi. The expansion was contested at the time. ([FCC adopts changes to E-Rate for off-premises hotspots](https://www.fcc.gov/consumer-governmental-affairs/fcc-adopts-changes-e-rate-support-premises-wi-fi-hotspots).)

**The 2025 rescission.** On September 30, 2025, the FCC under new leadership voted 2–1 in an Order on Reconsideration to remove off-premises Wi-Fi hotspots and school-bus Wi-Fi from E-Rate eligibility. The legal rationale: the previous order's expansion exceeded the FCC's statutory authority under Section 254 of the Communications Act, which the new majority read as limited to on-premises connectivity at schools and libraries. ([FCC ends E-rate support — Funds For Learning](https://www.fundsforlearning.com/news/fcc-ends-e-rate-support-for-hotspot-bus-wi-fi-services/); [K-12 Dive coverage](https://www.k12dive.com/news/fcc-removes-school-bus-wi-fi-hotspots-from-e-rate/761481/); [Lerman Senter analysis](https://www.lermansenter.com/fcc-eliminates-e-rate-support-for-off-premises-and-school-bus-wi-fi/); [StateScoop](https://statescoop.com/fcc-e-rate-funding-for-wifi-hotspots-school-bus-connectivity/).)

**The scale of the cut.** During fiscal year 2025, schools and districts requested $42.6 million for hotspots and $15.3 million for school-bus Wi-Fi. 1,762 libraries had applied for E-Rate hotspot funding. The FCC's order directed the Universal Service Administrative Company to deny pending FY25 requests for these services, including for the 2025–26 school year already underway.

**The library protest.** The American Library Association called the decision "one more way to limit access to information" and noted the lack of due process in the reversal. ([ALA September 2025 statement](https://www.ala.org/news/2025/09/ala-disappointed-fcc-takebacks-lack-due-process-decision-end-library-hotspots-school).)

**The compounding with the phone ban.** This is the chapter's hardest fact. The state phone bans and the FCC rescission were independent policy decisions — different actors, different institutional logics, different stated rationales. They were not coordinated. Their combined effect is a double subtraction of internet access from the same students:

1. The state removes the phone, which for ~25% of low-income students was the primary or only home internet access.
2. The FCC removes the federal substitute (the school-provided hotspot) that was designed to fill that gap.

Both decisions arrived in the same policy window (late 2024 through fall 2025). Both fell hardest on the same demographic. Nobody designed the combined outcome. Nobody is accountable for it. The chapter should name this clearly without overclaiming coordination.

## C. Connections and dependencies

**Prerequisites.** None — this is Chapter 1. The chapter must function as the entry point to the whole book and assume no prior commitment to its argument. The reader who picks up *Trust the Teacher* expecting a defense of phones in classrooms or a takedown of teachers must encounter the argument from the ground up.

**What the chapter opens upward.** Chapter 1 is the load-bearing column for three later chapters:

- **Chapter 2 (The App).** Chapter 1 establishes that the ban is a wrong answer because it is not about teachers. Chapter 2 establishes that the app is the *other* wrong answer for the same reason. The "neither answer is about teachers" framing is the single argument that holds the first three chapters together.
- **Chapter 3 (The Clause That Runs the Country).** Chapter 1 introduces the educational exemption as a problem; Chapter 3 dissects it as the central policy lever and proposes a framework for filling it in. Chapter 1 must end with the exemption naming itself as the next chapter's subject.
- **Chapter 11 (The Equity Argument).** Chapter 1 introduces the regressive structure of the ban (the Figlio race effect, the FCC double-subtraction). Chapter 11 returns to equity comprehensively and connects the Chapter 1 findings to the Bastani cognitive offloading literature and the Tutor CoPilot tutor-equity findings. Chapter 1 plants the equity argument; Chapter 11 develops it.

**What the chapter explicitly does NOT do.**

- It does not argue against phone bans. The ban is right. The chapter must hold this line cleanly so the equity critique doesn't read as opposition to the policy itself.
- It does not develop the Bastani PNAS cognitive offloading finding in depth. That belongs to Chapter 7.
- It does not propose the AI+1 teacher training model. That belongs to Chapter 8.
- It does not solve the educational exemption. That belongs to Chapter 3.

**Cross-reference logic.** When the chapter wants to say more about the equity dimension, it should hand the reader forward: "Chapter 11 returns to this." When it wants to say more about what's wrong with the exemption, it should hand forward to Chapter 3. The chapter's job is to *open* the problem cleanly, not to close it.

## D. Current state of the field

This is the section the book's "Notes on Structure" commits to most explicitly: the chapter that makes a strong claim engages seriously with the evidence that complicates it. Below is what is settled, what is contested, and what the chapter cannot ignore.

### What is settled (or near-settled)

1. **Phones disrupt classroom attention and time-on-task.** This is the part of the harm story with the strongest evidence base. Beland & Murphy (2016) is the cleanest causal study — 91 schools, four English cities, 6.4% standard deviation gain on tests for the average student post-ban, 14.23% for the lowest quintile. ([Beland & Murphy 2016, *Labour Economics*](https://www.lpbeland.com/uploads/7/8/7/5/7875420/lpblabour_1-s2.0-s0927537116300136-main.pdf).) Figlio & Özek (2025) extends this to U.S. context with administrative-data rigor.
2. **Smartphone use during school is high and pervasive.** Common Sense Media's 2023 tracking study: 97% of teens use their phones at least once during school hours; median 43 minutes/day during school; about 23% of all notifications arrive during school hours. ([Common Sense Media report](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/2023-cs-smartphone-research-report_final-for-web.pdf).) Pew (December 2024): teens average ~5 hours/day on smartphones overall, ~70 minutes during the school day. ([Pew Teens, Social Media and Technology 2024](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2024/12/12/teens-social-media-and-technology-2024/).)
3. **Adolescent mental-health indicators have worsened since ~2012.** The CDC's 2023 Youth Risk Behavior Survey: 40% of high school students reported persistent feelings of sadness or hopelessness; 20% seriously considered suicide; 10% attempted. Long-term trend lines (2013–2023) show significant increases. Some 2021→2023 indicators improved slightly (especially for female students), but the level remains historically high. ([CDC 2023 YRBS results](https://www.cdc.gov/yrbs/results/2023-yrbs-results.html); [CDC MMWR Mental Health and Suicide Risk supplement](https://www.cdc.gov/mmwr/volumes/73/su/su7304a9.htm); [CDC YRBS 2013–2023 trend report PDF](https://www.cdc.gov/yrbs/dstr/pdf/YRBS-2023-Data-Summary-Trend-Report.pdf).)

### What is contested

This is the part of the field the chapter cannot fudge. The book's commitment ("counter-evidence engaged seriously") demands the dispute be presented honestly, not flattened into either direction.

**The Haidt thesis.** Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024) argues that smartphone adoption and the rise of "phone-based childhood" are *the* primary cause of the post-2012 adolescent mental health crisis. Haidt identifies four foundational harms: social deprivation (phones as "experience blockers"), sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, and addiction. The book is responsible for much of the political coalition behind the 2024–2025 phone ban wave. ([The Anxious Generation Wikipedia overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Anxious_Generation); [Haidt's own site](https://jonathanhaidt.com/anxious-generation/).)

**The Odgers rebuttal (Nature, March 2024).** Developmental psychologist Candice Odgers reviewed *The Anxious Generation* in *Nature* and argued that Haidt's central claim — that social media is rewiring children's brains and causing an epidemic of mental illness — is "not supported by science." Her core complaints:

- The evidence Haidt marshals is largely correlational; the experimental studies are fewer and weaker than the book implies.
- Hundreds of researchers (Odgers, Orben, Przybylski, others) have searched for the large effects Haidt posits and have produced a mix of "no, small, and mixed associations" — not the consistent, large effects the causal story would require.
- Reverse causation is plausible: depressed teens may turn to social media rather than social media causing the depression.
- Odgers' position is *not* that nothing should be done — she explicitly says "changes to the ways these platforms work would nonetheless be wise" — but that the evidence does not support the strong rewiring claim. ([Odgers, *Nature*, March 2024 — "The great rewiring"](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-024-00902-2); [Odgers article PDF mirror](https://internet.psych.wisc.edu/wp-content/uploads/532-Master/532-UnitPages/Unit-11/Odgers_Nature_2024.pdf).)

**The Haidt response.** Haidt has responded that critics undercount the experimental evidence. He cites 22 experimental studies, 16 of which found significant harm, plus 64 correlational studies. His central rejoinder: when many small effects across multiple causal pathways (sleep, attention, social comparison, addiction) compound, the resulting harm is large even if no single channel produces a large effect on its own. ([Haidt's Twitter response — March 2024](https://x.com/JonHaidt/status/1774571680511508601); [Haidt's "After Babel" Substack response](https://www.afterbabel.com/p/phone-based-childhood-cause-epidemic).)

**The Orben & Przybylski school (the "small effects" position).** Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski's 2019 *Psychological Science* paper, *"Screens, Teens, and Psychological Well-Being: Evidence From Three Time-Use-Diary Studies,"* found that digital technology use accounts for about 0.4% of variation in adolescent well-being — small enough to be "more or less inconsequential." This finding has been heavily cited as evidence *against* the strong-effects position. ([Orben & Przybylski 2019 in *Psychological Science*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0956797619830329).) Haidt and colleagues have published critiques arguing that the Vuorre & Przybylski follow-on studies should not be used to assess social media impact, citing methodology concerns. ([Sigaud, Rausch, McClean, Haidt 2026, *Clinical Psychological Science*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/21677026261425910).) The fight is technical and ongoing.

**The Twenge position.** Jean Twenge, *iGen* (2017) and continuing research, argues that the rise in adolescent depression starting around 2012 lines up too precisely with smartphone adoption (and with the gendered pattern — girls hit harder, which matches social-media-heavy use patterns) to be coincidence. Twenge has acknowledged that the data is correlational but has argued that "the biggest change in teens' lives between 2010 and 2015" was smartphone adoption. ([Twenge Senate Commerce Committee testimony, January 2026 PDF](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/media/doc/ScreensKidsSenateTestimonyTwenge1-12-26%20PDF.pdf); [NPR coverage 2023](https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2023/04/25/1171773181/social-media-teens-mental-health).)

**The National Academies position.** A December 2023 / early 2024 National Academies report concluded that the evidence does not support a universal claim that social media is harmful, but does support that effects are heterogeneous, dose-dependent, and platform-dependent. The Academies' position is essentially "it depends." [verify exact NAS report citation at draft time].

### How the chapter should handle this

The book is committed to engaging counter-evidence seriously. The honest framing for Chapter 1:

> The evidence that phones harm classroom attention and academic performance is strong. Beland & Murphy (2016) and Figlio & Özek (2025) are the two most rigorous causal studies and they converge. The evidence that phones cause the adolescent mental health crisis is genuinely contested — Haidt and Twenge argue yes; Odgers, Orben, and Przybylski argue the effect sizes are small or mixed; the National Academies stay agnostic. The chapter does not depend on the mental health causal claim. The ban is justified by the in-classroom evidence alone. Conflating the two evidence bases — using the mental-health frame to justify a classroom-attention policy — is the move the chapter avoids.

This is the most important methodological move in the chapter. It is also the one most easily lost. The legislative coalition behind the 2024–2025 ban wave is built largely on the Haidt/Twenge narrative. The classroom-attention evidence alone is plenty to justify the policy; the chapter should make that argument and let the mental-health debate stand or fall on its own.

### Recent developments (2025 wave)

- **2025 was the year states acted.** As of January 2026, 22 of the 26 statutory bell-to-bell states had enacted their laws in 2025 alone. ([Civic IQ tracker](https://blogs.civiciq.com/2026/01/10/26-states-now-require-school-cell-phone-bans-2026-k-12-policy-tracker-implementation-guide/).)

- **The Figlio & Özek paper landed in October 2025**, after most of the legislative wave was already in motion. The disciplinary-cost finding has not yet had time to influence the next round of legislation.
- **The FCC E-Rate rescission landed September 30, 2025.**

## E. Teaching considerations

### Who is the reader?

The book's primary audience is K-12 principals, district leaders, instructional coaches, and education policymakers. This is the reader the chapter is for. The secondary audience (teachers, teacher educators, parents) will read it, but the chapter's load is on the practitioner-decision-maker who has either already had to implement a phone ban or is about to.

This reader is not neutral. She is:

- Already implementing some version of the ban (because she has to).
- Frustrated with the implementation details (because they are hard).
- Sympathetic to the bipartisan consensus that prompted the ban (because she has seen what phones do in classrooms).
- Suspicious of arguments that say "but wait" (because she has heard them in many varieties, and most of them have been industry-aligned).

The chapter must earn its "but wait" by speaking with the reader, not against her. The opening Figlio finding does this work — it accepts the ban (test scores go up, the policy works) and identifies the cost (suspensions surge in year 1, racially patterned) without asking the reader to abandon her commitment.

### Where readers get stuck

Three predictable failure modes. The chapter should anticipate each.

**Failure mode 1: "Phones cause harm" → "bans help"** — the implicit syllogism.

Most readers will assume that because phones cause harm, banning them helps. This is mostly true but not automatically true. The Beland & Murphy and Figlio & Özek findings support the inference. But "the ban helps" is *not* the same as "the ban is the right response in this district given everything else this district is doing." The chapter must make space for the principal who reads the evidence, accepts that the ban helps on average, and still has to decide what specifically to do about (a) the disciplinary surge in year 1, (b) the equity loss to low-income students, (c) the educational exemption that is now her problem to define. "Helps on average" and "is the right move here, this way, now" are not the same statement.

**Failure mode 2: Treating the exemption as solved when it isn't.**

The principal reading the chapter will already have signed off on her district's exemption language. It will say something like "phones may be used during instructional time for educational purposes as directed by the teacher." She will read this and not see the problem. The chapter has to make her see that this sentence is doing enormous work and is structurally underdefined — it transfers the decision from the legislature back to the teacher, with no training, no framework, and no support for making the call well.

The pedagogical move: ask her to apply her own exemption to three specific cases.

- Case A: A student is using Khan Academy on her phone to practice a math problem the textbook didn't have enough examples of.
- Case B: A student is using TikTok in study hall to watch a "5 tips for writing your college essay" video.
- Case C: A student is using ChatGPT on her phone to summarize the chapter she didn't read.

All three fall under "educational purposes" by some reading. None of them are obviously what the legislature meant. The reader cannot apply her own exemption without first making a judgment call — and she is not the only adult in the building making these judgment calls. Three teachers in the same school will produce three different answers. The variance is the problem.

**Failure mode 3: Reading the equity argument as anti-ban.**

The chapter argues that the ban is regressive in its implementation effects. A defensive reader will hear this as "you're saying we shouldn't ban phones." She isn't. The chapter must hold the distinction:

- The ban is right.
- The ban *plus* nothing else is regressive.
- The ban *plus* guaranteed supervised technology access + defined exemption + teacher training is not regressive.

The chapter argues for the third position, not against the ban itself.

### Analogies that work

**The "double subtraction" analogy.** Imagine a family on a tight food budget. The state passes a law removing sugary drinks from school lunches (good policy; obesity rates drop). The same week, the USDA reduces the SNAP benefit. The two policies were not coordinated. The combined effect: a family that was using SNAP to buy juice (because the school lunch had the only real meal the kid would eat) now has neither. The school lunch removal is correct on the merits. The combined policy is regressive. The chapter can use this — or something like it — without belaboring the analogy. The structure is the point.

**The "took the backup" framing.** From the equity essay: the phone was the backup the school's tech infrastructure failed to provide. The ban took the backup. It did not fix the school. This is a sharp formulation, short enough to land, accurate to the structural finding, and not requiring a long explanation. It earns its place.

### Analogies to avoid

- **"Phones are like cigarettes."** A common move in the legislative rhetoric. It conflates regulation of addictive products (where the user is the victim) with regulation of a tool the user might also be using productively. It also implies the only honest action is total prohibition, which the educational exemption already contradicts. Skip it.
- **"The phone is like the slingshot in *Lord of the Flies*."** A literary flourish that doesn't carry weight; cut on sight.
- **"Phones in school are like horses on the highway."** Cute, but the technology-becomes-infrastructure analogy doesn't map cleanly because the displaced-infrastructure issue (broadband) is exactly what makes the ban regressive. The analogy hides the equity story rather than illuminating it.

### Exercises the chapter could end with

The chapter doesn't owe a formal exercise set, but a "what would you do tomorrow morning" prompt for the principal-reader is consistent with the book's Movement III practitioner sections. Candidates:

1. **Audit your district's exemption language.** Pull the actual text. Ask three teachers to interpret it on three test cases (Khan Academy on a phone; TikTok study-hall study-tips video; ChatGPT chapter summary). Compare answers.
2. **Map the home-broadband gap in your student population.** Pull your enrollment data, cross with the ZIP-code-level broadband data from the FCC's National Broadband Map. Identify which students lost home internet when the ban took effect.
3. **Calculate the equity-conditional cost.** Estimate the cost of providing supervised technology access during school hours for the students who lost home internet access. Compare to the cost of the pouch contract. Decide.

These are the kinds of moves a district leader can actually do in the week after reading the chapter.
