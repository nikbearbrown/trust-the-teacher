# Bookmap: *Magnifica Humanitas* (Leo XIV, 2026)
## Analytical Focus: The Foundation Problem

---

# PART 1: SECTION-BY-SECTION LOGICAL MAP

---

## FRONT MATTER & STRUCTURAL ARCHITECTURE

**Core Claim:**
The encyclical positions itself as a direct heir to *Rerum Novarum* (1891), arguing that just as Leo XIII applied the Gospel to the industrial "new things" (*rerum novarum*) of his era, Leo XIV applies the Church's accumulated Social Doctrine to the digital "new things" of the present — particularly artificial intelligence. The document's governing structural device is a binary biblical typology: Babel (centralized, pride-driven, dehumanizing technology) versus Jerusalem-under-Nehemiah (collaborative, God-centered, human-scale reconstruction). This typology functions as both moral frame and rhetorical architecture throughout all five chapters.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Para. 3: Explicit invocation of the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum* as the occasion and model for this document
- Para. 4: Direct echo of Leo XIII's *rerum novarum* as framing device for AI, digitalization, and robotics
- Paras. 7–10: Extended exegesis of Genesis 11 (Babel) and Nehemiah 2–6 (Jerusalem) as the document's two structuring poles
- Para. 17: Chapter One explicitly frames its purpose as demonstrating the "dynamic character" of Social Doctrine — living, not fixed

**Logical Method:**
Typological framing: two scriptural narratives are abstracted into ideal types (dehumanizing vs. humanizing technology deployment) and then applied as evaluative criteria across all subsequent chapters.

**Logical Gaps:**
- The Babel/Jerusalem typology is generative as rhetoric but underspecified as analysis. Babel is condemned for "uniformity that eliminates diversity" and self-sufficient pride (para. 7); Jerusalem is praised for shared responsibility and divine orientation (para. 8). But algorithmic infrastructure does not map cleanly onto either: a recommendation algorithm is not the Tower of Babel (it was not built in pride), nor is open-source AI development obviously Nehemiah (it lacks the covenantal community). The typology directs moral attention but does not provide analytical traction on specific cases.
- The claim of continuity with *Rerum Novarum* loads an argument that must be earned but is largely assumed: that the conceptual vocabulary developed for capital-labor relations in industrial economies is adequate for governing algorithmic power. This assumption is the foundation problem the document never fully interrogates.

**Methodological Soundness:** Good
The front matter establishes a coherent rhetorical architecture and situates the document clearly within tradition. The biblical typology is exegetically responsible. The weakness is that the continuity claim is treated as a premise rather than a conclusion requiring demonstration.

## INTRODUCTION (Paras. 1–16)

**Core Claim:**
Humanity faces a choice between constructing a new Tower of Babel through AI or rebuilding a just city through shared responsibility. Christians are called to remain "profoundly human" in the AI era by safeguarding the dignity bestowed in Christ, building for the common good, and accepting human limitation rather than seeking technological transcendence.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Para. 4: Francis, *Laudato Si'* 104: "Never has humanity had such power over itself"
- Para. 5: Private, transnational technological actors now command resources exceeding those of many governments — identified as the new structural problem
- Paras. 7–10: Babel/Jerusalem typology (see above)
- Paras. 11–14: Four "building principles" — relationship with God, acceptance of limits, shared responsibility, evangelical language

**Logical Method:**
Problem statement by contrast: the document names the scale of technological power (para. 5), names the available moral resources (Social Doctrine principles), and declares the task of alignment between them. The introduction does not argue; it situates.

**Logical Gaps:**
- Para. 5 makes the most analytically significant observation in the document — that the main drivers of technological development are now private and transnational, exceeding state capacity — but treats this as a problem to be named rather than a structural condition that might stress the inherited framework. The observation that private actors command more resources than governments is precisely what makes subsidiarity (which presupposes a functioning state as the upper tier) difficult to apply, but this tension is not developed here.
- Para. 9 claims technology "is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate and use it." This is a strong and defensible claim, but it creates an immediate problem for the Social Doctrine framework: if every design choice is a moral act, who is morally responsible for diffuse, multi-actor systems where no single agent controls the outcome? The document raises this without resolving it.

**Methodological Soundness:** Good
The introduction performs its situating function well. Para. 5 shows genuine structural awareness. The weakness is that the most analytically challenging observations are stated and then not followed through.

## CHAPTER ONE: A Dynamic Approach Faithful to the Gospel (Paras. 17–45)

**Core Claim:**
The Church's Social Doctrine is not a fixed code but a living process of shared discernment that has developed through dialogue with history, science, and human experience since *Rerum Novarum*. This dynamic character — demonstrated by surveying the Magisterium from Leo XIII through Francis — means it is capable of addressing AI as it addressed industrialization, not by abandoning its principles but by deepening their application.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Para. 29: John Paul II designating *Rerum Novarum*'s method a "lasting paradigm" (Centesimus Annus 5)
- Paras. 30–44: Systematic survey of major social encyclicals: *Quadragesimo Anno*, *Mater et Magistra*, *Pacem in Terris*, *Gaudium et Spes*, *Populorum Progressio*, *Laborem Exercens*, *Sollicitudo Rei Socialis*, *Centesimus Annus*, *Caritas in Veritate*, *Laudato Si'*, *Fratelli Tutti*
- Para. 27: Social Doctrine defined as "a process of shared discernment" rather than "a handbook of principles and norms"
- Para. 45: The development is characterized as "harmonious, though not always linear" — acknowledging variation without conceding rupture

**Logical Method:**
Historical genealogy: the chapter constructs a lineage of doctrinal development in which each pope addressed the "new things" of his era, establishing a pattern that implies the current document can do likewise.

**Logical Gaps:**
- The historical survey demonstrates that Social Doctrine *has* developed in response to new circumstances — but the argument requires more than precedent. The question is whether the *kind* of transformation required by AI governance is commensurate with prior adaptations (adding workers' rights to natural law theory, extending solidarity to the global South) or whether it requires a different order of conceptual revision. The chapter argues by analogy without examining whether the analogy holds.
- Para. 27 defines Social Doctrine as "a process of shared discernment" born from the encounter between "the eternal truth of the Gospel and the questions of history." This is epistemologically significant: it means the doctrine can change. But the chapter nowhere specifies what would count as a case where the existing principles fail — what a disconfirming instance would look like. Without that specification, the claim of dynamic adequacy is unfalsifiable.
- The survey omits any reckoning with the category of cases where Social Doctrine's application was delayed or wrong (slavery is addressed in Chapter Four, but not here, in the context of doctrinal adequacy). This selective genealogy strengthens the continuity argument rhetorically while weakening it analytically.

**Methodological Soundness:** Good
The historical survey is accurate, fair, and usefully synthetic. The chapter succeeds at demonstrating doctrinal development. It does not succeed at demonstrating that prior development is a reliable guide to the current challenge — it asserts the analogy rather than arguing it.

## CHAPTER TWO: Foundations and Principles of the Social Doctrine of the Church (Paras. 46–89)

**Core Claim:**
Five interlocking principles — common good, universal destination of goods, subsidiarity, solidarity, and social justice — derived from the theological foundation of the human person as *imago Dei*, constitute the evaluative framework for assessing AI and digital transformation. These principles must be considered collectively, not in isolation, and their application begins with the Church's own internal structures.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Para. 50: Human dignity as gift preceding and transcending all capacities, grounded in *imago Dei* (Gen 1:26–27)
- Para. 60: Common good defined via *Gaudium et Spes* 26: "the sum total of social conditions which allow people, either as groups or as individuals, to reach their fulfillment more fully and more easily"
- Para. 65: Universal destination of goods extended explicitly to "new forms of property, such as patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure and data" (para. 67)
- Para. 71: Subsidiarity applied to digital context — major tech actors as a *de facto* highest tier requiring transparency, accountability, and participation
- Para. 80: Social justice in the digital age requires preventing "new forms of exclusion and deprivation of freedoms" through algorithmic discrimination and surveillance

**Logical Method:**
Principled extension: each classical Social Doctrine principle is defined in its traditional form and then extended by analogy to cover digital phenomena. The method is explicit and iterative.

**Logical Gaps:**
- Para. 67's extension of the universal destination of goods to algorithms and data is the document's most structurally ambitious move. The argument runs: physical goods are given by God for all humanity; therefore immaterial goods like data and algorithmic systems are also universally destined. But the analogy is not defended — it is asserted. Physical goods are scarce, rivalrous, and locationally specific; data and algorithms are non-rivalrous (one person's use does not deplete them) and can be replicated at near-zero marginal cost. These structural differences are morally relevant. The question of *why* the universal destination principle extends to non-rival goods is not answered; the extension is declared rather than derived.
- Para. 71's application of subsidiarity to digital platforms identifies the correct structural problem — that tech giants constitute a *de facto* top tier — but the classical subsidiarity framework was designed for a vertical political hierarchy (individual → family → intermediary → state → international), where each level has defined competencies and a coercive authority capable of enforcing limits on the level above. Digital platforms are not in that hierarchy. They are private actors with extraterritorial operations, no democratic mandate, and no clear superior authority to which subsidiarity's upward correction mechanism could appeal. The principle is invoked but its institutional preconditions are absent.
- The *imago Dei* foundation is robust as a basis for inalienable dignity, but the document also needs it to ground participation, creativity, and labor — the dimensions of humanity that AI threatens. Para. 99 will later argue that AI lacks conscience, embodiment, and relational experience. But the *imago Dei* claim in Chapter Two does not distinguish between the dignity that cannot be threatened (ontological, para. 52) and the dignity that can be eroded (social, existential). This conflation creates ambiguity throughout the document about what exactly is at stake.

**Methodological Soundness:** Moderate
The chapter is doctrinally coherent within its own tradition. The principled extensions to digital phenomena demonstrate genuine engagement with the subject. The weaknesses are structural: the analogical extensions are asserted rather than argued, and the institutional preconditions for subsidiarity and the universal destination principle are not present in the digital context. The chapter builds the evaluative framework competently while leaving the hardest justificatory work undone.

## CHAPTER THREE: Technology and Dominance — The Grandeur of Humanity in Light of the Promises of AI (Paras. 90–130)

**Core Claim:**
The technocratic paradigm — the reduction of all value to efficiency, control, and profit — constitutes the cultural root of AI's dehumanizing potential. Against transhumanist and posthumanist narratives that promise technological transcendence, Christian humanism insists that the authentic "more than human" is found through grace, not enhancement: in limitations accepted, in relationships cultivated, in the inexhaustible love of God that no algorithm can simulate or replace.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Paras. 92–93: Francis, *Laudato Si'* on the technocratic paradigm as the ideological driver behind AI's expansion
- Para. 99: Detailed phenomenological analysis of what AI lacks — embodiment, conscience, moral judgment, relational experience, inner growth — presented as a distinguishing argument against anthropomorphization
- Paras. 115–117: Analysis of transhumanism and posthumanism as ideological currents shaping collective imagination and technical investment
- Paras. 118–125: Argument that limitation, suffering, and finitude are not defects to be corrected but conditions through which humanity matures
- Para. 127: Thomas Aquinas on grace as the only adequate "more than human" (*Summa Theologiae* I-II, q.112)
- Para. 130: Augustine's two cities as the structural frame for the concluding civilizational choice

**Logical Method:**
Phenomenological distinction followed by theological counter-offer: the chapter first establishes what AI cannot do (by arguing from what it lacks), then argues that what it lacks is precisely the locus of authentic human transcendence.

**Logical Gaps:**
- Para. 99's account of what AI lacks is philosophically careful and largely persuasive, but it creates a problem the chapter does not resolve: if AI lacks conscience, moral judgment, and the capacity for genuine relationship, then it cannot be a moral agent — yet para. 104 argues that AI systems embed moral choices and can constitute "criteria that contradict the inalienable dignity of the human person." Both claims may be true simultaneously (designers have consciences; the systems embody their choices without having consciences of their own), but the document does not articulate the theory of distributed moral responsibility that would connect them. The gap between "AI has no conscience" and "AI systems enact moral choices" is the central ethical problem of the field, and the chapter sidesteps it.
- The anti-transhumanist argument in paras. 118–125 is theologically coherent but strategically problematic: it argues against the strongest version of the opposition (full posthumanist enhancement) rather than against the more likely and tractable cases (cognitive augmentation, AI-assisted diagnosis, adaptive learning tools). This rhetorical choice leaves the document without principled guidance on the spectrum between "AI as hammer" and "AI as replacement human."
- Para. 129's key question — does AI make human life "more human"? — is borrowed from John Paul II's *Redemptor Hominis* and is the right question, but "more human" is not defined precisely enough to serve as a decision criterion. Human life involves dignity, suffering, relationship, labor, creativity, and finitude. An AI system that eliminates back-breaking agricultural labor makes life "more human" on some dimensions while potentially eroding others (craft, community, embodied skill). The criterion is gestured at rather than operationalized.

**Methodological Soundness:** Good
Chapter Three is the document's philosophically richest section. The phenomenological analysis of AI's limits (para. 99) is the strongest single passage in the encyclical. The theological anthropology is coherent and carefully argued. The weaknesses are in the middle range: the chapter handles the extreme cases (transhumanism, full posthumanism) well and the theological counter-argument well, but the space between — where most actual decisions are made — is underspecified.

## CHAPTER FOUR: Safeguarding Humanity at a Time of Transformation — Truth, Work, Freedom (Paras. 131–181)

**Core Claim:**
Three domains — truth in public communication, the dignity of work, and freedom against dependency and commercialization — are the concrete arenas where digital transformation produces its most damaging effects on human dignity. In each domain, the Social Doctrine principles elaborated in Chapter Two provide criteria for both diagnosis and prescription: protecting epistemic commons, ensuring access to dignified work, and resisting the commodification of attention and personal data.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Paras. 132–134: Disinformation and algorithmic amplification of conflict as threats to democratic epistemology; Hannah Arendt cited on the prerequisites of totalitarianism (*Origins of Totalitarianism*, III, 474)
- Para. 141: Documented psychological harms from early unsupervised digital exposure — sleep, attention, emotional regulation, with grooming and CSAM explicitly named
- Paras. 150–151: ILO-adjacent concerns about the "fourth industrial revolution" — deskilling, automated surveillance, relentless pace — cited from *Antiqua et Nova* (Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith, 2025)
- Para. 159: Call to move beyond GDP as the primary development metric toward complementary indicators including dignity of work and inequality reduction
- Paras. 173–179: Supply chain analysis of AI's hidden labor — data labelers, content moderators, rare earth miners — as a contemporary form of slavery subject to the document's condemnation
- Para. 176: Historical acknowledgment of the Church's delayed condemnation of slavery, with explicit request for pardon

**Logical Method:**
Applied principles: the chapter takes the five principles from Chapter Two and traces their implications across three concrete domains, moving from epistemic goods (truth) to economic goods (work) to personal goods (freedom).

**Logical Gaps:**
- The labor analysis in paras. 150–156 is the most empirically grounded section of the document, but it operates within the inherited *Laborem Exercens* framework of "worker" and "employer" with a defined employment relation. Gig economy workers, platform contractors, and AI-augmented piecework do not fit this framework cleanly: they are formally self-employed, have no employer, are subject to algorithmic management rather than managerial direction, and lack the associational capacity the Church has consistently identified as the workers' primary form of self-defense. The document recognizes these as "new forms of job insecurity" (para. 151) but does not revise the framework to account for them.
- Para. 159's call for complementary metrics to GDP is well-placed but unargued. The encyclical does not specify what the metrics would measure, who would govern them, or how they would be incorporated into policy decisions. This is the prescription without the mechanism.
- The slavery section (paras. 173–179) is historically and morally serious, and para. 176's request for pardon regarding the Church's delayed condemnation of historical slavery is remarkable. But the structural analysis does not follow through: if the Church's prior delayed response to slavery reveals a pattern of institutional moral inertia, what prevents the same pattern from operating now with respect to algorithmic exploitation? The historical acknowledgment is not converted into institutional self-scrutiny.
- Para. 170's framing of digital addiction as arising from "business models that monetize attention and time" is accurate but treats the problem as one of exploitation of pre-existing vulnerability. The stronger version of the argument — that the architecture of these platforms *constructs* the vulnerability rather than merely exploiting it — is implied but not stated. The difference is morally significant: exploitation has a victim and a perpetrator; architectural construction of dependency is a diffuse harm without clear attribution.

**Methodological Soundness:** Moderate to Good
Chapter Four is the most practically engaged section of the document and contains its most empirically specific claims. The labor and digital slavery analysis is strong. The weaknesses are systematic: the chapter consistently applies the inherited Social Doctrine framework to new phenomena without revising the framework when the fit is imperfect. The inherited categories do real analytical work, but the gaps where they don't fit are not acknowledged.

## CHAPTER FIVE: The Culture of Power and the Civilization of Love (Paras. 182–228)

**Core Claim:**
AI is not only an economic and social challenge but a military and geopolitical one: autonomous weapons systems, hybrid warfare, algorithmic disinformation, and the erosion of multilateral institutions constitute a "culture of power" that threatens to normalize violence. Against this, the "civilization of love" proposed by Paul VI requires translating charity into institutional structures — through diplomacy, dialogue, protection of civilians, and reform of international governance — with AI specifically subject to the most rigorous ethical constraints in its military applications.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Paras. 197–200: Holy See's position on autonomous weapons: lethal or irreversible decisions cannot be delegated to automated systems; effective human control is non-negotiable; international framework required to curb the arms race
- Para. 192: Formal declaration that the "just war" theory "is now outdated" — the strongest doctrinal claim in the document
- Para. 194: The Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (2021) noted as important but "largely symbolic" without major power adherence
- Paras. 201–203: Diagnosis of the multilateral system's crisis as structural, not merely contingent — caused by both institutional limitations and a "frequent lack of shared will"
- Para. 213: J.R.R. Tolkien, *The Return of the King* — "It is not our part to master all the tides of the world"
- Para. 222: Direct address to heads of government: "Let us meet, let us talk, let us negotiate! War is never inevitable."

**Logical Method:**
Civilizational contrast: the chapter extends the Babel/Jerusalem typology to the geopolitical domain, arguing that the same choice between pride-driven power and covenant-based community structures international relations as it structures AI governance.

**Logical Gaps:**
- Para. 192's declaration that just war theory is "now outdated" is the most consequential doctrinal claim in the encyclical, and it appears in a single sentence attributed to *Fratelli Tutti* 258 — in a footnote acknowledging that it "has all too often been used to justify any kind of war." This is a significant doctrinal development that deserves the analytical apparatus applied elsewhere (principled extension, historical genealogy, theological grounding), but receives none of it. A claim of this magnitude — that a framework deployed across centuries of Catholic moral theology has been superseded — cannot be established by footnote.
- The chapter correctly identifies AI as an accelerant of hybrid warfare and disinformation (paras. 183, 225) but does not address the attribution problem: when an AI-orchestrated influence campaign destabilizes a democratic election, who is responsible, under what legal framework, and through what mechanism? The document calls for "shared regulations on the use of digital technologies" (para. 225) and "a shared framework at the international level" (para. 200) without specifying what the content or institutional form of such frameworks would be. The prescription names the need without filling it.
- The "civilization of love" as a political category relies on actors choosing dialogue and diplomacy on the basis of moral conviction — but the chapter's own diagnosis (paras. 204–208) establishes that the current political culture is one of false realism, nihilism, and manufactured fear. The prescriptive framework assumes the motivational conditions for its own adoption. This is not a logical error unique to this document — it is a structural tension in any prophetic political theology — but it should be named rather than papered over.

**Methodological Soundness:** Moderate
Chapter Five's diagnosis of the geopolitical situation is sharp and in several places courageous (the just war claim; the explicit acknowledgment that the multilateral system has failed). The prescriptions remain at the level of aspiration without institutional specification. The chapter makes the right enemies and the right friends without providing the actionable criteria that would allow the document's principles to operate in contested cases.

## CONCLUSION (Paras. 229–245)

**Core Claim:**
A "Eucharistic spirituality" — anchored in the Incarnation, expressed in ecclesial unity, directed toward the common good, and sustained by Marian prayer — is the spiritual program adequate to the AI era. The Magnificat is the document's closing image: Mary's vision of God scattering the proud and lifting the lowly is the template for how Christians should read AI's transformations.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Para. 233: The mystery of "recapitulation" (Eph 1:10) — all things gathered into Christ — as the theological guarantee that authentic human achievement will not be lost in the digital transformation
- Paras. 236–240: Four concrete commitments: fidelity to truth, investment in education, cultivation of relationships, love of justice and peace
- Para. 241: Return to Nehemiah as the document's closing exemplum — listener, pray-er, discerner, organizer, rebuilder

**Logical Method:**
Doxological resolution: the document moves from analysis to prayer, from prescription to hope, from argument to contemplation. The conclusion does not advance the argument; it invites a disposition.

**Logical Gaps:**
- The Magnificat as the document's final frame is theologically resonant but analytically significant in a way the conclusion does not acknowledge: Mary's hymn is a reversal song — the mighty cast down, the lowly raised. Applied to AI governance, this would imply that adequate Christian response is not merely regulatory but structurally redistributive — breaking up monopolies, mandating open access, subordinating private technological capital to common ownership. The document gestures at this in Chapter Two (para. 67) but never follows it to its redistributive implications. The Magnificat is invoked as spiritual consolation rather than as the radical social program its content implies.
- Para. 240's injunction to examine "supply chains of digital production" and "mechanisms that profit from manipulation and war" is the most practically specific prescription in the conclusion — but it is framed as an individual spiritual practice ("examine your conscience") rather than a structural mandate. The gap between the encyclical's structural diagnosis and its primarily dispositional prescriptions is widest here.

**Methodological Soundness:** Good as liturgical closure; not analytically resolved.
The conclusion is what it intends to be: a prayerful call to action. It does not close the analytical questions opened by the document, nor is it designed to. Taken as spiritual program, it is coherent and appropriately demanding. Taken as a response to the foundation problem it identifies, it remains incomplete.

# BRIDGE SYNTHESIS: THE LOGICAL ARCHITECTURE OF THE WHOLE

---

## Three tensions running through the book's full argument

**Tension 1: Ontological certainty vs. structural inadequacy**

The encyclical rests on an *imago Dei* anthropology that is, within its own theological framework, logically secure: human dignity is inalienable because it is grounded in God's love, not in human capacity or productivity. This is a strong foundation precisely because it is immune to the arguments of efficiency and utility that tend to erode dignity claims in market contexts.

But the encyclical also needs a framework for governing *institutions* — platforms, regulatory bodies, international agreements, labor markets — and here the ontological certainty provides no traction. The *imago Dei* tells us what human beings are worth; it does not tell us how to structure a data governance regime, who should hold training data, or what enforcement mechanism should compel a transnational corporation to comply with subsidiarity. The document is at its most powerful when asserting the foundation and at its most strained when trying to derive operational prescriptions from it.

This tension appears most clearly in the gap between Chapter Two's principled extensions and Chapter Four's applied prescriptions. The extension of the universal destination of goods to data (para. 67) follows logically *if* we accept that data is analogous to physical goods — but the argument for that analogy is never made, only asserted. The subsidiarity requirement for algorithmic transparency (para. 71) follows logically *if* there is a functioning institutional hierarchy to enforce it — but Chapter Five's own diagnosis (paras. 201–203) establishes that the multilateral system that would constitute such a hierarchy has structurally failed.

The document does not resolve this tension. It proceeds as though the principles can be applied while quietly acknowledging, at the structural level, that the preconditions for their application are absent. This is not intellectual dishonesty — it may be the appropriate disposition of a document that is prophetic rather than legislative — but it means the analytical work of demonstrating sufficiency has not been done.

**Tension 2: Private power named vs. private power unaddressed**

The encyclical's most analytically acute observation is that the main drivers of technological development are now private, transnational, and command resources exceeding those of many governments (para. 5). This observation — if followed through — would require a fundamentally different prescriptive framework than the one the document offers. If the state can no longer govern technology because private actors are more powerful than states, then prescriptions addressed to states (legislate this, regulate that, reform the UN) are addressed to the wrong level.

The document names this problem but does not solve it. Chapter Three (paras. 95, 107–109) returns to the concentration of power in private tech actors with real specificity — "a handful of actors" dictating who trains AI models and who is merely subjected to them. Para. 108 explicitly calls for managing data "as a common or shared good," which is a structural claim about ownership, not merely a procedural claim about governance. But the mechanism for this transformation — how private ownership of AI infrastructure would be converted into a common good — is never specified.

The tension becomes most visible in Chapter Five, where the prescriptions (diplomatic dialogue, multilateral reform, international frameworks) are addressed primarily to state and ecclesiastical actors. The private technology companies that Chapter Three identifies as the actual locus of power are addressed only in para. 111's "special appeal to those who develop artificial intelligence" — a moral exhortation, not a structural constraint. The document diagnoses a failure of public governance without providing tools adequate to that diagnosis.

**Tension 3: Continuity claimed vs. rupture required**

The encyclical's entire architecture depends on the claim that Social Doctrine's development from Leo XIII to the present constitutes a living tradition adequate to new challenges. Chapter One demonstrates that the tradition has changed — it has extended from workers' rights to environmental justice to global solidarity. The implicit argument is that AI governance is the next extension in an already-established pattern.

But the prior extensions shared a common structural feature: they applied the tradition's principles to *new populations* or *new phenomena* within an already-understood institutional framework. Extending solidarity to the Global South (John Paul II), extending the universal destination of goods to the environment (Francis), extending subsidiarity to supranational institutions (Benedict XVI) — all of these preserved the basic architecture of state authority, civil society, market, and Church operating within a recognizable framework of political governance.

AI governance requires something different: principles adequate to a context where the relevant actors are private, extraterritorial, and non-democratic; where harm is diffuse, non-attributable, and structurally embedded in systems rather than arising from identifiable acts; where the "goods" at stake are non-rivalrous and infinitely replicable; and where the institutional preconditions for classical Social Doctrine application (functional states, enforceable international law, organized labor) are precisely what is under threat. This is not a new application of existing principles — it is a challenge to the institutional substrate within which those principles were designed to operate.

The document does not acknowledge this as the character of the challenge. It treats AI governance as a harder version of industrial governance — harder because the actors are more powerful, the harms more diffuse, the pace faster — rather than as a potentially different *kind* of governance problem. The continuity claim is therefore not so much argued as performed: the document proceeds in the genre of Social Doctrine encyclicals, deploys their categories, and invokes their authorities, as if the genre itself demonstrated adequate coverage of the subject.

---

## The book's most proven claims

- Human dignity is ontologically grounded and cannot be reduced to capacity or productivity (paras. 50–53, *Dignitas Infinita* cited): established with philosophical rigor and doctrinal authority.
- AI systems lack conscience, embodiment, and the relational conditions for genuine understanding (para. 99): the most analytically careful claim in the document; the phenomenological distinctions hold.
- The concentration of AI infrastructure in private, transnational actors constitutes a new form of power that exceeds democratic state capacity (paras. 5, 95): empirically accurate and analytically well-framed.
- The digital attention economy operates through business models that exploit human vulnerability, making individual resistance insufficient and requiring institutional response (paras. 170–172): well-supported and structurally accurate.
- AI's hidden supply chains — data labelers, content moderators, rare earth miners — involve serious labor exploitation that is structurally enabled by the opacity of digital infrastructure (paras. 173–179): empirically grounded, appropriately specific.
- Disinformation preceded AI but finds in AI a "powerful amplifier" that accelerates the epistemic conditions for totalitarianism (paras. 132–134, Arendt cited): historically and theoretically sound.
- The "just war" doctrine has been routinely misapplied to justify wars that fail its own criteria (para. 192, footnote): historically accurate as a descriptive claim.

---

## The book's most significant unproven claims

- That the Social Doctrine principles of subsidiarity and universal destination of goods extend to algorithmic infrastructure without requiring structural revision (para. 67, 71): the analogical extension is asserted but the disanalogies — non-rivalry, extraterritoriality, absence of a hierarchy with coercive authority — are not addressed.
- That "just war" theory is "now outdated" as such (para. 192): a claim of major doctrinal significance made in a footnote without the principled argument the claim requires.
- That AI can become "disarmed" and "human-friendly" through restored access to "the plurality of human cultures and ways of life" (para. 110): the mechanism for this transformation is unspecified; the claim functions as aspiration rather than prescription.
- That international frameworks adequate to governing autonomous weapons and AI-driven hybrid warfare can be constructed in the current geopolitical environment (paras. 200, 225): the document's own diagnosis of multilateral failure (paras. 201–203) undermines the plausibility of this prescription without acknowledging the contradiction.
- That managing data as "a common or shared good" is adequate without specifying the property regime, governance structure, or enforcement mechanism that would make this operational (para. 108): the claim names a destination without describing a path.

---

## The book's most significant acknowledged gaps

- Para. 98: "All of us, including those who design them, possess only a limited understanding of their actual functioning" — the document frankly acknowledges that the opacity of AI systems is a fundamental epistemic problem, including for those seeking to govern them.
- Para. 153: No "single model of change or universal solution exists, since there are places and situations that require different responses" — the document acknowledges that the global inequality of AI's effects resists unified prescription.
- Para. 192 (footnote): The "just war" doctrine has been used to justify conflicts that failed its own criteria — an acknowledgment that moral frameworks can be systematically captured by the interests they were meant to constrain.
- Para. 176: The Church's "wound in Christian memory" regarding slavery — an acknowledgment that institutional moral inertia can persist for centuries, implicitly raising the question of whether it is operating now.

# PART 2: LITERARY REVIEW ESSAY

## Performing Continuity: On Whether the Church's Social Doctrine Can Govern Algorithmic Power

*Magnifica Humanitas* arrives at a historically charged moment. Issued on the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*, Leo XIV's first social encyclical positions itself explicitly in the line of descent from Leo XIII: same genre, same occasion, same claim of prophetic pastoral relevance to the new technological conditions reshaping labor, dignity, and social organization. It is a document that knows what it is and where it comes from, and it wears that knowledge as a credential. The question this essay examines is whether the credential holds — whether the Social Doctrine tradition that Leo XIII set in motion in 1891 provides adequate conceptual foundations for governing the algorithmic infrastructure that now constitutes the most consequential distribution of social power since industrialization, or whether the encyclical is performing continuity it cannot fully deliver.

The question matters not as theological score-keeping but because the stakes are real. If the inherited framework is adequate, then the prescriptions *Magnifica Humanitas* derives from it — transparency requirements, subsidiarity-based governance structures, management of data as a common good — have the force of derived necessity behind them. If the framework is inadequate, those same prescriptions are moral exhortations in need of a different justificatory foundation. The difference is not trivial in a document addressed to legislators, entrepreneurs, researchers, and technologists alongside the faithful.

---

The encyclical's most analytically serious observation comes early and quietly. Paragraph 5 notes that "the main drivers of development are private, often transnational, parties that are endowed with resources and the capacity to intervene that surpass those of many Governments." This is not a rhetorical flourish. It is an empirical observation about the structure of power in the AI era, and it identifies the precise condition that makes classical Social Doctrine application difficult. The tradition from Leo XIII through Benedict XVI was developed within a framework that, whatever its other differences, shared a common institutional substrate: states capable of legislation and enforcement; civil society organizations capable of collective action; labor with associational capacity; and some version of an international order capable of producing binding agreements. The principles of subsidiarity, solidarity, and the common good were designed to operate within and through that substrate. They presuppose institutions capable of being reformed, labor capable of being organized, and governments capable of governing.

What para. 5 observes — and what Chapter Three elaborates in paras. 95 and 108 — is that the institutional substrate is under strain precisely where it is most needed. Platform companies with market capitalizations exceeding the GDP of most nations operate across jurisdictions, hold intellectual property in low-tax regimes, employ their most critical labor in jurisdictions without strong labor protections, and are subject to democratic accountability only through the attenuated mechanism of consumer choice. This is not a harder version of the industrial problem that *Rerum Novarum* addressed. It is a structurally different problem: the relevant actors are not subject to the institutional mechanisms through which Social Doctrine has historically been applied.

*Magnifica Humanitas* knows this. The document is not naive. But having named the structural novelty, it proceeds to apply the inherited framework as though the novelty were a matter of scale rather than kind. Chapter Two extends the universal destination of goods to "algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure and data" (para. 67) and applies the subsidiarity principle to "major economic and technological actors" as a *de facto* top tier (para. 71). Both moves are analytically intended and morally defensible as directions of travel. The problem is in the inferential gap between naming the principle and deriving the prescription.

---

Consider subsidiarity, the principle Pius XI developed in *Quadragesimo Anno* and that has become one of Social Doctrine's structural pillars. In its classical formulation, subsidiarity holds that decisions should be made at the lowest level consistent with effective action, and that higher authorities should support rather than supplant lower ones. The institutional image is vertical: individual, family, intermediary organization, state, international body — each with defined competencies, each capable of appealing upward when overwhelmed and of being corrected downward when overreaching.

Para. 71 applies this principle to digital platforms: "The principle of subsidiarity requires that such processes not be imposed from above in an opaque and unilateral manner, but instead be directed toward the common good with transparency, accountability and meaningful forms of participation." The prescription is correct in its direction. But subsidiarity in its classical form requires not just that power be distributed, but that the levels above have the capacity and authority to enforce the distribution. When Pius XI invoked subsidiarity against state totalitarianism, he could rely on civil society, the Church, and eventually international bodies as the corrective mechanisms. When Leo XIV invokes subsidiarity against platform power, the corrective mechanism he implicitly appeals to — states and "transnational institutions" (para. 72) — is precisely what para. 5 established cannot match the resources of the actors to be corrected.

This is not a fatal objection to subsidiarity as a principle. It is an observation that the principle's institutional preconditions are not met in the present case, and that a document invoking the principle has an obligation to address this gap. *Magnifica Humanitas* does not. It invokes subsidiarity; it does not argue that subsidiarity can operate in the conditions the document itself describes.

---

The extension of the universal destination of goods to data and algorithms (para. 67) faces a different but related problem. The classical principle holds that the goods of the earth — material, rivalrous, depletable — are given by God for the sustenance of all, and that private property rights, while legitimate, are subordinate to this universal destination. The theological foundation is in the original gift of creation; the economic implication is that those goods which are necessary for human sustenance cannot be permanently alienated to private hands without social consequence.

Data and algorithmic systems share some features with physical goods — they can be owned, withheld, leveraged for power, and used to exclude — but they differ in ways that are morally relevant to how the principle applies. Data is non-rivalrous: my access to a dataset does not deplete yours. Code is replicable at near-zero marginal cost. A large language model trained on a billion documents does not consume those documents in the way a mine consumes ore. These structural differences do not make data harmless or ownership of it morally irrelevant — para. 108's observation that "data is the product of many contributors and should not be treated as something to be sold off or entrusted to a select few" is both accurate and important. But they do mean that the governance framework adequate to data cannot simply be derived from the framework adequate to physical goods. The universal destination of goods principle needs a new argument for its extension to non-rival information goods, not just an assertion that the extension follows.

The encyclical is aware of this at some level: para. 67 explicitly calls for "creative" thinking about managing data as a common good. But the document does not supply that creative thinking; it names its absence and moves on. This is the foundation problem in microcosm: the inherited principle is invoked, its inadequacy to the new case is gestured at, and the needed conceptual development is deferred rather than attempted.

---

There is a more generous reading of what the encyclical is doing, and fairness requires stating it clearly. *Magnifica Humanitas* is not primarily an academic treatise in political economy or AI governance. It is a pastoral letter to a global Church, and its function — like that of its predecessors — is prophetic rather than legislative: to name what is morally at stake, to identify the directions in which justice requires movement, and to summon moral energy for the journey, without pretending to specify every institutional detail of the destination. Interpreted in this light, the document's principled extensions are not claims that subsidiarity and universal destination already cover AI governance — they are claims about the values that should govern whatever institutional arrangements are ultimately developed. The framework is aspirational, not operational.

This reading is defensible, and it is arguably how Leo XIV intends the document. But it creates its own analytical problem: if the prescriptions are aspirational rather than derived, then the claim of continuity with the Social Doctrine tradition is doing less work than it appears to do. The tradition's authority derives in part from the rigor with which its principles were developed and the precision with which they have been applied to specific institutional arrangements. *Rerum Novarum* did not merely assert that workers had dignity; it derived from that dignity specific claims about fair wages, the right of association, and the limits of state intervention. The derivations were contestable, but they were derivations. They gave the document purchase on the specific institutions it was criticizing.

*Magnifica Humanitas* is less derivationally rigorous in its treatment of AI governance than *Rerum Novarum* was in its treatment of industrial labor — not because Leo XIV is less careful than Leo XIII, but because the institutional arrangements adequate to AI governance do not yet exist and cannot therefore be specified. The document knows this. Para. 153 explicitly acknowledges that no universal solution exists. But acknowledging the gap while proceeding in the genre of derivational Social Doctrine creates a mismatch between the document's rhetorical register and its actual analytical situation.

---

The single most powerful passage in the encyclical is also the one most resistant to the continuity argument. Para. 99 — the phenomenological account of what AI lacks — argues that current AI systems "do not undergo experiences, do not possess a body, do not feel joy or pain, do not mature through relationships and do not know from within what love, work, friendship or responsibility mean." The account is philosophically careful, and its implications for the document's governance prescriptions are profound. If AI systems cannot bear moral responsibility, then the moral responsibility for what they do is distributed across designers, developers, deployers, users, and investors in ways that the inherited category of "employer" — and the labor framework built on it — cannot capture. Para. 104 follows correctly: "ethical discernment cannot be limited to asking whether we are using a system for good or bad purposes; it must also examine how that system is designed and what vision of the human person and society is embedded in the data and models that guide it."

This is a genuinely new moral category — design ethics at the level of training data and model architecture — and it has no direct precedent in the Social Doctrine tradition. The tradition has principles about the moral responsibilities of employers, states, investors, and workers. It does not have principles adequate to the moral responsibility of the team that fine-tuned a model on biased data, or the venture capitalist who funded a company knowing its product would be deployed in jurisdictions without privacy protections, or the developer who optimized for engagement metrics knowing those metrics would amplify inflammatory content. These are not failures of individual moral actors making clear choices; they are diffuse harms embedded in systems that no single actor fully controls or understands — as para. 98 acknowledges.

The Social Doctrine tradition has a concept for structurally produced harm: John Paul II's "structures of sin" (*Sollicitudo Rei Socialis*, para. 36), cited approvingly in para. 79. But structures of sin in the Social Doctrine tradition are identified and condemned — they are not governed by a principled framework for attributing responsibility and designing corrective mechanisms. The concept diagnoses the phenomenon; it does not provide the institutional tools for response.

---

*Magnifica Humanitas* ends with the Magnificat — Mary's hymn of reversal in which God scatters the proud, casts down the mighty, and lifts up the lowly. It is an image of structural transformation, not merely moral exhortation: the redistribution of power from those who command it to those who are subjected to it. Read as a political text — which it is — the Magnificat implies that adequate Christian response to the concentration of AI power would be redistributive at the level of ownership and control, not merely regulatory at the level of procedure.

The encyclical never makes this inference explicit. Para. 108 comes closest: data should be managed as "a common or shared good," and ownership of data "cannot be left solely in private hands." But the mechanism — nationalization? mandatory open-source licensing? data commons governed by international bodies? — is not specified. The Magnificat's radicalism is invoked spiritually but not applied structurally.

This is, perhaps, where the document is most honestly itself: a prophetic letter that names what is morally required without providing the institutional architecture to achieve it, that performs continuity with a tradition because the tradition is genuinely relevant, while acknowledging — in its own gaps and deferrals — that the tradition's resources are not yet fully adequate to the challenge it faces. The foundation problem is real. *Magnifica Humanitas* does not solve it. What the document does, with considerable moral seriousness and analytical honesty, is identify where the problem is hardest and where the work remains to be done.

The residual question it leaves unanswered — and that the Social Doctrine tradition has not yet answered — is this: when the institutional preconditions for a moral principle's application do not exist, is the principled response to invoke the principle anyway (betting on prophetic force) or to build new institutional forms before invoking the principles that require them? The Church has historically done the former. The evidence from industrial history suggests this was not wrong. Whether it is adequate to the pace and scale of algorithmic transformation is the question the next encyclical will have to answer.
