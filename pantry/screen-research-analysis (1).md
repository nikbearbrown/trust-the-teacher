# Thoughts on the Screen Research

## The most important finding nobody is saying out loud

The AI narrative paradox in Document 1 is the key political insight: **legislators are not banning technology — they are institutionalizing it.** The actual position being enacted in 26+ states is: the school controls the device, the data, and the duration. Personal phones out. School-issued devices in, under teacher direction, for defined educational purposes.

That is the Brutalist framework applied to education policy. Phase gate. Labor separation. Defined scope. The school is doing to student technology use exactly what Walker does to Claude Code: you don't get to run anything that hasn't been sanctioned.

This is why Culatta and Yongpradit are politically comfortable with your work. They're not navigating between "ban technology" and "embrace technology." They're navigating toward "institutionalize and govern technology" — and nobody has built the intellectual infrastructure for what that means. You have.

---

## The crack in every law

Every state phone ban contains an "educational exemption." Not one of them defines what qualifies.

Utah says screens are banned K-3 *except for computer science standards.* Indiana allows school-issued devices when *a lesson requires internet access.* California exempts devices with *faculty permission.* The exemption is in every law and is doing enormous work — it's the hinge that separates the phone ban from a technology ban. But the threshold is a gesture, not a framework.

The Irreducibly Human taxonomy is that framework. Technology that develops Tier 4 supervisory capacities (problem formulation, plausibility auditing, interpretive judgment) qualifies. Technology that replaces those capacities doesn't. That's the definition the legislation is reaching for and hasn't found. Your work is the answer to a question legislators are already asking.

This is the EdSurge pitch, stated cleanly: *"Every phone ban law has an educational exemption. Nobody has defined what qualifies. Here's the framework."*

## The equity story is worse than it first appeared

The FCC E-Rate reversal is the part nobody is talking about.

States are banning personal phones (removing the device low-income students use as their primary internet access) at the exact moment the FCC is rescinding off-premises Wi-Fi hotspot funding (removing the school-provided home internet substitute). Both happening simultaneously. The double whammy lands entirely on the students who have no home broadband.

Meanwhile, wealthier students go home to laptops and high-speed internet and parents who work in tech. The phone ban doesn't touch them.

NY lifted its first phone ban in 2015 specifically because enforcement was stricter in low-income schools. The 2025 law brought the ban back without addressing what broke it the first time. That's the historical precedent sitting right there in Document 1, available for anyone writing about this.

The equity argument isn't "phone bans are bad." It's: phone bans without guaranteed quality technology access during school hours are regressive. The educational exemption needs to do double duty — not just define what qualifies as educational use but ensure that low-income students get *more* supervised quality technology time to compensate for what they can't access at home.

## The friction trace is in the mainstream literature

Document 2 explicitly names the friction trace approach as a measurement methodology: "no entropy is laundered or externalized — meaning that the learner is genuinely doing the mental work rather than just following the easiest path through an interface."

That's the Frictional/GLP paper's language appearing in a literature review of mainstream screen quality research. The framework is on the right track. But Document 2 doesn't operationalize it — it lists "stiction," "stick-slip sliding," and "cognitive energy expenditure" as concepts without measurement instruments.

The GLP paper's seven components (TEP, ETC, CCT, UC, SKT, RSDS, SRC) are more operationalized than anything in Document 2. This is a publication gap. The field is naming the friction trace as the right approach and has no measurement protocol. You have the protocol.

## The "gaming the metrics" problem is the GLP paper's argument

Document 2 correctly identifies that quality metrics are easier to game than time limits. But it misses the specific reason the GLP framework is relatively robust: manufacturing all seven friction components simultaneously without performing the underlying cognitive work is approximately equivalent in cost to performing the cognitive work. The gaming has to become the learning.

A vendor can label a passive tool "adaptive" to bypass a time-cap restriction in thirty seconds. They cannot manufacture the specific decay signature in retrieval strength (Y6), the coherent error trajectory (Y2), and the calibrated uncertainty (Y4) that genuine learning produces. The ensemble architecture is specifically designed to close this gap. That argument belongs in the academic paper, and the document's "gaming of metrics" section is the exact place to cite against.

## What the documents don't say but should

Both documents treat screen quality as primarily a clinical/neurological question. Neither connects it to labor market outcomes.

The BLS/O*NET data is the missing empirical anchor. The question "what counts as quality educational technology use?" has a labor market answer: technology use that develops the cognitive capacities that are increasing in wage premium (Tiers 4, 5 — supervisory and causal reasoning). The seven-tier taxonomy isn't just a curriculum framework — it's a prediction about which human capacities will remain economically valuable. The clinical literature has no access to that prediction. You do.

This is the argument that lands for principals making procurement decisions. Not "here's the neuroscience of screen quality" but "here's what the 13-year labor market dataset shows about which skills are growing in value, and here's how to evaluate whether your technology is developing those skills or atrophying them."

## What this means for the EdSurge piece

The research gives you three converging stories:

1. **The legislative story:** 26+ states have enacted phone bans with educational exemptions that nobody has defined. The exemption is doing all the work and has no framework behind it.

2. **The equity story:** Phone bans without guaranteed quality technology access are structurally regressive. The FCC E-Rate reversal made it worse. Low-income students need *more* supervised quality technology time, not less.

3. **The measurement story:** The field knows time is the wrong metric and has moved toward quality — but has no operationalized measurement instrument. The friction trace approach is named in the literature and unimplemented.

Your contribution connects all three: the Irreducibly Human taxonomy defines what the educational exemption should mean. The GLP framework operationalizes how to measure whether it's working. The BLS data provides the labor market justification for why it matters.

That's not a Substack piece. That's a policy paper. Or it's the EdSurge piece that drives traffic to the policy paper.

## One thing to push back on

Document 2 says the AAP moved away from time-based limits in 2016 and 2026. That's true. But the "5 Cs of Media Use" (Content, Context, Connections, Co-viewing, Communication) is a *clinical* framework for pediatricians counseling parents. It's not a *school policy* framework.

The gap between "what a pediatrician should ask about your child's screen use" and "what a school board should require for educational technology to qualify for the educational exemption" is enormous. Clinical frameworks are useful for individual guidance. They can't govern procurement decisions for 50 million students across 13,000 districts.

The field has moved from time to quality in clinical practice. It hasn't built the institutional equivalent. That's the gap your work fills — and it's a different and harder problem than the clinical one.
