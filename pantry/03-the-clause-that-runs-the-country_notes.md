## 1. The Opening Hook — How Many States, How Many Definitions

By the close of 2025, somewhere between 31 and 35 states had enacted statutes or state-board policies restricting student cell-phone use in K-12 schools, with 22 enacted in 2025 alone. The counts vary by source and by what counts as a "ban" — a binding statute, a board regulation tied to state aid, or a governor's executive order with implementation guidance. The headline number most commonly cited in mid-2025 is **31 states plus DC** (KFF, September 2025); by January 2026 the count was 35 (ABC News tracker), and ExcelinEd reported 28 with full-day commitments by January 2026.

What every one of these laws has in common is a carve-out — for "educational purposes," "instructional purposes," "academic purposes," "specific educational use," or some variant. What none of them shares is a definition. Every state writes its own phrase. Every district reads it differently. Every teacher is left to interpret it in the moment.

This is the chapter's puzzle. The exemption is doing all the policy work — distinguishing the device-as-distraction from the device-as-instrument. And the policy work has been outsourced to whoever happens to be standing at the front of the room.

## 2. The Statutes — Verbatim Educational-Exemption Language

The point of this section is to put the *actual* phrases side by side and let the variance show. The variance is the chapter's central claim: the same idea is expressed seven different ways in seven different states, and the seven ways are not interchangeable.

### Florida — HB 379 (2023), now Fla. Stat. § 1006.07(2)(o)
Effective July 1, 2023, codified at Ch. 2023-36.

> "A student may not use a wireless communications device during instructional time, except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes."

The statute also requires teachers to "designate an area for wireless communications devices during instructional time," and prohibits district-network access to social media platforms "except when expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes."

Note: the operative phrase carries three modifiers — *expressly*, *solely*, *for educational purposes* — and none of the three is defined further in the bill text. The 2025 update extends the prohibition to the entire school day (not just instructional time) for elementary and middle schools.

- [Florida Senate: HB 379 (2023)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379)
- [Florida House: CS/HB 379 bill detail](https://www.flhouse.gov/Sections/Bills/billsdetail.aspx?BillId=77056)
- [CS/HB 379 staff analysis (PDF)](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2023/379/Analyses/h0379z.CIS.PDF)

### California — AB 3216 (2024), Phone-Free School Act, codified at Cal. Educ. Code § 48901.7
Signed September 23, 2024.

The statute is a *mandate-the-policy* law: every district must develop its own policy by July 1, 2026. The exceptions enumerated in the statute itself are narrower than Florida's — and pointedly do **not** use the phrase "educational purposes" as a stand-alone category. The exemptions enumerated in the bill text are:

> (1) In the case of an emergency, or in response to a perceived threat of danger.
> (2) When a teacher or administrator of the school grants permission to a pupil to possess or use a smartphone, subject to any reasonable limitation imposed by that teacher or administrator.
> (3) When a licensed physician and surgeon determines that the possession or use of a smartphone is necessary for the health or well-being of the pupil.
> (4) When the possession or use of a smartphone is required in a pupil's individualized education program.

Exception (2) is the one doing the educational-purposes work — but it does so by handing the entire judgment to the teacher or administrator with no criterion at all. The teacher *is* the rule.

### Louisiana — Act 313 (2024), La. R.S. 17:239
Effective 2024-2025 school year.

The statute is the strictest of the early adopters — there is **no** explicit "educational purposes" exemption in the statutory text. The exceptions are limited to:

- Required use under an IEP, IAP, Section 504 plan, or Individualized Health Plan.
- An emergency, defined as "an actual or imminent threat to public health or safety which may result in loss of life, injury, or property damage."

What this means in practice: when Louisiana teachers use student devices for instruction, they are operating outside the statute's enumerated exceptions. Districts (Tangipahoa, Assumption, Caddo) have written implementation guidance that quietly assumes some teacher-directed use is permitted, but the statute does not authorize it.

### Virginia — Executive Order 33 (July 9, 2024), Governor Youngkin
Followed by VDOE guidance.

EO 33 directed VDOE to issue "Cell Phone-Free Education" guidance for K-12 public schools. The final guidance defines the requirement as "bell-to-bell" — phones off and stored from the first bell to dismissal, including lunch and passing periods. The allowable exceptions:

- "Cell phones are not an option for instructional use." (Final guidance, December 2024.)
- Exemptions must be written into a 504 plan, IEP, or Individualized Health Care Plan.

Virginia is the only state where the guidance explicitly refuses the "educational purposes" carve-out as a category. The state has chosen, in writing, that the device itself is not an instructional instrument. The trade-off: a clearer rule, harder enforcement when teachers in fact need devices for translation, accessibility, or AI-assisted lessons.

### Texas — HB 1481 (2025), codified at Tex. Educ. Code § 37.082(b) [verify exact subsection number]
Signed June 20, 2025. Districts must adopt policy by September 18, 2025.

The exemptions enumerated in the statute (as relayed through district implementation guidance — full statutory text could not be opened directly in this research pass and is flagged for verification against the LegiScan/Capitol PDF):

> "The student's use is necessary for implementation of the student's individualized education program, a 504 plan, or a similar program or plan; the student's use is required due to a documented need based on a directive from a qualified physician; or the student's use is necessary to comply with a health or safety requirement imposed by law or as part of the District or campus safety protocols."

Texas, like Louisiana, does not include a stand-alone "educational purposes" exemption in the statute itself. The term "personal communication device" is also defined to *exclude* devices the district issues, which is the implicit pathway through which classroom AI use enters: bring the Chromebook, not the phone.

### Ohio — HB 250 (2024), codified at Ohio Rev. Code § 3313.7210 [verify section]
Signed May 15, 2024. Districts must adopt policy by July 1, 2025.

The statute is a *mandate-the-policy* law. It does not itself ban phones — it requires every district and community school to adopt a policy "minimizing cell phone use during school hours." The exceptions districts must provide for include:

- Students who require a cell phone to monitor a health concern.
- Students for whom phone use is needed for "student learning as determined by school officials."

The Ohio formulation is the most explicit *delegation*: "student learning as determined by school officials." It writes the open-endedness into the statute itself. ODEW released a model policy in 2024 (Ohio School Boards Association tracks this) that districts can adopt as a default.

### Arkansas — Act 122 of 2025 ("Bell to Bell, No Cell Act"), SB 142
Signed February 20, 2025; effective 2025-2026 school year.

Arkansas takes a distinct path. The statute does **not** include "educational purposes" among the required exemptions. Required exemptions are:

- Health reasons (documented).
- Individualized Education Programs.
- After-school extracurricular activities.
- Emergencies.

The pathway for instructional use is explicit: *school-issued* devices are exempt from the ban; personal devices are not. If a lesson plan requires technology, the school distributes and monitors the technology. The personal device is fully removed from the instructional toolkit.

### South Carolina — General Appropriations Bill H. 5100 (2024), Proviso 1.103; SC State Board of Education policy (September 2024)
Effective January 2025 semester.

South Carolina is the most specific. The statute conditions state aid on adoption of a State Board policy. The policy defines "Classroom Use" as:

> "The use of a Personal Electronic Communication Device that is explicitly approved by the District Superintendent or his/her designee in writing, and the approval must include alignment with a standards-aligned educational objective that cannot be reasonably achieved using district-owned devices."

This is, to my reading, the **only** state-level definition in the country that ties the educational exemption to two operational tests: (1) *written* prior approval from a named administrator, and (2) demonstration that the *district-owned device* could not reasonably accomplish the same objective. It is the closest thing to a teachable rule. It is also so administratively heavy that one wonders how often a teacher actually invokes it.

### New York — Education Law § 2803 (added by FY2026 budget bill, Part C; Article VII), signed May 9, 2025
Effective 2025-2026 school year.

New York's statute is the most recently enacted of the major state laws and contains the most explicit "educational purpose" language. The statute creates a new section of Education Law and authorizes exceptions for:

- Authorization by a teacher, principal, or the school district, charter school, or BOCES "for a specific educational purpose."
- Students who require an internet-enabled device to manage a medical condition.
- Required by IEP or Section 504 plan.
- Translation, family caregiving, and emergencies.

The operative phrase — "specific educational purpose" — is not further defined in the statute. NYC DOE Chancellor's Regulation A-413, updated July 24, 2025, implements the state law for the nation's largest school district. A-413 likewise authorizes principals or designees to grant exceptions "for an educational purpose" but does not define what qualifies.

### Indiana — SB 78 (2026), bell-to-bell ban
Signed by Gov. Mike Braun March 5, 2026 (ceremonial signing May 13). Effective July 1, 2026. Replaces and extends the earlier Indiana SB 185 (2024), which was instructional-time-only.

Exceptions in SB 78 (per legislative summaries; full enrolled text flagged for verification):
- Emergencies.
- Medical needs.
- IEP or 504 plan requirements.
- Translation needs.
- Audio recording for notetaking.
- Students can use *home-brought* laptops or tablets "at a teacher's direction" — but this provision sunsets July 1, 2028.

The Indiana sunset clause on teacher-directed home device use is unique. It signals legislative awareness that the policy is provisional — but offers no plan for what replaces it.

### The variance summary

Lay the phrases next to each other and the texture of the chapter becomes visible:

| State | Operative phrase | Who decides | Documentation required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Florida | "expressly directed by a teacher solely for educational purposes" | Teacher | None |
| California | "When a teacher or administrator … grants permission … subject to any reasonable limitation" | Teacher or administrator | "Reasonable limitation" |
| Louisiana | (no educational-purposes exemption) | N/A | N/A |
| Virginia | (no educational-purposes exemption — explicit refusal) | N/A | N/A |
| Texas | (no educational-purposes exemption; district devices excluded from definition) | N/A — by definition | Per IEP/504/health |
| Ohio | "student learning as determined by school officials" | Each district decides | Per district policy |
| Arkansas | (no educational-purposes exemption; school-issued devices exempt) | N/A — by definition | Per IEP/health |
| South Carolina | "explicitly approved … in writing … aligned with a standards-aligned educational objective that cannot be reasonably achieved using district-owned devices" | Superintendent or designee | Written approval + showing of necessity |
| New York | "authorization … for a specific educational purpose" | Teacher, principal, district, or BOCES | None specified in statute |
| Indiana | "at a teacher's direction" (sunsets 2028) | Teacher | None |

Ten state approaches. Six distinct phrasings of the exemption. Zero shared definition. The same student moving across state lines hits a different rule. The same teacher moving across district lines hits a different application of the same rule. The same lesson — Claude as a writing tutor — is permitted in Florida (if the teacher says so), permitted in New York (if the principal authorizes), permitted in South Carolina (if the superintendent signs off in writing and the district Chromebook can't do it), and impermissible in Virginia.

## 3. The District-Level Implementation — What Schools Wrote In When the Legislature Wrote Nothing

The exemption is undefined in statute. Districts have had to fill the gap. The fills are wildly inconsistent and reveal — better than any abstract argument — how unworkable the legislature's outsourcing is.

### LAUSD (Los Angeles Unified)

LAUSD implemented an all-day ban effective February 18, 2025. The board policy specifies:
- Cellphones, smartwatches, and earbuds turned off and stored on campus during the school day.
- Exception for IEP/504/medical accommodation.
- Exception for emergencies — but "there isn't a formal definition of 'emergency,'" per LAUSD Chief of School Operations Andrés Chait, "common sense applies."

What LAUSD does **not** publish: a definition of "educational purposes." The earlier (pre-2025) LAUSD policy limited social media access to "educational purposes" but the new policy mostly removes the device entirely. The classroom-AI question — when a teacher *wants* student phone access for an AI-tutor lesson — is unanswered in district guidance.

- [EdSource: What parents and students need to know about LAUSD's cellphone ban](https://edsource.org/2025/what-parents-and-students-need-to-know-about-lausds-cellphone-ban/726516)
- [LAist: One semester evaluation of LAUSD's ban](https://laist.com/news/education/los-angeles-unified-phone-ban-end-of-first-semester-summer-starts)

### NYC DOE — Chancellor's Regulation A-413 (updated July 24, 2025)

Principal-level discretion. The regulation requires each principal to establish a written school-based policy "consistent with this Regulation" setting forth:
1. "the circumstances under which communication devices, computing devices, and portable music and entertainment systems may be used on school property"
2. "the procedures for collection, storage and return of personal electronic devices"

The state law passed the educational-purposes definition to the district. The district passed it to the principal. The principal — depending on school — passes it to the teacher. There are roughly 1,600 NYC public schools. There are roughly 1,600 implementations.

### Chicago Public Schools

Illinois has *not* yet passed a statewide ban as of mid-2026 (the Pritzker-backed bill is still in progress as of April 2026). CPS gives principal-level discretion, which means roughly 600 schools have roughly 600 implementations. The CPS Acceptable Use Policy governs *district* devices; personal devices are governed by school-level policies that range from full bans to free-use cafeteria-only rules.

This is the country's third-largest district operating without statewide guidance. It is the natural-experiment view of what "principal discretion" actually looks like at scale: enormous variance and effectively no published district-level definition of educational use.

### Houston ISD

Houston ISD policy for 2025-2026, in response to Texas HB 1481:

> "Students may not use personal communication devices on school property during the instructional day. The instructional day is defined as: From the time a student arrives on campus in the morning until the final period of the day concludes."

Houston explicitly limits the ban to "personal" devices — district-issued devices are exempt. The phrase "educational purposes" does not appear in the published district guidance. The pathway through which classroom AI enters Houston instruction is the district-issued Chromebook, not the personal phone.

### Miami-Dade County Public Schools

Florida statewide law (HB 379 and 2025 update) covers M-DCPS. District parent handbook for 2024-2025 references the statutory prohibition and the teacher-direction exemption verbatim. The district does *not* publish a more specific definition of "educational purposes." The exemption sits at the same level of abstraction at which the legislature left it.

- [Miami-Dade District Parent Handbook 2024-2025 (PDF)](https://api.dadeschools.net/SMAv2/1761/Handbook/District%20Parent%20Handbook%202024-2025.pdf)

### The pattern

Across LAUSD, NYC, Chicago, Houston, and Miami-Dade — five of the country's ten largest districts — the district-level implementation does **not** add definitional clarity to the state statute. The phrase passes through. The administrator passes the judgment down to the principal. The principal passes it to the teacher. Statutory vagueness becomes administrative vagueness becomes classroom-by-classroom inconsistency.

This is not malice. It is what happens when a rule cannot be specified at the level the rule operates.

## 4. International Comparisons — How Other Jurisdictions Wrote the Exemption

### France — 2018 ban (Law No. 2018-698 of August 3, 2018)

The original phone ban — the model bipartisan policymakers in the U.S. invoke most frequently. France's law banned smartphones and "other electronic communication devices" from kindergartens, elementary schools, and middle schools (collèges), covering students up to roughly age 15. The exemptions:

- Students with disabilities.
- Emergencies.
- "Within the framework of explicit and specific pedagogical use, supervised by the teachers" (*dans le cadre d'usages pédagogiques explicites et spécifiques, encadrés par les enseignants*).

This is the most teacherful definition in any major jurisdiction. Three modifiers: **explicit**, **specific**, and **teacher-supervised**. The French formulation does what no U.S. state statute does — it requires the educational use to be *pre-declared* (explicit) and *bounded* (specific). It still does not define what counts as "pedagogical," but it imposes structural conditions on the invocation that no U.S. law does.

The internal rules of each school establish the exception conditions, which means the rule has a definite locus of accountability: *l'établissement*, the school institution, owns the definition.

- [Library of Congress: France Adopts Law Banning Cell Phone Use at School (October 2018)](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2018-10-30/france-government-adopts-law-banning-cell-phone-use-at-school/)
- [CNN: France bans smartphones from schools](https://edition.cnn.com/2018/07/31/europe/france-smartphones-school-ban-intl/index.html)

### United Kingdom — DfE non-statutory guidance (February 2024; updated January 2026)

The 2024 Department for Education guidance is *non-statutory* — it doesn't compel schools, but it strongly recommends them to prohibit "the use of mobile phones and other smart technology with similar functionality throughout the school day, including during lessons, the time between lessons, breaktimes and lunchtime."

The 2026 update sharpened the language: "All schools should be mobile phone-free environments by default; anything other than this should be by exception only."

The exception conditions in the DfE guidance — like the U.S. — are not centrally defined. Schools write their own. The Children's Commissioner's April 2025 survey on school phone policies found enormous variance.

### Australia — state-by-state (Victoria 2020; Queensland 2021; SA 2023; NSW 2023)

Australia is the international comparison the U.S. most closely resembles — bans implemented state by state, no single national definition. Victoria was first (2020), followed by Queensland (2021), South Australia (2023), and NSW (2023). Each state writes its own implementation.

The Australian exemption pattern: medical and educational reasons, with "exceptional circumstances" allowed at school discretion. The South Australia Department for Education guidance ("Phones off while school's on") allows individual case-by-case exemption decisions at the school level.

NSW Department of Education guidance does not centrally define "educational purpose" — it directs schools to write local policy.

- [SchoolNews Australia: Off and away — mobile phone bans](https://www.school-news.com.au/administration/off-and-away-mobile-phone-bans/)

### The international pattern

Three approaches to the educational exemption:
1. **Pre-declared and supervised** (France): the use must be explicit, specific, and supervised — structural conditions short of substantive definition.
2. **School-defined** (UK, Australia, U.S.): each school writes its own. National rule is silent.
3. **Refused** (Virginia, partially): the device is simply not an instructional tool.

No major jurisdiction has produced a substantive, operational definition of educational use that scales across schools. The closest is France's structural framing. No one — anywhere — has solved the problem.

## 5. The Constitutional Question — Statutory Vagueness as Delegation to the Teacher

The chapter doesn't argue that any of these statutes is *unconstitutionally* vague. The point is more pedagogically useful than that: examining the doctrine clarifies what kind of question the educational-purposes exemption actually is.

### Vagueness doctrine — the framework

The void-for-vagueness doctrine, rooted in the Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, holds that laws must be written with enough clarity that:
1. Ordinary people can understand what conduct is prohibited, and
2. Enforcement is not arbitrary or discriminatory.

The classic statement, from *Grayned v. City of Rockford* (1972) and a long line of subsequent cases: a law is impermissibly vague when it "impermissibly delegates basic policy matters to policemen, judges, and juries for resolution on an ad hoc and subjective basis, with the attendant dangers of arbitrary and discriminatory application."

- [Cornell Wex: vagueness doctrine](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/vagueness_doctrine)
- [Cornell Wex: void for vagueness](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/void_for_vagueness)
- [Constitution Annotated: Void for Vagueness overview](https://constitution.congress.gov/browse/essay/amdt5-8-1/ALDE_00013739/)
- [Virginia Law Review: Vagueness and Nondelegation](https://virginialawreview.org/articles/vagueness-and-nondelegation/)
- [Texas Law Review: Vagueness as Impossibility](https://texaslawreview.org/vagueness-as-impossibility/)

### Why the educational-exemption laws aren't actually void

Two reasons. First, vagueness doctrine has been most rigorously applied to *criminal* statutes, where the consequences for the defendant are severe. School technology policies are civil/administrative. The bar for vagueness invalidation is lower. Second, the school-discipline context has historically tolerated significant administrator discretion — *Tinker v. Des Moines* (1969) and its progeny establish a regime in which school authorities have substantial leeway to make in-the-moment judgments about student conduct.

So no successful federal challenge is likely.

### Why the doctrine still illuminates the problem

The doctrine names what the educational-purposes exemption *is*: a delegation of policy authority from the legislature to the front-line enforcement actor. In a criminal statute, the worry is "arbitrary and discriminatory application." In a phone-ban exemption, the same worry attaches — the application falls harder on Black boys in high-suspension schools (Figlio & Özek), on students whose teachers haven't been trained to recognize substantive AI use, on schools that lack the institutional bandwidth to write internal definitions.

The doctrine's underlying intuition — that a rule which cannot specify what it forbids is a rule which hands the forbidding to whoever happens to be enforcing it — applies precisely here. The legislature has, in effect, written: *the rule is whatever the teacher decides*. The legal system tolerates this. The pedagogical system shouldn't.

This is the chapter's pivot. The legal frame says the exemption is permissible. The pedagogical frame says it is the central problem: a rule whose enforcement depends entirely on the trained judgment of the person enforcing it requires that the person enforcing it be trained.

### Adjacent jurisprudence — *Tinker*, school discretion, and AG opinions

The chapter doesn't need a deep case-law dive, but it can briefly note:
- *Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District*, 393 U.S. 503 (1969) — establishes substantial school administrator discretion over student conduct.
- *Bethel School District v. Fraser*, 478 U.S. 675 (1986) — extends school discretion to determination of what is "educationally appropriate."
- *Hazelwood School District v. Kuhlmeier*, 484 U.S. 260 (1988) — schools can regulate speech "reasonably related to legitimate pedagogical concerns" without precise prior definition.

The line through these cases is that *Hazelwood*'s "legitimate pedagogical concerns" formulation is the closest thing to a definition of educational purposes in U.S. constitutional law — and it is, deliberately, open-textured. The Court has approved an *unspecified* educational-purposes standard for forty years, on the explicit grounds that the alternative — judges defining education — is worse. The phone ban statutes are an inheritance of that posture. They are constitutional precisely because they are vague. That doesn't make them workable.

## 6. The Pedagogical Frameworks That Try to Fill the Gap — and Why They Don't

The research and policy community has produced several attempts to specify what "educational technology use" actually means. Each is useful. None is operational at classroom scale.

### AAP 5 Cs of Media Use

The American Academy of Pediatrics' Center of Excellence on Social Media and Youth Mental Health published the 5 Cs framework as a tool for pediatricians counseling families. The 5 Cs are:
- **Child** — who is this child, what are their individual characteristics and vulnerabilities?
- **Content** — what are they consuming?
- **Calm** — is media being used to regulate emotion?
- **Crowding out** — what other activities is media displacing?
- **Communication** — how is the family talking about media?

(Note: the TIKTOC spec lists the older Three Cs / 5 Cs formulation as "Content, Context, Connections, Co-viewing, Communication" — that earlier version is associated with Lisa Guernsey's work (*Into the Minds of Babes*, 2007; *Screen Time*, 2012; *Tap, Click, Read*, 2015) and was the precursor to the AAP framework. Worth picking the formulation the chapter uses; both are valid. The 2024 AAP version is "Child, Content, Calm, Crowding Out, Communication.")

- [AAP: The 5 Cs of Media Use](https://www.aap.org/en/patient-care/media-and-children/center-of-excellence-on-social-media-and-youth-mental-health/5cs-of-media-use/)
- [AAP News: 5 C's framework provides new approach](https://publications.aap.org/aapnews/news/28769/5-C-s-framework-provides-new-approach-for)
- [HealthyChildren.org: Kids & Screen Time — How to Use the 5 C's](https://www.healthychildren.org/English/family-life/Media/Pages/kids-and-screen-time-how-to-use-the-5-cs-of-media-guidance.aspx)

**Why it doesn't govern classroom decisions:** the 5 Cs were built for pediatricians counseling individual families. They produce a structured *conversation*, not a real-time enforcement rule. A teacher cannot, mid-lesson, conduct a 5-C interview with each student about their device use.

### Screen-value frameworks — Culatta and ISTE

Richard Culatta, CEO of ISTE+ASCD (the proposed publisher of this book), has argued for years that the right framing is "screen value, not screen time." From his ISTE platform: technology should be used to make students "knowledge constructors and problem solvers" rather than to present content on a screen.

The framework is useful as a *direction*. It is not operational. "Is this screen-time use producing value?" is the same question as "is this use educational?" — restated.

- [NASBE Interview: Richard Culatta](https://www.nasbe.org/the-nasbe-interview-richard-culatta/)
- [EdWeek: Making Better Ed-Tech Choices — Q&A With Richard Culatta of ISTE](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/making-better-ed-tech-choices-q-a-with-richard-culatta-of-iste/2018/06)
- [ISTE: ISTE CEO shares his vision](https://iste.org/blog/iste-ceo-shares-his-vision-for-iste)

### "Productive friction" — Hess, Reich, others

The screen-quality research literature (Lisa Guernsey, Justin Reich, Rich Halverson, Frederick Hess in various policy work) coalesces around a concept of "productive friction" — technology use is educational when it requires the student to exert cognitive effort, not when it removes effort. This connects directly to the Bastani PNAS 2025 finding on cognitive offloading.

The trouble: "is the student exerting cognitive effort?" is a question only the teacher in the room can answer in real time. The framework, properly applied, recapitulates the conclusion the chapter is moving toward: the only mechanism that operationalizes the distinction is a trained teacher.

### The convergence

Every framework points the same direction. The educational-use distinction is real (it isn't arbitrary — there is a meaningful difference between an AI tutor that requires reasoning and a YouTube recommendation feed that drains attention). But the distinction is fine-grained enough that it can only be made in context, by someone with relevant training, in the moment. No legislature can write the rule. No district can write the rule. The frameworks are useful as *training materials* for teachers. They are not enforcement instruments.

This is the affirmative claim. The exemption is not under-defined because legislators were lazy. It is under-defined because the distinction it tries to capture is irreducibly contextual.

## 7. The Equity Tilt and the Chapter's Pivot

The exemption works *better in schools that have already invested in teacher training*. This is the equity dimension that closes the chapter.

### The training-investment gap

- **SETDA's 2025 national research:** fewer than 40% of local education agencies use Title II-A federal funds for technology-related professional learning. Only nine states prioritize these funds for technology training. The majority of Title II-A PD funding goes to short-term workshops rather than sustained development.
- **EdWeek's reporting (November 2025):** billions of federal dollars are spent on teacher training; less than half goes to tech PD.
- **OECD TALIS 2024 (cited in TIKTOC):** 70% of teachers not using AI report lacking the skills or knowledge to do so.
- **Gallup-Walton 2025:** teachers using AI weekly save 5.9 hours per week — but the savings accrue to teachers who received training. Teachers in under-resourced schools who received no training save nothing.

The pattern is consistent across every dataset. Training is correlated with district wealth. Districts that can pay for sustained, subject-specific AI training are also the districts whose students arrived already advantaged.

- [SETDA: Improving Professional Learning Systems (Nov 2025) (PDF)](https://www.setda.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/Improving-Professional-Learning-Systems-to-Better-Support-Todays-Educators-2.pdf)
- [SETDA: Press release on Strategic Professional Learning Investments](https://www.setda.org/news/press-releases/press-release-2025/setda-releases-national-guide-on-strategic-professional-learning-investments-to-close-the-digital-design-divide/)
- [EdWeek: Billions of federal dollars are spent on teacher training. Less than half goes to tech PD (Nov 2025)](https://www.edweek.org/technology/billions-of-federal-dollars-are-spent-on-teacher-training-less-than-half-goes-to-tech-pd/2025/11)
- [GovTech: SETDA recommends underutilized federal dollars for ed-tech PD](https://www.govtech.com/education/k-12/setda-recommends-underutilized-federal-dollars-for-ed-tech-pd)

### Implementation experience confirms the gap

EdWeek's coverage of state cellphone bans in 2025 captured what teachers in less-resourced districts are saying. The mass rollout of state ban mandates revealed "how little planning was put into the policies for execution, with almost all of the state bans being unfunded and brought on rather quickly, placing the burden of implementation on teachers." Enforcement varies by classroom — "If I can do it in someone's classroom, why can't I do it here?" — and the variance is not random. It is sharper in schools where teachers have less training, less administrative support, less curriculum infrastructure to anchor a defensible call.

- [EdWeek: Most Students Now Face Cellphone Limits at School — What Happens Next? (July 2025)](https://www.edweek.org/technology/most-students-now-face-cellphone-limits-at-school-what-happens-next/2025/07)
- [EdWeek: These Schools Restricted Cellphone Use — Here's What Happened Next (Dec 2025)](https://www.edweek.org/technology/these-schools-restricted-cellphone-use-heres-what-happened-next/2025/12)
- [Washington State Standard: Teachers see benefits from classroom phone bans (Oct 2025)](https://washingtonstatestandard.com/2025/10/23/from-bell-to-bell-there-is-no-cell-teachers-see-benefits-from-classroom-phone-bans/)
- [NASBE: Curbing Cell Phone Use in Classrooms](https://www.nasbe.org/curbing-cell-phone-use-in-classrooms/)
- [Center for American Progress: 10 Policy Recommendations to Address Cellphone Use in Schools](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/10-policy-recommendations-to-address-cellphone-use-in-schools/)

### The chapter's pivot — to the affirmative claim

The exemption is not a legislative error. It is the right thing to do — the device is neutral, the use depends on context, no rule can specify it. The error is what the legislatures *didn't* do at the same time: build the teacher capacity that the exemption presumes.

The four-word phrase has been doing all the work since 2023. It will continue doing all the work for as long as the bans are in force. The question is whether the teacher who reads it has the training to apply it. Right now, in most districts, she does not.

The chapter closes by handing forward — to the rest of Movement II of the book (the evidence) — the affirmative case for what training that teacher requires.
