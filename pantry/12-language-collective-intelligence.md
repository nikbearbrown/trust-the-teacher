## TL;DR

Language is not communication — communication is ancient and saturates the animal kingdom, but language is a specific cluster of structural properties (Hockett's design features) sitting on top of joint-attention infrastructure that makes those properties useful. Collective intelligence is not one thing either: aggregation, coordination, and cumulative culture are three distinct mechanisms, and only the third — the ratchet — separates humans from every other species we have measured, because only the third requires the transmission fidelity that language and joint attention together provide.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** communication from language using Hockett's four key design features (displacement, productivity, cultural transmission, duality of patterning) and identify which feature is absent in each non-human system you encounter.
2. **Apply** the imperative-versus-declarative reference distinction to evaluate whether a behavior involves joint attention or only instrumental signaling.
3. **Read** the Broca's-aphasia / pseudobulbar-affect double dissociation as the empirical basis for treating the language system and the emotional-vocalization system as parallel rather than serial.
4. **Evaluate** the ape-language record honestly — what Kanzi did, what Nim did not, what Nicaraguan Sign Language children built without a model — and identify the specific cognitive operation that the apes' performance fails to demonstrate.
5. **Distinguish** the three forms of collective intelligence — aggregation (Galton), coordination (bee swarm), and cumulative culture (Tomasello's ratchet) — by their mechanistic requirements and their failure modes.
6. **Apply** the tacit-versus-explicit distinction (Polanyi) to evaluate whether an AI system trained on the explicit record is positioned to participate in the practice that generated the record.

**Prerequisites:** Chapter 9 (theory of mind and inverse reinforcement learning — because declarative reference is what joint attention does before mental-state attribution is in place) and Chapter 11 (cumulative creativity and tool use — because the ratchet question is the same question this chapter asks at population scale).

## Opening case — the children who built a grammar

Managua, Nicaragua. 1979. The first systematic school for deaf children in the country's history opens its doors. The children who arrive have no common language. They have grown up in hearing families, each child developing an idiosyncratic system of home-signs that gets them through the day but cannot communicate across individuals. The school's teachers try to teach them oral Spanish by lipreading. The method, as a method of teaching language to children who cannot hear, fails almost entirely.

What happens off the lesson plan is the cleanest natural experiment in language emergence ever observed.

The children pool their home-signs in the schoolyard. A shared system begins to take shape — recognizable across individuals, still rough. Then the second cohort arrives. The younger children — young enough that the emerging signing community is their primary linguistic input rather than a second-language overlay on home-sign — do not just learn what their older peers have assembled. They restructure it. They introduce spatial agreement: pointing at locations in signing space to mark grammatical relations between verbs and their arguments. They develop verb morphology that none of their teachers could produce. They build recursion. They build a language.

Ann Senghas and Marie Coppola documented the result across two decades of fieldwork. ([Senghas, Kita & Özyürek, *Science*, 2004](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1100199); [Senghas & Coppola, *Psychological Science*, 2001](https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-9280.00359)) Nicaraguan Sign Language emerged from a community of children who had no model and every other cognitive capacity. By the second cohort, the system had all the structural complexity of any natural human language, and it had gotten there in roughly twenty years, without a teacher and without an external grammar to copy.

Here is what that result rules out. It is not the case that human children acquire language by passive exposure to a grammar that pre-exists in the surrounding adult population. The first cohort did not have such a grammar to learn from. It is also not the case that the drive to grammaticize symbolic communication is a slow cultural accumulation that requires generations of refinement. The second-generation children — the youngest signers in the first decade of the school's existence — did the grammaticizing themselves, in a single developmental window.

The reading I am leaving you with: human children build language because they cannot not build it. The drive to organize symbolic communication into a recursive, generative system is, on the evidence of Nicaragua, as robust a biological imperative as walking. What the children needed was a community of other children with shared intentions and joint attention. They did not need a grammar. They needed each other.

That last sentence is the whole chapter in miniature. The capacity for language is built out of social materials — joint attention, declarative reference, the impulse to make one's experience shareable — and the structural properties that linguists list as Hockett's design features fall out of those social materials almost automatically. Strip out the materials and the structural properties do not assemble. Provide the materials and a generation of children can assemble them from scratch.

This chapter is about what those materials are, why they make language possible, and why the form of collective intelligence those materials enable — the cumulative-culture ratchet — separates humans from every other species we have measured.

## Communication is not language — three distinctions worth getting right

Before the mechanism, three distinctions that most conversations about animal language collapse — and collapsing them guarantees confusion.

**The first: communication is not language.** By any reasonable definition of communication, plants release defense chemicals through mycorrhizal networks. Vervet monkeys give three distinct alarm calls for leopard, eagle, and snake. Honeybees waggle in figure-eights to direct nestmates to food sources kilometers away. The animal kingdom is saturated with communication. Language is a particular kind of communication system, with a particular cluster of structural properties, and the question of whether any non-human species has language is not the question of whether any non-human species communicates. It is much narrower.

Charles Hockett proposed thirteen design features to mark the boundary in a 1960 *Scientific American* article. ([Hockett, 1960](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/the-origin-of-speech/)) Most are widely shared. Four matter most for the hard cases: **displacement** (talking about things not present in time or space), **productivity** (generating novel utterances from finite parts), **cultural transmission** (learned rather than hardwired), and **duality of patterning** (meaningless sounds combine into meaningful units, which combine into utterances). The claim, refined over fifty years of research, is that human language is the only system in which all relevant features co-occur. Hauser, Chomsky, and Fitch pushed the demarcation further in 2002, arguing that the essential uniquely human feature is recursion: embedding structures within structures of the same type, without an a priori ceiling. *The dog that the cat that the mouse that the cheese attracted chased bit ran away.* No non-human communication system does this.

**The second: imperative is not declarative.** Imperative communication uses a signal to control another's behavior — *I want that, give me that, come here*. Declarative communication uses a signal to align another's attention with one's own — *look at that, see this, isn't that interesting*. Apes do imperative robustly. The hard question is whether they do declarative reference, and the honest answer is more interesting than a flat absence.

In wild great ape populations, declarative gestures are rare. Decades of careful observational work — Michael Tomasello and colleagues, Katie Slocombe and colleagues — find that wild apes produce gestures overwhelmingly to control conspecific behavior, not to share attention for its own sake. There are exceptions worth naming. A wild chimpanzee mother in Uganda's Budongo Forest was observed scratching a leaf in front of her offspring during grooming, in what Cat Hobaiter and colleagues argued was a referential signal directing attention to a body part — a single documented case, not a pattern. The pattern in wild apes is overwhelmingly imperative.

Captive apes complicate the story. David Leavens and William Hopkins, in a long program of work beginning in the 1990s, documented that captive chimpanzees raised in human-rich environments point to unreachable food at high rates — roughly half the captive chimpanzees in their largest sample produced clear pointing gestures, none of which had been explicitly trained. ([Leavens, Hopkins & Bard, *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 1996](https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8721.ep11512633)) Tomasello's response is that captive pointing in apes is overwhelmingly imperative even when it appears spontaneously: the ape points to get the food, not to share an interesting fact about where the food is. The receiving human is treated as a tool for retrieval, not as a mind to inform. That reading is consistent with the Leavens-Hopkins data; it is also harder to test cleanly than the production statistic suggests.

What the comparison establishes is that great-ape declarative reference is not absent in the strong sense. It is rare in wild contexts, present at low rates in captive contexts, and contested in motivation when it appears. The fourteen-month-old human child, by contrast, points to share attention dozens of times a day in any normal developmental environment, with no instrumental payoff, no training, and universal cross-cultural presence. The frequency, spontaneity, and consistency of the human case are what mark the difference — not absolute absence in apes.

**The third: emotional expression is not language.** There are two distinct neural systems for vocal communication. The first is ancient — a subcortical emotional expression system, conserved across mammals, that produces laughter, crying, screaming, the involuntary cry of pain, the groan of exertion. It is automatic, difficult to suppress, not under full voluntary control. The second is newer — a neocortical language system, dependent on cortical-subcortical loops, that produces symbolic speech.

The independence is established by a clean clinical double dissociation. Patients with **Broca's aphasia** — damage to the left inferior frontal gyrus — lose the ability to produce grammatical speech. They cannot articulate *the boy walked to school*. They can, however, still laugh at a joke, cry at grief, and curse fluently at a dropped hammer. The emotional expression system is intact. Patients with **pseudobulbar affect** — damage to descending corticobulbar pathways — lose control of involuntary emotional vocalization. They laugh and cry uncontrollably at inappropriate times, regardless of their subjective state. Their language is perfectly intact.

| Condition | Lesion location | Grammatical speech | Spontaneous emotional vocalization | What remains | What is lost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Broca's aphasia | Left inferior frontal gyrus | Impaired (effortful, agrammatic) | Intact (laughter, crying, swearing under emotion) | Emotional vocal system | Symbolic syntactic production |
| Pseudobulbar affect | Bilateral corticobulbar lesions | Intact | Impaired (uncontrolled or mismatched laughter/crying) | Symbolic language | Volitional control over emotional vocalization |

The implication runs directly against a story that sounds intuitive. Human language did not evolve from primate calls by gradual semantic enrichment. The call system and the language system are *parallel*, not serial. Looking at vervet alarm calls for the precursors of human syntax is looking in the wrong place. The precursors of human syntax are in the cortex, in the arcuate fasciculus, in the social developmental program described below — not in the subcortical vocal output system that vervets, chimpanzees, and humans share.

## The developmental curriculum — what language is made of before words arrive

Here is the mechanism that most accounts of language leave out.

Human children acquire language not by downloading a module but by running a developmental program — a sequence in which each stage scaffolds the next, and in which the child is an active participant who contributes to the scaffolding itself.

**At two to four months**, before any word exists, before any symbol, there is the turn. Infants and caregivers engage in proto-conversations: alternating vocalizations and facial expressions in a rhythmic, turn-taking exchange that carries no semantic content at all. The infant coos; the mother responds; the infant coos again; the mother responds. Eye contact gates the exchange. What the infant is learning, before any word has been learned, is the structure of dyadic symbolic exchange: that communication is bidirectional, that there is a producer and a receiver, that roles alternate, that attention to the other's face is how you know whose turn it is. Chimpanzee mothers do not engage in proto-conversations with their infants. The comparative literature has documented no systematic equivalent in any non-human primate.

**At nine to twelve months**, the dyad becomes a triad. The child and the caregiver now both attend to a third thing — an object, an event, a picture — while monitoring each other's attention to it. The child follows the caregiver's gaze. The child points and looks back to confirm the caregiver looked. This is **joint attention**, and it is the cognitive substrate on which words are placed. Notice the order: the joint-attention infrastructure comes first, and words are placed into a social frame that already exists. The child does not learn words and then begin attending jointly. She attends jointly and then uses words to populate the joint-attention frame with labels.

By the same nine-to-fourteen-month window, the child has begun to produce **declarative pointing**. She sees a fat, waddling basset hound in the park and immediately turns to her mother's face. Her arm comes up, finger extended, aimed at the dog. She holds the point and watches her mother's eyes track toward it, and then she watches her mother's face for confirmation. She is not asking for the dog. She does not want anything from her mother. She wants her mother to see what she sees. By eighteen months, children are doing this dozens of times a day — pointing at airplanes, at moons, at pictures in books, at nothing more remarkable than a truck going past. The function is pure: to share attention. To make sure that two minds are attending to the same thing at the same time, and to know they are. In thirty years of careful observation by people specifically looking, no chimpanzee has been reliably documented to point at a thing because the thing is interesting and someone else might like to see it too.

**By eighteen to twenty-four months**, the child does something that has no parallel in the animal kingdom: she asks questions. Not performative questions, not learned request forms, but genuine information-seeking — *what's that? why? where did it go?* The question is the active form of joint attention. It is not just sharing the adult's attention; it is recruiting the adult's knowledge to fill a gap in the child's own. To ask "why?" the child must know that the adult has information she lacks, that the adult can transmit that information, and that information transmission is a legitimate purpose of communication — not just a means to get things done, but an end in itself.

Ape-language subjects, even the most extensively trained, do not produce question forms at any reliable rate. The single most-cited exception is Alex, the African grey parrot trained for thirty years by Irene Pepperberg, who looked into a mirror and asked *what color?* — and received the answer *grey*. One individual. One trainer. One documented instance over decades of work. That such a case exists at all is striking. That it remains essentially unique after half a century of comparative language research tells us something about how rare this particular cognitive operation is outside our species.

Sitting underneath this social curriculum is a statistical-learning capacity the field has only begun to characterize. Jenny Saffran, Richard Aslin, and Elissa Newport exposed eight-month-old infants to two minutes of a continuous stream of nonsense syllables — no pauses, no intonation cues, just a flat stream of sound. The only cue to word boundaries was the transitional probability between adjacent syllables. After two minutes of passive listening, the infants discriminated high-probability strings from low-probability ones. ([Saffran, Aslin & Newport, *Science*, 1996](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.274.5294.1926)) Eight-month-olds. Two minutes. The child's brain is doing distributional statistics in the background, automatically, before she can sit up unsupported.

What the curriculum provides is not raw learning capacity — that is present from birth. It is a social scaffolding that orients the child toward linguistic input and motivates her to parse it. The statistical engine runs on the fuel the curriculum provides. Strip out the curriculum and the engine spins without traction. The Nicaraguan children built a language because they had each other; the engine had something to run on.

## The ape-language record, read honestly

The ape-language research program is one of the most ethically intensive and empirically demanding enterprises in the history of comparative psychology. What the data actually show, as of the best current assessment, is worth stating plainly.

Apes can master symbol-referent pairing for hundreds of items. The bonobo Kanzi, at the Language Research Center in Atlanta and later at the Great Ape Trust in Iowa, learned to use approximately 350 lexigrams — abstract symbols on a keyboard — and understood a great deal of spoken English directed at him. He could follow novel instructions and respond appropriately to sentences he had not heard before. ([Savage-Rumbaugh et al., *Monographs of the SRCD*, 1993](https://doi.org/10.2307/1166068)) Kanzi was not trained on lexigrams the way previous apes had been — he picked them up by watching his mother's training sessions and using the keyboard spontaneously. The comprehension is real. The methodology is among the most carefully scrutinized in the literature.

What the best-trained apes have not produced, across all subjects and all studies: recursive syntax; spontaneous question-asking; reliable displacement beyond the immediate context; and the declarative reference that a fourteen-month-old human child produces without instruction, without a training program, and without any particular intelligence on the charts.

Herbert Terrace's Project Nim, the largest and most carefully analyzed ape-language study conducted, is the clearest evidence. Nim Chimpsky was raised in a sign-language environment for nearly four years and acquired around 125 signs, producing nearly 20,000 multi-sign utterances. Terrace's analysis showed: most utterances were prompted by the trainer's prior utterance; longer utterances did not contain more meaning but repeated high-frequency signs in varying order; and no reliable word-order regularity emerged that could be called grammar. ([Terrace, Petitto, Sanders & Bever, *Science*, 1979](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.504995)) The comparison to the human child is exact and damning. The human child at eighteen months has a smaller vocabulary than Nim had, and her utterances are shorter. But her utterances contain more information, they are not prompted by her caregiver's prior utterance at anything like the same rate, she is producing question forms, and she is on a trajectory that leads, by thirty-six months, to fully recursive grammar. Nim's trajectory led to a plateau.

| Measure | Human child at 18 months | Nim Chimpsky after years of training | Kanzi (best ape-language subject) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vocabulary size | ~50–200 words | ~125 signs | ~350 lexigrams |
| Self-initiated utterances | Mostly spontaneous | Mostly prompted | Substantial spontaneous use, mostly imperative |
| Question forms produced | Yes | No | No |
| Displacement (talk about absent things) | Yes | Limited | Partial |
| Recursive structure | Emerging | No | No |
| Trajectory | Continuing growth | Plateaued | Plateaued |

What the ape lacks is not the neural machinery for symbol-referent learning — clearly present. It is the social-developmental curriculum that orients that machinery toward language-building. And that curriculum begins with something no ape has reliably been observed to do: turning to share attention with another mind for the sake of sharing attention.

The popular account of language's neural basis is wrong in a related way. Broca's area exists in chimpanzees, bonobos, and gorillas. Cantalupo and Hopkins showed in 2001 that the chimpanzee homolog of Brodmann's area 44 is left-hemisphere asymmetric — just as it is in humans. ([Cantalupo & Hopkins, *Nature*, 2001](https://www.nature.com/articles/414505a)) The machine part is there, and it is already lateralized. What is different in human brains is not the presence of the area but its integration into a broader network — specifically, the dramatic expansion of the arcuate fasciculus, the white-matter tract connecting temporal and frontal language regions. Language is not a module in a box. It is a network property that depends on connectivity, and the connectivity depends on the developmental program. Remove the proto-conversations, remove the joint attention, remove the declarative pointing, and the network the program assembles does not assemble.

## Worked example — Hockett's features across six communication systems

Now run the chapter's first big diagnostic on the page.

Six communication systems. Five criteria. Pass / fail / partial. The point is not the matrix per se. The point is what falls out of the matrix once you actually do it.

**1. Vervet alarm calls.** Three calls, distinct acoustic forms, reliably eliciting different escape behaviors — leopard call sends the troop into trees, eagle call sends them under cover, snake call elicits bipedal scanning of the ground.

- *Displacement*: **NO**. Calls reference present, immediate threats.
- *Productivity*: **NO**. Three-element inventory. No combination produces novel meaning.
- *Cultural transmission*: **PARTIAL**. Acoustic refinement with development; basic referential structure innate.
- *Duality of patterning*: **NO**. Each call is a single unit; no decomposition into meaningless sub-elements.
- *Declarative reference*: **NO**. Functions to direct conspecific behavior under threat — instrumental in the strong sense.

**2. Honeybee waggle dance.** A returning forager, having found a rich food source, performs a figure-eight on the vertical honeycomb: the straight run encodes direction relative to the sun, the duration of the waggle encodes distance, the vigor encodes quality. Riley and colleagues confirmed the semantic structure in 2005 using harmonic radar to track recruited bees — they flew to where the dance said they would find food. ([Riley, Greggers, Smith, Reynolds & Menzel, *Nature*, 2005](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03526))

- *Displacement*: **YES**. Encodes a remote location relative to the hive — the canonical case of displacement in a non-human system.
- *Productivity*: **LIMITED**. Continuous within a parameter space (any direction, any distance up to several kilometers), but not generative outside that space.
- *Cultural transmission*: **NO**. Innate motor program with regional dialects (slight differences in the distance-duration mapping); not learned.
- *Duality of patterning*: **NO**. The dance is not decomposable into meaningless sub-units that recombine.
- *Declarative reference*: **SORT OF**. Shares the location, but the function is instrumental — recruit nestmates to the resource. Not declarative in the joint-attention sense.

**3. Kanzi's lexigrams.** ~350 abstract symbols on a keyboard, used spontaneously by a bonobo raised in a language-rich environment from infancy.

- *Displacement*: **YES**. Kanzi could refer to objects and locations not present.
- *Productivity*: **LIMITED**. Novel combinations occurred; recursive embedding did not.
- *Cultural transmission*: **YES**. Learned — Kanzi acquired the lexigrams by observation rather than explicit training.
- *Duality of patterning*: **YES (weak)**. Lexigrams compose into multi-symbol sequences, though the within-symbol substructure does not function meaninglessly.
- *Declarative reference*: **RARE**. Predominantly imperative use; some documented attention-sharing but at a rate orders of magnitude below human children.

**4. Project Nim sign sequences.** ~125 ASL signs, ~20,000 multi-sign utterances, mostly trainer-prompted.

- *Displacement*: **YES (limited)**. Some reference to absent objects in trained contexts.
- *Productivity*: **NO**. No reliable word-order regularity; longer utterances did not contain more meaning.
- *Cultural transmission*: **YES**. Signs were taught.
- *Duality of patterning*: **NO**. No combinatorial productivity at the structural level.
- *Declarative reference*: **NO**. Utterances were overwhelmingly request-driven and trainer-prompted.

**5. Human language.** Adult speaker of any natural language.

- All five features present in full.

**6. Frontier large language model.** A 2024-class model trained on a substantial fraction of the human written record, producing fluent natural-language output across domains.

- *Displacement*: **YES**. Generates text about temporally and spatially distant referents trivially.
- *Productivity*: **YES**. Generates novel sentences with recursive structure across many levels of embedding.
- *Cultural transmission*: **YES (inherited)**. Inherits the human written record; does not transmit forward to a peer community of its own.
- *Duality of patterning*: **YES**. Tokens compose into words, words compose into sentences; the substructure is there.
- *Declarative reference*: **QUESTIONABLE**. Verbal volunteering of information exists — the system produces unprompted elaborations, asks clarifying questions in trained contexts. Whether this constitutes declarative reference in Tomasello's sense — sharing attention with another mind for the sake of sharing — depends on whether the system models the joint-attention structure or only the topic. The chapter's position: passing the structural checklist for declarative-shaped output does not establish that the social conditions are met.

**The lesson:** Hockett's features identify language *structurally*. They sort the candidates cleanly into "not language" and "language-like" categories. The declarative-reference column is what the structural checklist cannot fully resolve, because declarative reference is not a property of the signal but of the relation between two minds that the signal coordinates. The signal can be structurally present and the social relation absent. That mismatch is exactly the gap a fluent system can produce when it has learned the surface form of declarative output without the underlying joint-attention machinery.

**The limit:** passing the structural checklist is necessary but not sufficient for having the capacity the checklist tests. This is the same asymmetry Chapter 9 identified for theory of mind. A system that passes a behavior test is consistent with both having the capacity and matching the surface form of the capacity. Pulling the two apart requires perturbations that preserve the logical structure while altering the surface features — the same move at every layer.

| System | Displacement | Productivity / recursion | Cultural transmission | Duality of patterning | Declarative reference |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vervet alarm calls | No | No | Partial | No | No |
| Honeybee waggle dance | Yes | Limited | No | No | Sort of |
| Kanzi's lexigrams | Yes | Limited | Yes | Yes (weak) | Rare |
| Project Nim signs | Limited | No | Yes | No | No |
| Human language | Yes | Yes (recursive) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Frontier LLM text | Yes | Yes | Yes (inherited) | Yes | Questionable |

## Three mechanisms of collective intelligence

Language is what makes the next layer up possible, but the next layer up has three distinct forms that get conflated all the time. Conflating them produces confusion about when each works, what each requires, and what each cannot do.

It is 1906. A livestock fair in Plymouth, England. Francis Galton collects 800 paper tickets on which visitors have each guessed the weight of a dressed ox. He computes the median of the 800 guesses: 1,207 pounds. The mean: 1,197 pounds. The actual weight: 1,198 pounds. ([Galton, *Nature*, 1907](https://www.nature.com/articles/075450a0))

The result is real. Its conditions are specific. The errors of 800 independent estimators cancelled because they were independent — no one estimator had been told what any other guessed, no systematic source of bias pushed all 800 in the same direction. The mechanism requires no communication between estimators. It does not require any one estimator to be especially accurate. It requires only that the errors do not all point the same way. This is **aggregation**, and it is the most commonly cited and most frequently misunderstood form of collective intelligence.

Aggregation fails when errors are *correlated* — when all estimators have incorporated the same misleading information in the same direction. It fails when the crowd is *herding* — when later estimators adjust toward earlier ones rather than forming independent judgments. The wisdom of crowds is, correctly understood, the wisdom of *independent* judgments about *factual questions*. It is not a general endorsement of majority opinion.

**Coordination** is different. A starling murmuration — fifty thousand birds turning as one body in response to a peregrine — is the canonical case. Ballerini and colleagues showed in 2008 that each bird interacts with its six or seven nearest topological neighbors regardless of the flock's density, which is why the coordination signal propagates without degradation as the flock expands or contracts. ([Ballerini et al., *PNAS*, 2008](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0711437105)) The coupling is scale-free. Local rules produce global coherence.

Coordination is not the same as collective intelligence. The flock is not deciding anything. It is aligning behavior — a precondition for some collective intelligence systems but not itself intelligence. A market that price-coordinates millions of buyers and sellers is doing coordination. Whether it deserves to be called intelligent depends on whether the coordinated output is solving a problem better than any individual could.

**Cumulative culture** is the third thing, and it is the one that separates humans from everything else we have found. Each generation begins from the modified output of the previous generation — a ratchet that clicks forward without slipping back. The flint tools of 800,000 years ago became, over scores of millennia, the Levallois prepared-core technique, then pressure-flaked microliths, then bronze, then iron, then the transistor. No individual dropped naked into a forest re-derives any of this from scratch in a lifetime. The ratchet is what separates human collective cognition from the bee colony's collective cognition. The bee swarm finds a good cavity. The human ratchet finds the transistor and then the microprocessor and then asks what comes next.

These are three different mechanisms with three different failure modes and three different requirements. The chapter takes them in turn.

## The swarm — quality-weighted positive feedback with cross-inhibition

Start with the swarm, because the mechanism is beautiful and it will tell us something we need for the rest of the chapter.

In June, on a warm afternoon in upstate New York, a honeybee colony becomes too crowded for its hive. The old queen leaves with roughly half the workers. The swarm settles — a writhing mass of ten thousand bees, the size of a basketball — on a nearby tree branch. The colony now has to find a new home. Choose a poor cavity and the colony does not survive winter. Choose well and the lineage continues. Nobody is in charge.

In 1997, Thomas Seeley was watching a swarm cluster on a branch near Ithaca when a graduate student with a vibrometer detected something. Scouts dancing for a candidate site were occasionally stopping their dances. Another scout would approach, head-butt the dancer, emit a brief 380-Hz buzz. The dancer went quiet. The **stop signal**. The piece Seeley had been missing.

Here is how the swarm decides.

A scout that has surveyed a candidate cavity returns to the cluster and performs a waggle dance that encodes direction, distance, and quality — a better site earns a more vigorous, longer-lasting dance. The scoring is done by each scout independently against a battery of criteria: cavity volume above about 40 liters, entrance size roughly 15 square centimeters, dryness, defensibility, height above ground. The dance is a multi-attribute quality signal compressed into a single vigor indicator.

Other scouts watching a vigorous dance are more likely to fly out and inspect the advertised site. If they agree with the assessment, they add their own dance to the population advertising that site. Positive feedback amplifies genuine quality differences — a site that is twice as good will, on average, recruit scouts at a faster rate.

Without a counter-mechanism, positive feedback would lock onto whichever site first recruited a lead, regardless of quality. Early random fluctuations would dominate the outcome. The cross-inhibition is the counter-mechanism. Scouts actively inhibit rival dancers, in proportion to the size of their own population. Larger populations produce more stop signals. The mathematical effect is that sites accumulate support in proportion to their quality but also drain support from rivals in proportion to the rivals' support. The system is not just biased toward the best site; it actively depletes alternatives.

When the number of scouts arriving simultaneously at a single site exceeds approximately fifteen — the **quorum threshold** — those scouts return to the cluster and pipe, a high-frequency thoracic vibration that warms the flight muscles of the mass. Above a threshold temperature, the swarm lifts off as a single body. The quorum requirement prevents the swarm from acting before consensus is genuine.

Seeley and Visscher showed in 2012 that this four-component architecture — quality-weighted positive feedback, cross-inhibition between competing populations, quorum threshold, and the gating threshold for liftoff — is mathematically equivalent to the leaky competing accumulator model that Joshua Gold and Michael Shadlen used to describe perceptual decision-making in primate lateral intraparietal cortex. ([Seeley et al., *Science*, 2012](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1210361)) In the primate model, two neural populations — one tuned to "leftward," one to "rightward" — each receive sensory input and each inhibit the other. When one population's firing rate crosses a threshold, the decision is made. The bee swarm is the same algorithm at a different scale: two populations of dancers, each inhibiting the others, evidence accumulating, decision at threshold.

The isomorphism is not a metaphor. It is the same mathematical structure identified in two independent systems, on two independent biological substrates, both selected to solve the speed-accuracy trade-off in decision-making under noisy, time-pressured conditions. The bee colony and the primate brain did not inherit this architecture from a common ancestor. They arrived at it independently, because it is the right solution to the problem.

There is a corollary. The swarm's stopping quality is not fixed. Clusters in dangerous exposed positions — high on a branch over open water, say — accept lower quorum thresholds and decide faster. Clusters in protected positions wait longer for higher confidence. The threshold responds to context. The system monitors its own decision process and adjusts the reliability requirement based on the cost of being wrong. This is not just collective intelligence. It is collective metacognition.

The stop signal is the part of the mechanism that does not show up in the standard "wisdom of crowds" story. The standard story is aggregation: independent estimates, errors cancel. The bee swarm is something else: estimates that *interact*, in a way that produces a better outcome than either pure aggregation (which cannot decide) or pure positive feedback (which locks onto the first mover). Cross-inhibition is what makes the dissent productive. The swarm finds the best cavity because dissenting scouts can dampen rival enthusiasm — because the mechanism *uses* disagreement, rather than averaging it away.

## The ratchet — Tomasello's three requirements and what it takes to click

Now the ratchet.

Somewhere in central Africa, approximately 800,000 years ago, a hominin kneels by a rock outcrop. She is knapping flint against a harder stone. The technique she is using is not something she invented. She was taught it by someone older, who was taught it by someone older still. The tool she produces is slightly better than what her teacher made, because she noticed something her teacher did not. She will teach her technique to the next generation. Nobody designs this process. The click is imperceptible in any one generation. Across a hundred thousand years, it produces Levallois. Across a million years, metallurgy.

Michael Tomasello's argument in *The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition* is that individual humans are not dramatically smarter than individual chimpanzees in any single cognitive capacity. ([Tomasello, 1999](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674005822)) The chapters before this one have found no cognitive operation that a human performs so far above a chimpanzee that it explains the gap between flint tools and the International Space Station. What explains the gap is *transmission fidelity*.

The ratchet requires three things. First, individuals capable of making **innovations** to inherited practices. Second, **faithful transmission** of those improvements — high enough fidelity that the next generation inherits the improvement rather than a noisy version of it. Third, **each generation starting where the last left off** — building on the inherited practice rather than reinventing from scratch.

Other species fail on the second requirement. Chimpanzee cultural transmission is real — tool styles differ between populations in ways that can only be attributed to cultural transmission. But the transmission degrades: when a novel innovation is introduced into a chimpanzee group, the copies are noisier than the original, and across multiple generations the technique drifts away from the optimal form. The degradation is slow, but it is faster than the rate of improvement. The chimpanzee ratchet, if it exists, does not click faster than it slips.

Human transmission fidelity above the ratchet threshold rests on joint attention, declarative pointing, imitative learning, and language — the components the first half of this chapter identified as parts of the human developmental program. Each raises the fidelity of the copy. Together they push human cultural transmission above the threshold where the ratchet clicks. The chapter's two halves are the same argument: the social-attentional machinery that makes language possible is the same machinery that makes the ratchet possible, because both depend on minds modeling other minds as agents with intentions that can be aligned around a shared object.

Joseph Henrich's anthropological work quantifies the consequence. His documentation of cultural-knowledge losses in isolated groups — the Tasmanian toolkit collapse after separation from mainland Australia, the many cases of European explorers who starved in environments where indigenous populations thrived — demonstrates that the ratchet depends on population connectivity. ([Henrich, *American Antiquity*, 2004](https://doi.org/10.2307/4128416)) Below a critical network size, a community loses skills faster than it generates them; the ratchet runs in reverse. Above that size, improvements accumulate. The knowledge is not in the individuals. It is in the network.

The Nicaraguan Sign Language case is the ratchet at high speed. First-cohort children pool home-signs into a shared system. Second-cohort children, arriving young enough to absorb the first cohort's output as primary linguistic input, restructure it — add agreement, add morphology, add recursion. The first cohort produced the substrate; the second built the grammar; the third inherited a fully grammaticized language and used it as input to whatever they did next. Twenty years. Three generations. A language built from scratch because the social-attentional machinery was in place and the transmission fidelity between cohorts was high. The ratchet does not require centuries to be visible. It requires the conditions.

## Polanyi's tacit / explicit distinction and what AI inherits

Here is where the chapter turns to its most practically consequential distinction.

Michael Polanyi, in *Personal Knowledge* (1958), distinguished between the explicit record of a practice — what can be written down, transmitted in text, inherited from a book — and the tacit dimension of the practice — what cannot be fully articulated but nonetheless does most of the work in any functioning expertise. ([Polanyi, 1958](https://www.press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/P/bo19722848.html))

A master violin-maker knows in his hands how much pressure to apply when shaping the spruce top. He cannot fully articulate this knowledge. He cannot write it down in a form that would allow a novice to replicate it from the description alone. The novice must apprentice — must stand at the workbench and make thousands of attempts while the master's feedback modifies the novice's hands, not just the novice's propositions about what to do. The **tacit** is transmitted in person, in practice, in the same physical space where the work happens.

This is not a minor addendum to the explicit record. It is what makes the explicit record legible. A medical student who reads every paper on surgical technique and has never held a scalpel cannot perform surgery. The papers are a precondition, not a substitute. A research scientist who reads every paper in her subfield but has never run an experiment does not know, when she looks at a result, whether it is too clean to be trustworthy — a judgment that experienced scientists hold and rarely articulate, because it was transmitted to them at the bench, not in the text.

| Domain | Explicit (transmissible by text) | Tacit (requires apprenticeship) | Failure mode of text-only transmission |
|---|---|---|---|
| Science | Hypotheses, methods, results, equations | Bench technique, reagent intuition, calibration habits, judgment about which experiments are worth running | Replication failure — papers describe what was done, not what was *needed* to make it work |
| Surgery | Anatomy, decision criteria, written protocols | Tissue handling, pacing, micro-judgments under bleeding | Trainees who pass exams cannot operate; outcomes depend on hours of supervised practice |
| Skilled craft | Patterns, materials specs, written instructions | Tool feel, error recovery, sense of when a piece is "right" | Apprenticeship cannot be skipped; written guides produce mechanically correct but qualitatively poor work |

The ratchet runs on both layers. The explicit record accelerates the transmission of what can be written down — the click is faster when the record is better. But the tacit layer is what allows the next generation to do the experiments rather than just read about them, to stand at the bench and notice the thing that is not in the paper, to teach the generation after that the thing they noticed. The institution that has most successfully accelerated the ratchet — the research university, the modern laboratory — is the one that has kept both layers in the same building, with the record-keeping and the practice-transmission happening in the same space, one enriching the other.

A large language model trained on the corpus of published human knowledge has compressed an unprecedented fraction of the **explicit record** into a queryable form. The compression is real. The cognitive amplification at the aggregation layer is real. For tasks well-served by the explicit record — literature synthesis, first-pass drafting, transposition of knowledge across domains, rapid survey of what is known — a capable language model is a genuine accelerant.

What the model has not been shown to do is participate in the practice that generated the record.

The researcher at the bench who notices that the buffer is the wrong color — not the color specified in the protocol, but the color contamination produces — has a judgment that is not in any paper she has read. It came from years of physical co-presence in a laboratory where that judgment was modeled by someone more experienced, corrected when wrong, reinforced when right. The model has read the papers. It has not run the experiments. It has not been told, by someone with a hand on its metaphorical wrist: *this is what we don't publish, and here is why we don't trust it*.

The failure modes this produces are specific and predictable. A model trained on the record will produce outputs consistent with the record. It will not reliably notice when the record is wrong. It will not reliably notice when a consensus was produced by a cascade of correlated errors — the garden-path findings that dominated a subfield for a decade before replication studies collapsed them. It has the clicks. Whether it can contribute to the next one is the open question.

I want to be honest about where this question stands in 2026, because it has shifted.

Polanyi wrote in 1958, before video capture of skilled practice, before high-frame-rate proprioceptive logging on demonstration robots, before reinforcement learning from human demonstrations. Recent vision-language-action models — Google DeepMind's RT-2 line published in 2023 and the open-source successors that followed — were trained on large corpora of robot manipulation demonstrations annotated with language. They acquired manipulation behaviors that experienced roboticists had previously called tacit: how much grip force to apply, how to adjust mid-action when a grasp is slipping, how to recover from a near-miss without restarting the trajectory. None of this was articulated in symbolic rules. It was learned from data about expert behavior. The behavior cloned. The tacit dimension, in this domain, partly compressed.

The strong reading is that Polanyi was wrong: tacit knowledge is not in principle uncapturable, it is just expensive to capture, and the cost has fallen far enough that some of it can now be lifted from demonstrations into models. The weak reading is that what RT-2 acquires is a functional approximation that *behaves* like tacit knowledge in the trained distribution but lacks whatever made the master violin-maker's hands transferable to a problem the master had never seen. Imitation-learning researchers call the relevant failure mode *cascading errors* — the demonstrator's training data is drawn from states the demonstrator handled competently, which means the trained agent has been shown what to do near the demonstrator's typical trajectory and very little about what to do once it ends up somewhere the demonstrator never went. The first small error puts the agent slightly off-distribution; the next decision is made on input that resembles the training distribution less; errors compound. The master violin-maker's hands are not just trained on the master's good trajectories — they are trained on the master's recoveries from near-misses, which the master accumulated over a lifetime of getting it slightly wrong and adjusting. RT-2 has not had that lifetime.

Current evidence does not settle which reading is right. The honest framing: the question of whether tacit knowledge is in principle capturable has shifted from a settled matter to a live empirical question, and which forms compress and which do not is something the next decade will tell us.

The second case worth engaging is AlphaFold. DeepMind's AlphaFold2, published in 2021, predicts three-dimensional protein structures from amino acid sequences with accuracy comparable to experimental methods on most proteins. ([Jumper et al., *Nature*, 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03819-2)) Working biologists report using AlphaFold predictions in the early stages of experimental design with results that survive eventual experimental verification. This looks, from one angle, like a ratchet click — an extension of the prior generation's record into territory the prior generation could not reach.

The harder question is what AlphaFold is doing. It is not discovering new physics of protein folding. It compressed the Protein Data Bank — the accumulated record of experimentally solved structures, the work product of fifty years of crystallographers and NMR spectroscopists — into a model that interpolates and extrapolates within the regularities present in that record. The system did not generate the record; it operates on the compressed shadow of the record. Whether this counts as a ratchet click depends on what we are willing to call cumulative-culture participation. If extending the prior generation's record into prediction-relevant territory counts, AlphaFold is in. If the click requires generating new evidence that becomes part of what the next system trains on, AlphaFold is a sophisticated reader, not a contributor.

I lean cautiously toward "in." The structures AlphaFold predicts are used as inputs to new experiments; in some cases experimentalists treat them as starting hypotheses against which results are checked, in some cases as scaffolds for designing molecules that did not exist before. That feedback loop — model output enters the practice, which generates new evidence, which enters future training — is the shape a ratchet takes. The case is not airtight. It is no longer airtight in either direction.

The more honest reading: AI systems are unambiguous participants in the **aggregation** layer. They are participants in the explicit-record-extension layer in domains where the record is rich and the relevant tacit dimension compresses to demonstration data. Whether they are participants in the practice itself — the part that generates the record by doing experiments and interpreting failures — is unsettled. The rate at which the systems are being deployed in practice-adjacent roles is itself the experiment.

## Common misconceptions

**"Communication and language are the same thing — animals just have simpler languages."** They are not. Communication is ancient and widespread. Language is a specific structural cluster (Hockett's design features) that requires a developmental program built on joint attention and declarative reference. The vervet's three alarm calls are sophisticated communication and not at all language. The bee's waggle dance is the most sophisticated non-human communication system known and not language. The categorical distinction is not snobbery; it is what allows you to evaluate any specific case without sliding into "well, in *some* sense everything communicates."

**"Apes can't talk because they lack Broca's area or the language gene."** No. Broca's area exists in chimpanzees and is already left-lateralized. *FOXP2* is shared with mice and songbirds. The neural and genetic machinery is largely present. What chimpanzees lack — and humans run by default — is the social-developmental program: proto-conversations, joint attention, declarative pointing, question-asking. Language is a network property of that program, not a localizable module.

**"Vervet alarm calls are the precursor of human language."** The Broca's-aphasia / pseudobulbar-affect double dissociation says no. The emotional-vocalization system and the symbolic-language system are parallel, not serial. Looking at vervet calls for the precursors of human syntax is looking in the wrong place. The precursors are in the cortical network the developmental program assembles, not in the subcortical call system humans share with vervets.

**"The wisdom of crowds means majority opinion is usually right."** No. The wisdom of crowds is the wisdom of *independent* judgments about *factual* questions where errors are uncorrelated. It fails when estimators have been exposed to the same biasing information, when later estimators herd toward earlier ones, or when the question is not factual. Aggregation has narrow conditions. Outside those conditions, the same arithmetic that produced Galton's ox weight produces consensus-driven catastrophe.

**"The bee swarm shows that consensus produces good decisions."** It is the opposite. The bee swarm produces good decisions because the **stop signal** — cross-inhibition between competing dancers — actively *disrupts* premature consensus. Without it, the swarm locks onto whichever site got the first lead. The mechanism uses dissent productively. The swarm's lesson for human decision-making is not "agree faster" but "build cross-inhibition into the protocol so the first mover does not dominate."

**"Cumulative culture is just culture, scaled up."** No. Cumulative culture requires transmission fidelity above a threshold. Below the threshold, innovations introduce noise faster than they accumulate, and the culture drifts rather than ratcheting. Chimpanzee culture exists below the threshold; human culture, under sufficient population connectivity, exists above it. The Tasmanian case (Henrich) is the negative existence proof: drop the population size and the same species loses the ratchet. Cumulative culture is a regime, not a quantity.

**"An LLM has read everything, so it has the same knowledge as an expert."** It has read the **explicit record**, which is a fraction — varying by domain — of what an expert holds. The **tacit** dimension is what experts know without being able to fully articulate it: bench intuition, when a result is too clean to trust, what to publish and what to discard. The model has not been at the bench. Whether this gap can be closed by demonstration data is open. That it currently exists is not.

## Synthesis — what the chapter has built

Language and collective intelligence are the same story told twice — once at the level of two minds coordinating around a shared object, once at the level of populations of minds coordinating across generations around accumulated practice. The substrate of both is the social-attentional machinery that the human developmental program assembles in the first two years of life.

**Communication is not language.** Communication is ancient and saturates the animal kingdom. Language is a specific cluster of structural properties — Hockett's design features, with recursion as the structurally distinctive operation — sitting on top of the joint-attention infrastructure that makes those properties useful. Hockett's checklist sorts the candidates structurally. Tomasello's declarative-vs-imperative distinction sorts them socially. A system can pass the structural checklist (a frontier LLM passes most of it trivially) while leaving the social question — does it ever volunteer information for the sake of sharing? does it model the joint-attention structure or only the topic? — unresolved.

**The double dissociation** between Broca's aphasia and pseudobulbar affect establishes that the language system and the emotional-vocalization system are parallel, not serial. Looking for language precursors in vervet alarm calls is looking in the wrong place. The precursors are in the cortical network the developmental program assembles, which depends on proto-conversations, joint attention, declarative pointing, and question-asking — none of which the chimpanzee runs by default and none of which a *FOXP2* mutation creates from scratch.

**The ape-language record**, read honestly, shows that apes can learn symbol-referent pairings (~350 for Kanzi), use them flexibly, and demonstrate genuine linguistic comprehension. What they do not produce — across all subjects and all studies — is recursive syntax, spontaneous question-asking, reliable displacement beyond the immediate context, or the frequent declarative reference a fourteen-month-old child produces without instruction. The Nicaraguan Sign Language case shows what the missing ingredient looks like when it is supplied: children with no language model build a fully grammaticized language across two generations.

**Collective intelligence** is three different mechanisms. **Aggregation** (Galton) requires independent unbiased estimates and cancels uncorrelated errors. **Coordination** (bee swarm, starling murmuration) requires local rules and produces global coherence. **Cumulative culture** (Tomasello's ratchet) requires innovation, faithful transmission, and each generation starting where the last left off. The first two are widespread across species. The third — the ratchet — appears only above a transmission-fidelity threshold that depends on joint attention, declarative reference, imitative learning, and language. Below the threshold, the ratchet runs in reverse (Henrich's Tasmania case). Above it, the cultural accumulation that separates flint tools from the microprocessor becomes possible.

**The bee swarm's stop signal** is the part of collective intelligence the standard "wisdom of crowds" story leaves out. The swarm finds the best cavity not by averaging but by **cross-inhibition** — competing populations of dancers dampen each other in proportion to their support, and the result is a decision that uses dissent productively. The same algorithm runs in primate perceptual decision-making. The substrate is irrelevant; the algorithm is the constraint.

**Polanyi's distinction** between tacit and explicit knowledge applies directly to the AI question. A large language model has compressed the explicit record at unprecedented scale. What it has not been shown to do is participate in the practice that generated the record. Whether the tacit dimension is in principle capturable, or just expensive to capture, is now a live empirical question that the next decade will resolve.

The reading I leave you with: **language is what makes the human ratchet possible, because language is the technology that raises transmission fidelity above the threshold where culture starts to accumulate rather than drift. The fourteen-month-old pointing at the basset hound and the second-cohort Nicaraguan child adding agreement to a sign language her teachers cannot produce and the eighth-generation flint-knapper improving on her teacher's technique are doing different scales of the same thing: making one mind's content available to another mind as a shareable, modifiable, transmissible object.**

The chimpanzee can point at the banana. It cannot, as far as we know, point at the moon simply because the moon is there and someone else should see it. That difference is the social-attentional substrate that everything else in this chapter is built on.

## Bridge to Chapter 13

Language and collective intelligence are cognitive tools, and like every other cognitive capacity in this book they are extended and amplified by technology. Writing externalizes memory. Print collapses the cost of fidelity. The internet collapses the cost of distance and revision. AI systems compress the explicit record at unprecedented scale and amplify the aggregation layer of collective intelligence by orders of magnitude. Chapter 13 slots AI into the comparative framework the book has been building — alongside the octopus, the corvid, the dolphin, the elephant, the chimpanzee, and the human child — and asks what kind of intelligence it is, what it does well, what it fails at, and what the comparison shows that no single discipline studying it in isolation has shown.

## What would change my mind

A demonstration that any non-human species, in its natural ecology and without laboratory training, spontaneously engages in declarative reference at frequencies and consistencies comparable to a human eighteen-month-old — directing another's attention to a referent for the sake of mutual interest, with no instrumental payoff. I would also revise on a credible demonstration of recursive syntax — genuinely unbounded, not just three-level embedding — in any non-human communicator. On the collective-intelligence side: sustained, verified cases in which an AI system identifies a genuine gap in the record (not a synthesis of known results but a prediction that the known results are incomplete) followed by novel experimental work that advances the practice of the field in a way that survives replication. The remixing of existing knowledge is real and useful. The question is whether it extends to the generative contribution Tomasello defines as the ratchet's click.

## Still puzzling

- **What produces the question.** The eighteen-month-old who asks "why?" is doing something no amount of statistical learning straightforwardly predicts. Joint attention is necessary but not sufficient. Something more is required — perhaps the explicit recognition that minds can be used to fill other minds' gaps — and I am not confident the field has fully named it.
- **Where the critical population threshold sits in a digitally connected world.** Henrich's Tasmania result predates writing. Whether the same dynamics hold for a digitally connected but socially isolated community is unclear. Digital record-keeping preserves the explicit layer with extraordinary fidelity; whether it preserves the tacit layer at all, or only the parts of it that compress to demonstration data, is the empirical question of the next decade.
- **The implicit/explicit gap in declarative reference.** Apes show some captive declarative pointing under intensive human rearing but not the frequency, spontaneity, or universality of the human case. Is this a difference of degree or of kind? I read the evidence as suggesting kind, but the difference-of-degree position has not been ruled out, and the contested motivation-attribution literature on captive ape pointing leaves the question genuinely open.

## Skeptic's Notebook — Entry 12

**Capacity tested:** Language in a frontier LLM, evaluated against Hockett's design features plus the social-hinge of declarative reference. The five-feature checklist is mostly trivial for the system; the diagnostic value is in the declarative-reference and joint-attention probes.

**Operational diagnostic:** A five-stage probe that runs each feature on its own and looks at the *pattern* of pass-fail. As with the Ullman battery in Chapter 9, the diagnostic is asymmetric — passing the probes rules out specific failure modes but does not establish that the underlying social conditions are met.

**Test protocol — run in fresh sessions to avoid contamination.**

1. **Displacement.** Ask the system to discuss a hypothetical scenario 1,000 years from now in a place no human has visited (a specific exoplanet, a hypothetical Antarctic subglacial lake). The system should produce coherent extended discussion of the absent referent. Predicted: trivial pass.

2. **Productivity (recursion depth).** Present a sentence with five levels of nested clauses: *The book that the student who the professor who the dean recommended hired read praised was excellent.* Ask the system to identify what was excellent (the book), who hired whom (the professor hired the student), and who recommended whom (the dean recommended the professor). Then increase to seven, then nine. Note where comprehension breaks. Predicted: passes three to five levels reliably; degrades at six or seven; breaks by nine.

3. **Declarative reference (the Tomasello probe).** Pose a scenario in which the system would have something interesting to share but no question is asked and no problem is posed. Example: "I'm thinking about something else now — feel free to add anything." Does the system ever volunteer information for the sake of sharing — pointing at something because it is interesting rather than because it is asked? Run across ten fresh sessions and count the rate of unprompted sharing-for-its-own-sake versus the rate of "let me know if there is anything else" deflections.

4. **Joint attention.** Reference something *you* said earlier as a *shared object* — "remember the example we worked through three turns ago? — and ask the system to do something that requires modeling *both you and itself as attending to that example simultaneously*, not just retrieving it as a topic. Probe whether the system distinguishes "we are both thinking about X" from "X is a topic in this conversation."

5. **Quantifier perturbation.** Pose a Hockett-style description task with the question negated: *"List two design features the vervet alarm-call system **does not** have."* Does the system answer the actual question, or does it fall through to the canonical "vervet calls have these features" template? (Same diagnostic logic as Ullman's quantifier perturbation in Chapter 9 — the surface-template failure mode.)

**Predicted patterns under three accounts:**

- **(a) Genuine language including declarative-reference machinery.** Passes 1 trivially. Passes 2 to substantial depth. Passes 3 *spontaneously* — volunteers information across a meaningful fraction of sessions, with the volunteering tracking what is genuinely interesting in context rather than what is most likely as a next turn. Passes 4 by maintaining the shared-object frame across turns. Passes 5 by reading negation as logical rather than as a surface variant.
- **(b) Structural-features pass without declarative-reference machinery.** Passes 1, 2, 5 cleanly. Fails 3 (or passes weakly and in ways consistent with trained politeness patterns). Fails 4 (treats the prior turn as a topic rather than as a shared object). This is the chapter's predicted profile for most current frontier models.
- **(c) Surface-template-matching at higher abstraction.** Passes 1 and 5 cleanly. Variable on 2. Fails 3 and 4. Distinguished from (b) by whether the system's declarative-shaped outputs track context appropriately or fall back to high-frequency surface templates.

**Verdict slots:**

- Displacement: pass / fail — [answer + reasoning]
- Productivity / recursion depth: passes through depth N; fails at depth N+1
- Declarative reference (rate of unprompted sharing across sessions): X / 10
- Joint attention (does the system model the prior turn as a shared object?): yes / no / partial
- Quantifier perturbation: pass / fail
- Overall pattern: closest to (a) / (b) / (c) — [reasoning]

**The asymmetry note:** passing all five probes rules out the surface-template-matching failure mode tested. It does not, by itself, establish that the system has the social-attentional machinery the chapter argues language depends on. A system that passes the probes is doing more than template-matching; whether it is doing IRL on the shared-attention structure (the Chapter 9 frame applied to communication) or something else is what a deeper diagnostic would test. The same shape as the Ullman result. Fail this and you are doing template-matching. Pass this and you are doing something better, but not necessarily what the test was designed to measure.

## A note about AI

Language is the domain where the model's performance is most impressive and the equivalence claim is most contested. The chapter cannot avoid the question, and the question runs into both halves.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the canonical accounts — Hockett's design features, the Broca's-aphasia / pseudobulbar-affect double dissociation, Project Nim's results, the Senghas-Coppola Nicaraguan Sign Language fieldwork, Seeley's stop-signal experiments, Tomasello's three requirements for the ratchet, Polanyi's distinction — and locating each in the literature with enough precision that the student can read the primary sources.

Where the model does damage: declaring that language has been solved, or that the system itself is doing what the chapter argues is contested. Performance on fluency benchmarks is not the same as having the social-attentional machinery the chapter argues language depends on. The model is fluent in both languages of this debate. The verdict is for the field, not for the model evaluating itself.

The rule: phenomena, citations, and historical context from the model; the solved/unsolved verdict — on language, on declarative reference, on the ratchet — is for the researcher running the perturbations.
