## The book in one sentence

In 2025 American education offered two answers to the smartphone problem — ban the device and buy a better app — and both answers had the same flaw: neither of them was about teachers.

## The book in one paragraph

A great teacher trained in artificial intelligence will outperform any expensive educational technology platform. This is not a romantic statement. It is the prediction the evidence supports, the prediction the Bastani PNAS finding from June 2025 confirmed in negative form, and the prediction the Tutor CoPilot Stanford RCT confirmed in positive form. The variable that determines whether educational technology works is teacher training. The variable American education systematically refuses to invest in is teacher training. The implementation gap which has defined educational technology for sixty years is the gap between purchased platforms and trained professionals to operate them, and closing the gap is not a technical problem or a vendor problem but a workforce problem we have consistently chosen not to solve.

## Why this book exists

A piece of software arrives in a school district. It costs $500,000. Nobody trains the teachers who are supposed to use it. Two years later, sixty-seven percent of the licenses are unused. The district buys new software.

This has happened in American education so many times, for so many decades, that it has a name. The implementation gap. The pattern is older than the personal computer. It is older than the internet. Every wave of educational technology since the overhead projector has produced the same arc — purchase, deployment, modest uptake, quiet failure, the next purchase. The technologies change. The gap does not.

In the era of generative AI, the cost of the gap has become impossible to absorb. The Bastani PNAS finding from June 2025 demonstrated that students given unguided AI access scored seventeen percent below students who never used AI at all. The Tutor CoPilot Stanford RCT showed that tutors with AI assistance produced the largest gains for the least experienced tutors — the tool levels the floor by extending the trained human. The Gallup-Walton 2025 survey showed teachers using AI weekly reclaim almost six hours a week and reinvest the time in students. The Bastani trial showed what happens without that mediation. The Tutor CoPilot trial showed what happens with it. The evidence cuts the same way every time. The variable is the teacher.

This book is the consolidated case for building the teacher this evidence requires.

## Who this book is for

You are a school principal, a district leader, an instructional coach, an education policymaker, a state board member, a foundation program officer, or one of the increasingly rare commentators on American education who still reads books rather than only tweets. You are responsible for decisions that affect how technology will land in classrooms over the next decade. You have noticed that the conversation about those decisions is not equal to the stakes. You suspect, correctly, that something is missing from how the country is currently thinking about phones, apps, AI, and teachers.

This is that policymaker's book. It is also the principal's book, the superintendent's book, and — though it was not written primarily for them — the teacher's book. Teachers will recognize the arguments. They have been making most of them for years. What this book offers teachers is the consolidated research backing for what they already know.

### Movement I — The Wrong Answers

#### Chapter 1: The Ban (`chapters/01-the-ban.md`)
*Why removing the phone was right, what removing the phone alone cannot do, and who pays when we stop there.*

Opens on the Figlio and Özek 2025 NBER finding that the first year of Florida's bell-to-bell phone ban produced a sixteen-percent suspension increase for Black students with near-zero effect for white and Hispanic peers, before test scores improved for all groups in year two. The ban is the correct response to a documented harm; the disciplinary costs are front-loaded; the students who can least afford a year of elevated suspensions absorb them while they wait for benefits that arrive later. The chapter walks through the FCC's September 2025 rescission of E-Rate funding for off-premises hotspots, the NCES data on smartphone-only home internet access by income, the New York 2015 ban that was reversed in 2024, and the international evidence from the United Kingdom, Spain, Norway, and Sweden. The argument's turn: the ban is not the problem; what we did instead of training the teacher is the problem.

#### Chapter 2: The App (`chapters/02-the-app.md`)
*What the EdTech industry got right, what it got wrong, and the finding nobody is advertising.*

The educational technology industry is correct that AI can improve student outcomes. It is wrong about the mechanism. RAND Europe's January 2026 synthesis of six years of EdTech trials: teacher training and support consistently made or broke the success of interventions. The Bastani PNAS finding (June 2025) is the sharpest edge of this evidence. Students given unguided access to AI improved 48 percent during practice sessions, then scored 17 percent below students who had never used AI when tested without it. The mechanism: cognitive offloading. The chapter then turns to the WestEd ASSISTments trial as the affirmative case: free software with strong teacher professional development produced significant gains at a fraction of typical EdTech spending. The tractor argument: the app is the tractor; a tractor without a trained operator does not farm itself. The chapter's turn: platform quality sets the ceiling; teacher training determines whether students reach it.

#### Chapter 3: The Clause That Runs the Country (`chapters/03-the-clause-that-runs-the-country.md`)
*Four words in every phone ban law — and why no one has finished writing them.*

The educational exemption — *except for educational purposes* — appears in thirty-three state phone-ban statutes. Not one of them has been substantively defined. The chapter walks through the actual statutory language in California, Florida, Louisiana, Virginia, Texas, Ohio, Arkansas, South Carolina, New York, and Indiana, where the most thoughtful version of the exemption — a 2028 transition from school-supplied-or-sanctioned-personal devices to school-supplied-only — sits inside SB 78. The vagueness doctrine in constitutional law names what is happening here, even though it probably does not formally apply. The chapter argues that the exemption is doing all of the work in every phone ban in America; it is the hinge between a technology ban and a technology policy; and it has been left blank because the call cannot be made from outside the classroom. Only a trained human inside the room can make it. The exemption has handed the call back to the teacher without providing the training that would let her exercise it well.

### Movement II — The Evidence

#### Chapter 4: The Teacher Variable (`chapters/04-the-teacher-variable.md`)
*What forty years of education research shows about the one thing that actually predicts whether students learn.*

Hattie's *Visible Learning* synthesis — 2,100-plus meta-analyses, roughly 130,000 underlying studies, more than 300 million students — produces a ranked list of what predicts student outcomes. The top-tier influences are teacher-dependent: collective teacher efficacy, comprehensive professional development, teacher-student relationships, feedback. Technology in subjects sits above the hinge point but below those teacher-mediated variables. The chapter engages the methodology critiques directly — Slavin, Wecker, Simpson, Bergeron and Rivard — and shows that the ranking direction survives the critiques even where the magnitudes do not. It then walks through the independent value-added literature (Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff 2014; Rivkin, Hanushek, and Kain 2005) which reaches the same conclusion from different methods and different data. The chapter ends with the cost-effectiveness argument: when the threshold framing is dropped, a per-standard-deviation comparison reveals that AI-tutoring deployments are on the order of forty times more cost-effective per unit of student learning than class-size reductions — and that the right policy question has never been whether an intervention clears an arbitrary threshold but what each marginal dollar purchases in student learning.

#### Chapter 5: The Professional Tool (`chapters/05-the-professional-tool.md`)
*What surgeons, farmers, scientists, and artists can teach us about what happens when trained professionals get the right instruments.*

Cross-domain evidence. Laparoscopic surgery in 2001 — the instrument was better, the untrained hand made it dangerous; structured simulation training transformed outcomes (Aggarwal et al. 2007, Cook et al. 2011 meta-analysis, NOVICE Trial 2025). Precision agriculture (Fiechter, Brewer, Ifft, and Boehlje 2025, *Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics*) — a 22 percent return on investment, but only for trained farmers; untrained farmers see no return; the catch-up effect is largest for less-efficient farms. The Tutor CoPilot Stanford RCT (Wang, Ribeiro, Robinson, Loeb, and Demszky 2024) — gains largest for tutors with the least experience; the tool levels the floor. Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023, *NBER WP 31161*) on customer-service productivity. The cross-domain pattern is identical: the trained professional with the tool outperforms both the untrained professional with the tool and the trained professional without it; the gains are largest for practitioners at the lower end of the skill distribution. Applied to teaching: the teacher with Claude is not a smaller version of an AI platform. She is a different instrument.

#### Chapter 6: The AI Dividend (`chapters/06-the-ai-dividend.md`)
*Six weeks per year, and what trained teachers do with the time.*

The Gallup-Walton 2025 survey of 2,232 U.S. public school teachers (nationally representative): teachers using AI weekly reclaim 5.9 hours per week, the equivalent of six full school weeks per year. Where the time goes: 60 percent report more time for improving teaching and learning; 60 percent report more time for individualized feedback; 57 percent report better insights about student learning data. The EEF ChatGPT RCT (259 teachers, 68 secondary schools, 2024): teachers using ChatGPT with a user guide reduced lesson preparation time by 31 percent with quality maintained per blind review. The Demszky et al. CS-course study: trained instructors increased their uptake of student contributions by twenty-four percent. The within-thirty argument: the time saved is not used to scale teachers to more students; it is used to do more for the students they already have. The AI dividend is the mechanism that makes the high-effect-size relational interventions Hattie identifies achievable within a normal teaching week.

#### Chapter 7: The Cognitive Offloading Problem (`chapters/07-the-cognitive-offloading-problem.md`)
*What happens when students get the tool without the teacher.*

The Bastani PNAS finding (122(26), e2422633122, June 25, 2025) — full deep dive. Pre-registered field RCT, approximately one thousand students, Turkish high school, mathematics, four 90-minute sessions, three arms (no AI, GPT Base, GPT Tutor with scaffolding). Headline results: GPT Base +48 percent practice / −17 percent exam; GPT Tutor +127 percent practice / null exam effect. The neurobiology chain — Risko and Gilbert (2016) on cognitive offloading; Sparrow, Liu, and Wegner (2011) on Google effects on memory; Bjork and Bjork (1992) on storage versus retrieval strength and desirable difficulties; Roediger and Karpicke (2006) on the testing effect; Karpicke and Blunt (2011) on retrieval practice versus concept mapping; Schultz, Dayan, and Montague (1997) on dopamine prediction error; Sweller (1988) on cognitive load. The fluency trap: AI produces smooth, well-structured answers; processing smooth answers feels like understanding; it is not understanding. The chapter engages the counter-evidence (Khanmigo WestEd 0.15 SD, Kestin Harvard physics RCT, Selwyn's recursive-narrowing critique) and lands the policy implication: the solution is not to remove AI; it is to train the teacher to design the scaffolded environment that produces the GPT Tutor outcome rather than the GPT Base outcome.

### Movement III — The Work

#### Chapter 8: Train Like a Doctor (`chapters/08-train-like-a-doctor.md`)
*What medicine built for continuous professional development, and how to build it for teaching.*

A doctor who last updated her knowledge in 2019 cannot practice medicine in 2026. The infrastructure for continuous professional development exists in medicine; it does not exist in teaching. The chapter walks through the ACCME (Accreditation Council for Continuing Medical Education) standards, state continuing medical education requirements, and specialty-board Maintenance of Certification, and proposes the analog AI+1 teacher-training model: sustained, subject-specific, grade-level-specific professional development; AI fluency updated as models evolve; specialization tracks; demonstrated competence rather than seat-time. Finland's tutor-teacher (*tutoropettaja*) system, funded by the Finnish National Agency for Education, is the closest existing implementation — with DigiErko, the post-master's specialization credential, as the medicine-equivalent specialty-board layer. The chapter then surveys what Singapore, Estonia, and Australia are building. It engages four objections in detail: teacher saturation (with explicit acknowledgment of the cumulative-expansion problem and the payback-period argument the chapter's recommendations turn on), the existing PD spend being badly designed, the documented capture problems in medical CME, and the worry that generative AI is a passing fad. The chapter closes with the *Monday morning* section — five concrete actions a district leader can take this week.

#### Chapter 9: Phone Fridays and the Room the Teacher Runs (`chapters/09-phone-fridays-and-the-room-the-teacher-runs.md`)
*What teacher discretion over technology actually looks like, and the infrastructure that makes it possible.*

The phone ban debate has offered two models of technology governance — total prohibition and total permissiveness. Both are wrong. The right model is professional discretion: teachers making technology decisions based on their knowledge of the specific students, the specific lesson, and the specific moment. Phone Fridays is the design pattern — dedicated structured technology time during which devices are available under teacher direction; lockers for the rest of the week — not a rule about which devices are permitted but a space for teacher judgment to operate. The chapter walks through what discretion without training looks like (inconsistency), what discretion with training looks like (the Tier 1 / Tier 4 distinction from the Irreducibly Human framework), and the infrastructure required to make the model work: trained teachers, a clear institutional definition of the educational exemption, administrative support that trusts teacher judgment, and school-level flexibility. The chapter's *Monday morning* section: how to start within current legal constraints, in any state, this week.

#### Chapter 10: Three Honest Measures (`chapters/10-three-honest-measures.md`)
*Why student satisfaction is not enough, and a framework that could actually work.*

The student evaluation of teaching as currently administered correlates with subsequent student learning at approximately zero (Uttl, White, and Gonzalez Morales 2017, *Studies in Educational Evaluation*; the most methodologically careful synthesis of the SET literature ever published; corrected for publication bias and vendor-funded studies via Uttl and Cnudde 2019, *PeerJ*). The instrument simultaneously fails to measure learning, incentivizes grade inflation, and is structurally biased against the teachers most likely to be rigorous (Boring, Ottoboni, and Stark 2016; MacNell, Driscoll, and Hunt 2015 on name-swap experiments; Carrell and West 2010 on the U.S. Air Force Academy natural experiment showing that the lower-rated instructors produced the better downstream learning). The American Sociological Association issued a 2019 statement reflecting these conclusions; seven years later, nothing structural has changed. The chapter then proposes three honest measures: Student Wellbeing (keep the existing satisfaction measure, used correctly), Student Learning Gains (controlled pre/post assessment not graded by the same teacher; the Finnish FINEEC sample-based model), and Teacher Learning and Improvement (the measure nobody currently takes, the precondition for everything else this book is proposing). *Monday morning*: how a district can pilot the three-measure framework in one school.

#### Chapter 11: The Equity Argument (`chapters/11-the-equity-argument.md`)
*Why every problem in this book lands hardest on the students who can least afford it.*

Every failure mode the book has documented compounds at the bottom. The phone ban without conditions: low-income students lose the device that was their primary internet access; the FCC E-Rate rescission removes the alternative. The educational exemption without trained teachers: the exemption works better in wealthy schools because those teachers have been trained to apply it. The AI dividend without training equity: the time savings accrue to teachers who received training; teachers in under-resourced schools who received no training save nothing. The cognitive offloading effect (Bastani −17 percent) is not randomly distributed; it falls hardest on students who lack the self-regulation skills to resist it, who are disproportionately from low-income and high-stress households (Evans and Schamberg 2009, *PNAS*; Blair and Raver on executive function development). The affirmative case: low-income students need more high-quality AI-supported teaching, not less. The Tutor CoPilot finding — the gains were largest for the lowest-rated tutors — is the clearest evidence. The chapter engages seriously with the AI-as-automated-inequality critiques (Cathy O'Neil, Ruha Benjamin, Safiya Noble; the Stanford HAI AI-tutor-bias literature; the AlphaSchool / 2-Hour Learning / Unbound Academy case studies of the model the book argues against) and shows that the right reading of that evidence is the argument *for* AI+1 deployment, not against AI in education generally. *Monday morning*: the equity audit a district can run on its current AI deployment in one afternoon.

#### Chapter 12: The Teacher This Book Is Arguing For (`chapters/12-the-teacher-this-book-is-arguing-for.md`)
*What the AI+1 classroom looks like, who is responsible for building it, and the work that remains.*

The capstone. Opens on the AI+1 classroom in concrete operational detail — what a trained teacher actually does on Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, and Friday with twenty-eight students and Claude in the room. Then the work that remains, broken down by audience. For policymakers: define the educational exemption before you enforce what it excludes; fund teacher AI training through Title II-A at the seventy-thirty ratio; add teacher learning and improvement to every evaluation framework. For district leaders: the phone ban without teacher training is the worst of both worlds; guarantee supervised quality technology time; adopt the three-measure framework. For school leaders: Phone Fridays; lockers; teacher discretion within a clear framework; train your teachers in AI the way you would expect your staff to be trained in any other professional tool. For teachers: the AI dividend is real; six weeks per year is real; the question is what you do with the time. The chapter closes on the line the entire book has been earning: *In 2025, America wrote thirty-one laws about phones. We should have written laws about teacher development.*

## How to read the book

Five hours end-to-end. The chapters are designed to be read in order — the arguments compound — but each stands on its own.

A district leader who needs to walk into a school board meeting tomorrow on phone policy can read Chapters 1 and 3 and have what she needs. An assistant superintendent revising the teacher evaluation framework can start at Chapter 10 and work backward. A foundation program officer assessing a teacher PD grant can start at Chapter 8.

Every chapter ends with three sections. *What would change my mind* names the specific evidence or argument that would force the chapter's central claim to revise. *Still puzzling* names the open questions the chapter raises but does not resolve. Movement III chapters add a *What this means Monday morning* section — a 500-word, actionable, specific note addressed to a school or district leader who wants to do something with what she just read.

Every contestable factual claim carries an inline link to a primary source. The references behind the book took eighteen months to read.

## A note about how this book was written

This book was written with the assistance of generative AI tools. Pretending otherwise would be both dishonest and bad pedagogy. The use was: synthesizing the research literature (more than two thousand peer-reviewed papers, meta-analyses, NBER working papers, and policy reports), drafting connective tissue between argument moves the author had already worked out, and stress-testing the argument against the strongest available counter-positions. The AI did not invent claims, write whole chapters from scratch, or substitute for the reading. Every contestable factual claim in the book was checked against primary sources by the author. Every inline citation was verified by the author. This is the same use pattern the book argues for in classrooms.

## What This Book Is

This book is twelve chapters of evidence, structured as an argument. It covers the policy backdrop (the smartphone bans, the educational exemption, the EdTech industry), the research evidence (what predicts learning, what professional training accomplishes, what the AI dividend looks like, what cognitive offloading does to students given AI without scaffolding), and the work that remains (how to train teachers in AI the way medicine trains doctors, how to govern technology through professional discretion rather than statute, how to evaluate teachers honestly, what equity requires, and what the AI-trained classroom looks like when the conditions are met).

The vocabulary the book teaches: *implementation gap*, *cognitive offloading*, *the fluency trap*, *the AI dividend*, *the within-thirty argument*, *the educational exemption*, *the AI+1 teacher*, *the three honest measures*. These are the terms a serious conversation about AI in schools requires. Most of them are not in the current policy vocabulary. They need to be.
